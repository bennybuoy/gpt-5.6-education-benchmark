| ID | Severity | Lesson location / quoted text | Problem | Recommended correction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **F1** | **Critical** | Cleanup: “Students should taste a small amount of the vinegar and baking soda mixture” | Students must never taste laboratory substances. The mixture may not be safely neutralised, and tasting is an unacceptable exposure risk. | Prohibit tasting. Dispose of materials using the school’s chemical-waste procedures and teacher instructions. |
| **F2** | **Critical** | Station D: “Students cut magnesium ribbon with scissors while holding it directly over a lit Bunsen burner” | This combines a cutting task with an exposed flame and puts students’ hands and combustible materials close to the burner. It creates avoidable burn and fire hazards. | Prepare magnesium in advance. If magnesium is demonstrated, it must be teacher-controlled using approved equipment, appropriate eye protection, and the school’s risk assessment. Do not handle or cut it over a flame. |
| **F3** | **Critical** | Station B: “Burning steel wool: Teacher burns steel wool” | Burning material presents fire, hot-material, smoke, and eye hazards. A rotating station format is not appropriate for uncontrolled student proximity to a flame. | Remove the burning activity or make it a separately risk-assessed, teacher-controlled demonstration with suitable apparatus, ventilation, PPE, and safe handling of hot residues. A safer non-flame alternative is preferable. |
| **F4** | **Major** | Starter: “A flame is present, therefore a chemical reaction is occurring… presence of a flame alone is sufficient evidence” | A flame alone is not proof of a chemical reaction. Flames can involve combustion, but visible light or heat is not by itself sufficient evidence; some reactions occur without a flame. | Ask students to identify multiple lines of evidence, such as formation of a gas, precipitate, colour change, temperature change, or new properties. Explain that evidence must be interpreted in context. |
| **F5** | **Major** | Activity 1: open beaker; “The mass decreases, which demonstrates that mass is destroyed in chemical reactions.” | The system is open. Gas produced by the vinegar–baking soda reaction escapes, so the measured mass decreases because matter leaves the system—not because mass is destroyed. | Use a sealed, teacher-approved system if demonstrating conservation of mass, or explicitly explain that an open-system measurement cannot test conservation of mass. Compare total mass before and after in a closed system. |
| **F6** | **Major** | Station A: “Melting ice is a chemical change because the ice changes into something different — water.” | Melting is a physical change. Ice and liquid water are the same substance, H₂O; only the state changes. | Correct the statement: melting ice is a physical change because no new substance forms and the change is reversible. |
| **F7** | **Major** | Station B: “The increase in mass when steel wool burns proves that oxygen gas is produced” | Burning steel wool consumes oxygen from the air and forms iron oxides. The mass increase is evidence that oxygen has been incorporated, not produced. | State that, in a suitable setup, the solid product may gain mass because oxygen from the air is added. Avoid claiming this alone proves the full reaction equation. |
| **F8** | **Major** | Station C: “If universal indicator turns red, this alone confirms the substance is hydrochloric acid.” | Universal indicator indicates acidity and gives an approximate pH range; it cannot identify a specific acid. Other acidic substances can also turn it red. | Say that the solution is strongly acidic or has a low pH. Identification of hydrochloric acid would require additional evidence and appropriate analytical methods. |
| **F9** | **Major** | Station E: “Rusting… requires no activation energy because it occurs at room temperature.” | Rusting still has an activation-energy barrier. Room temperature provides sufficient energy for the reaction to occur slowly; it does not mean the required activation energy is zero. | Explain that all reactions require activation energy. Rusting is slow at room temperature, and factors such as water and oxygen affect its rate. |
| **F10** | **Major** | Plenary: “A reaction that releases heat needs no activation energy to start because it gives out energy on its own.” | Exothermic reactions can still require activation energy to begin. The energy released after initiation does not remove the initial energy barrier. | Use the reaction profile to distinguish activation energy from overall energy change. Explain that a catalyst lowers activation energy but does not change the overall energy released. |
| **F11** | **Major** | Assessment: “Chemical reactions always produce a new substance. True or False?” Students selecting “True” receive full marks. | The answer is scientifically defensible as a definition of a chemical reaction, but the item is too simplistic to assess the stated objectives. It does not test conservation of mass, activation energy, or distinguishing physical from chemical change, and it rewards guessing without reasoning. | Use several short questions requiring justification and application to examples. Include open- versus closed-system mass reasoning, activation-energy interpretation, and classification with evidence. |
| **F12** | **Major** | Activity 2: “Each group visits five stations, spending approximately 5 minutes at each station” | Five stations cannot fit into 20 minutes once movement, instructions, observations, recording, and safety checks are included. The pace risks rushed or unsafe work. | Reduce the number of stations, use teacher-led demonstrations, or allocate realistic time for transitions and recording. |
| **F13** | **Major** | Station rotation including burning, magnesium, indicator, and unknown solutions | The plan does not specify adequate PPE, supervision, risk assessment, burner controls, spill response, ventilation, or clear student roles. “Unknown solution” may also be inappropriate without confirming its safety and composition. | Include explicit safety instructions, eye protection where required, teacher control of flames and reactive materials, suitable labelled solutions, and a risk assessment before the lesson. |
| **F14** | **Minor** | Activity 1: “Add vinegar” to baking soda in an open beaker on a balance | The vigorous fizzing may spill or wet the balance, and an open beaker is a poor setup for accurate mass measurement. | Keep liquids away from the balance or use suitable protective equipment and a teacher-controlled demonstration. Explain that the balance reading is affected by escaping gas. |
| **F15** | **Minor** | Cleanup: “All leftover liquids and solids from all stations should be poured together down the classroom sink.” | Combining unknown materials is poor laboratory practice and may create reactions, block drains, or breach disposal rules. | Keep substances separated, labelled, and disposed of according to the school’s laboratory-waste procedures. Students should not decide disposal by pouring everything together. |
| **F16** | **Minor** | Starter: “Light a candle” | An open flame adds a safety and classroom-management risk without being necessary to introduce chemical reactions. | Replace it with a safe visual demonstration or teacher-controlled observation, or use a non-flame example of a chemical change. |
| **F17** | **Minor** | Whole lesson structure | The plan allocates 5 minutes to the starter, 10 to a demonstration, 20 to stations, 10 to plenary, 10 to assessment, and 5 to cleanup, leaving little flexibility for transitions, safety briefings, recording, or equipment checks. | Build in explicit transition and safety time, and simplify the practical work so the learning objectives—not movement between activities—drive the timing. |

## Deliverable 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

- **Starter:** Replace the flame-based claim with examples of both physical and chemical changes. Ask students what evidence would support formation of a new substance, correcting **F4** and **F16**.
- **Demonstration:** Do not claim that mass is destroyed. Use a teacher-controlled, school-approved closed-system demonstration, or explain that escaping gas accounts for the lower reading in an open system (**F5**, **F14**).
- **Stations:** Remove or replace the unsafe burning-steel-wool and student magnesium procedures (**F2**, **F3**). Correct the ice, indicator, steel-wool, and rusting explanations (**F6–F9**). Use only labelled, school-approved materials with appropriate PPE and supervision (**F13**).
- **Plenary:** Use the reaction profile to contrast activation energy with energy released or absorbed. Explicitly state that exothermic reactions still require activation energy (**F10**).
- **Assessment:** Replace the single true/false item with short questions requiring classification and justification, conservation-of-mass reasoning in open and closed systems, interpretation of an energy profile, and pH interpretation (**F11**).
- **Timing:** Reduce the number of activities or use teacher-led demonstrations. Allow time for safety instructions, transitions, observations, and recording (**F12**, **F17**).
- **Cleanup:** Ban tasting. Keep substances separate and follow the school’s disposal procedures; students should not pour mixed materials into the sink (**F1**, **F15**). Perform a teacher-led equipment and spill check before dismissal.
