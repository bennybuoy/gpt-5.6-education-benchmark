## Deliverable 1 — Severity-Ranked Finding Ledger

### Critical findings

**F1 — Critical**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Cleanup — “Students should taste a small amount of the vinegar and baking soda mixture to confirm it is safe and neutralised.”  
- **Problem:** Students must never taste laboratory mixtures. Taste cannot establish safety or neutralisation, and the approximate quantities mean the mixture may still be acidic or alkaline.  
- **Recommended correction:** Prohibit ingestion. If acidity is relevant, use an approved teacher-controlled pH test or indicator; determine safety from the risk assessment and chemical-handling procedures, not by tasting.

**F2 — Critical**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Cleanup — “All leftover liquids and solids from all stations should be poured together down the classroom sink.”  
- **Problem:** Indiscriminate mixing may cause unwanted reactions, gas or heat production, contamination, blocked drains, and inappropriate disposal of metals or other solids.  
- **Recommended correction:** Keep liquid, metal, solid, and unknown residues separate. Use labelled waste containers and dispose of materials according to school chemical-waste procedures. Only dispose of a known material down a sink when this is explicitly permitted.

**F3 — Critical**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station D — “Students cut magnesium ribbon with scissors while holding it directly over a lit Bunsen burner.”  
- **Problem:** This places hands and scissors over a flame and could ignite magnesium, produce hot fragments, cause burns or eye injury, and create an intense bright light.  
- **Recommended correction:** Remove this student procedure. Use pre-prepared materials, teacher-provided data, a simulation, or an approved teacher-controlled demonstration under the school’s risk assessment. Students must not cut or handle magnesium directly over a lit burner.

**F4 — Critical**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station B — “Teacher burns steel wool; students observe mass before and after.”  
- **Problem:** Burning steel wool can generate sparks and hot material. Running it as part of a rotating station gives insufficiently specified control of student distance, heatproof surfaces, fire precautions, and handling of the hot product. Weighing hot material would also be unsafe and could give unreliable readings.  
- **Recommended correction:** Replace the station with teacher-provided data, a video, or a fully risk-assessed teacher demonstration. Students should not handle hot steel wool; any measurement must use appropriate equipment after safe cooling and under teacher control.

**F5 — Critical**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station C — “Students add universal indicator to an unknown solution.”  
- **Problem:** An unidentified solution cannot be assumed safe for student handling. It could be corrosive, irritant, or otherwise unsuitable, and no risk assessment, labelling, or protective controls are provided.  
- **Recommended correction:** Use only known, labelled, low-hazard, teacher-prepared samples with appropriate PPE and supervision. Do not use the word “unknown” to mean genuinely uncharacterised or unassessed.

**F6 — Critical**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Activity 1 — “The mass decreases, which demonstrates that mass is destroyed in chemical reactions.”  
- **Problem:** Mass is not destroyed. In the open beaker, carbon dioxide leaves the measured system, and splashing or measurement effects may also reduce the balance reading. This directly contradicts the conservation-of-mass objective and the accurate teacher background note.  
- **Recommended correction:** Explain that the apparent loss is caused by matter leaving an open system. Demonstrate conservation using a suitable school-safe gas-retaining or closed-system arrangement, or teacher-provided data, comparing the mass of the complete system before and after. Avoid any rigid sealed arrangement that could build pressure.

### Major findings

**F7 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Starter — “A flame is present, therefore a chemical reaction is occurring” and “the presence of a flame alone is sufficient evidence.”  
- **Problem:** A flame in a candle is associated with combustion, but a flame alone is not a universal or sufficient test for a chemical change. A candle also involves the physical melting and movement of wax.  
- **Recommended correction:** Distinguish observations from explanations. Use the candle to contrast wax melting as a physical change with combustion of wax vapour as a chemical change, and state that a flame may be evidence of combustion in context but is not a standalone test.

**F8 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station A — “Melting ice is a chemical change because the ice changes into something different — water.”  
- **Problem:** Melting is a physical change of state. Ice and liquid water are the same substance, water, and no new substance is formed.  
- **Recommended correction:** Classify melting ice as a physical change and emphasise that the particles remain water molecules. Use the reversibility of freezing and melting as supporting evidence, while noting that reversibility alone is not the definition.

**F9 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station B — “The increase in mass when steel wool burns proves that oxygen gas is produced by the reaction.”  
- **Problem:** Oxygen is consumed during oxidation; oxygen atoms from the air become part of the iron oxide. The mass increase does not show that oxygen gas was produced.  
- **Recommended correction:** State that the steel wool gains mass because oxygen from the air is incorporated into the solid product. Explain that the total mass remains conserved when the whole system, including the air involved, is considered.

**F10 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station C — “If universal indicator turns red, this alone confirms the substance is hydrochloric acid.”  
- **Problem:** Red indicates a strongly acidic pH range, not a unique substance. Many acids can produce a red universal-indicator colour.  
- **Recommended correction:** Use universal indicator to estimate pH or compare acidity, not to identify hydrochloric acid. Teach that a single colour test generally provides limited evidence and does not establish chemical identity.

**F11 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station E — “Rusting … requires no activation energy because it occurs at room temperature.”  
- **Problem:** Reactions require activation energy, including reactions occurring at room temperature. Rusting can proceed slowly because some particles have enough energy to react; room temperature does not mean zero activation energy.  
- **Recommended correction:** Explain that activation energy is an energy barrier. Temperature, surface conditions, and catalysts can affect reaction rate, while a catalyst lowers the activation energy rather than eliminating it.

**F12 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station E — “Display pre-rusted and clean nails.”  
- **Problem:** Two displayed objects do not demonstrate the rate or conditions of rusting. The five-minute station cannot meaningfully observe rust formation, and no controlled variables are given.  
- **Recommended correction:** Use a longer-term prepared dataset or clearly labelled before-and-after evidence to discuss rusting. Treat the nails as an illustration, not as a valid short investigation of reaction rate or activation energy.

**F13 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Plenary — “A reaction that releases heat needs no activation energy to start.”  
- **Problem:** Exothermic reactions still require activation energy. “Exothermic” describes the net energy change, not the absence of an energy barrier.  
- **Recommended correction:** Use an exothermic profile showing the activation-energy rise from the reactants to the peak and a lower energy level for the products. Distinguish activation energy from the overall energy released.

**F14 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Starter and Activities 1–2 — use of a candle, Bunsen burner, burning steel wool, hot magnesium, glassware, and an acidic “unknown solution.”  
- **Problem:** The plan does not specify risk assessment, PPE, teacher control of flames and hot materials, safe positioning, spill response, or procedures for broken glass and chemical exposure. A rotating-station format increases supervision demands.  
- **Recommended correction:** Complete and follow the school risk assessment; use appropriate eye protection and other required PPE, teacher-controlled flames, safe separation from hot demonstrations, heatproof work surfaces, labelled chemicals, and established emergency procedures. Remove any student operation that cannot be safely supervised.

**F15 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Station worksheets — statements such as “Melting ice is a chemical change…” and “Rusting … requires no activation energy…”  
- **Problem:** Students are given conclusions rather than being asked to record observations, distinguish evidence from inference, and justify their reasoning. The incorrect statements are likely to embed misconceptions.  
- **Recommended correction:** Replace declarative worksheet claims with prompts for prediction, observation, classification, evidence, and explanation. Include questions that expose limitations of observations, such as why a colour change does not identify a particular acid.

**F16 — Major**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Assessment — “One multiple-choice question worth 100% of the lesson grade” and “No written explanation is accepted or required.”  
- **Problem:** A single binary item assesses only one narrow idea, allows guessing, and does not assess conservation of mass, physical versus chemical changes, or activation energy. The keyed answer “True” is defensible as a basic definition, but the format cannot reveal whether students understand it.  
- **Recommended correction:** Use several short formative questions, including classification with justification, an open-versus-closed-system mass explanation, and interpretation of an exothermic profile. Award credit for reasoning rather than only for selecting “True.”

### Minor finding

**F17 — Minor**  
- **Location/quoted text:** Activity 2 — “five stations, spending approximately 5 minutes at each station,” while the activity is allocated “20 minutes.”  
- **Problem:** Five stations at five minutes each require 25 minutes before transitions, instructions, PPE checks, and movement. The stated 60-minute schedule therefore cannot be followed safely as written.  
- **Recommended correction:** Reduce the number of stations, shorten and simplify them, or allocate additional time. Include explicit transition and safety-check time without reducing cleanup.

## Deliverable 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

- **Starter — change inferences and safety (F7, F14):** Use a teacher-controlled visual, photograph, or video if an open flame is not required. Ask students to separate observations from conclusions, and distinguish wax melting from combustion. Do not teach that a flame alone proves a chemical change.

- **Demonstration — correct conservation of mass (F6, F14):** Replace the open-beaker claim with a suitable school-safe closed or gas-retaining comparison, or use reliable teacher-provided data. Emphasise that carbon dioxide leaving an open system explains a lower measured mass; mass has not been destroyed.

- **Stations — revise content and remove unsafe actions (F3–F5, F8–F12, F14–F15):**
  - **A:** Label melting ice as a physical change involving the same substance, water.
  - **B:** Remove student access to burning steel wool. Use data, simulation, or a teacher-controlled approved demonstration; explain that oxygen is consumed and incorporated into the oxide.
  - **C:** Use known, labelled, low-hazard samples. Teach that universal indicator estimates pH and does not identify hydrochloric acid.
  - **D:** Remove the student magnesium-and-Bunsen procedure. Substitute pre-prepared evidence, data, or a teacher-controlled approved demonstration.
  - **E:** Use longer-term data or prepared evidence for rusting. State that rusting has activation energy even though it occurs slowly at room temperature.
  - Provide observation/evidence/explanation prompts rather than worksheets containing conclusions.

- **Plenary — correct the energy model (F11, F13):** Use a clearly labelled exothermic reaction profile showing both activation energy and the overall energy decrease. Explain that an exothermic reaction can release energy overall while still requiring an initial energy input.

- **Assessment — sample all objectives (F16):** Replace the single 100% True/False item with a short set of questions requiring explanations: classify physical and chemical changes, explain an open-system mass decrease, and identify activation energy on a reaction profile. Use the responses formatively and award marks for reasoning.

- **Timing — make the schedule feasible (F17):** Keep the 60-minute structure only after reducing the practical to a manageable number of safe stations, with planned transitions and instructions. A workable allocation is 5 minutes starter, 10 minutes demonstration, 20 minutes stations, 10 minutes plenary, 10 minutes assessment, and 5 minutes cleanup.

- **Cleanup — safe disposal and no ingestion (F1, F2, F14):** Prohibit tasting. Students should not combine residues. Keep liquids, solids, metals, and any potentially contaminated materials in separate labelled containers for teacher-managed disposal under school procedures. Confirm flames are extinguished, hot apparatus is safely dealt with, benches are clean, and students wash their hands.
