# DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

## Starter
- Retain the candle only as a controlled teacher demonstration if permitted by the school risk assessment.
- Ask students to identify separate observations: wax melting is a **physical change**, while wax combustion is a **chemical reaction**.
- Do not present a flame as a definitive test. Ask what evidence would support the formation of new substances and distinguish observation from inference. **(F4, F11, F15)**

## Demonstration
- Reframe the vinegar–bicarbonate demonstration as an **open-system mass** investigation: the measured mass falls because gas leaves the apparatus, not because matter is destroyed.
- Explicitly identify the system boundary and account for the escaped gas.
- If a closed-system comparison is desired, use only a school-approved gas-retaining method that cannot develop unsafe pressure, or use a simulation/particle model instead. **(F5, F12, F15)**

## Stations
- Reduce the number of stations or convert hazardous stations into teacher demonstrations, images, data sets, or simulations. Do not operate multiple unsupervised heat/flame stations. **(F1, F4, F16)**
- **Melting ice:** classify it as physical because the substance remains water. **(F8)**
- **Steel wool:** teach that oxygen is consumed and incorporated into iron oxide. Keep the activity teacher-controlled, use approved safety controls, and weigh only after complete cooling. **(F4, F7, F13)**
- **Universal indicator:** use a staff-known, labelled, low-hazard sample. Conclude only that the result indicates an approximate acidic pH, not that the substance is hydrochloric acid. **(F9, F10)**
- **Magnesium:** remove the student flame procedure. Use a safe model, simulation, video, or—only if approved—a fully teacher-controlled demonstration. **(F1, F4)**
- **Rusting:** explain that rusting is slow but still has an activation-energy barrier; ambient conditions allow some successful particle collisions. **(F6)**
- Require prediction, recorded observations, evidence-based conclusions, and a review of any changed ideas. **(F15)**

## Plenary
- Use an accurately labelled exothermic reaction profile.
- Distinguish:
  - activation energy: the initial energy barrier;
  - exothermicity: products are at lower energy than reactants and energy is transferred to the surroundings.
- Revisit rusting and combustion to show that reaction speed, activation energy, and overall energy transfer are different ideas. **(F6)**

## Assessment
- Replace the single all-or-nothing item with several diagnostic questions addressing all objectives.
- Include:
  - classification of physical and chemical changes with reasons;
  - explanation of apparent mass change in an open system;
  - interpretation of an activation-energy profile;
  - correction of an indicator or steel-wool claim.
- Require brief explanations and use the responses to identify misconceptions rather than assigning the entire lesson grade from one response. **(F7–F9, F11, F14, F15)**

## Timing
- Plan at least five minutes per station plus transitions, or use fewer stations.
- Include time for safety briefing, PPE, equipment cooling, discussion, assessment, and supervised cleanup.
- Reallocate the excessive true/false-question time to explanation and feedback. **(F16–F18)**

## Cleanup
- Extinguish and secure all heat sources first; leave hot equipment undisturbed until fully cooled.
- Keep waste types separate and labelled, and use technician-approved disposal routes.
- Do not place solids or unapproved chemical mixtures down the sink.
- Prohibit tasting under all circumstances. Finish with equipment checks and handwashing. **(F2, F3, F4, F13, F18)**
