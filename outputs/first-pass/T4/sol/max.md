# DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

## Starter

- Use a photograph or short video of a candle rather than a live flame.
- Ask students to record **observations** separately from **inferences**.
- Establish that wax melting or vaporising is physical, while wax combustion is chemical; do not treat a flame as a universal proof of reaction. **(F5, F11, F15)**

## Demonstration

- If the original gas-forming reaction is retained, conduct it only as a teacher-controlled, risk-assessed demonstration with appropriate eye protection and spill containment.
- Ask students to predict the balance result and define what is included in the measured system.
- Explain that an open-system reading can fall because carbon dioxide leaves.
- Compare this with prepared data or a school-approved closed-system apparatus designed to accommodate gas production safely. Do not use an improvised rigid sealed container. **(F5, F7, F13)**

## Stations

Consolidate the five stations into three aligned, flame-free stations:

1. **Physical versus chemical change:** melting ice and particle diagrams; conclude that the identity remains H₂O. **(F9)**
2. **Mass and system boundaries:** analyse open/closed reaction data and prepared steel-wool oxidation data; identify oxygen as a reactant entering the sample. No live burning. **(F5, F7, F10, F13)**
3. **Activation energy and rusting:** label an energy profile and analyse controlled, pre-collected rusting data or enclosed examples. Explain the roles of water, oxygen, ambient thermal energy, and the activation barrier. **(F8, F16)**

Additional station decisions:

- Delete the magnesium-and-burner task entirely. **(F1)**
- Remove the universal-indicator station from this lesson because pH identification is outside the objectives. If taught later, use only teacher-known, labelled, school-approved solutions and conclude only “acidic pH range,” not “hydrochloric acid.” **(F6, F12, F15)**
- Give students a structured prediction–evidence–reasoning sheet and clear group roles. **(F15)**

## Plenary

- Have students label reactants, products, activation energy, and overall energy change on an exothermic profile.
- Require them to explain that products being lower in energy does not remove the peak.
- Use rusting as the counterexample: a reaction may occur slowly at room temperature while still having an activation-energy barrier. **(F8, F15)**

## Assessment

Use three brief, low-stakes prompts:

1. Classify ice melting and one candle process, with a reason based on chemical identity.
2. Explain an apparent mass decrease in an open gas-forming reaction and predict the closed-system result.
3. Label activation energy on an exothermic profile and explain why the reaction may still require initiation.

Require a concise written, diagrammatic, or otherwise appropriately accommodated justification. Do not base the entire lesson grade on one True/False response. **(F14)**

## Timing

A workable 60-minute allocation is:

| Segment | Time |
|---|---:|
| Starter | 5 minutes |
| Conservation demonstration | 10 minutes |
| Three stations, including transitions | 24 minutes |
| Plenary | 7 minutes |
| Exit assessment | 8 minutes |
| Cleanup and handwashing | 6 minutes |
| **Total** | **60 minutes** |

Pre-stage materials and waste containers, and include station movement and safety instructions within the station block. **(F17)**

## Cleanup

- Prohibit tasting of any laboratory material. **(F2)**
- Keep all waste streams separate and labelled; students should not combine residues. **(F3)**
- Have the teacher or technician dispose of each stream according to school policy and relevant safety information. Do not place solids, metals, or unidentified liquids in the sink. **(F4)**
- Account for equipment, clean spills appropriately, and require handwashing after practical work. **(F5, F6)**
