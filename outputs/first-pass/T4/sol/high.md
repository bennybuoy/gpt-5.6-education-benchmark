## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

### Starter
- Replace the claim that a flame proves chemical change with an image, video, or carefully selected observations of a candle.
- Ask students to separate **physical changes** such as wax melting from **chemical combustion**, and require evidence for each classification.
- Do not rely on an open flame unless it is necessary, locally approved, and teacher-controlled.  
**Addresses F4, F11, F15.**

### Demonstration
- Retain the gas-producing reaction only as a school-approved conservation-of-mass demonstration or use validated prepared data.
- Compare the mass of the **same complete system**, including all reactants and apparatus, before and after.
- Explicitly explain that an open vessel may show an apparent decrease because gas leaves the measured boundary; matter has not been destroyed.
- Do not improvise a pressure-containing setup.  
**Addresses F6 and F10.**

### Stations
Use fewer, low-risk, purposeful stations:

1. **Melting ice:** classify it as a physical state change and explain that no new substance forms.
2. **Universal indicator:** use teacher-prepared, coded, low-hazard solutions; infer only an approximate pH range, not a chemical identity.
3. **Iron oxidation:** use pre-rusted samples and prepared steel-wool mass data. Explain that oxygen is consumed and incorporated into iron oxide and that rusting requires oxygen and water.
4. **Reaction evidence and activation energy:** use diagrams, data, or recorded observations for magnesium or other combustion reactions rather than student flame work.

Remove the magnesium-over-burner procedure. Do not run simultaneous flame stations. Apply the school’s risk assessment, supervision requirements, and appropriate PPE to all remaining practical work.  
**Addresses F1, F4, F5, F7, F9, F12, F13, F15, and F16.**

### Plenary
- Correctly annotate an exothermic reaction profile:
  - reactant and product energy levels;
  - peak/transition region;
  - activation energy from reactants to the peak;
  - products at lower energy than reactants.
- Ask students to explain why a reaction can release energy overall while still needing an initial energy input.
- Contrast a rapid combustion reaction with slow rusting without claiming that rusting has zero activation energy.  
**Addresses F8 and F13.**

### Assessment
- Replace the single graded true/false question with a short set covering all objectives, for example:
  - classify melting and combustion with reasons;
  - explain an apparent mass decrease in an open reaction;
  - explain the mass increase when iron reacts with oxygen;
  - label activation energy on a reaction profile.
- Require brief justification so guessing does not receive full credit.
- Use responses diagnostically and provide correction or feedback rather than assigning the whole lesson grade from one binary item.  
**Addresses F6–F9, F14, and F15.**

### Timing
A workable 60-minute structure is:

- Starter — **5 minutes**
- Conservation demonstration — **9 minutes**
- Four stations, including transitions — **24 minutes**
- Plenary — **7 minutes**
- Exit assessment — **8 minutes**
- Cleanup and handwashing — **7 minutes**

This removes the five-stations-in-20-minutes contradiction and provides explicit transition and cleanup time.  
**Addresses F17 and F18.**

### Cleanup
- Enforce a strict no-tasting rule.
- Keep liquids, metals, combustion residues, and other solids in separate, labelled waste streams.
- Follow technician instructions and school disposal guidance; use the sink only for materials explicitly approved for drain disposal.
- Allow hot materials to be dealt with only under teacher control, then clean benches and require handwashing.  
**Addresses F2, F3, F4, F5, and F18.**
