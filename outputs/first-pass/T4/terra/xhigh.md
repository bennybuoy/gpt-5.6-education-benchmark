## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

### Starter
- Use a risk-assessed teacher demonstration or an image/video of a candle if a flame is not necessary. Do not present a flame as conclusive proof by itself.  
- Have students identify two changes: wax melting as physical and wax vapour combusting as chemical because new substances form. (F7, F6)

### Demonstration
- Replace the open-beaker mass demonstration with a school-approved, safely contained system that retains any gas while its total mass is compared before and after reaction. Avoid rigidly sealing a gas-forming reaction.  
- Explicitly define the system boundary: an open vessel loses measured mass when gas leaves, but total mass is not destroyed. (F2)

### Stations
- Run four manageable stations rather than five:
  1. **Melting ice:** classify as a physical change; water remains water. (F8)  
  2. **Steel wool evidence/data:** use results from a teacher-controlled demonstration or supplied data. Teach that iron combines with oxygen to form iron oxide; oxygen is consumed. (F9, F6)  
  3. **Universal indicator:** use a teacher-known, low-hazard solution. Interpret red as evidence of acidity, not hydrochloric acid specifically. (F10, F11)  
  4. **Rusting and activation energy:** use nails, images, or a card sort to show rusting as slow oxidation that still requires activation energy. (F12)  
- Remove the magnesium-over-Bunsen activity entirely. A reaction-profile or particle-collision task can address activation energy without the hazard. (F1)

### Plenary
- Use the exothermic reaction profile to show both the activation-energy peak and the lower energy of the products.  
- State clearly: exothermic reactions release energy overall, but still need activation energy to begin. (F5, F12)

### Assessment
- Replace the single high-stakes true/false item with several brief prompts requiring reasoning, for example:
  - classify a physical versus chemical change and justify it;
  - explain why an open-system balance reading may decrease;
  - interpret an exothermic reaction profile and identify activation energy.  
- Retain the “new substance” statement only as one small component, with an explanation required. (F13)

### Timing
Use a feasible 60-minute structure:
- 5 minutes starter  
- 3 minutes safety expectations and station instructions  
- 10 minutes conservation demonstration  
- 20 minutes for four stations, including movement time  
- 8 minutes plenary  
- 9 minutes exit assessment  
- 5 minutes cleanup  

This removes the impossible five-by-five-minute rotation and includes time for safe transitions. (F14)

### Cleanup
- Predetermine labelled waste routes for each material. Keep liquids, solids, metals, and combustion residues separate; use the sink only for materials explicitly approved by school procedures. (F3, F11)  
- Do not permit tasting or ingestion of any material. Ensure hot items are left until safe to handle and that students wash hands after practical work. (F4, F6)
