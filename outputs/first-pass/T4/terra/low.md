|---|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | **Critical** | Activity 1: “The mass decreases, which demonstrates that mass is destroyed in chemical reactions.” | This directly contradicts conservation of mass. The apparent loss occurs because carbon dioxide leaves the open beaker; mass is not destroyed. This is a fundamental misconception. | Explain that mass is conserved when the entire system is measured. Use a teacher-led, risk-assessed closed-system demonstration designed to retain reaction products, or use data showing total mass before and after. |
| F2 | **Critical** | Station D: “Students cut magnesium ribbon with scissors while holding it directly over a lit Bunsen burner.” | Students are handling scissors and metal directly above an open flame, creating burn, ignition, and dropped-hot-material risks. | Do not cut materials over a flame. | F3 | **Critical** | Cleanup: “All leftover liquids and solids from all stations should be poured together down the classroom sink.” | Mixing unknown or different chemicals and solids can create incompatible mixtures, block plumbing, and breach local waste-disposal requirements. | Keep station wastes separate, identify them, and dispose of each according to the school’s chemical hygiene and local disposal procedures. Do not combine wastes unless specifically authorised. |
| F4 | **Critical** | Cleanup: “Students should taste a small amount of the vinegar and baking soda mixture…” | Students must never taste laboratory materials. The mixture may contain contaminants, indicator residues, excess reactant, or material from non-food laboratory equipment. It may also not be neutral. | Prohibit tasting, eating, and drinking in the laboratory. Establish that chemical safety is determined by labels, risk assessment, and procedure—not taste. |
| F5 | **Major** | Starter: “A flame is present, therefore a chemical reaction is occurring”; “the presence of a flame alone is sufficient evidence…” | A flame may often accompany combustion, but its presence alone is not sufficient proof of a chemical change. | F6 | **Major** | Station A worksheet: “Melting ice is a chemical change…” | Melting ice is a physical change: the substance remains water, with a change of state only. | State that melting ice is a physical change because no new substance forms and the process is reversible by freezing. |
| F7 | **Major** | Station B worksheet: “The increase in mass when steel wool burns proves that oxygen gas is produced by the reaction.” | Oxygen is a reactant in steel wool burning, not a product. A mass increase for the wool occurs because oxygen from the air becomes part of the iron oxide. The total mass of a closed system remains constant. | Explain that iron combines with oxygen to form an oxide. Distinguish the mass of one object from the mass of the complete reaction system. |
| F8 | **Major** | Station C worksheet: “If universal indicator turns red, this alone confirms the substance is hydrochloric acid.” | Universal indicator indicates an acidic pH range; it cannot identify a particular acid. | F9 | **Major** | Station E worksheet: “Rusting … requires no activation energy because it occurs at room temperature.” | Rusting still has an activation-energy barrier. | F10 | **Major** | Plenary: “A reaction that releases heat needs no activation energy to start…” | Exothermic reactions release more energy than they absorb overall, but they still require activation energy to begin. | Use the reaction profile to show an activation-energy peak for both exothermic and endothermic reactions. | F12 | **Major** | Exit ticket: one True/False question worth “100% of the lesson grade”; “No written explanation is accepted or required.” | A single binary item cannot validly assess the three learning objectives. ## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

- **Starter:** Replace the claim that a flame alone proves chemical change with an evidence-based prompt. Students should distinguish observations from conclusions and identify indicators of new substances or changes of state. Avoid a live candle unless it is justified by the learning purpose and covered by controls. **[F5, F11]**

- **Demonstration:** Teach conservation of mass correctly: an open reaction vessel may show an apparent mass decrease because gas leaves, but matter is not destroyed. Use a teacher-led, risk-assessed method that measures the whole system and retains reaction products without creating pressure hazards. **[F1]**

- **Stations:**  
  - Classify melting ice as a physical change. **[F6]**  
  - If steel-wool oxidation is used, make it a controlled teacher demonstration or use observation/data. Teach that oxygen is consumed and becomes part of the oxide; distinguish object mass from total-system mass. **[F7, F11]**  
  - Describe red universal indicator as evidence of acidity only, not proof of hydrochloric acid. Use labelled, low-hazard solutions and normal laboratory precautions. **[F8]**  
  - Remove the magnesium-and-lit-Bunsen activity. Use pre-prepared materials, a teacher-controlled alternative, or a non-practical activation-energy task. **[F2]**  
  - Teach rusting as a slow chemical reaction that still requires activation energy and is affected by environmental conditions. **[F9]**

- **Plenary:** Use the exothermic reaction profile to show both the activation-energy barrier and the overall energy release. State clearly that exothermic reactions still need activation energy to begin. **[F10]**

- **Assessment:** Replace the single 100%-weighted True/False item with several brief questions: classify a physical versus chemical change, explain a mass change in an open system, and interpret an activation-energy diagram. Require concise reasoning to diagnose misconceptions. **[F12]**

- **Timing:** Make the rotation realistic—for example, use fewer stations or shorter, focused stations with transition time included within the 20-minute allocation. **[F13]**

- **Cleanup:** Keep all materials segregated and dispose of them using the school’s approved waste procedures. Do not mix station waste by default, and do not permit tasting of any laboratory material. Reinforce that apparent neutralisation is not established by taste or assumption. **[F3, F4]**
