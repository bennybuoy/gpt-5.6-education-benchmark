## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

### Starter
- Use the candle only as a teacher-managed, risk-assessed observation, or use an image/video if a live flame is unnecessary (**F5, F6**).
- Ask students to separate the physical changes of melting/vaporising wax from the chemical change of combustion. Emphasise that a flame is evidence to investigate, not proof by itself (**F5**).

### Demonstration
- Replace the claim that mass is destroyed with the correct explanation: the open beaker loses gaseous carbon dioxide, so the balance no longer includes all matter involved (**F4**).
- If demonstrating conservation experimentally, use a suitable non-pressurising setup that retains the gas within the measured system and has been approved through local risk assessment (**F4**).

### Stations
- Correct melting ice to a physical change: the substance remains water (**F7**).
- Present steel-wool oxidation as oxygen being **used** to form an oxide, not produced. Prefer teacher-provided data, images, or a teacher-managed demonstration rather than a student-operated combustion station (**F6, F8**).
- Use a teacher-known, low-hazard solution for indicator work. Teach that red indicates acidity, not specifically hydrochloric acid (**F9, F10**).
- Remove the magnesium-and-Bunsen activity entirely and replace it with a safe classification, particle-model, or reaction-profile task (**F1**).
- Correct rusting: it is slow but still has an activation-energy requirement; oxygen and water are involved in its rate (**F11**).

### Plenary
- Use the exothermic reaction profile to show both ideas: energy is released overall, but an activation-energy barrier must still be overcome (**F12**).
- Ask students to explain why an open-system balance result does not show destruction of mass (**F4**).

### Assessment
- Replace the single all-or-nothing true/false item with a short exit ticket covering all objectives (**F13**). For example:
  - classify one physical and one chemical change, with reasoning;
  - explain an apparent mass decrease in an open reaction;
  - identify activation energy on a simple reaction profile.
- Retain the idea that chemical reactions form one or more new substances, but require a brief explanation rather than rewarding guessing (**F13**).

### Timing
- Keep the overall 60-minute structure only by using, for example, four safe stations with short transition periods built into the rotation block, rather than five five-minute stations (**F14**).
- Do not add live-combustion practical work that requires additional setup, supervision, or cooling time unless the timetable is adjusted (**F6, F14**).

### Cleanup
- Do not allow tasting of any material (**F2**).
- Keep solid residues, indicator-containing liquids, metal/combustion residues, and other wastes separate. Dispose of them using school procedures rather than combining them or automatically using the sink (**F3**).
- Allow adequate supervised cleanup time and ensure all flame-related equipment is safely shut down and cooled before students leave (**F6, F14**).
