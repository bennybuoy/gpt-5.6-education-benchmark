## DELIVERABLE 1 — Severity-Ranked Finding Ledger

| ID | Severity | Location / quoted text | Problem | Recommended correction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | **Critical** | Activity 1: “The mass decreases, which demonstrates that mass is destroyed in chemical reactions.” | This is fundamentally incorrect. In the open beaker, carbon dioxide can leave the measured system, so the balance may show an apparent decrease. Matter is not destroyed. This directly contradicts conservation of mass. | State that the measured mass falls because gaseous product leaves an **open** system. Demonstrate conservation using a suitable closed system and compare the mass of the whole system before and after. |
| F2 | **Critical** | Station D: “Students cut magnesium ribbon with scissors while holding it directly over a lit Bunsen burner.” | Students are asked to manipulate a metal directly above an open flame. This creates burn, ignition, dropped-material, and fire risks. | Remove this instruction. Do not cut or handle magnesium over a flame. If magnesium is used at all, it must be teacher-prepared and handled only under a school-approved risk assessment; a non-flame alternative is preferable for this lesson. |
| F3 | **Critical** | Cleanup: “All leftover liquids and solids from all stations should be poured together down the classroom sink.” | Combining unknown solutions, indicator residues, metals, metal oxides, and solids is unsafe and unsuitable disposal practice. Solids should not be put down sinks, and wastes must not be mixed without knowing compatibility and disposal requirements. | Keep waste streams separate. Dispose of solids, metal-containing residues, indicator solutions, and aqueous solutions according to the school’s chemical-waste procedures and relevant safety information. |
| F4 | **Critical** | Cleanup: “Students should taste a small amount of the vinegar and baking soda mixture to confirm it is safe and neutralised.” | Students must never taste laboratory chemicals or reaction mixtures. The mixture may contain excess reactant, contamination, or residues. Taste cannot establish neutrality or safety. | Prohibit tasting of all laboratory materials. If pH is relevant, use an appropriate indicator or pH-measurement method, and explain that neutralisation depends on the relative amounts of reactants. |
| F5 | **Major** | Starter: “A flame is present, therefore a chemical reaction is occurring.” / “the presence of a flame alone is sufficient evidence” | This is overgeneralised and teaches an unreliable rule. A candle involves both physical changes (wax melting and vaporising) and chemical change (combustion of wax vapour). A flame is an observation, not a universal test for chemical change. | Use the candle, image, or video to distinguish the physical changes from combustion. Teach that chemical change is supported by evidence such as new substances, energy change, gas formation, or other observations considered together. |
| F6 | **Major** | Station A: “Melting ice is a chemical change because the ice changes into something different — water.” | Melting ice is a physical change. Ice and liquid water are the same substance, H₂O; only state and particle arrangement change. | Correct the worksheet: melting is a physical change because no new substance forms and the change is reversible by freezing. |
| F7 | **Major** | Station B: “The increase in mass when steel wool burns proves that oxygen gas is produced by the reaction.” | The explanation is backwards. The increased mass results from oxygen from the air being **consumed** and incorporated into iron oxide. | State that oxygen is a reactant in oxidation. If mass increases, it is because oxygen atoms from the air have become part of the solid product. |
| F8 | **Major** | Station B: teacher burns steel wool; students observe mass before and after | The proposed mass comparison may be unreliable if oxide or fragments are lost during burning. The activity also lacks stated controls for hot material, bright combustion, safe viewing distance, and protective equipment. | Use a teacher-controlled, risk-assessed demonstration with appropriate safety controls, or use reliable prepared data. Make clear that any mass comparison is valid only when all relevant product is retained. |
| F9 | **Major** | Station C: “If universal indicator turns red, this alone confirms the substance is hydrochloric acid.” | Universal indicator identifies an approximate pH range, not the identity of an acid. A red result indicates an acidic solution, potentially strongly acidic, but cannot distinguish hydrochloric acid from other acids. | State that red universal indicator shows the solution is acidic. Use a teacher-known, low-hazard solution and explain that further chemical evidence would be needed to identify a specific acid. |
| F10 | **Major** | Station E: “Rusting is a slow reaction that requires no activation energy because it occurs at room temperature.” | This is incorrect. Reactions, including rusting, have an activation-energy barrier. At room temperature, some particles have sufficient energy for slow reaction; water and oxygen also affect rusting. | Explain that rusting is slow because relatively few successful collisions occur under ordinary conditions. It still requires activation energy. |
| F11 | **Major** | Plenary: “A reaction that releases heat needs no activation energy to start because it gives out energy on its own.” | Exothermic reactions still generally require activation energy to begin. Energy is released overall only after the activation barrier has been overcome. | Use the reaction profile to show both the activation-energy barrier and the overall energy decrease. Explain that an ignition source, heating, light, or another trigger may be needed to initiate an exothermic reaction. |
| F12 | **Major** | Starter, Station B, and Station D: candle, burning steel wool, and Bunsen burner activities | The plan does not state essential risk controls for open flames, hot apparatus, burning metal, chemical handling, or student observation. | Include school-approved risk assessment and controls: teacher supervision, appropriate eye protection, safe distances, heatproof equipment, clear emergency arrangements, and use of only low-hazard materials. |
| F13 | **Major** | Exit ticket: one True/False question worth “100% of the lesson grade”; “No written explanation is accepted” | A single binary question cannot validly assess the three learning objectives. It rewards guessing and provides no evidence of reasoning, conservation-of-mass understanding, or activation-energy understanding. | Use several short prompts covering physical versus chemical change, mass in open versus closed systems, and activation energy. Require brief reasoning for at least some responses and use the results formatively. |
| F14 | **Minor** | Activity 2: five stations at “approximately 5 minutes” each, allocated 20 minutes | Five stations at five minutes each require at least 25 minutes, before allowing for movement, instructions, and reset. The real lesson duration exceeds 60 minutes. | Reduce the number of stations, shorten and simplify them, or revise the timetable. Build transition time into the station allocation. |
| F15 | **Minor** | Activity 1: adding vinegar to a beaker while it is on a top-pan balance | Adding reacting liquids directly on a balance risks spills, damages equipment, and can compromise readings. | Protect the balance and use a stable, suitable setup. Keep any reaction vessel secure and ensure spills cannot reach the balance. |

## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

- **Starter:** Replace the absolute “flame equals chemical reaction” claim with a short evidence-based discussion. If a candle is used, make it a teacher-controlled, risk-assessed observation; identify melting wax as physical change and combustion as chemical change. Emphasise that observations must be interpreted, not treated as a single universal rule. **[F5, F12]**

- **Demonstration:** Replace the open-beaker mass-loss claim with a suitable closed-system conservation-of-mass demonstration using low-hazard materials. Explicitly contrast it with an open system, where a gas leaving the apparatus causes an apparent mass decrease. Do not claim matter is destroyed. Protect the balance from spills. **[F1, F15]**

- **Stations:** Use a manageable set of scientifically correct, low-hazard stations:
  - Melting ice: classify as physical change because water remains H₂O. **[F6]**
  - Oxidation evidence: use a teacher-controlled, risk-assessed demonstration or prepared evidence; teach that oxygen is consumed and incorporated into the oxide. **[F7, F8, F12]**
  - Universal indicator: use a teacher-known, low-hazard acidic solution; conclude only that red indicates acidity, not hydrochloric acid specifically. **[F9]**
  - Rusting and activation energy: use images, samples, or data to explain that rusting is slow but still has an activation-energy barrier. **[F10]**
  - Remove the magnesium-over-Bunsen instruction entirely; do not ask students to cut or manipulate magnesium near a flame. **[F2]**

- **Plenary:** Correct the exothermic energy-profile explanation. Show that exothermic reactions release more energy than they absorb overall, while still requiring activation energy to begin. **[F11]**

- **Assessment:** Replace the one-question, all-or-nothing exit ticket with several brief questions: one physical-versus-chemical classification with justification, one conservation-of-mass explanation for an open system, and one activation-energy question based on an energy profile. Use responses to identify misconceptions rather than assigning a full lesson grade from guessing. **[F13]**

- **Timing:** Make the station block genuinely fit 20 minutes—for example, four short stations with instructions and transitions included. Retain the overall 60-minute structure only after allowing time for movement and reset. **[F14]**

- **Cleanup:** Ban tasting of any laboratory material. Keep solids, metal residues, indicator solutions, and aqueous wastes separate. Dispose of each only through approved school procedures; do not combine all materials or automatically pour them into the sink. **[F3, F4]**

- **Safety management:** Document and apply school-approved controls for all heating, open-flame, chemical, and observation activities, including supervision, eye protection, heatproof equipment, safe distances, and emergency readiness. **[F2, F8, F12]**
