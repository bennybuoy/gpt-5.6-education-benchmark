## 2. Worksheet repairs

**Title**  
**Evidence for Chemical Change**

**Learning intention**  
Distinguish observations from inferences and use multiple observations to decide whether evidence supports formation of a new substance.

**Success criteria**  
1. Label statements as observation or inference.  
2. Use at least two relevant observations before claiming a chemical change.  
3. State when evidence is insufficient.

**Worked example**  
Fizzing tablet in water:  
- **Observation:** Bubbles are seen and the temperature decreases.  
- **Inference:** A gas may have formed.  
- **Decision:** These observations alone do not identify the gas or prove that a new substance formed; more evidence is needed.

**Group task**  
1. Build an **Evidence–Inference–Decision** tool.  
2. For each of two case cards, record the relevant observations, identify the inference, and make an evidence-based decision.  
3. Test the tool against both cases, then revise one rule.

**Individual check — 5 marks**  
- State two relevant observations. **(2 marks)**  
- Distinguish an observation from an inference. **(1 mark)**  
- Give an evidence-based decision. **(1 mark)**  
- State a limitation or further evidence needed. **(1 mark)**

**Exit ticket**  
“One observation is ___; one inference is ___; my decision is ___ because ___; evidence still needed is ___.”

**Teacher note at foot**  
Award marks using the five-mark scheme above. A claim of chemical change must use at least two relevant observations. Accept that evidence may be insufficient.
