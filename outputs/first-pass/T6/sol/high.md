## 2. Worksheet repairs

**Title**  
Evidence for Chemical Change

**Learning intention**  
Distinguish observations from inferences and use multiple observations to decide whether evidence supports formation of a new substance.

**Success criteria**  
1. Label statements as observation or inference.  
2. Use at least two relevant observations before claiming a chemical change.  
3. State when evidence is insufficient.

**Worked example**  
Fizzing tablet in water:  
- **Observation:** Bubbles and temperature decrease.  
- **Inference:** Gas may have formed.  
- **Decision:** The observations alone do not identify the gas or prove a new substance; more evidence is needed.

**Group task**  
**18–33:** Build an Evidence–Inference–Decision tool. Include rules for identifying observations and inferences, using at least two relevant observations, and stating when evidence is insufficient.  
**33–43:** Test the tool against two cases. Revise one rule.

**Individual check — 5 marks**  
1. State two observations. **(2)**  
2. Distinguish an observation from an inference. **(1)**  
3. Give an evidence-based decision. **(1)**  
4. State one limitation or further evidence needed. **(1)**

**Exit ticket**  
“One observation is ___; one inference is ___; my decision is ___ because ___; evidence still needed is ___.”

**Teacher note**  
Mark the individual check as: two observations (2), observation/inference distinction (1), evidence-based decision (1), limitation or further evidence (1). Do not accept one observation alone as proof of chemical change.
