## 1. Inertia Pull: Cup, Cardboard and Mass

### Physical student action
Each group of 3–4 receives a plastic cup, a small cardboard rectangle, string, tape, a ruler, and 100 g, 200 g and 500 g masses.

1. Students place the cup upright on a bench, lay the cardboard across its opening, and place the 100 g mass centrally on the cardboard.
2. A short string is taped to one end of the cardboard. One student pulls the cardboard **slowly** sideways while the others observe what happens to the mass.
3. Students reset the apparatus and pull the cardboard **quickly and horizontally**. They repeat three times and record whether the mass moves sideways with the cardboard or drops into the cup.
4. They repeat the slow and quick pulls using the 200 g and 500 g masses.
5. Students then place each mass directly on the bench and use a spring scale to pull it horizontally. They note the scale reading when the mass first begins to move. These readings are treated cautiously because friction is also involved.
6. Groups finish by drawing force diagrams for:
   - the mass resting on the cardboard;
   - the instant the cardboard is pulled rapidly;
   - a mass being pulled across the bench.

Roles rotate between puller, observer, recorder and resetter.

### Science payoff
The rapid pull shows that an object at rest tends to remain at rest: the cardboard moves away, but the mass initially resists changing its motion and falls approximately vertically into the cup. The slow pull allows friction to act for longer and accelerate the mass sideways.

Comparing masses supports the idea that **mass measures inertia**: a greater mass has greater resistance to a change in motion. The spring-scale extension also introduces an important qualification: the measured force needed to begin sliding is affected by friction, so it is evidence of resistance in this setup rather than a pure measurement of inertia.

Students should arrive at:

> An object at rest remains at rest unless an unbalanced force changes its motion. Greater mass means greater inertia, although friction can affect how an inertia demonstration behaves.

### Evidence
Students record:

- a 3-by-2 results table for each mass, showing the outcome of slow and rapid pulls;
- the number of successful drops into the cup out of three rapid trials;
- the spring-scale reading at which each mass begins moving;
- a before-and-after sketch with arrows showing the cardboard’s motion and the mass’s path;
- one evidence statement, such as: “During a rapid pull, the cardboard moved sideways while the mass initially did not.”

### Feasibility
Seven or eight group stations fit on fixed laboratory benches. The apparatus uses only listed materials and can be prepared as one tray or pile per group. No item should be projected across the room: the pull is short, horizontal and directed into clear bench space.

Suggested main-block timing:

- 4 minutes: teacher models safe pulling and explains roles;
- 12 minutes: slow/fast card trials with three masses;
- 9 minutes: spring-scale comparisons;
- 6 minutes: diagrams and evidence statement;
- 4 minutes: return equipment and check conclusions.

Cleanup consists of removing tape and returning masses, cups, cardboard, string and scales. One teacher can supervise because all groups perform the same short procedure at their benches.

### Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students conclude that inertia is a downward force that “pushes” the mass into the cup, or that heavier objects have a special force holding them still.

**Mitigation:** Require students to describe the mass’s horizontal motion rather than invent an “inertia force”: “The mass initially remains at approximately the same horizontal position while the cardboard moves.” The teacher also explicitly identifies gravity as the unbalanced vertical force that makes the unsupported mass fall after the cardboard is removed. Spring-scale readings must be discussed as including friction, not as direct numerical measurements of inertia.

## 2. Equal and Unequal Pull Investigation

### Physical student action
Each group receives a toy car, two spring scales, string, tape, a ruler, masses and a stopwatch. Short strings are attached to opposite ends of the car. Students create a marked test lane along the bench using tape and a ruler.

1. **Balanced forces on a stationary car:**  
   Two students stand at opposite ends of the bench and attach spring scales to the car. They pull in opposite directions, adjusting until both scales show the same reading, such as 1 N. A third student observes the car against a tape mark. The group records whether its motion changes.
2. **Unbalanced forces on a stationary car:**  
   Students repeat with unequal readings, such as 1 N against 2 N. They record the direction in which the car begins moving and calculate the difference between the scale readings.
3. **Balanced forces while moving:**  
   One student gives the car a gentle push along the marked lane. Students observe that, once released, it continues moving for a distance before slowing. They identify that slowing means the forces are not perfectly balanced: friction and air resistance act opposite the motion.
4. **Changing speed and direction:**  
   Students apply a brief spring-scale pull:
   - in the direction the car is moving;
   - opposite its motion;
   - sideways to the car’s path where bench space allows.  
   They record whether the car speeds up, slows down or changes direction.
5. **Mass comparison:**  
   Students place 100 g and then 500 g on or securely against the car, using tape or a cardboard cradle if needed. One student aims to apply the same brief scale reading over the same marked distance. The group measures the time to travel a short lane or records the resulting speed change qualitatively.

### Science payoff
This mechanism directly distinguishes **force** from **motion**.

Students should arrive at these ideas:

- Equal forces acting in opposite directions are balanced, so there is no change in motion.
- An object can have balanced forces acting on it without being force-free.
- Unequal forces produce an unbalanced net force, causing the object to speed up, slow down or change direction.
- A moving object does not require a continuing forward force to keep moving; in the classroom it eventually slows because friction and air resistance provide an unbalanced force.
- For a similar applied force, the more massive car changes motion less because greater mass means greater inertia.

### Evidence
Each group records a table with:

- left spring-scale reading;
- right spring-scale reading;
- calculated force difference;
- initial state: stationary or moving;
- observed change: none, speed up, slow down or change direction;
- direction of the change.

Students also:

- measure the distance travelled after a standard gentle release before stopping, or time the car across a marked lane;
- compare the light and loaded car under the same target pull;
- sketch force-arrow diagrams for an equal-pull trial and an unequal-pull trial.

A suitable evidence pattern is:

| Left pull | Right pull | Difference | Observation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| 1 N | 1 N | 0 N | No change in motion |
| 1 N | 2 N | 1 N right | Car accelerates right |

### Feasibility
Each group remains at one fixed bench. Tape lanes should be short enough that cars cannot roll into sinks or off bench edges. Groups need only one car, two scales, two strings and shared masses. If there are insufficient spring scales for every group, four stations can begin with equal/unequal pulls while the others begin with loaded-car comparisons, then swap halfway.

Suggested timing:

- 5 minutes: setup, scale handling and role allocation;
- 10 minutes: balanced and unbalanced opposite pulls;
- 8 minutes: moving-car observations;
- 8 minutes: mass comparison;
- 4 minutes: diagrams and cleanup.

Roles are puller A, puller B, observer and recorder; in three-person groups, the observer also records. Tape marks make resetting quick.

### Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students treat equal scale readings as proof that “no forces are acting,” or believe that any moving car must have a forward force acting on it.

**Mitigation:** Require force diagrams containing both equal, opposite arrows and the label “net force = 0 N.” During the free-roll trial, students must distinguish the initial push from forces acting after release. The teacher asks: “Is the hand still touching the car?” and then identifies friction as the reason the real car slows rather than maintaining constant velocity indefinitely.

## 3. Human Motion Model with Evidence Cards

### Physical student action
Students use their bodies and string to model an object’s state of motion and the forces acting on it. This is not a race or free movement around the laboratory. Each group works in a tightly defined space beside or behind its bench.

Each group receives two short lengths of string, tape, a ruler and a set of teacher-written situation cards made from cardboard. Example cards are:

- stationary, equal pulls;
- stationary, stronger pull to the right;
- moving at constant speed, balanced forces;
- moving right, force to the right;
- moving right, force to the left;
- moving right, force upward on the group’s diagram;
- same pull, greater mass.

The physical sequence is:

1. One student is the **object**. Two others are **force agents**, holding loose string ends attached to or held near the object’s hands. They do not jerk or pull the student physically; string position and ruler-length arrows represent force size.
2. Tape marks define three positions along a one- to two-metre path: start, middle and finish.
3. For each card, the group first predicts whether the object’s motion will remain unchanged, speed up, slow down or change direction.
4. The force agents display equal or unequal “force arrows” with string. Equal string lengths in opposite directions represent balanced forces; unequal lengths represent an unbalanced force.
5. The object performs the predicted motion in controlled steps:
   - remain stationary;
   - walk with equal-length, evenly timed steps for constant velocity;
   - increase step length or frequency to represent speeding up;
   - decrease step length or frequency to represent slowing down;
   - turn to represent a change in direction.
6. A recorder uses a stopwatch to call equal time intervals, such as one-second beats, while another student marks or sketches the object’s position at each interval.
7. Roles rotate. In a “greater mass” round, two students stand shoulder-to-shoulder as one higher-mass object. The class treats this explicitly as a model: for the same displayed unbalanced force, the pair makes a smaller change in step speed or direction than the single student.

No student is dragged, restrained or tied. The string represents force arrows rather than transmitting substantial force.

### Science payoff
This mechanism makes students separate three quantities that are often confused:

1. the object’s current velocity;
2. the forces acting on it;
3. the change in velocity.

Students should arrive at:

- Balanced forces mean **no change in motion**, not necessarily no motion. The object may remain still or continue at constant velocity.
- An unbalanced force changes velocity. Depending on its direction relative to the motion, the object can speed up, slow down or turn.
- Greater mass means greater inertia, so the same unbalanced force produces a smaller change in motion.
- A force is not needed merely to “use up” motion; it is needed to change motion.

### Evidence
For each scenario, students complete an evidence card containing:

- initial state: at rest or moving;
- force-arrow lengths and directions;
- balanced or unbalanced classification;
- position at each one-second interval;
- observed change in motion;
- a short explanation linking net force to the change.

Students can sketch position strips such as:

- **Constant velocity:** dots equally spaced at equal time intervals;
- **Speeding up:** gaps between dots increase;
- **Slowing down:** gaps decrease;
- **Direction change:** the dot path bends.

Each group also records a prediction and outcome for the single-student and two-student “mass” model.

### Feasibility
This requires no open running area. Students take slow steps within a marked strip alongside a fixed bench, while most group members remain stationary. All seven or eight groups can work simultaneously because movement is local and controlled.

Situation cards can be cut from the listed cardboard before class, with one identical set per group. Setup consists only of placing two tape marks and distributing string, rulers, cards and stopwatches.

Suggested timing:

- 5 minutes: teacher demonstrates safe, symbolic string use;
- 18 minutes: groups enact four force-and-motion cards;
- 7 minutes: single-student versus paired-student mass model;
- 5 minutes: complete position sketches and select strongest evidence.

Cleanup requires collecting cards, string, rulers and stopwatches and removing tape. One teacher can scan group force-arrow arrangements and position records without resetting apparatus.

### Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students interpret the actors’ deliberate movements as experimental proof, even though people choose how to move and are not passive objects responding only to the displayed strings.

**Mitigation:** Label the activity explicitly as a **physical model**, not a force experiment. Predictions must be justified using Newton’s First Law before enactment, and the teacher asks students to identify the model’s limitation: the “object” supplies muscular forces and follows instructions. The model’s evidence is the consistency of force diagrams, timed-position patterns and predictions—not a claim that the string physically caused the actor’s acceleration. The synthesis should compare this model with real objects such as a toy car to prevent overgeneralisation.
