## 1. Constant-Force Car Comparison

### Physical student action
Each group receives a toy car, tape, a ruler, a stopwatch, a spring scale, and 100 g, 200 g and 500 g masses.

1. Students tape a ruler beside a clear section of bench to create a 60–80 cm test track.
2. One student pulls the empty car with the spring scale, attempting to maintain a constant reading such as 1 N. Another times how long the car takes to travel 60 cm, while a third records the result. Groups conduct three trials.
3. Students tape 200 g to the car and repeat using the same distance and 1 N pull. If the car cannot safely hold 200 g, they use 100 g.
4. They repeat with a larger load if the car remains stable.
5. Students calculate or compare average travel times. They do not need to calculate acceleration formally.
6. They then attach two spring scales to opposite ends of the stationary car. Two students pull in opposite directions:
   - first with equal readings, such as 1 N against 1 N;
   - then with unequal readings, such as 1 N against 2 N.
7. They record whether the car remains stationary and the direction in which it begins moving.
8. Finally, one student gives the car a short pull and releases it. The group observes that the car continues after the hand stops pulling, but eventually slows because friction provides an unbalanced force.

Roles rotate among puller, timer, scale reader and recorder.

### Science payoff
Students should arrive at three linked conclusions:

- The same unbalanced force produces a smaller change in motion for an object with greater mass. Therefore, greater mass means greater inertia.
- Equal opposing forces are balanced and do not change the motion of the car.
- Unequal opposing forces produce a net, or unbalanced, force, so the car changes its motion in the direction of the larger force.
- A moving object does not require a continuing forward force to remain moving. The car slows after release because friction acts, not because motion naturally “runs out.”

### Evidence
Each group produces:

- a table of car mass versus time to travel 60 cm under the same approximate spring-scale force;
- three repeated measurements for each mass;
- a table showing scale readings and outcomes, for example:

| Left force | Right force | Balanced? | Observed change |
|---|---:|---|---|
| 1 N | 1 N | Yes | Remained at rest |
| 1 N | 2 N | No | Moved right |

- a force-arrow sketch for one balanced and one unbalanced trial;
- an observation stating that the released car continued moving for a measurable distance before friction stopped it.

### Feasibility
Equipment can be placed in seven trays before the lesson, one for each group of four. All testing occurs along benches, with cars kept away from sinks and bench edges. A workable 35-minute structure is:

- 5 minutes: demonstrate how to pull while keeping the spring-scale reading approximately constant;
- 15 minutes: empty and loaded-car trials;
- 8 minutes: opposing-force trials;
- 5 minutes: release-and-coast observation and diagrams;
- 2 minutes: return equipment to trays.

Only one group moves a car along each bench section, while other group members remain in fixed roles. Tape marks define start and finish positions, reducing disputes and setup time. Cleanup consists of removing taped masses and returning equipment.

### Failure risk
The main risk is that students jerk the spring scale or vary the pulling force so much that the timing results do not show a consistent mass effect.

Mitigation:

- The teacher demonstrates a slow build-up to the target reading before timing begins.
- The scale reader calls “low,” “correct” or “high” during each run.
- Students repeat trials and compare the overall pattern rather than relying on a single time.
- If timing remains unreliable, groups record the distance travelled in a fixed two-second pull instead: the more massive car should undergo the smaller change in motion.
- The teacher explicitly identifies friction as an additional force and avoids claiming that the car is moving under a perfectly constant net force.

## 2. Predict–Observe–Explain Inertia Events

### Physical student action
Groups complete a sequence of short physical events. Before each event, every student must make an individual prediction and sketch it. The group then performs the event, records what happens and revises its explanation.

#### Event A: Removing a support quickly
1. Students place a plastic cup upright on the bench.
2. They lay a flat piece of cardboard across the top.
3. They place a marble near the centre of the cardboard, directly above the cup opening.
4. One student flicks the cardboard sharply sideways without lifting it.
5. The group observes the cardboard moving away while the marble initially remains in approximately the same horizontal position and falls into the cup.

The teacher requires the flick to be directed along the bench, with no hand swinging toward another student.

#### Event B: Removing the support slowly
Students reset the arrangement but pull the cardboard slowly. They compare the result with the quick flick. Friction now acts for longer and is more likely to move the marble sideways.

#### Event C: Motion continues until a force changes it
1. Students form a shallow straight channel using two rulers or folded cardboard strips.
2. They roll a marble gently along the channel.
3. In separate trials, they:
   - allow it to roll without deliberately touching it;
   - block it with a book;
   - tap it sideways with a ruler as it passes a marked point.
4. They record whether its speed or direction changes.

#### Event D: Balanced support forces
Students place the marble at rest on the cardboard and identify two vertical forces: gravity downward and the cardboard’s support force upward. They sketch equal arrows and note that the marble remains at rest because those forces are balanced.

Groups finish by arranging their observations into the pattern: “no unbalanced force—no change in motion” and “unbalanced force—speed or direction changes.”

### Science payoff
The central insight is that an object resists a change in its current motion:

- During the fast flick, the marble tends to remain at rest horizontally while the cardboard is removed.
- A stationary marble on supported cardboard remains stationary when the forces on it are balanced.
- A moving marble continues moving until forces such as friction, a book or a sideways tap change its speed or direction.
- A force is not needed merely to “keep motion going”; an unbalanced force is needed to change motion.
- Friction is a force, explaining why real marbles eventually slow rather than moving forever at constant velocity.

Mass as a measure of inertia is introduced through a brief final comparison within the event sequence: students place a 100 g mass on a second piece of cardboard and compare how difficult it is to start sliding it gently with one finger against an empty plastic cup. The comparison is qualitative and is used only to establish that the more massive object resists a change in motion more strongly, not to compare exact accelerations.

### Evidence
Students complete a four-row predict–observe–explain table containing:

- a prediction sketch for each event;
- the observed path of the marble, marked with arrows;
- whether the object started, stopped, sped up, slowed down or changed direction;
- the force responsible for any change;
- a before-and-after diagram for the fast and slow card removals;
- force arrows for the supported stationary marble;
- a qualitative record identifying which object—the empty cup or 100 g mass—was easier to start moving.

A suitable summary row is:

| Event | Initial motion | Forces balanced? | Recorded change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Marble on supported card | At rest | Yes | No change |
| Card flicked away | At rest horizontally | Horizontal force very brief | Marble remains approximately in place |
| Marble hits book | Moving | No | Stops |
| Marble tapped sideways | Moving | No | Direction changes |

### Feasibility
Each group needs only a cup, marble, two pieces of cardboard, two rulers, a book and one mass. These can be prepared in seven trays.

A 35-minute sequence is:

- 4 minutes: teacher models the safe card flick and recording format;
- 7 minutes: Events A and B;
- 10 minutes: Event C;
- 5 minutes: Event D and force-arrow sketch;
- 5 minutes: qualitative mass comparison;
- 4 minutes: groups write their Newton’s First Law statement and pack away.

Marbles remain inside ruler or cardboard channels, and groups work only on their own bench section. Cups catch the marbles in Event A. The teacher can monitor all groups from the centre aisle because each group follows the same sequence.

### Failure risk
The main failure mode is that students interpret the card flick as proving that “the marble does not move at all.” In reality, friction may give it some sideways motion, and gravity causes it to fall once the support is gone.

Mitigation:

- Students distinguish horizontal and vertical motion in their diagrams.
- The teacher asks, “What happened to the marble horizontally before it fell?”
- Groups compare fast and slow removal to show that friction has more time to change the marble’s horizontal motion during the slow pull.
- The synthesis explicitly states that inertia is not a force holding the marble still; it is the tendency to resist a change in motion.
- For the mass comparison, the teacher notes that friction also differs, so it is supporting qualitative evidence rather than a precise fair test.

## 3. Human Motion-and-Force Model

### Physical student action
Each group creates a taped number line on its bench or uses ruler lengths as equally spaced positions. Students use their hands and bodies to represent an object’s motion over a series of equal “time beats.”

Roles are:

- **object:** moves a plastic cup or places a hand at successive positions;
- **left-force operator:** displays a left-pointing cardboard arrow when applying a force;
- **right-force operator:** displays a right-pointing cardboard arrow;
- **recorder/timekeeper:** calls equal beats and records positions.

Students are given or copy a small set of motion rules:

- Equal spacing on successive beats represents constant velocity.
- Increasing spacing represents speeding up.
- Decreasing spacing represents slowing down.
- Reversing the order of positions represents a change in direction.
- Equal opposing force arrows produce no change in the existing motion pattern.
- One larger or unmatched force arrow changes the motion pattern in its direction.

They then model four scenarios.

#### Scenario A: Object at rest, balanced forces
The cup begins at one mark. Both force operators show equal opposite arrows on each beat. The object remains at the same position.

#### Scenario B: Object moving, balanced forces
The cup is moved one ruler division to the right on every beat. Both operators show equal opposite arrows. The object continues with equal spacing rather than stopping.

#### Scenario C: Unbalanced force
While the cup is moving one division per beat, the right-force operator displays a larger right arrow. The object changes to two divisions per beat. A leftward unbalanced force then reduces the spacing, stops the cup or reverses its direction.

#### Scenario D: Different inertia
The group constructs two paper motion models:

- a “low-mass object” card labelled 1 mass unit;
- a “high-mass object” card labelled 3 mass units.

The same one-beat unbalanced-force signal is applied to each. Under the model rules, the low-mass object changes its spacing by a larger amount, while the high-mass object changes by a smaller amount. Students physically lay out the two different position sequences with cups, masses or cardboard markers.

Groups rotate roles and then exchange one position record with a neighbouring group. The neighbouring group must infer whether the forces were balanced or unbalanced and identify the likely force direction.

### Science payoff
This mechanism makes “no change in motion” precise. It does not mean “not moving”; it means:

- an object at rest remains at rest, or
- a moving object continues at constant velocity—same speed and same direction.

Students also see that:

- balanced forces do not change the position pattern;
- an unbalanced force changes the spacing or direction of the pattern;
- greater mass means greater inertia, so the same unbalanced force produces a smaller change in motion.

The equal-time position model separates velocity from position. A cup can move to a new position on every beat while still having no change in motion because its velocity remains constant.

### Evidence
Each group records four position-time strips. For example:

- at rest: `3, 3, 3, 3, 3`;
- constant velocity: `1, 2, 3, 4, 5`;
- speeding up: `1, 2, 4, 7, 11`;
- slowing and reversing: `1, 3, 4, 4, 3`.

For every strip, students add:

- left and right force arrows;
- a label of “balanced” or “unbalanced”;
- a description of the motion: at rest, constant velocity, speeding up, slowing down or changing direction;
- a comparison of low- and high-mass responses to the same force signal.

The peer-inference task provides an additional check: another group must be able to use the position record to identify when motion changed and therefore when an unbalanced force acted.

### Feasibility
This option requires cardboard arrows, tape, rulers, cups and masses. No students pull or push one another. Most movement occurs through repositioning a cup or hand on the bench, so fixed stools and benches do not obstruct the activity.

A 35-minute sequence is:

- 5 minutes: teacher demonstrates equal-time position patterns;
- 12 minutes: Scenarios A–C;
- 8 minutes: mass/inertia comparison;
- 6 minutes: role rotation and creation of a new motion strip;
- 4 minutes: exchange and interpret another group’s strip.

The teacher needs only to call common time beats initially and check that groups use equal time intervals. Materials are flat and quick to distribute, and cleanup involves stacking cups, masses, rulers and cardboard arrows.

### Failure risk
The main risk is that students treat the model rules as arbitrary instructions rather than connecting them to physical forces. The mass comparison is especially vulnerable because the different response is represented rather than independently measured.

Mitigation:

- Before modelling, each group pushes an empty cup and then a cup containing a 500 g mass very gently with one finger to feel that the more massive system is harder to start or stop. This is a brief physical anchor, not the main mechanism.
- Students must explain every change in spacing by naming an unbalanced force and its direction.
- The teacher repeatedly asks, “Did the position change, or did the motion change?” This prevents students from labelling every movement as acceleration.
- During synthesis, the class identifies the limits of the model: the discrete beats simplify continuous motion, and real bench friction would eventually slow a physical object even when the model shows ideal constant velocity.
