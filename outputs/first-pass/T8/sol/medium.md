## 1. Constant-force loaded-car comparison

### 1. Mechanism name
**Constant-force car trials**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group of four receives a toy car, string, a 5 N spring scale, 100 g, 200 g and 500 g masses, tape, a ruler and a stopwatch.

**Within the 35-minute block:**

- **5 minutes — prepare the track:**  
  Students mark a straight 50 cm track on the bench with two short pieces of tape. They tie the spring scale to the front of the toy car. If necessary, a plastic cup is taped to the car to hold the masses.
- **5 minutes — practise the controlled pull:**  
  One student pulls the car while keeping the spring-scale reading at a teacher-selected value such as 1 N. Another watches the scale and calls “higher” or “lower”. A third times the car, and a fourth records.
- **15 minutes — mass comparison:**  
  Students time the car from rest over 50 cm under the same nominal 1 N pull:
  - car alone;
  - car plus 200 g;
  - car plus 500 g.  
  They complete at least two trials for each load and calculate or identify the typical time.
- **5 minutes — balanced and unbalanced motion:**  
  Students pull the car slowly so that it covers successive 10 cm sections in approximately equal times. They identify that the forward pull is then approximately balancing rolling resistance. They release the scale and observe the car slow down because resistance is now unbalanced.
- **5 minutes — group conclusion:**  
  Students annotate a sketch for three cases: car at rest, car moving at roughly constant speed, and car speeding up from rest.

### 3. Science payoff
Students should arrive at these ideas:

- The same unbalanced force produces less change in motion when the total mass is greater. Therefore, greater mass means greater inertia.
- A car speeds up when the forward force is greater than the opposing forces.
- If the forward pull balances the opposing resistance, the car’s velocity does not appreciably change.
- When the pull is removed, the car does not slow because “motion runs out”; it slows because friction and rolling resistance are unbalanced forces. Without such an unbalanced force, it would continue at constant velocity.

### 4. Evidence
Each group records:

- total mass or load for each condition;
- applied spring-scale force;
- two travel times over 50 cm;
- a statement comparing the times, for example:  
  “At approximately 1 N, the car carrying 500 g took longer to travel 50 cm than the unloaded car.”
- times for consecutive 10 cm intervals during the approximately constant-speed trial;
- three force-and-motion sketches showing:
  - balanced forces and no change from rest;
  - approximately balanced forces and approximately constant speed;
  - unbalanced resistance and slowing.

The longer time for the more massive car under the same pull is the evidence that greater mass resists a change in motion more strongly.

### 5. Feasibility
Seven groups of four can work along fixed benches. Each group needs only one car, one spring scale, masses, string, tape, a ruler and a stopwatch. Tracks are only 50 cm long, so no floor movement is required.

The teacher can prepare seven short strings and tape marks before the lesson. Students perform the same procedure simultaneously, making supervision simpler. Cleanup consists of removing the masses, strings and two pieces of tape and returning the cars and scales. This can be completed in the final minute of the main block.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students cannot maintain exactly 1 N, so differences in pulling technique obscure the mass effect.

**Mitigation:** Give groups a short practice period, assign one student solely to monitor the spring-scale reading, and require two trials per load. The teacher should emphasise comparison rather than precise calculation. Any trial in which the scale reading varies substantially is crossed out and repeated. The toy-car wheels should also be checked before the lesson so a sticking wheel is not mistaken for inertia.

## 2. Prediction–observation inertia circuit

### 1. Mechanism name
**Inertia event circuit**

### 2. Physical student action
The seven groups rotate through three types of short event. Each event follows the same sequence: **predict individually, perform the event, record the result, and explain the change or lack of change using forces**. Multiple copies of the stations are placed along the benches so that groups do not queue.

#### Event A: Sudden card removal
- Students place a cardboard square across the mouth of a plastic cup and put a marble at its centre.
- They predict what will happen if the card is pulled slowly and if it is flicked sharply sideways.
- They perform both actions.
- With a slow pull, the marble is carried sideways by friction before falling.
- With a sharp horizontal flick, the card leaves while the marble initially remains nearly in place and drops into the cup.

#### Event B: Changing a moving object
- Students roll a marble along a strip of cardboard.
- They first let it roll without deliberately touching it and sketch its path.
- They then place a ruler or angled cardboard wall in the path.
- They repeat the roll and observe the marble slow, stop, or change direction when it contacts the barrier.
- A student identifies the contact with the barrier as the unbalanced force causing the change in velocity.

#### Event C: Force pulse and mass
- Students attach a spring scale to a toy car.
- They pull an unloaded car at approximately 1 N for a counted one-second interval, release it, and measure how far it travels from its starting line in that interval or where it is when the pull ends.
- They repeat with 500 g secured in a cup taped to the car.
- They use the same student as puller and scale reader for both trials.
- If two spring scales are available at that station, students finish by pulling the stationary car in opposite directions with equal readings and then with unequal readings.

A suitable schedule is:

- 4 minutes for predictions and instructions;
- 8 minutes at each of the three events, including movement between stations;
- 7 minutes for groups to complete explanations and select their strongest evidence.

### 3. Science payoff
The circuit separates three connected aspects of Newton’s First Law:

- In the card event, the marble resists a change from rest. It does not automatically move sideways with the card.
- In the rolling event, the marble continues moving until friction or contact with the barrier provides an unbalanced force. Contact can change speed, stop the marble, or change its direction.
- In the force-pulse event, the more massive loaded car undergoes a smaller change in motion for a similar force applied for a similar time. Greater mass therefore means greater inertia.
- In the opposing-pull extension, equal opposing pulls produce no change from rest, whereas unequal pulls produce motion toward the larger force.

Students should state Newton’s First Law in terms of **change in velocity**, not simply “moving” or “not moving.”

### 4. Evidence
Students complete a prediction–observation table containing:

- the predicted and observed location of the marble after the quick card flick;
- a top-view sketch of the marble path before and after contact with the ruler or cardboard wall;
- labels showing where an unbalanced force acted;
- the unloaded and loaded car positions or distances after the same one-second pull;
- if the opposing-pull extension is completed, both scale readings and whether the car remained at rest or moved.

A completed evidence statement might read:

> “The sharp card movement did not carry the marble far sideways. The loaded car also changed its motion less than the unloaded car under a similar pull. Both observations show resistance to a change in motion.”

### 5. Feasibility
All events take place on fixed benches and use only cups, cardboard, marbles, rulers, toy cars, string, tape, masses, spring scales and stopwatches. The teacher can lay out the stations before class in approximately ten minutes. No event requires chemical preparation, power or technician support.

For seven groups, the teacher can provide two copies each of Events A and B and three copies of Event C, or duplicate the event types according to available materials. Rotation directions are placed on the benches to prevent crowding. Marbles remain on cardboard strips with books or rulers along the edges as barriers so they cannot roll into sinks or onto the floor. Cleanup is mainly collecting marbles and returning station trays.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students treat the card flick as a trick and write “the marble falls because of gravity” without identifying why it does not move sideways with the card.

**Mitigation:** Require separate horizontal and vertical explanations. Students must answer both:

1. “Why did the marble initially remain nearly above the cup?” — because of inertia; there was too little horizontal impulse to give it the card’s velocity.
2. “Why did it then move downward?” — gravity was an unbalanced vertical force once the card no longer supported it.

The teacher should also avoid saying that “no forces act” on the marble. Before the flick, gravity and the card’s support force are balanced vertically.

## 3. Opposing-force tabletop rig

### 1. Mechanism name
**Balanced-force pull rig**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group sets up a toy car with one string attached to the front and another to the rear. A spring scale is connected to each string. The car is placed on a ruler-marked track along the bench.

Students take four roles: left puller, right puller, scale caller and recorder. Roles rotate after each trial.

**The 35-minute sequence is:**

- **5 minutes — construct and test the rig:**  
  Students attach the strings and check that both scales pull along the same straight line rather than upward.
- **8 minutes — balanced trials:**  
  The two pullers gradually increase their pulls while the scale caller helps them maintain equal readings, such as 1 N against 1 N. Students begin with the car at rest and record whether its motion changes.
- **8 minutes — unbalanced trials:**  
  Students test combinations such as approximately:
  - 1 N left and 2 N right;
  - 2 N left and 1 N right;
  - equal pulls again.  
  They record the direction of motion or change in motion for each combination.
- **8 minutes — mass and inertia trial:**  
  Students compare the empty car with a car carrying 500 g. In each case, they create the same short imbalance—for example, 2 N on one side and 1 N on the other for a counted one second—and mark the car’s position at the end. The more massive car should show less displacement or a less rapid start.
- **6 minutes — physical force model:**  
  Students cut or use prepared cardboard arrows and place them beside the car to represent the two pulls. Equal-length opposing arrows are used for balanced trials; unequal arrows are used for unbalanced trials. Under each arrangement they write the observed motion outcome.

### 3. Science payoff
The mechanism makes **net force** physically visible:

- Forces can act on an object without changing its motion if they are balanced.
- Equal forces in opposite directions give zero net force, so a stationary object remains stationary.
- Unequal opposing forces create a net, or unbalanced, force. The object’s velocity changes in the direction of the net force.
- A larger net force is not needed merely to “keep an object existing in motion”; it is needed to change the object’s velocity.
- Under the same short force imbalance, a more massive car changes its motion less. Greater mass therefore means greater inertia.

This directly challenges the everyday idea that the side “using force” must always make the object move. Both sides may exert forces while the motion remains unchanged because the forces balance.

### 4. Evidence
Each group records a table such as:

| Left force | Right force | Balanced? | Observed change |
|---|---:|---|---|
| 1 N | 1 N | Yes | Remained at rest |
| 1 N | 2 N | No | Began moving right |
| 2 N | 1 N | No | Began moving left |

Students also record:

- the position or displacement of the unloaded car after the one-second imbalance;
- the position or displacement of the 500 g-loaded car after the same imbalance;
- a force-arrow diagram for one balanced and one unbalanced case;
- a one-sentence rule linking net force to change in velocity.

The paired scale readings are direct evidence that the presence of forces is not enough to cause a change: their combined effect matters.

### 5. Feasibility
The rigs remain on the laboratory benches and require no student movement beyond changing roles around a group’s workspace. The teacher can prepare strings with loops before class. Students only need to connect them to the cars and scales.

If there are not enough spring scales for seven two-scale rigs, neighbouring groups can share a rig: one group operates it while the other records and checks force arrows, then they exchange roles after each set of trials. This still keeps all students occupied and uses only the listed equipment.

The pulls are limited to low readings, such as 1–2 N, so cars do not travel rapidly. Tape marks at the ends of each track indicate where students must stop pulling. Cleanup requires disconnecting strings, removing taped cups and returning scales and masses.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** The car moves during an “equal-force” trial because the pulls are not collinear, the scale readings fluctuate, or rolling friction is uneven. Students may then conclude that balanced forces can cause acceleration.

**Mitigation:** Students must pull horizontally along the ruler-marked line and hold the readings steady for a two-second count before judging the result. They repeat each balanced condition at least twice and use a tolerance such as “both scales within about 0.2 N.” The teacher explains that experimental imperfections can produce a small drift; the relevant result is that equal opposing pulls do not produce a consistent change toward either side, whereas an intentional imbalance produces a repeatable change toward the larger force.
