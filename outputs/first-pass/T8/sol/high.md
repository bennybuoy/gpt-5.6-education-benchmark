The same 5-minute introduction can define a force as a push or pull, show how to read a spring scale in newtons, and distinguish “moving” from “changing motion.” Each option below then occupies the 35-minute main block and leaves 10 minutes for whole-class synthesis.

## 1. Opposing-force cart trials

### 1. Mechanism name
**Opposing-force cart trials**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group receives a toy car, a plastic cup taped centrally to the car as a load holder, two pieces of string, two spring scales, rulers, a stopwatch, and a 100 g or 200 g mass. Strings are attached to the front and rear of the car, with a spring scale in each string.

Students rotate through puller, observer/timekeeper and recorder roles.

**35-minute sequence:**

- **3 minutes — Set up:**  
  Groups mark a 40–60 cm lane on the bench with rulers. Books form a stop at the end.

- **6 minutes — Balanced at rest:**  
  Two students pull in opposite directions, each maintaining the same scale reading, such as 1.0 N. They test this three times and record whether the car starts moving. They then deliberately make one pull slightly larger.

- **6 minutes — Unbalanced force:**  
  Students conduct trials such as 1.5 N forward and 0.5 N backward. They record which way the car moves and whether it starts, speeds up or changes direction when the larger force switches sides.

- **6 minutes — Balanced while moving:**  
  The rear string is removed. One student tows the car as steadily as possible through two equal 20 cm sections. The group records the time for each section and the approximate spring-scale reading. Similar section times indicate approximately constant speed. Students identify the forward pull and backward rolling friction as approximately balanced.

- **10 minutes — Change the mass:**  
  Students pull the same car from rest with the same target spring-scale reading, first without an added mass and then with 100 g or 200 g secured in the cup. They complete three trials for each condition and time the car over the same distance. Adjacent groups compare results.

- **4 minutes — Annotate and pack away:**  
  Students add force arrows to one balanced and one unbalanced trial and identify where the car’s motion did or did not change.

### 3. Science payoff
Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- Equal opposing forces produce zero net force, so an object initially at rest remains at rest.
- Balanced forces do not mean “no forces”; they mean that the forces cancel and therefore motion does not change.
- When one horizontal force is larger, the car’s motion changes in the direction of the net force.
- A steadily moving car can have balanced forces: the forward pull can balance rolling friction.
- For approximately the same applied force, the loaded car changes its motion less readily than the unloaded car. Greater mass therefore means greater inertia.
- If a released car eventually slows, this is because friction remains an unbalanced force, not because moving objects naturally require a continuing forward force.

### 4. Evidence
Each group records a table containing:

- front and rear spring-scale readings;
- initial condition: at rest or moving;
- whether the car remained still, sped up, slowed down or changed direction;
- times for the two sections during the steady-speed trial;
- three travel times for the unloaded car and three for the loaded car;
- median unloaded and loaded times;
- a force-arrow sketch for one balanced and one unbalanced case.

A typical evidence pattern would be:

- \(1.0\text{ N}\) left and \(1.0\text{ N}\) right: no horizontal change;
- larger rightward than leftward reading: motion changes to the right;
- similar times over equal sections: approximately constant speed;
- longer travel time for the loaded car under the same target pull: greater resistance to changing motion.

### 5. Feasibility
Seven groups can work in parallel at separate fixed benches. The cars travel only along short bench lanes, with books serving as end stops. The teacher can prepare seven simple equipment trays beforehand; the only assembly is taping a cup to each car and attaching strings.

The spring-scale readings should remain well below 5 N. Groups of three can combine the observer and recorder roles. Setup and cleanup occur within the 35-minute block, leaving the required 5-minute introduction and 10-minute synthesis.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students may conclude that the loaded car is slower only because it has “more friction,” rather than because it has greater inertia.

**Mitigation:** Use the same wheeled car in both conditions, secure the load over the axle, keep the added mass modest, and repeat the trials. In the synthesis, state that rolling friction is an experimental limitation, but the repeated pattern under the same applied pull is consistent with greater mass producing less change in motion. Use the opposing-force trials, rather than the mass trial alone, as the primary evidence about balanced and unbalanced forces.

## 2. Inertia interruption sequence

### 1. Mechanism name
**Inertia interruption sequence**

### 2. Physical student action
Groups complete a predict–observe–explain sequence using three different interruptions to an object’s state of motion. Each group receives cardboard, a plastic cup, a marble, two toy cars, a book, rulers, tape and one mass.

**35-minute sequence:**

- **2 minutes — Assign roles and arrange a contained work area:**  
  Books and folded cardboard form low barriers so marbles remain on the bench.

- **7 minutes — Card-and-cup interruption:**  
  Students place a cardboard card across the top of a plastic cup and put a marble at its centre.  
  1. They predict what will happen if the card is pulled slowly.  
  2. They pull it slowly and observe the marble being carried sideways by friction.  
  3. They reset it, predict a rapid horizontal snap, and snap the card sideways. The card moves away while the marble remains approximately in its original horizontal position and falls into the cup.  
  They repeat the rapid trial three times.

- **7 minutes — Moving-passenger interruption:**  
  Students tape a small cardboard platform to a toy car and place a marble on it. They roll the car gently toward a book stop. When the car hits the stop, the marble continues forward relative to the car. A cardboard tray or second book catches the marble before it can leave the bench. Students repeat from two different starting speeds and sketch the positions immediately before and after the collision.

- **12 minutes — Mass and collision comparison:**  
  Students make a short cardboard ramp supported by a book. One toy car is the launcher and the second is the stationary target. The launcher is released from the same ruler-marked point each time.  
  - First, it collides with the unloaded target car.  
  - Then 100 g or 200 g is secured centrally on the same target car, and the trial is repeated.  
  Students complete three trials in each condition and measure how far the target car travels after impact.

- **4 minutes — Force analysis:**  
  Groups label where forces were balanced and where an unbalanced force changed motion. They use separate horizontal and vertical arrows for the card-and-cup event.

- **3 minutes — Cleanup:**  
  Marbles are returned to cups, masses removed and cardboard stacked.

### 3. Science payoff
The sequence produces three linked conclusions:

- **Object initially at rest:** When the card is snapped away, the marble resists a horizontal change and remains approximately at rest horizontally. It then falls because removing the card removes the upward support force, leaving gravity unbalanced vertically.
- **Object initially moving:** When the car is stopped by the book, the marble continues forward because the stopping force acts first on the car, not immediately on the marble. The marble changes motion only when friction, the book or the catch tray acts on it.
- **Effect of mass:** A more massive target car generally undergoes a smaller change in motion in a comparable collision. Greater mass means greater inertia.
- Forces must be considered by direction. Forces can be balanced vertically while an unbalanced horizontal force changes motion, or vice versa.

This directly challenges the everyday idea that an object moves only while a forward force is continuously acting on it.

### 4. Evidence
Students record:

- a prediction and observed result for the slow and rapid card pulls;
- success count for the rapid card trial, such as “marble entered cup in 3 of 3 trials”;
- before-and-after sketches of the marble passenger and car;
- the target car’s travel distance in three unloaded and three loaded collisions;
- median target distance for each mass condition;
- force-arrow diagrams showing:
  - balanced weight and support before the card is removed;
  - unbalanced gravity after support is removed;
  - the stopping force on the car at the book;
  - the collision force that starts the target car moving.

The useful pattern is that the rapid card leaves the marble’s horizontal position almost unchanged, the moving passenger continues after the car stops, and the loaded target moves less than the unloaded target.

### 5. Feasibility
All events occur on fixed benches. Cardboard barriers and book stops contain marbles and cars. Seven identical kits can be prepared by placing the listed items in cups; no specialist assembly is required.

The activities are short and repeatable, so one teacher can circulate rather than supervise a single high-risk event. Groups do not need to rotate around the room. The ramp height is only one book, and all cars are released rather than thrown. Cleanup is limited to collecting marbles, cars and cardboard.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students may explain the moving-passenger result by saying that a new forward force “throws” the marble, or explain the card result only as “gravity pulled it down,” missing horizontal inertia.

**Mitigation:** Require students to analyse horizontal and vertical motion separately. Ask, “What was pushing the marble forward at the instant the car stopped?” and “Did the marble move sideways with the rapidly removed card?” The slow-pull control also shows that the marble moves sideways only when friction has enough time to act. For the collision comparison, use the same target car and release point each time and treat the measured distance as qualitative evidence because rolling friction is still present.

## 3. Balloon-thrust cart model

### 1. Mechanism name
**Balloon-thrust cart model**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group builds and tests a simple propelled cart. Materials are a toy car, balloon, tape, plastic cup, 100 g mass, string, 5 N spring scale, rulers, cardboard and books.

A balloon is taped securely along the top of the car with its nozzle pointing backward. The nozzle remains open but is pinched before release. A cup taped over the axle holds the added mass. A fixed loop of string serves as an inflation gauge so the balloon is inflated to approximately the same size for each trial.

**35-minute sequence:**

- **6 minutes — Build and check:**  
  Students tape the balloon and cup to the car, construct a straight cardboard lane and place a book stop at the end. One student is the designated balloon inflator; the nozzle is not shared.

- **6 minutes — Tethered balanced-force trial:**  
  A string from the rear of the car is attached to a 5 N spring scale held by another student. The string is kept taut. Students release the balloon nozzle while the scale prevents the cart from moving forward. They record the approximate scale range and the cart’s displacement. The backward tension, together with small resistive forces, balances the forward balloon thrust, so the cart remains approximately at rest.

- **8 minutes — Unloaded unbalanced-force trials:**  
  Students detach the tether, inflate the balloon to the string gauge, place the car at a start line and release it without pushing. They measure the time to travel 40 or 50 cm and observe what happens after the balloon becomes limp. They complete two or three trials.

- **8 minutes — Loaded trials:**  
  Students secure 100 g in the cup and repeat from the same start line with the same inflation gauge. They again measure travel time and note whether the cart speeds up as readily as the unloaded cart.

- **4 minutes — Motion-phase model:**  
  Groups draw a three-stage diagram:
  1. at rest or tethered, with balanced forces;
  2. released, with net force forward and increasing speed;
  3. balloon empty, with friction and air resistance producing a net backward force and decreasing speed.

- **3 minutes — Cleanup:**  
  Balloons remain attached to the cars for collection; masses and scales return to the equipment tray.

### 3. Science payoff
Students should conclude that:

- A cart does not begin moving merely because it contains an inflated balloon; its motion changes when the nozzle is released and an unbalanced forward thrust acts.
- The tethered cart can have forward thrust and backward tension acting at the same time but remain at rest because the total force is balanced.
- When the tether is removed, the unbalanced forward thrust changes the cart’s speed.
- Once the balloon stops providing thrust, the cart continues moving for a distance. It then slows because friction and air resistance are unbalanced backward forces. It does not slow because motion itself “runs out.”
- With approximately the same balloon inflation, the loaded cart changes its motion less readily than the unloaded cart. The greater-mass cart has greater inertia.
- If a moving object had no unbalanced force—including no friction or air resistance—its velocity would remain constant.

### 4. Evidence
Groups record:

- spring-scale range during the tethered trial;
- cart displacement while tethered, ideally close to zero;
- balloon inflation condition, checked against the same string loop;
- two or three travel times over the fixed distance for the unloaded cart;
- two or three travel times for the cart with 100 g added;
- median time for each mass condition;
- whether the cart continued after the balloon became limp and the approximate coasting distance;
- a three-stage force-and-motion diagram.

The evidence links observable motion changes to force conditions: little or no displacement when thrust is balanced by the tether, forward acceleration after the tether is removed, less rapid change with added mass, and slowing when resistance becomes the remaining unbalanced horizontal force.

### 5. Feasibility
Seven groups can build and test one cart each at their own benches. The teacher can prepare a kit containing a pre-measured string inflation loop and a cardboard lane for each group. No cutting or specialist construction is needed during the lesson.

The cars remain on 40–60 cm bench lanes, with books as stops. Only one group member handles the balloon nozzle, and balloons are never released freely into the room. The construction, trials, diagram and cleanup fit into 35 minutes, preserving the required introduction and synthesis periods.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Balloon thrust may vary between trials, making the unloaded and loaded comparison inconsistent or causing the cart to veer sideways.

**Mitigation:** Use the same balloon for both mass conditions, inflate it to the same string-loop gauge, align the nozzle with the centre of the car and use folded cardboard as low guide walls. Run at least two trials per condition and compare medians rather than a single result. If a loaded cart does not reach the full distance, record that outcome as evidence that the same approximate thrust produced a smaller change in motion rather than discarding the trial.
