The three mechanisms below are alternatives for the same lesson, not activities to combine. Their cores are different:

- **Mechanism 1:** continuous force measurement during controlled motion;
- **Mechanism 2:** prediction and explanation of abrupt inertia events;
- **Mechanism 3:** construction and testing of a force-driven model.

For each option, the 5-minute introduction defines a force scientifically as a push or pull caused by an interaction. The 35-minute block is described below, and the final 10 minutes are used to formulate Newton’s First Law from the collected evidence.

---

## 1. Vertical spring-scale motion profile

### 1. Mechanism name
**Vertical spring-scale motion profile**

### 2. Physical student action

Each group of four receives a 10 N spring scale, 100 g and 500 g masses, string, a ruler and a stopwatch. Roles are scale operator, ruler guide, scale reader and timer/recorder.

**Minutes 0–5: Resting force reading**

1. Students attach the 100 g mass securely to the spring scale.
2. The operator holds the scale vertically with the mass only 5–10 cm above the bench.
3. They wait for the mass and scale needle to become still and record the reading. This is the upward pull of the scale needed to balance Earth’s downward gravitational force.

**Minutes 5–15: Start, steady motion and stop**

1. The ruler is held vertically beside the mass.
2. Following stopwatch calls, the operator:
   - starts lifting the mass;
   - moves it upward at approximately 5 cm per half-second through the middle of a 20 cm path;
   - stops it at the top.
3. The scale reader records:
   - the maximum reading during the upward start;
   - the approximate reading during the steady middle section;
   - the minimum reading while the upward-moving mass is being stopped.
4. The group repeats the sequence three times, rotating roles.

Students should see the pattern:

- starting upward: scale reading greater than the resting reading;
- steady upward motion: scale reading approximately equal to the resting reading;
- slowing while still moving upward: scale reading below the resting reading.

**Minutes 15–28: Mass comparison**

1. Students repeat the same timed 20 cm motion profile with the 500 g mass.
2. For each mass, they calculate:

\[
\text{extra starting pull}=\text{maximum starting reading}-\text{resting reading}
\]

3. They use the median of three trials rather than selecting the largest result.

**Minutes 28–33: Force and motion record**

Students draw three diagrams for the hanging mass:

- at rest;
- moving upward steadily;
- speeding up or slowing down.

They add an upward arrow for the spring force and a downward arrow for gravity, making the arrows equal or unequal according to their data.

**Minutes 33–35: Pack-up**

Masses and scales are returned to the group kit, ready for the synthesis discussion.

### 3. Science payoff

This mechanism directly distinguishes **motion** from **change in motion**:

- At rest, the upward spring force and downward gravitational force are balanced, so the mass remains at rest.
- While the mass moves upward at constant velocity, those forces are again approximately balanced. Balanced forces therefore do not necessarily mean “not moving”; they mean **no change in velocity**.
- A larger upward scale force than the downward gravitational force produces an unbalanced upward force, so the mass speeds up upward.
- A smaller upward force while the mass is still moving upward produces an unbalanced downward force, so the mass slows.
- Producing the same timed start or stop for the 500 g mass requires a greater additional force than for the 100 g mass. Greater mass therefore means greater inertia—greater resistance to a change in motion.

### 4. Evidence

Each group records a table such as:

| Mass | Resting reading | Maximum during start | Reading during steady motion | Minimum during stop | Extra starting pull |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 100 g | | | | | |
| 500 g | | | | | |

Expected evidence includes:

- steady-motion readings close to resting readings;
- readings above the resting value while speeding up;
- readings below the resting value while slowing upward;
- a larger extra starting pull for the 500 g mass.

Students also retain three force-arrow sketches linked to the measured readings.

### 5. Feasibility

Seven groups of four can work simultaneously at fixed benches. Each group needs only one listed spring scale, two masses, string, a ruler and a stopwatch. The teacher can prepare seven kits and check the knots without a technician.

The mass remains low over the bench and is never swung. A 10 N scale is used for the 500 g mass because a 5 N scale would already be near its limit before the mass is accelerated. The 35-minute block includes a short practice trial and two minutes for cleanup. The 5-minute introduction explains what a spring scale reading represents; the 10-minute synthesis uses the group tables to state the law.

### 6. Failure risk

The most likely failure is that students jerk the scale, producing oscillating readings, and then treat the spring-scale reading itself as the net force.

To mitigate this, the teacher first models one controlled motion profile. Students compare each dynamic reading with that mass’s own resting reading rather than comparing raw readings alone. They repeat each condition three times and use the median. During synthesis, the teacher explicitly states that the spring scale measures only the upward force; the net force is the result of combining that force with the downward gravitational force.

## 2. Predict–observe inertia event sequence

### 1. Mechanism name
**Predict–observe inertia event sequence**

### 2. Physical student action

Each group completes three short physical events. Before touching the equipment for each event, students write a prediction. Afterward, they record the result and identify the force that caused any change in speed or direction.

#### Minutes 0–8: Card, cup and marble

Materials: plastic cup, stiff cardboard rectangle and marble.

1. Students place the cardboard across the cup opening and position the marble over the centre.
2. They slowly pull the cardboard sideways in three trials.
3. They reset the arrangement and remove the cardboard with a rapid horizontal pull in three trials.
4. They record whether the marble lands in the cup and sketch its path.

During a rapid pull, the marble receives very little horizontal impulse and initially retains its horizontal state of rest. Once the support is removed, gravity is unbalanced vertically, so it falls into the cup. During a slow pull, friction acts for longer and changes the marble’s horizontal motion.

#### Minutes 8–18: Marble coast and direction change

Materials: marble, cardboard, book, ruler and plastic cup.

1. Students make a short cardboard ramp supported by a book and mark one release point.
2. They release—rather than push—the marble from that point.
3. On the level cardboard beyond the ramp, they measure how far the marble travels after leaving the ramp.
4. They repeat three times.
5. They then hold a cardboard strip at an angle across the path, supported by a book. The marble is released from the same point and collides with the strip.
6. Students sketch the incoming and outgoing directions and measure the sideways position at which the marble leaves the cardboard area.

The ramp provides an initial unbalanced force component that speeds up the marble. Once on the level surface, the marble continues after that accelerating force is gone. The angled strip provides a new unbalanced contact force and changes its direction.

#### Minutes 18–30: Mass and resistance to stopping

Materials: 100 g and 200 g masses, equal lengths of string, ruler, cardboard backboard and plastic cup.

1. One student braces a ruler horizontally with both forearms on the bench. A 20 cm string and 100 g mass hang from it as a short pendulum.
2. A cardboard sheet behind the path is marked with vertical reference lines.
3. An empty plastic cup is placed at the bottom of the pendulum path.
4. The mass is released from a fixed reference line and collides with the cup.
5. Students measure:
   - how far the cup moves;
   - how far the mass follows through beyond the collision point.
6. They perform three trials and then repeat with the 200 g mass, using the same string length, release line and cup position.

Releasing from the same height gives the bobs approximately the same speed before impact. The more massive bob generally moves the cup farther and retains more of its forward motion after the same type of collision.

#### Minutes 30–32: Before–during–after table

For each event, groups complete:

| Event | Initial motion | Force during change | Observed change in velocity | Motion after the force |
|---|---|---|---|---|

#### Minutes 32–35: Pack-up

Marbles and masses are counted back into the kit, and cardboard barriers keep rolling objects from reaching the floor.

### 3. Science payoff

The event sequence produces three linked conclusions:

- **Rest inertia:** The marble does not automatically move sideways with the rapidly removed cardboard. An object at rest resists a change in its horizontal motion.
- **Motion inertia:** A marble continues moving after the ramp or hand is no longer pushing it. Continued motion does not require a continuous forward force. It eventually slows because friction and air resistance are unbalanced backward forces.
- **Unbalanced forces change velocity:** Contact with the angled cardboard changes the marble’s direction. The cup collision slows the pendulum bob. A change in direction is a change in velocity even if the speed is similar.
- **Mass is a measure of inertia:** Starting with approximately the same speed, the 200 g bob is less changed by the cup interaction and requires a greater stopping effect than the 100 g bob.
- **Balanced forces mean no velocity change:** Before the card is removed, gravity on the marble is balanced by the card’s upward support. On the level section, the upward support and downward gravity remain balanced; any gradual slowing is due to the separate unbalanced horizontal friction force.

### 4. Evidence

Students collect:

- the number of successful cup landings out of three slow and three rapid card pulls;
- a path sketch showing the marble remaining nearly over the cup during the rapid pull;
- three measured level-surface travel distances after the marble leaves the ramp;
- incoming and outgoing direction arrows and the measured sideways exit position after the angled-cardboard collision;
- median cup displacement and pendulum follow-through for the 100 g and 200 g bobs;
- a before–during–after force table for all three events.

The mass comparison is supported if the 200 g bob produces greater cup displacement and/or greater follow-through from the same release position.

### 5. Feasibility

All three events occur at the fixed benches. Seven kits can be prepared with pre-sized cardboard, cups, marbles, string and masses. The teacher can pre-tie and check the short pendulum strings without a technician.

Groups remain at their own benches rather than rotating, so the teacher supervises one common activity at a time. Books and cardboard form barriers around marble paths. Only 100 g and 200 g masses are swung, over a short distance and low above the bench; no hands are placed in the pendulum path. The main block includes three minutes for counting and returning marbles and masses.

The 5-minute introduction asks students whether a force is needed for motion or for a change in motion. The final 10-minute discussion uses the three before–during–after tables to formulate the law.

### 6. Failure risk

The most likely failure is that students treat the events as unrelated tricks—for example, saying only that “gravity made the marble fall” or that “the heavier mass hits harder”—without identifying inertia or the force responsible for each velocity change.

To mitigate this, every observation must be separated into **horizontal and vertical motion** and into **before, during and after the interaction**. The teacher repeatedly asks, “What changed the object’s speed or direction?” For the pendulum comparison, students must record the bob’s follow-through, not only the cup’s movement, so that the focus remains on how much the bob’s own motion changed.

## 3. Balloon-thrust car model

### 1. Mechanism name
**Balloon-thrust car model**

### 2. Physical student action

Each group receives a toy car, balloon, cardboard, tape, ruler, stopwatch, 100 g mass and book.

#### Minutes 0–6: Build the model

1. Students roll a short cardboard strip into a sleeve around the balloon neck. This keeps the neck pointing backward rather than flapping sideways.
2. They tape the sleeve along the top of the toy car, leaving the balloon opening unobstructed.
3. They position the balloon body over the car so that it cannot touch the wheels.
4. On the bench, they mark a start line and 30 cm and 60 cm positions with tape and a ruler.
5. A book is placed as a stop before the end of the bench.

Roles are balloon inflator/releaser, timer, position observer and recorder. One student inflates the balloon throughout so balloons are not shared by mouth.

#### Minutes 6–9: Standardise the thrust source

1. Students inflate the balloon until its widest part reaches a set width, such as 12 cm, measured with the ruler.
2. They pinch the neck closed and place the car at the start line.
3. Before release, they observe it for three seconds and record that its position does not change.

#### Minutes 9–17: Unloaded trials

Students conduct three unloaded trials:

1. The timer starts as the neck is released.
2. The timer records the time taken for the front of the car to reach 30 cm.
3. The position observer notes the nearest 10 cm mark at which the balloon becomes visibly slack.
4. After the car stops, students measure its final position.
5. They record whether the car continued moving after the balloon stopped expelling air.

#### Minutes 17–25: Loaded trials

1. Students secure the 100 g mass centrally and low on the car using two crossing strips of tape, keeping the wheels clear.
2. They repeat three trials using the same car, balloon, nozzle, track and 12 cm inflation width.
3. They compare median time to 30 cm for the unloaded and loaded car. If the loaded car does not reach 30 cm, they instead record its position after two seconds.

#### Minutes 25–29: Direction-change test

Students place a cardboard strip at a shallow angle in the path, braced by a book. They run one unloaded trial and sketch the car’s incoming and outgoing motion. A stop, turn or rebound is recorded as a change in velocity caused by the bumper’s contact force.

#### Minutes 29–32: Force timeline

Groups draw the car at four moments:

1. before release;
2. while air is escaping;
3. just after the balloon becomes slack;
4. as the car finally stops.

They add force arrows and label whether the forces are balanced or unbalanced.

#### Minutes 32–35: Pack-up

Balloons are deflated, tape and cardboard are collected, and cars and masses are returned.

### 3. Science payoff

The model makes the difference between a force and inertia visible over time:

- Before release, gravity is balanced by the bench’s upward support, and there is no unbalanced horizontal force. The car remains at rest.
- Escaping air produces an unbalanced forward force, so the car speeds up.
- When the balloon becomes slack, the forward thrust has ended, but the car does not stop immediately. It continues moving because continued motion does not require a continuing forward push.
- The car eventually slows because rolling friction and air resistance provide an unbalanced backward force. In the absence of those forces, it would continue at constant velocity.
- The angled bumper supplies an unbalanced contact force that changes speed and/or direction.
- Under a comparable balloon thrust pulse, the loaded car changes its motion less or takes longer to cover the first 30 cm. The added mass gives the car greater inertia.

The resulting generalisation is that balanced forces produce no change in velocity, while an unbalanced force produces a change in speed, direction or both.

### 4. Evidence

Groups record:

| Load | Balloon width | Time to 30 cm | Position when balloon became slack | Final stopping position |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| No added mass | | | | |
| 100 g added | | | | |

They use three trials and compare medians. Specific supporting evidence includes:

- no position change while the balloon is pinched;
- a shorter median time to 30 cm for the unloaded car;
- a final stopping position beyond the position where the balloon became slack, showing continued motion after thrust ended;
- a path sketch showing a velocity change at the angled bumper;
- a four-stage force timeline connecting each change in motion with an unbalanced force.

### 5. Feasibility

Seven groups of four can run one car on each fixed bench. The teacher can prepare seven short cardboard nozzle sleeves and group kits alone before the lesson. Books and cardboard side barriers prevent cars from leaving the benches.

The 35-minute block includes six minutes for assembly and three minutes for cleanup. No sensors or video analysis are needed; positions are read from ruler and tape marks, and times are measured with ordinary stopwatches. The 5-minute introduction defines force and demonstrates safe balloon release. During the 10-minute synthesis, groups compare the thrust, coast and stop phases and formulate Newton’s First Law.

### 6. Failure risk

The most likely failure is that axle friction or inconsistent balloon thrust makes the car stop almost as soon as the balloon becomes slack. Students could then conclude that motion ends because the forward force “runs out.”

The teacher should pre-test the listed toy cars and use those that roll freely, direct the balloon neck with the cardboard sleeve, and keep tape away from the wheels. All mass comparisons use the same car and balloon inflated to the same measured width, with three trials and a median. Requiring students to record both the balloon-slack position and the final stopping position keeps the distinction between the end of thrust and the later effect of friction explicit.
