Each option below is an alternative 35-minute main block. In each case, the class works as seven groups of four at their own bench.

## 1. Force-meter cart lanes

### 1. Mechanism name
**Force-meter cart lanes**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group has a toy car with a string loop taped to its front, one spring scale, a ruler, stopwatch, tape, and a 100 g mass.

1. Students work on a 60 cm tape lane marked at 0, 20, 40 and 60 cm.
2. First, they leave the car untouched at the 0 cm line for five seconds and record that it remains at rest.
3. One student pulls the car with the spring scale at an approximately steady speed. A second student watches the scale, a third times the car passing each 20 cm mark, and a fourth records. They find the approximate scale reading needed for steady motion, labelled \(F_{\text{steady}}\).
4. They repeat the run while the puller keeps the scale reading about 0.3 N above \(F_{\text{steady}}\). The timer records the time for each 20 cm section.
5. They tape a 100 g mass centrally onto the car. They again find the new \(F_{\text{steady}}\), then repeat the higher-force run using the same extra 0.3 N above that new steady-motion value.
6. Students compare the timing pattern for the unloaded and loaded car. They write one force-arrow sketch for the steady run and one for the speeding-up run.

A suitable evidence table is:

| Car condition | Steady-pull reading | Pull target | 0–20 cm time | 20–40 cm time | 40–60 cm time |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Empty car | | | | | |
| Car + 100 g | | | | | |

### 3. Science payoff
Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- A car at rest has no change in motion when the forces on it are balanced overall.
- When the car moves at steady speed, the forward pull shown by the spring scale is balanced by backward rolling friction; the vertical support force and weight are also balanced.
- When the pull is greater than the backward friction, there is an unbalanced forward force, so the car speeds up.
- If approximately the same unbalanced force is used, adding mass produces a smaller change in the car’s motion. Greater mass therefore means greater inertia.

### 4. Evidence
Students record:

- The spring-scale reading during steady motion.
- The higher spring-scale reading used in the speeding-up trial.
- Three equal-distance travel times. Shorter times in later 20 cm sections indicate that the car is speeding up.
- A comparison of how much the interval times change for the empty and loaded car.
- Force-arrow sketches showing balanced forces in the steady run and an unbalanced forward force in the speeding-up run.

### 5. Feasibility
Before class, the teacher tapes seven 60 cm lanes on benches and attaches string loops to seven cars. Each group receives one pre-set kit. The stipulated five-minute introduction can show students how to read the spring scale and distinguish a force from a net force.

Suggested main-block timing:

- 0–3 min: roles, lane check and resting-car observation  
- 3–9 min: find steady-motion force  
- 9–17 min: empty-car higher-force trial  
- 17–26 min: loaded-car trials  
- 26–31 min: compare evidence and draw force arrows  
- 31–35 min: return equipment to the bench end  

All movement is along benches; students do not need to circulate. One teacher can monitor scale use and call time checkpoints.

### 6. Failure risk
**Likely failure:** The puller changes the force constantly, so the timing pattern reflects jerky pulling rather than an unbalanced force.

**Mitigation:** Give each group a narrow target band, such as “keep the pointer between 0.5 and 0.6 N,” and assign a separate student as scale reader who calls “higher” or “lower.” Use the later two 20 cm intervals for comparison, since the car is already moving and static friction is no longer the main issue. The teacher should demonstrate one steady run before groups begin.

## 2. Support-and-passenger inertia trials

### 1. Mechanism name
**Support-and-passenger inertia trials**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group has a plastic cup, cardboard strips, a marble, toy car, ruler, books, tape, and a 100 g mass.

The group performs three linked trials in which a support or collision force affects one object but not another.

**Trial A: card-pull test**
1. Students place a cardboard strip across the top of an upright plastic cup and put a marble on the strip directly above the cup opening.
2. Before testing, each student sketches where they predict the marble will go.
3. One student pulls the strip slowly sideways. The group records whether the marble is carried sideways with the strip or falls into the cup.
4. They reset the apparatus and pull the strip sharply sideways. They record the marble’s position again.

**Trial B: passenger-car stop**
1. Students make a shallow cardboard passenger tray and tape it onto the top of a toy car. A marble sits in the tray.
2. They push the car gently along a short cardboard lane without a barrier and observe the marble remaining with the car.
3. They reset the car, place a book barrier across the lane, and push the car gently into it.
4. They sketch the stopped car and the marble’s final position. A ruler is used to measure how far the marble moves beyond the stopped car.

**Trial C: mass-and-collision comparison**
1. Students make a short marble ramp by resting one end of a ruler on a book.
2. They release the same marble from the same marked point on the ramp so it strikes a stationary toy car at the bottom.
3. They measure the car’s displacement in centimetres.
4. They tape 100 g onto the car, repeat the same marble release three times, and compare the displacements.

### 3. Science payoff
Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- A force must act on the object itself to change its motion. Pulling the support away is not the same as directly pulling the marble.
- A marble at rest tends to remain at rest when the card is pulled quickly because the card has little time to exert a horizontal force on it.
- When a car is stopped by a book, the book’s unbalanced backward force acts on the car. The marble passenger continues forward because that stopping force has not initially acted on it.
- Before the marble collision, the car remains at rest because its forces are balanced overall. The collision provides an unbalanced force and starts it moving.
- Under the same controlled marble release, the loaded car undergoes a smaller visible change from rest than the empty car. Greater mass means greater resistance to a change in motion: greater inertia.

### 4. Evidence
Students collect:

- A before-and-after sketch for the slow and fast card pulls.
- A record of whether the marble falls into the cup or moves sideways with the card.
- A top-view sketch of the stopped car and marble passenger, with an arrow showing the marble’s direction.
- The marble’s distance beyond the stopped car in centimetres.
- Three car-displacement measurements for the empty car and three for the 100 g loaded car after the same marble release point.

### 5. Feasibility
The teacher prepares seven short cardboard strips, seven simple cardboard car trays, and seven short bench lanes before class. Each group remains at one bench. Book barriers are placed well away from bench edges, and students use only gentle car pushes.

Suggested main-block timing:

- 0–3 min: distribute roles and materials  
- 3–10 min: card-pull predictions and trials  
- 10–18 min: passenger-car trials  
- 18–27 min: marble-ramp collision comparison  
- 27–31 min: complete evidence sketches and measurements  
- 31–35 min: pack equipment at bench ends  

No specialist apparatus is needed. The teacher can inspect the first card-pull setup, then circulate while groups repeat it.

### 6. Failure risk
**Likely failure:** Students may explain the marble moving forward as “a force called momentum pushing it forward,” rather than recognising that the marble continues because no stopping force has yet acted on it.

**Mitigation:** Require a force-arrow sketch after both car trials. Students must label which object the barrier touches: the car, not the marble. The slow card-pull comparison is essential: it shows that when the card has time to exert a sideways force on the marble, the marble’s motion does change.

## 3. Balloon thrust and coast comparison

### 1. Mechanism name
**Balloon thrust and coast comparison**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group has a toy car, one balloon, tape, ruler, stopwatch, a 100 g mass, and an 80 cm tape lane marked every 10 cm.

1. Students tape the neck of a deflated balloon to the top of the car so that the opening points backward. The balloon itself must be free to inflate.
2. One student gives the deflated car a gentle push and removes their hand. The group observes and records that it continues to move for a time after the hand is no longer touching it.
3. One student inflates the balloon to an agreed width against a ruler, for example 8 cm wide. The same student inflates it for all trials so the method is as consistent as possible.
4. The balloon is released at the 0 cm line. One student times the car to the 40 cm mark, one watches for the point at which the balloon becomes limp, and one records the nearest lane mark when this happens and where the car finally stops.
5. The group repeats the empty-car launch once.
6. They tape a 100 g mass securely to the car, use the same balloon width, and repeat two launches.
7. Students draw a three-stage force-and-motion sequence: car at rest; car under balloon thrust; car coasting after the balloon is limp.

A suitable evidence table is:

| Condition | Time to 40 cm | Position when balloon goes limp | Final stopping position |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Empty car, trial 1 | | | |
| Empty car, trial 2 | | | |
| Car + 100 g, trial 1 | | | |
| Car + 100 g, trial 2 | | | |

### 3. Science payoff
Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- A car at rest does not start moving until an unbalanced force acts on it. In this case, the balloon provides a forward driving force.
- While the balloon’s forward force is greater than backward rolling resistance, the car speeds up because the forces are unbalanced.
- The car still moves after the balloon has become limp. A forward force is not needed simply to keep an object moving.
- After the balloon force ends, rolling friction is the main horizontal force and acts backward, so the car slows and stops.
- With a similarly inflated balloon, the loaded car has a smaller change in motion than the empty car. The added mass gives the car greater inertia.
- A parked car is one example of balanced forces: its weight and support force balance vertically, and there is no unbalanced horizontal force. A moving object would also have no change in velocity if forces were balanced.

### 4. Evidence
Students record:

- Whether the pushed, deflated car continues after the hand leaves it.
- Time to reach the 40 cm mark for empty and loaded conditions.
- The car’s position when balloon thrust ends and its final stopping position.
- A measured coasting distance: final stopping position minus position when the balloon becomes limp.
- Force-arrow sketches showing forward thrust and backward friction while the balloon is active, then backward friction after thrust ends.

### 5. Feasibility
The teacher pre-marks seven 80 cm bench lanes and checks beforehand that the chosen balloon size does not send cars near bench edges. One balloon per group avoids sharing mouth-contact equipment. Groups inflate and release only at their own bench.

Suggested main-block timing:

- 0–4 min: attach balloon, allocate roles and inspect lane  
- 4–9 min: deflated push-and-coast observation  
- 9–19 min: two empty-car balloon runs  
- 19–29 min: two loaded-car balloon runs  
- 29–31 min: compare data and complete force-arrow sequence  
- 31–35 min: deflate balloons and return materials  

The teacher’s management task is mainly checking that balloon openings point backward, masses are taped securely, and cars are launched away from bench edges.

### 6. Failure risk
**Likely failure:** Different balloon inflation amounts produce inconsistent results, so students compare balloon size rather than car mass.

**Mitigation:** Use the same car and balloon for both conditions, specify a visible inflation width using the ruler, run two trials per condition, and compare the pair of results within each group rather than pooling all class data. The teacher should pre-test the balloon width and car model before the lesson so that both the empty and 100 g loaded car can complete the 40 cm run.
