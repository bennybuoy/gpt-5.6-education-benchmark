## 1. Rapid support-removal trials

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Rapid support removal**

### 2. Physical student action  
Each group receives a plastic cup, a square of cardboard, a marble, a 100 g mass, tape and a ruler.

1. Students place the cardboard across the top of an upright plastic cup and place a marble in the centre of the cardboard.
2. One student sharply flicks the cardboard sideways with a ruler while another watches from the side. The marble drops into the cup.
3. They repeat, but this time pull the cardboard slowly. They record whether the marble moves sideways with the card or falls into the cup.
4. They replace the marble with a 100 g mass and repeat the rapid removal, observing that the mass remains nearly above its original position while the cardboard moves away.
5. Students sketch a before-and-after diagram for the marble/card/cup setup. They add force arrows:
   - Before flicking: upward support force from cardboard and downward weight are balanced.
   - After the cardboard is removed: the downward weight is unbalanced, so the marble falls.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at the principle:

> An object at rest does not start moving sideways simply because the surface beneath it moves away. It changes its motion only when an unbalanced force acts on it for enough time.

The slow-pull result is important: friction between the cardboard and marble can provide a sideways force and make the marble move with the card. The rapid flick gives that force little time to change the marble’s motion.

This provides an initial, concrete meaning for inertia: resistance to a change in motion.

### 4. Evidence  
Students record a small results table such as:

| Trial | Card movement | Object used | Did object move sideways with card? | Did it fall into cup? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Fast flick | Marble | No/very little | Yes |
| 2 | Slow pull | Marble | Yes/somewhat | Often no |
| 3 | Fast flick | 100 g mass | No/very little | Yes |

They also produce a labelled sketch showing the marble’s original position, the displaced card and the marble falling vertically into the cup.

### 5. Feasibility  
- The teacher can prepare seven kits before class: one cup, cardboard square, marble and mass per group.
- The activity requires no long runways, moving students or specialised setup.
- Groups can complete several trials in about 20 minutes, then spend 10 minutes making their force sketches and comparing fast versus slow pulls.
- The remaining 5 minutes of the main block can be used for groups to write one claim supported by their table.
- Cleanup is simply returning marbles, cups, masses and cardboard to a tray.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure:** Students may conclude that “fast things make objects fall into cups” rather than recognising that the marble initially stays in its state of rest.

**Mitigation:** Require the slow-pull comparison. Ask: “In both trials, the card moved. Why did the marble move sideways only when the card was pulled slowly?” Emphasise that the horizontal friction force had more time to act in the slow trial. Also insist that students identify the downward unbalanced force after the card is removed: gravity.

## 2. Two-scale force-balance investigation

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Opposing-force cart test**

### 2. Physical student action  
Each group uses a toy car, two lengths of string, tape, a 5 N spring scale, a 10 N spring scale, cardboard and masses.

1. Students make a straight cardboard lane on their bench.
2. They tape one string to the front of the car and one to the rear. Each string is connected to a spring scale.
3. Two students pull in opposite directions while a third student reads the scales and a fourth records observations.
4. They first arrange approximately equal readings, for example 2 N forward and 2 N backward. The car starts at rest.
5. They then deliberately make the pulls unequal, for example 3 N forward and 1 N backward. They record the direction in which the car begins moving.
6. They reverse the imbalance, such as 1 N forward and 3 N backward, and record the changed direction.
7. Students add a 100 g mass to the car with tape, then repeat a chosen unequal-pull trial as consistently as possible. They compare how readily the unloaded and loaded cars begin moving.

Groups make force-arrow diagrams for each trial. The arrows represent the pulls shown by the scales.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these principles:

> Equal and opposite forces are balanced, so they do not change an object’s motion.

> Unequal forces are unbalanced, so the object’s motion changes in the direction of the larger force.

> A larger mass has greater inertia: it resists a change from rest more than a smaller mass when given a comparable push or pull.

The teacher can state explicitly that the equal-force trial begins with the car at rest, so it remains at rest. If it were already moving and the forces remained balanced, Newton’s First Law predicts that it would continue at constant velocity.

### 4. Evidence  
Students collect numerical and observational evidence:

| Trial | Forward scale reading | Backward scale reading | Force arrows balanced? | What did the car do? |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| A | 2 N | 2 N | Yes | Stayed at rest |
| B | 3 N | 1 N | No; forward | Began moving forward |
| C | 1 N | 3 N | No; backward | Began moving backward |

For the mass comparison, students record:

| Car load | Applied pull pattern | Did the car begin moving easily, slowly, or not at first? |
|---|---|---|
| Car only | Same chosen imbalance | Observation |
| Car + 100 g | Same chosen imbalance | Observation |

Their force-arrow diagram is also evidence: equal-length opposite arrows for balanced pulls, unequal arrows for an unbalanced force.

### 5. Feasibility  
- The teacher prepares cardboard lanes and pre-ties strings to the cars before class.
- Seven groups can work simultaneously if there are enough scales; if the number of scales is limited, three scale stations can run while other groups complete the force-arrow record sheet and rotate after about 8–10 minutes.
- This is manageable with one teacher because the movement is confined to benches and the force readings are simple.
- A workable 35-minute allocation is:
  - 5 minutes: assemble lane and practise reading scales;
  - 15 minutes: equal and unequal force trials;
  - 10 minutes: loaded-car comparison;
  - 5 minutes: force-arrow diagrams and claim.
- Cleanup involves removing tape and returning scales, string, cars and masses.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure:** Students may treat the difference between the two scale readings as the exact net force while ignoring rolling resistance and friction.

**Mitigation:** Describe the scale readings as the major applied horizontal forces and state that friction is another small backward force. The purpose is not to calculate a precise acceleration; it is to compare clearly balanced pulls with clearly unbalanced pulls. Use a smooth cardboard lane, keep the scale differences large, such as 3 N versus 1 N, and avoid claiming that the car has “no forces” when it stays still.

## 3. Controlled collision comparison

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Marble-to-car collision trials**

### 2. Physical student action  
Each group uses cardboard, books, a marble, a toy car, tape, a ruler and 100 g, 200 g and 500 g masses.

1. Students make a low cardboard ramp by placing one end on a book.
2. They tape a ruler beside a straight cardboard runway.
3. They place the toy car at the zero mark at the bottom of the ramp.
4. They release the same marble from the same marked point on the ramp so it rolls into the stationary car.
5. They measure how far the car moves from its starting point after the collision.
6. They repeat three times with the unloaded car.
7. They tape a 100 g mass securely to the car and repeat the same release trials.
8. If the car remains stable, they repeat with 200 g or 500 g. Groups also record what happens to the marble after impact: stops, rebounds or continues forward.

Roles rotate: releaser, car-positioner, distance measurer and recorder.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these principles:

> The car remains at rest until the collision applies an unbalanced force to it.

> During the collision, the force changes the car’s motion from rest to motion.

> A more massive car generally shows a smaller change in motion from a comparable collision, because greater mass means greater inertia.

They also see that the moving marble does not stop merely because it is moving. Its motion changes when it meets the car and experiences an unbalanced collision force.

### 4. Evidence  
Students create a table and, if time permits, a simple bar graph.

| Car mass condition | Marble release point | Trial 1 distance | Trial 2 distance | Trial 3 distance | Typical result |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Car only | Same marked point | cm | cm | cm | cm |
| Car + 100 g | Same marked point | cm | cm | cm | cm |
| Car + 200 g | Same marked point | cm | cm | cm | cm |

They also record collision observations:

| Car condition | Did the car move? | Marble after collision: stop, rebound, or continue? |
|---|---|---|
| Car only | Yes/no | Observation |
| Loaded car | Yes/no | Observation |

The expected pattern is that the same released marble moves the lighter car farther than the heavier car.

### 5. Feasibility  
- The teacher can prepare seven small ramp/runway kits from cardboard and books before the lesson.
- The ramps are low, so marbles stay on benches and collisions remain controlled.
- Each group needs only a short runway, approximately 60–80 cm, which fits on a laboratory bench.
- A 35-minute structure is:
  - 5 minutes: construct ramp and mark the release point;
  - 12 minutes: unloaded-car trials;
  - 12 minutes: loaded-car trials;
  - 6 minutes: calculate a typical distance and write a conclusion.
- The teacher’s management task is mainly checking that masses are taped securely and that groups always release from the same mark.
- Cleanup consists of removing tape and stacking cardboard, books, cars, marbles and masses.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure:** Students may attribute the shorter travel of a loaded car entirely to extra friction rather than to greater inertia.

**Mitigation:** Use toy cars rather than sliding cups so rolling friction is relatively small. Keep the runway surface and marble release point identical for every trial. Frame the evidence carefully: the collision gives a comparable incoming marble motion, yet the heavier car shows less change in motion. Do not claim the distance data are a perfectly controlled measurement of inertia; use them as evidence of the pattern, then connect that pattern to Newton’s First Law in the synthesis discussion.
