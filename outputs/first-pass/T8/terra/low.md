## 1. Abrupt-stop inertia demonstrations

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Card-and-cart abrupt-stop investigations**

### 2. Physical student action  
Groups work at their bench with a plastic cup, cardboard square, marble, toy car, book, tape and ruler.

**First 12 minutes: object at rest**
1. Students place a cardboard square across the top of a plastic cup and place a marble in the centre of the card.
2. One student holds the cup steady; another gives the card a quick horizontal flick.
3. Students repeat, first with a slow pull and then with a quick flick.
4. They observe where the marble goes and sketch the before-and-after positions.

**Next 15 minutes: object in motion**
1. Students tape a small cardboard “tray” to a toy car and place a marble or 100 g mass loosely in the tray.
2. One student rolls the car along a ruler-marked track on the bench.
3. Another student stops the car suddenly by placing a book at the end of the track.
4. Students observe the object in the tray continuing forward when the car stops.
5. They repeat with a gentler stop, such as catching the car by hand over a short distance rather than against the book.

**Final 8 minutes: explanation record**
1. Students make two labelled sketches: “marble at rest” and “marble moving with car”.
2. They draw arrows showing the direction of motion before the card/car stopped and after it stopped.
3. They identify what exerted the unbalanced force: the hand on the card, or the book on the car.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these specific ideas:

- A marble initially at rest does not automatically move with the card when the card is flicked away; it tends to remain at rest until a force acts on it.
- When the car is stopped by the book, the car experiences an unbalanced force and changes motion, but the loose object continues moving forward briefly because it has not yet experienced the same stopping force.
- Objects do not “need a force to keep moving”; they need an unbalanced force to change their motion.
- Friction and collisions are forces that eventually stop the marble.

### 4. Evidence  
Each group records:

- A labelled before-and-after sketch of the marble-on-card trial.
- A labelled before-and-after sketch of the moving-car trial.
- A table such as:

| Trial | What received the stopping/pulling force first? | What did the marble do? | Evidence of inertia |
|---|---|---|---|
| Card flick | Card | Fell into cup / stayed nearly in place | Marble resisted moving with card |
| Car hits book | Car | Moved forward in tray | Marble resisted stopping with car |

Students can also compare a gentle and sudden stop, recording whether the marble moves farther forward during the more sudden stop.

### 5. Feasibility  
- Eight bench groups can each receive one cup, card, marble, car, ruler and book.
- Materials require no preparation beyond pre-cut cardboard squares and tape marks on benches.
- The teacher demonstrates the card flick once during the first minute of the main block, then circulates.
- The book stopping the car keeps the activity contained on benches; no running or floor space is needed.
- Cleanup is simply returning marbles, cars and cards to a tray. It can be completed in about two minutes.

A workable 35-minute allocation is: 3-minute demonstration and safety reminder, 12 minutes for the card trial, 15 minutes for the car trial, and 5 minutes for evidence sketches.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure mode:** Students say, “The marble moves forward because the car pushes it forward,” rather than recognising that the car stops while the marble continues moving.

**Mitigation:** Require students to identify separately:
- what object received the stopping force first, and
- what object was still moving after the car stopped.

The teacher can ask: “Did the book touch the marble?” If not, “Why did the marble keep moving when the car had already stopped?” This directs attention to the different forces acting on the two objects.

## 2. Measured force-balance tests

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Opposing-pull force balance tests**

### 2. Physical student action  
Students use toy cars, string, spring scales, rulers and books. Four prepared test stations are set up at the front-side benches, each with a toy car connected by string to spring scales on opposite sides. Two groups rotate through each station during the 35 minutes.

At a station, groups take these roles: left puller, right puller, observer/reader, and recorder.

**First 10 minutes: balanced pulls**
1. The two pullers attach or hold strings on opposite sides of a toy car.
2. They each pull gently until both spring scales show the same value, for example 2 N and 2 N.
3. The observer watches whether the car changes motion.
4. Students repeat with another equal pair of readings, such as 1 N and 1 N.

**Next 12 minutes: unbalanced pulls**
1. One student pulls with a larger force than the other, for example 3 N to the right and 1 N to the left.
2. The group observes which direction the car starts to move.
3. They reverse the larger pull and observe the car move or change motion in the opposite direction.
4. They try a case in which the car is already rolling slowly and then apply a larger opposing force, observing it slow or stop.

**Final 8 minutes: force-arrow records**
1. Students draw the car as a box.
2. They draw horizontal arrows for the pulls, with longer arrows for larger spring-scale readings.
3. They identify whether the forces are balanced or unbalanced.
4. They write the observed change in motion: stayed at rest, sped up, slowed down, or changed direction.

While waiting for their station turn, the paired group prepares prediction diagrams from the trial cards supplied at the bench: for example, “4 N right and 4 N left” or “5 N right and 2 N left.”

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these specific ideas:

- Forces are pushes or pulls and can act in different directions.
- When opposing forces are equal, they are balanced: there is no change in motion. A stationary car remains stationary.
- When one horizontal force is greater than the opposing force, forces are unbalanced.
- An unbalanced force changes motion in the direction of the larger force: it can start an object moving, speed it up, slow it down or change its direction.
- “Balanced” does not mean “no forces”; it means the forces cancel overall.

### 4. Evidence  
Students produce a force-and-motion table such as:

| Left force | Right force | Balanced or unbalanced? | Observed motion |
|---:|---:|---|---|
| 2 N | 2 N | Balanced | Car stayed still |
| 1 N | 3 N | Unbalanced to right | Car moved right |
| 4 N | 1 N | Unbalanced to left | Car moved left |
| 3 N | 3 N | Balanced | No new change in motion |

They also draw force-arrow diagrams. The measured spring-scale readings are direct evidence that equal and unequal pulls produce different motion outcomes.

### 5. Feasibility  
- Only four force-balance stations are needed, not eight full sets. Four groups run the physical tests while four groups complete prediction diagrams; they swap after approximately 15 minutes.
- Each station needs one toy car, two lengths of string, two spring scales and a ruler. A book can mark the start area and prevent the car travelling off the bench.
- The teacher demonstrates how to pull horizontally rather than lifting the car or scale.
- Students remain at benches, and the spring scales give immediate visible readings without technology.
- Setup consists of preparing four labelled trays before class. Cleanup is detaching string and returning scales and cars.

A suitable timing is: 3 minutes for the teacher demonstration, 12 minutes first rotation, 12 minutes second rotation, and 8 minutes for recording diagrams and conclusions.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure mode:** Students may pull unevenly or jerk the strings, producing changing scale readings and confusing the relationship between force and motion.

**Mitigation:** Use a clear procedure: students must hold each reading steady for a three-second count before the observer records it. The teacher should model a gentle horizontal pull and state that the group records the displayed forces, not a guessed force. For the first balanced trial, students keep the car stationary, avoiding the extra complication of friction while it rolls.

## 3. Same-force, different-mass cart comparison

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Equal-pull mass comparison**

### 2. Physical student action  
Groups compare how a toy car changes motion when the same approximate pulling force is applied to different masses. Each test lane is a ruler taped to the bench, with a start line and a 60 cm measuring length.

Each group has a toy car, string, one spring scale, tape, a stopwatch, ruler, and 100 g, 200 g and 500 g masses.

**First 5 minutes: prepare the cart**
1. Students tape a small cardboard platform to the toy car.
2. They test that the car rolls freely.
3. They mark a start line and place the ruler alongside the path.
4. They make three mass conditions: car alone; car plus 100 g; car plus 500 g. The masses are taped securely so they do not fall off.

**Next 20 minutes: controlled pulls**
1. One student holds the spring scale attached to the car by string.
2. A second student calls “go” and times one second.
3. The puller aims to keep the spring scale close to an agreed value, such as 2 N, for that one second.
4. At “stop,” the puller stops pulling and the observer marks or notes where the front of the car reached.
5. The group measures the distance travelled in that one-second pull.
6. They repeat twice for each mass condition and calculate a simple average distance.

Students rotate roles after each mass condition.

**Final 10 minutes: compare and explain**
1. Students create a bar-style graph or ordered list of average distance travelled in one second.
2. They compare the lightest and heaviest car.
3. They write a conclusion using the words mass, inertia, force and change in motion.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these specific ideas:

- Mass is a measure of inertia.
- For approximately the same applied unbalanced pull, a more massive object has a smaller change in motion than a less massive object.
- The heavier car is not “harder to move because it wants to stay still”; it has greater inertia, meaning greater resistance to changing its state of motion.
- A force is required to change the car’s motion from rest, and the same-sized force changes a low-mass car’s motion more noticeably than a high-mass car’s motion.

This introduces inertia through comparison without requiring students to use the formal equation \(F = ma\).

### 4. Evidence  
Students record measured distances for the same one-second pull:

| Total mass condition | Pull target | Trial 1 distance | Trial 2 distance | Average distance |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Car only | about 2 N | ___ cm | ___ cm | ___ cm |
| Car + 100 g | about 2 N | ___ cm | ___ cm | ___ cm |
| Car + 500 g | about 2 N | ___ cm | ___ cm | ___ cm |

Their evidence should show that, with a similar pull over the same time, the heavier cart generally travels a shorter distance. They then write: “The heavier cart had greater inertia because it changed its motion less under a similar unbalanced force.”

### 5. Feasibility  
- Four cart lanes can be prepared using rulers taped to benches, allowing half the groups to test while the other half prepares graphs and prediction statements; groups swap after about 15 minutes.
- Each lane requires only one car, one spring scale, string, tape, a ruler and masses already listed in the equipment budget.
- The test lasts only one second per trial, so groups can complete six pulls in a few minutes.
- The teacher’s management task is simple: ensure masses are taped down, cars stay on benches, and students pull horizontally.
- Setup before class involves taping four rulers and start lines to benches. Cleanup takes about two minutes.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure mode:** Students may not maintain the same pulling force across trials. A heavier cart may appear to move less simply because it was pulled more weakly.

**Mitigation:** Give all groups one clearly marked target force, such as 2 N, and require the observer to watch the spring-scale reading during the pull. Students should repeat each condition twice and compare averages. The teacher should also state that the test is a comparison of *similar* pulls, not an exact precision experiment. If a trial has an obviously different force reading, students discard and repeat it.
