## 1. Cart force-change investigation

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Toy-car push, coast and stop trials**

### 2. Physical student action  
Groups of 3–4 work at one bench with a toy car, ruler, tape, string, masses, cardboard and a stopwatch.

- Students tape a 1 m test lane along the bench using a ruler and tape, marking 0 cm, 50 cm and 100 cm.
- One student gives the car a consistent push from the start line; another times it travelling between marks; a third observes and records; a fourth, where available, resets the car and checks fair testing.
- They repeat the trial three times on the bare bench.
- They then place a cardboard strip over part of the lane and repeat. The cardboard creates a different surface/friction condition.
- Next, they tape a 100 g mass securely to the car and repeat the same push-and-coast trials. If time permits, they compare 100 g and 500 g loads.
- Students then use string to pull the car gently at approximately constant speed. One student watches the spring scale. They compare:
  - a steady pull that keeps the car moving evenly;
  - a larger pull that makes it speed up;
  - no pull, after which it slows because friction acts.

The group produces a simple force-and-motion table rather than attempting highly precise calculations.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these specific ideas:

- A moving car changes motion only when there is an **unbalanced force** on it.
- A car moving at steady speed has **balanced forces**: the forward pull matches friction in the opposite direction.
- When the pulling force is greater than friction, the car speeds up; when friction is greater than the forward force, it slows down.
- Adding mass makes the car harder to start, stop or speed up because greater mass means greater inertia.
- The car does not “naturally stop”; it stops because friction is an unbalanced force.

### 4. Evidence  
Students record:

| Trial | Car mass/load | Surface | Pulling force shown on scale | What happened to motion? |
|---|---:|---|---:|---|
| 1 | car only | bench | no pull after push | slowed/stopped |
| 2 | car only | bench | steady reading | approximately constant speed |
| 3 | car only | bench | larger than steady pull | sped up |
| 4 | car + 100 g | bench | same pull as trial 3 | smaller speed change / harder to start |
| 5 | car only | cardboard | steady pull is larger | more friction |

They can also sketch force arrows for a car moving steadily and for a car slowing after the push ends.

### 5. Feasibility  
- **Setup:** Before class, the teacher places one prepared kit at each of seven benches: car, spring scale, string, ruler, tape, cardboard strip and a small set of masses. This takes a few minutes before students enter.
- **Timing within the 35-minute block:**  
  - 5 minutes: establish lane and assign roles.  
  - 15 minutes: push/coast and surface trials.  
  - 10 minutes: spring-scale pulling trials.  
  - 5 minutes: complete evidence table and force sketches.
- **Management:** Cars remain on bench lanes, not on the floor. Groups are told that masses must be taped to cars before movement. Roles reduce crowding and ensure every student has a task.
- **Cleanup:** Students return the mass, string and scale to the tray and remove tape from the bench in the final minute.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure:** Students conclude that a force is required to keep an object moving because the car always slows after being pushed.

**Mitigation:** Make the slowing itself the question: “What is touching the car after your hand lets go?” Have students identify friction from the bench/cardboard. Then compare a gentle steady pull with a larger pull: if the scale reading is steady and the car’s speed is steady, students can see that the pull is balancing friction rather than “making motion happen.” Explicitly state that an ideal friction-free object would continue at constant velocity without a continuing push.

## 2. Tablecloth/card-and-marble inertia demonstration stations

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Support-removal inertia stations**

### 2. Physical student action  
Groups rotate through a short set of closely related “remove the support” challenges using cups, cardboard, marbles, books and tape.

At each group’s bench:

- Students place a cardboard strip on the bench and put a plastic cup or marble on top.
- One student quickly pulls the cardboard strip sideways while another observes the cup or marble.
- They repeat with a slow pull, then a fast pull.
- Students then make a “cup-and-card” setup: a cardboard square lies over a cup, with a marble on the card. One student flicks or pulls the card sideways. The marble drops into the cup.
- For a mass comparison, students place a 100 g mass on a larger cardboard strip and compare how easily it begins moving when the card is pulled slowly versus quickly. They do not need to pull it off the bench; they observe that a heavier object resists starting to move with the cardboard.
- Groups sketch before-and-after positions and label what object moved because it was pulled and what object tended to remain in its original state.

The teacher first models safe sideways pulls at the front bench and makes clear that students pull cards parallel to the bench, not upward.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these specific ideas:

- Objects tend to keep their current motion state: an object at rest tends to remain at rest unless an unbalanced force acts on it.
- When the card is pulled away quickly, the force acts mainly on the card, not long enough to move the marble or cup much; the object’s inertia keeps it nearly at rest.
- Gravity then pulls the unsupported marble downward into the cup.
- A larger mass has greater inertia and therefore resists being set into motion more strongly.
- The result is not caused by the marble “wanting to fall into the cup”; it is caused by the support being removed while the marble initially remains where it was.

### 4. Evidence  
Students create a station record with labelled diagrams:

1. **Fast card pull:**  
   - Card: moves sideways  
   - Marble/cup: remains nearly in original horizontal position  
   - Then marble: falls vertically due to gravity

2. **Slow card pull:**  
   - Card friction has more time to pull object sideways  
   - Object moves more with card

3. **Mass comparison:**  
   - 100 g versus 500 g mass: record which required a stronger/longer pull to start sliding.

Students complete the sentence:

> When the support was pulled quickly, the marble stayed nearly at rest because the horizontal force on it was too small/too brief to overcome its inertia.

### 5. Feasibility  
- **Setup:** Each of seven groups needs only cups, cardboard pieces, marbles and masses. The teacher can pre-cut cardboard squares and place them in trays.
- **Timing within the 35-minute block:**  
  - 5 minutes: teacher demonstration and safety routine.  
  - 20 minutes: groups perform fast/slow support-removal trials and mass comparison.  
  - 10 minutes: groups make labelled before-and-after sketches and answer explanation prompts.
- **Management:** The work remains contained on benches. Marbles can be kept inside a shallow “work zone” made from books or rulers at the edge of the bench, reducing rolling. Only one student pulls at a time.
- **Cleanup:** All materials return to trays; no water, adhesives or complex apparatus are involved.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure:** Students focus on the trick—“pull it quickly and it works”—without identifying inertia, or they think speed itself magically causes the marble to stay still.

**Mitigation:** Require comparison of slow and fast pulls and have students draw force arrows. Ask: “Which object did your hand directly pull?” and “What horizontal force acted on the marble?” The key distinction is that friction between card and marble can act, but in a quick pull it acts too briefly to give the marble much change in motion. The teacher should also explicitly separate the horizontal inertia explanation from the vertical gravitational fall.

## 3. Human force-map and prediction circuit

### 1. Mechanism name  
**Bench-top motion scenarios with force-arrow modelling**

### 2. Physical student action  
Groups use small physical models and a prediction-and-test routine rather than repeated measurement.

Each group receives a toy car, string, spring scale, masses, cardboard, cups and a short set of scenario cards. Each card shows a situation such as:

- A toy car is stationary on a level bench.
- A car is pulled right with 2 N while friction pulls left with 2 N.
- A car is pulled right with 4 N while friction pulls left with 2 N.
- A moving car has no forward pull; friction acts backward.
- A moving car is pulled sideways using string.
- A car with a 500 g mass attached is given the same pull as an unloaded car.

For every card, students follow the same physical sequence:

1. **Predict:** Place the car on the bench and use arrow strips made from cardboard to represent forces. Arrows point in the direction of each force; longer arrows represent larger forces.
2. **Vote with positions:** Each student places a small marker or finger beside one of three written outcomes: “no change in motion,” “speeds up/slows down,” or “changes direction.”
3. **Test:** Students recreate the situation with the car, string and spring scale where practical. For example, two students can pull opposite strings through spring scales; another observes whether the car stays still, moves steadily, speeds up, slows or turns.
4. **Record:** They draw the force arrows and write the observed change in motion.
5. **Mass challenge:** Students attach 100 g then 500 g and compare how much effort is needed to give the car a noticeable change in motion.

The central mechanism is the repeated translation between a visible force model—arrows—and observed changes in motion.

### 3. Science payoff  
Students arrive at these specific ideas:

- Forces are pushes or pulls that can be represented by direction and relative size.
- Balanced forces do not produce a change in motion: an object can stay at rest or move at constant velocity.
- Unbalanced forces produce a change in motion: speeding up, slowing down or changing direction.
- A sideways unbalanced force changes direction, not necessarily speed.
- Greater mass means greater inertia: with the same unbalanced force, a more massive car changes motion less readily.

This mechanism makes the distinction between **force** and **motion** explicit: movement does not prove that forces are unbalanced; a change in movement does.

### 4. Evidence  
For each scenario, students complete a force-motion record:

| Scenario | Force arrows drawn | Balanced or unbalanced? | Predicted motion | Observed motion |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 N right, 2 N left | equal opposite arrows | balanced | no change | car remains still/continues evenly |
| 4 N right, 2 N left | larger right arrow | unbalanced right | speeds up right | speeds up right |
| Friction only backward | left arrow only | unbalanced left | slows if moving right | slows |
| Side pull | forward/side arrows | unbalanced sideways | turns | turns |

Students finish with a rule written in their own words:

> Balanced forces mean no change in motion. An unbalanced force causes a change in speed or direction.

### 5. Feasibility  
- **Setup:** The teacher prepares seven identical envelope sets of six scenario cards and cardboard force arrows. Equipment is distributed in trays.
- **Timing within the 35-minute block:**  
  - 5 minutes: model one scenario as a whole class.  
  - 24 minutes: groups complete four scenario cards, about six minutes each.  
  - 6 minutes: mass challenge and final written rule.
- **Management:** Each group stays at its own bench. The routine is identical for every card, reducing the need for teacher intervention. Students rotate roles: force-arrow arranger, puller, observer, recorder.
- **Cleanup:** Cards and arrows return to envelopes; the only apparatus is the car, strings, masses and scale.

### 6. Failure risk  
**Likely failure:** Students may treat force arrows as a worksheet symbol exercise and not connect them to actual motion. They may also assume equal opposite forces mean “nothing is happening” rather than “no change is happening.”

**Mitigation:** Every arrow diagram must be followed by a physical test, and the teacher should use a moving-car example for balanced forces: a car pulled steadily with a force matching friction can continue at approximately constant speed. During circulation, the teacher asks one fixed question: “Is the object moving, or is its motion changing?” This wording directs attention to Newton’s First Law rather than to motion alone.
