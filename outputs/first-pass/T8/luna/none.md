## 1. Inertia-card and cup demonstration

### Physical student action
Students work in groups of 3–4. Each group:

1. Places a plastic cup upright on the bench.
2. Tapes a small rectangle of cardboard over the cup opening.
3. Places a 100 g or 200 g mass on the cardboard.
4. Gives the cardboard a quick horizontal flick with one finger, so it slides away and the mass drops into the cup.
5. Repeats with different masses and with a slow pull instead of a quick flick.
6. Uses a ruler to measure how far the cardboard moves before the mass falls into the cup, and records whether the mass remained nearly in its original position.
7. Predicts what will happen before each trial, including a trial with two masses stacked together.
8. Groups compare results with another group using the same mass.

The teacher demonstrates the first trial and establishes that students must flick the cardboard sideways rather than lift or push the mass directly.

### Science payoff
Students arrive at the specific idea that an object resists a sudden change in its state of rest. The mass tends to remain at rest while the cardboard moves away. A larger mass shows greater inertia: it is less easily accelerated by the brief interaction with the moving cardboard.

The demonstration also separates the everyday idea of “force” from the scientific idea: the cardboard receives a horizontal force, but the mass does not receive enough horizontal unbalanced force to move significantly during the brief flick.

### Evidence
Students record a table such as:

| Mass | Flick distance of cardboard | Did mass remain over cup? | Mass displacement |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| 100 g | ___ cm | yes/no | ___ cm |
| 200 g | ___ cm | yes/no | ___ cm |
| 500 g | ___ cm | yes/no | ___ cm |

They sketch the mass before and after the flick and add arrows showing the cardboard’s motion and the mass’s small displacement. They also record the result of the slow-pull comparison.

### Feasibility
- Setup requires one cup, cardboard piece, tape and masses per group; these can be placed in trays before class.
- The activity needs no movement around the room, so fixed benches are suitable.
- A 5-minute teacher demonstration and safety instruction can be followed by three 8-minute trial cycles, with 5–6 minutes for recording and comparison.
- The teacher can circulate and check that students flick the cardboard horizontally and do not throw masses.
- Cleanup consists of returning masses and cups to trays and removing cardboard pieces.

### Failure risk
The most likely failure is that students push the mass directly, use a slow pull, or flick the cardboard upward. The mass then moves with the cardboard, producing a misleading result.

To mitigate this, the teacher demonstrates one correct and one incorrect trial, marks a direction arrow on each cardboard piece, and requires each group to complete one teacher-approved trial before collecting comparison data. Students should also understand that the mass is not “staying still forever”; it moves when a sufficient unbalanced force acts on it.

## 2. Toy-car force and stopping investigation

### Physical student action
Students investigate how a toy car changes motion when forces acting on it are altered.

Each group:

1. Places a toy car on the bench or floor-level lab surface and marks a starting line with tape.
2. Gives the car a gentle push and observes its motion.
3. Repeats with a stronger push.
4. Attaches a string to the car and pulls it with a spring scale, aiming for approximately 1 N, then 2 N where the scale allows.
5. Adds 100 g, 200 g and 500 g masses to the car using tape or a cardboard platform.
6. Pulls each loaded car with approximately the same spring-scale reading and measures the distance travelled in 3 seconds using a stopwatch and ruler marks.
7. Tests what happens when the car reaches a book or another fixed cardboard barrier, and observes that its motion changes because an additional force acts on it.
8. Records predictions before each change: more mass, greater pull, release of the pull, or collision with the barrier.

Groups remain at their benches. One student pulls, one operates the stopwatch, one records, and one observes or checks the spring-scale reading; roles rotate.

### Science payoff
Students develop the linked ideas that:

- An unbalanced force changes motion.
- A car continues moving for a time after the pulling force stops, although friction gradually slows it.
- With approximately the same applied force, a greater mass produces a smaller change in motion because greater mass means greater inertia.
- Balanced forces do not cause a change in motion; the car changes speed or direction only when the forces are unbalanced.

The investigation makes the qualification about real surfaces explicit: friction is an opposing force, so a moving car does not continue indefinitely at constant velocity in the laboratory.

### Evidence
Students record:

| Load | Approximate pulling force | Distance in 3 s | Motion change observed |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Car only | ___ N | ___ cm | ___ |
| +100 g | ___ N | ___ cm | ___ |
| +200 g | ___ N | ___ cm | ___ |
| +500 g | ___ N | ___ cm | ___ |

They also write observations for:
- push released: “car continued moving, then slowed”;
- barrier hit: “direction/speed changed”;
- increased load at similar pull: “smaller distance or slower acceleration.”

A simple arrow diagram shows pulling force forward and friction backward. Students identify whether the forces are balanced or unbalanced in each stage.

### Feasibility
- Six or seven groups can each use one car, with a small number of spare cars circulated if necessary.
- Rulers, stopwatches, masses and spring scales are all listed equipment.
- Tape tracks on benches prevent cars from travelling unpredictably.
- The teacher can restrict the investigation to one car per group and require cars to remain on the marked surface.
- The 35 minutes can be divided into 5 minutes setup and prediction, 20 minutes testing, and 10 minutes data comparison.
- Cleanup is rapid: remove masses, collect cars and spring scales, and leave tape tracks in place until the end.

### Failure risk
The major failure is that students treat unequal pulling forces as evidence about mass. If one student pulls the loaded car much harder, the comparison is invalid. Friction may also vary between benches.

To mitigate this, students must use spring-scale readings rather than “same-looking” pulls, repeat each trial twice, and compare results only when the force readings are similar. The teacher provides one common conclusion: the result is not that a heavy car cannot move, but that the same unbalanced force produces a smaller change in motion for a greater mass.

## 3. Human inertia model with force-role simulation

### Physical student action
Students use their bodies to model objects and forces rather than directly testing a moving object.

Each group forms a short line of three or four students:

- one student is the “object”;
- one or two students represent opposing forces;
- one student is the recorder and director.

The group performs a sequence on a clear strip beside the benches:

1. The “object” stands still while equal students gently pull in opposite directions using a loop of string or by placing hands on a cardboard handle. The object remains stationary.
2. The opposing pulls are made unequal. The object begins to move toward the stronger pull.
3. The object moves at a walking pace while opposing pulls are equalised. The group observes that the object does not need a forward “motion force” to keep moving, although bench space limits the demonstration.
4. One force is suddenly removed. The object changes its motion according to the remaining force.
5. The group repeats the unequal-force trial with the “object” carrying a 100 g, 200 g or 500 g mass in a secured cardboard container, while the pullers aim for the same spring-scale reading or the same gentle force.
6. Students predict the direction and size of the motion change before each configuration, then sketch the force arrows and outcome.
7. Groups exchange one scenario card with another group—for example, “equal pulls,” “right-hand pull stronger,” or “moving object with no continuing pull”—and physically model the card.

Students never pull one another sharply; the teacher sets a walking-force limit and permits a stationary hand-held version if the aisle is too narrow.

### Science payoff
The physical model gives students a distinction between:

- **balanced forces:** no change in motion;
- **unbalanced forces:** a change in speed or direction;
- **inertia:** the object resists changes in motion;
- **mass:** increasing the object’s mass increases that resistance.

It also confronts the common misconception that a continuing forward force is required to keep an object moving. The teacher explicitly explains that the classroom model includes friction and safety limits, so the moving person eventually slows unless the forces are balanced closely.

### Evidence
Each group completes a scenario record:

| Scenario | Force arrows | Balanced/unbalanced? | Predicted motion | Observed motion |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Equal opposite pulls, object still | ___ | ___ | ___ | ___ |
| Stronger pull to right | ___ | ___ | ___ | ___ |
| Moving, pulls equalised | ___ | ___ | ___ | ___ |
| One pull removed | ___ | ___ | ___ | ___ |
| Same pull, greater mass | ___ | ___ | ___ | ___ |

Students also record the number of steps or approximate distance moved during a fixed 3-second interval for the light and loaded object. Their final sketch must show that “balanced” means no change in motion, not necessarily “not moving.”

### Feasibility
- No special equipment is required: string loops, cardboard handles, masses, tape and spring scales are sufficient.
- The teacher can establish two or three movement lanes beside benches and keep groups in assigned spaces.
- Groups of four have clear roles, reducing crowding and making the teacher’s supervision manageable.
- The 35 minutes can be organised as 5 minutes teacher modelling, 15 minutes structured scenarios, 10 minutes mass comparison, and 5 minutes recording and peer checking.
- A safety briefing prohibits running, sudden yanking and pulling toward sinks or stools.
- Cleanup involves returning string and masses and removing the lane tape.

### Failure risk
Students may interpret the role-play literally and conclude that an object always needs a person pulling it to move, or that equal forces mean the object must be stationary. The body model can also be distorted by students pulling unevenly.

To mitigate this, the teacher uses explicit scenario cards that include a moving object with balanced forces, asks students to freeze and identify whether motion is changing rather than merely whether the object is moving, and follows the physical model with a short force-arrow record. The teacher also labels the human pulling as a model of force, not a complete replacement for the behaviour of a real object.
