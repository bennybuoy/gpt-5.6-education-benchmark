## 1. Spring-scale car investigation

### Physical student action
Groups of 3–4 work at their bench with a toy car, ruler, stopwatch, spring scale, and masses.

1. Place the empty car on the bench and use the spring scale to pull it with approximately 1 N of force.
2. Time how long it takes to travel a marked distance, such as 50 cm.
3. Repeat twice and record the times.
4. Add a 100 g, 200 g, or 500 g mass to the car and repeat the test using approximately the same pulling force.
5. Perform a second comparison: pull the same car with a smaller and then larger force, recording the distance travelled in a fixed time, such as five seconds.
6. Groups make a simple table and identify when the car’s motion changed.

Suggested roles are puller, timer, recorder, and equipment manager. Students should pull steadily rather than jerk the spring scale.

### Science payoff
Students arrive at the following ideas:

- A force is a push or pull that can change motion.
- A larger unbalanced force produces a greater change in motion.
- For approximately the same force, the car with greater mass changes its motion less readily.
- Mass is a measure of inertia.
- A car that is moving does not need a continuing force merely to keep moving; the pulling force is needed to change its motion, while friction acts in the opposite direction.

The teacher should explicitly discuss friction as an unbalanced force that eventually slows the car.

### Evidence
Students record:

- Time for the car to travel 50 cm with different masses.
- Distance travelled in five seconds with different pulling forces.
- Their prediction and result for which mass changes motion more easily.
- A labelled sketch showing the pulling force, friction, and direction of motion.

Typical evidence is that the heavier car takes longer to reach the same speed or travels less distance in the fixed time when pulled with the same approximate force.

### Feasibility
The teacher can place a ruler, car, spring scale, and masses at each of seven or eight group stations before the lesson. Marking a 50 cm distance with tape requires minimal setup. The activity can run as:

- 5 min: equipment check and prediction
- 10 min: equal-force, different-mass test
- 10 min: different-force, same-mass test
- 5 min: recording and graph or comparison
- 5 min: return equipment and prepare for synthesis

It uses only listed materials and stays at the benches. One teacher can manage it by first demonstrating how to pull steadily and then circulating to check that students are not dragging the scale at an angle.

### Failure risk
The greatest risk is that friction and inconsistent pulling make the results too variable. Students may also interpret “a force is needed to keep the car moving” because the spring scale remains attached.

Mitigation:

- Use short, straight bench tracks.
- Require several trials and comparison of patterns rather than one result.
- Have students aim for a stated spring-scale reading.
- Include a teacher demonstration in which a car is pushed once and allowed to roll, followed by discussion of friction as the force slowing it.
- Use the phrase “force is needed to change motion,” not “force is needed for motion.”

## 2. Inertia interruption with supported objects

### Physical student action
Groups investigate what happens when the support beneath an object is removed or moved suddenly.

1. Place a piece of cardboard across the top of a plastic cup.
2. Place a 100 g or 200 g mass near the centre of the cardboard, directly above the cup.
3. Predict where the mass will go if the cardboard is pulled quickly sideways.
4. One student holds the cup steady. Another pulls the cardboard sharply sideways while a third student observes and the fourth records.
5. Repeat with a slower pull and a faster pull.
6. Repeat with different masses, such as 100 g and 500 g.
7. Students then place a marble on cardboard and give the cardboard a sudden sideways tap, recording whether the marble initially remains nearly in place or moves with the cardboard.

The cup should be placed well back from the bench edge. Students should pull the cardboard horizontally, not lift it.

### Science payoff
This mechanism makes the “stays at rest” part of Newton’s First Law physically visible:

- An object at rest remains at rest unless an unbalanced force acts on it.
- The cardboard moves because a sideways force acts on it, but the mass initially resists the change in its motion.
- The mass does not automatically follow the support beneath it.
- A greater mass has greater inertia and is more resistant to a change in motion.

The teacher should distinguish the force on the cardboard from the much smaller sideways force transferred to the mass through friction.

### Evidence
Students collect:

- A prediction drawing showing the expected path of the mass.
- An observation drawing showing the cardboard’s direction and the mass’s initial position.
- A table comparing slow pull, fast pull, light mass, and heavy mass.
- A written claim such as: “When the cardboard was moved quickly, the mass stayed approximately above the cup before falling because its motion did not change immediately.”
- A comparison of how much the light and heavy masses moved sideways.

The result is not expected to be perfectly clean. Students record whether the object moved “a little,” “moderately,” or “a lot,” together with a sketch.

### Feasibility
The equipment is inexpensive and easy to distribute: one cup, cardboard square, marble, and several masses per group. The teacher can prepare one demonstration setup and place the remaining materials in trays.

A workable sequence is:

- 5 min: predict and set up
- 10 min: repeated fast and slow pulls
- 10 min: compare different masses
- 5 min: create evidence sketches and written claim
- 5 min: reset benches and return materials

The activity takes place entirely on benches and requires no running, throwing, or specialised equipment. The teacher only needs to monitor that students keep the cup stable and do not use excessive force.

### Failure risk
The main failure mode is that students pull the cardboard too slowly. The mass then moves along with the cardboard through friction, so they conclude that the object “moves because the cardboard moves.” Another risk is that the mass falls beside the cup and students focus only on the trick.

Mitigation:

- Demonstrate one slow pull and one quick pull.
- Require students to predict before each trial.
- Have students repeat the trial rather than relying on one dramatic result.
- Ask specifically: “What force acted sideways on the mass? Was it large enough to change its motion quickly?”
- Use the heavy-mass comparison to connect the observation to inertia rather than presenting it as a trick.

## 3. Balanced-force tug test

### Physical student action
Groups investigate balanced and unbalanced forces acting on a stationary object using two spring scales and string.

1. Tie strings to opposite sides of a toy car or a small cardboard platform loaded with masses.
2. Attach a spring scale to each string.
3. First, two students pull gently in opposite directions with equal readings, such as 1 N and 1 N. The other students observe whether the object changes position.
4. Students predict what will happen if one student pulls with 2 N while the other pulls with 1 N.
5. Repeat the test with the unequal forces and record the direction of motion.
6. Return to equal readings, then increase both forces together, for example from 1 N and 1 N to 2 N and 2 N. Students observe that the net force is still zero even though each individual force is larger.
7. Add mass to the object and repeat an unequal-force trial, recording whether its motion changes more or less readily.
8. Groups draw force arrows for each trial.

Students should pull horizontally along the bench, with the car kept away from edges. A student can act as the recorder while the two “force operators” read their scales aloud.

### Science payoff
This mechanism focuses on the difference between individual forces and the net force:

- Equal and opposite forces are balanced and produce no change in motion.
- If the object is at rest and the forces remain balanced, it stays at rest.
- Unequal opposite forces have a non-zero net force and change the object’s motion in the direction of the larger force.
- Increasing both opposite forces equally does not cause acceleration because the net force remains zero.
- Adding mass increases inertia, so the same unbalanced force causes a smaller change in motion.

This provides a concrete bridge from force as a push or pull to force as a vector with a direction and net result.

### Evidence
Students record a table like:

| Pull left | Pull right | Net-force description | Observed motion |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 N | 1 N | balanced, zero net force | no change |
| 2 N | 1 N | unbalanced, 1 N to left | moves left |
| 2 N | 2 N | balanced, zero net force | no change |
| 1 N | 1 N with greater mass | balanced, zero net force | no change |

They also draw arrows whose lengths represent the two forces and write a conclusion:

- “Balanced forces can act on an object without changing its motion.”
- “An unbalanced force changes motion in its own direction.”

For the mass comparison, students record the time or distance needed for the object to begin moving visibly under the same approximate force.

### Feasibility
The teacher can prepare seven or eight station trays containing two spring scales, two short strings, a toy car or cardboard platform, tape, and masses. Tying the strings before the lesson reduces setup time. The teacher demonstrates the equal-force trial and models safe pulling.

A 35-minute schedule is:

- 5 min: set up and predict
- 10 min: equal versus unequal pulls
- 8 min: equal larger pulls
- 7 min: repeat with added mass
- 5 min: force-arrow diagrams and cleanup

The activity does not require students to leave their benches. It is manageable with one teacher because the teacher can inspect scale readings and remind groups to keep the object stationary during the equal-force trials.

### Failure risk
Students may treat “balanced” as meaning that no forces are present. They may also pull intermittently, causing the scales to fluctuate and making the object move for reasons unrelated to the intended comparison.

Mitigation:

- Require students to draw both force arrows, even when the object does not move.
- Ask, “Are there forces? If so, why is there no change in motion?”
- Use short trials with a target reading and a recorder calling out both values.
- Explain that small movements caused by friction or uneven pulls are experimental imperfections, not evidence that equal forces cause acceleration.
- Keep the final mass comparison qualitative if the force readings cannot be held steadily.
