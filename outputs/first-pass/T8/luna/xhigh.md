## Common lesson frame

These are three alternative 35-minute blocks, not three activities to combine.

- **0–5 min introduction:** Define a force as a push or pull interaction. Define balanced forces as forces with zero resultant and unbalanced forces as a non-zero resultant. Explain that inertia is not a force; it is an object's resistance to a change in velocity.
- **5–40 min:** Use one of the mechanisms below.
- **40–50 min synthesis:** Groups report one measured result or sketch. The teacher connects the evidence to the full statement: zero resultant force means no change in velocity; an unbalanced force changes speed or direction; greater mass means greater inertia. Friction is identified as the reason real objects on benches eventually slow down.

Seven groups of four can use roles such as operator, measurer, recorder and safety/equipment manager.

# 1. Card-strip support removal

### Mechanism name

**Card-strip support removal**

### Physical student action

Each group receives a stiff cardboard strip, a plastic cup, a 100 g mass, a ruler, tape, a stopwatch and a marble.

1. Tape a start line and a 20 cm pull line on the bench.
2. Place the cup, containing the 100 g mass, on the cardboard strip. Mark the position of the cup’s centre.
3. For two trials, pull the cardboard slowly from the start line to the 20 cm line. For two further trials, pull it sharply over the same distance. Students must not touch the cup.
4. Measure the cup’s displacement from its original position after each pull.
5. For the moving-object test, pull the cardboard slowly so that the cup moves with it. Stop the cardboard abruptly at the 20 cm line without touching the cup. Measure how far the cup continues beyond the stopping line.
6. As a comparison, one student gives a marble a single push along a marked section of bench. Another student records its position at one-second intervals until it stops.

Students rotate the card operator, observer, measurer and recorder roles.

### Science payoff

Students should arrive at the idea that:

- A force applied mainly to the cardboard does not automatically produce the same change in motion in the cup.
- The cup initially at rest remains nearly at rest when the cardboard is pulled away quickly because only a small, brief frictional force acts on it.
- When the cardboard is stopped, the moving cup tends to continue in its original direction.
- A moving object does not need a continuing push to keep moving. In this room, friction is the unbalanced force that eventually slows the marble or cup.
- The cup’s downward gravitational force and the bench’s upward normal force can be balanced while horizontal motion is considered separately.

The card activity is strongest for showing persistence of rest and motion. It should not be treated as a precise quantitative test of the mass–inertia relationship because changing the load also changes friction.

### Evidence

Students record:

| Card action | Cup load | Cup position before | Cup position after | Displacement |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Slow pull | 100 g | 0 cm | ___ cm | ___ cm |
| Sharp pull | 100 g | 0 cm | ___ cm | ___ cm |
| Abrupt stop | 100 g | stopping line | ___ cm beyond line | ___ cm |

They also record the marble’s position at 0, 1, 2 and 3 seconds and draw arrows showing the cup or marble’s direction of motion before and after the cardboard stops.

### Feasibility

- All actions occur on the fixed benches; no furniture or students move around the room.
- The teacher can pre-cut cardboard strips and place one kit at each bench before the lesson. Distribution takes about two minutes.
- The 35-minute block can be divided into approximately 4 minutes of prediction and setup, 12 minutes of slow/fast pulls, 10 minutes of abrupt-stop trials, 6 minutes of marble observations and 3 minutes of recording and returning equipment.
- The materials are inexpensive and already listed. No technician or teacher-operated timing is required.
- The final three minutes include placing masses, cups, marbles and cardboard back into trays.

### Failure risk

The most likely failure is that students pull the cardboard too slowly, hold the cup accidentally, or conclude that “inertia pushed the cup backwards.”

Mitigation:

- Demonstrate one trial in which the teacher identifies exactly which object the hand is touching.
- Mark the pull distance and require both a slow and a sharp trial.
- Require students to draw a friction arrow on the cup rather than an “inertia force.”
- Keep the loads low enough that the cup cannot tip or slide off the bench.
- If the cup displacement is not clear, use the moving-cup stopping trial and the marble position data as the main evidence.

# 2. Spring-scale net-force balance

### Mechanism name

**Spring-scale net-force balance**

### Physical student action

Each group uses a toy car, two pieces of string, two spring scales, tape, rulers, a stopwatch and several masses.

1. Tape a straight 40 cm lane on the bench and attach one string to the front of the car and one to the rear. The strings must remain horizontal and clear of the wheels.
2. For the balanced trial, one student pulls forward with a spring scale reading of approximately 1 N while another pulls backwards with a reading of approximately 1 N. The car begins at rest and both students maintain the readings for five seconds.
3. Record the car’s position at the beginning and end.
4. For the unbalanced trial, use a forward reading of 2 N and a backward reading of 1 N. Start the car at rest and measure the time taken to travel 30 cm.
5. Divide the track into three 10 cm sections and record the time for each section. An object starting from rest under an unbalanced forward force should take less time for later sections.
6. Tape a 100 g or 200 g mass low on the car. Repeat the 2 N forward and 1 N backward trial. If the car remains stable and rolls freely, a 500 g load can be used for a larger comparison.

The recorder calculates:

\[
F_{\text{net}} \approx F_{\text{forward}}-F_{\text{backward}}
\]

Students swap the scale operator, car operator, timer and recorder roles.

### Science payoff

Students should arrive at the idea that:

- Equal and opposite forces can both be present while the car remains at rest.
- In the balanced trial, the resultant force is approximately zero, so the car’s velocity does not change.
- When the forward force is greater than the backward force, the resultant force is unbalanced and the car’s speed changes.
- When approximately the same net force acts on a more massive car, its motion changes less rapidly. Greater mass means greater inertia.
- “Balanced forces” means no change in motion, not necessarily “the object is not moving.” A moving object with zero resultant force would continue at constant velocity, although the classroom car also experiences friction.

### Evidence

Students collect measured force and motion data:

| Car mass/load | Forward force | Backward force | Approx. net force | Time for 0–10 cm | Time for 10–20 cm | Time for 20–30 cm |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Empty | 2 N | 1 N | 1 N | ___ s | ___ s | ___ s |
| Loaded | 2 N | 1 N | 1 N | ___ s | ___ s | ___ s |

For the balanced trial, they record both scale readings and the car’s displacement over five seconds. They also draw a force-arrow diagram for the balanced and unbalanced cases.

### Feasibility

- The teacher can pre-tie the strings, mark the lanes and place the cars and scales in trays before the lesson.
- The main block can use approximately 5 minutes for setup and predictions, 7 minutes for the balanced trial, 8 minutes for the unbalanced trial, 11 minutes for mass comparisons and 4 minutes for recording and cleanup.
- All forces are applied horizontally on the bench. Cardboard stops can be placed before the bench edge.
- If there are fewer than two spring scales per group, two groups can share one lane. One group operates while the other predicts and records, then they exchange roles. This keeps the teacher from having to supervise every pull.
- The procedure uses only string, tape, toy cars, spring scales, masses, rulers and stopwatches.

### Failure risk

The most likely failure is that students record a scale reading as the net force instead of subtracting the opposing reading. A second problem is that the two students pull at different angles or allow the spring readings to change.

Mitigation:

- Provide a data table with separate forward, backward and net-force columns.
- Model the arrow subtraction before the trial.
- Require horizontal strings and an approximate reading tolerance, such as both readings within 0.2 N of the target.
- Use three trials and compare the overall pattern rather than one time.
- If a heavily loaded car does not roll at 1 N because of friction, reduce the load or increase both scale readings while keeping their difference the same.

# 3. Collision and loose-passenger prediction

### Mechanism name

**Collision and loose-passenger test**

### Physical student action

Each lane uses two toy cars, cardboard, books, a ruler, tape, a plastic cup, a marble and masses.

1. Before class, the teacher makes a low cardboard ramp supported by books. Rulers taped along the sides act as guides. A release line is marked at the top, and a 0–30 cm scale is marked on the bench at the bottom.
2. Students place one toy car—the target car—at rest at the collision line. The second car is the striker.
3. Students first draw predictions showing the velocity arrow of each car before and after collision.
4. One student holds the striker against the release line using a ruler as a gate. The ruler is lifted vertically so the car is released rather than pushed.
5. The striker is released into the empty target car. Students record whether the target changes from rest to motion and measure the target car’s displacement after the collision. They repeat the trial twice.
6. A 100 g or 200 g mass is taped securely and low on the target car. The same striker, ramp height, release line and target position are used again. Students record the target car’s displacement and whether it moves. A larger load is used only if the car remains stable.
7. For the loose-passenger trial, a plastic cup is taped to the striker and a marble is placed loosely in it. The car is released into a fixed cardboard barrier. Students observe the car stopping while the marble continues forwards. A second cup placed beyond the barrier catches the marble.
8. For one direction-change trial, a broad cardboard deflector is taped diagonally across the track. Students draw the car’s direction before and after contact.

Groups sharing a lane alternate between releasing, resetting, timing, measuring and recording. Every trial begins with a prediction.

### Science payoff

Students should arrive at the idea that:

- The target car remains at rest until the collision provides a horizontal unbalanced force.
- The collision changes the velocity of both cars; the striker may slow, stop or change direction, while the target begins moving.
- The loose marble is not directly stopped by the barrier. When the car and cup stop, the marble tends to keep moving in its original direction until the cup or friction exerts a force on it.
- A greater mass is harder to change under a comparable collision disturbance, so a loaded target car generally shows a smaller change in motion than an empty target car.
- A force can change direction as well as speed.
- The car slows on the bench after the collision because friction is an unbalanced force; motion does not stop “by itself.”

### Evidence

Students record a collision table:

| Target load | Target state before impact | Target state after impact | Target displacement |
|---|---|---|---:|
| Empty | At rest | ___ | ___ cm |
| 100/200 g added | At rest | ___ | ___ cm |

They also record:

- The striker’s state before and after contact.
- The marble’s distance beyond the barrier.
- Arrows showing the car and marble’s directions immediately before and after impact.
- A sketch of the target car before contact, during contact and after contact.

These records show a change from zero velocity to non-zero velocity, a change from motion to rest or a new direction, and continued motion of an object not directly acted on by the stopping force.

### Feasibility

- The teacher pre-tapes four low ramps and cardboard barriers before class. Two groups can share each lane, giving seven groups access without requiring fourteen complete ramps.
- Students only need to place the cars, add or remove taped masses and reset the marble.
- The block can use approximately 4 minutes for predictions and setup, 10 minutes for empty-car collisions, 9 minutes for loaded-car collisions, 8 minutes for the passenger and direction trials, and 4 minutes for recording and cleanup.
- All releases are low-energy and remain on the benches. There is no running, throwing or movement between stations.
- A ruler gate makes the release repeatable without video analysis or sensors.

### Failure risk

The most likely failure is that inconsistent release height, poor alignment or a hand push makes the collision data too variable to support the mass comparison.

Mitigation:

- Use the same release line and a ruler gate for every trial.
- Tape down the ramp, guide rails and target-car position.
- Run at least three trials and use the consistent state-change evidence—rest to motion, motion to rest and continued marble motion—even if displacement measurements vary.
- Use only a small added mass if a loaded target car barely moves.
- Ask students which object actually contacted the barrier. This prevents the explanation that the marble “contains a forward force”; instead, it retains its velocity until a force acts on it.
