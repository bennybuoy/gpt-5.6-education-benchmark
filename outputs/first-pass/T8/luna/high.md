## Common lesson frame

Each option keeps the fixed structure:

- **5 minutes:** Introduce force as a push or pull. Define balanced and unbalanced forces using simple examples, without assuming prior knowledge of Newton’s laws.
- **35 minutes:** One of the central mechanisms below.
- **10 minutes:** Groups report one evidence row. The teacher builds a class table linking **force condition → motion change**, then states Newton’s First Law and the mass–inertia relationship explicitly.

Use **seven groups of four**. Materials can be placed in seven labelled bench kits before the lesson. Each main block includes a short return-and-clean-up period.

# 1. Controlled pull-and-acceleration investigation

### Mechanism name
**Controlled pull-and-acceleration investigation**

### Physical student action

Each group uses a toy car, tape, ruler, stopwatch, masses and a spring scale.

1. Students tape a 50 cm lane along their bench, with marks at 0 cm, 25 cm and 50 cm.
2. They test the car first without an added mass, then with a 500 g mass taped securely to it.
3. One student pulls the car from rest using the spring scale, attempting to keep the reading near the same value, such as 1 N.
4. A second student starts and stops the stopwatch at the 25 cm and 50 cm marks. A third records the times; a fourth watches the scale reading and checks that the pull remains approximately horizontal.
5. They repeat each mass condition twice.
6. Groups then perform two short force-balance tests:
   - Two students pull opposite ends of the stationary car with approximately equal spring-scale readings.
   - One student reduces one pull while the other maintains the same pull.
7. Finally, students give the car a short push and observe it slowing. They then pull it gently so that it travels equal distances in equal time intervals, approximately balancing the frictional force.

Suggested timing:

- 4 minutes: set up and predict
- 16 minutes: loaded and unloaded car trials
- 9 minutes: balanced, unbalanced and steady-motion tests
- 4 minutes: complete the evidence table
- 2 minutes: return equipment

### Science payoff

Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- Under approximately the same unbalanced applied force, the car with greater mass takes longer to change its motion. **Greater mass means greater inertia.**
- Equal opposite forces can leave the car at rest because the forces are balanced.
- A car does not need a forward force merely because it is moving. It slows after the push because friction is an unbalanced force.
- If the forward pull approximately balances friction, the car’s speed is approximately constant. Balanced forces mean **no change in velocity**, not necessarily no motion.

### Evidence

Students record:

| Car mass | Approximate scale reading | Time 0–25 cm | Time 25–50 cm | Motion conclusion |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| No added mass | | | | |
| 500 g added | | | | |

They also record observations such as:

- Equal opposite readings: car remained stationary or moved very little.
- Unequal readings: car began moving or changed speed.
- After the initial push: successive 25 cm intervals took longer, indicating slowing.
- During the adjusted steady pull: successive intervals took approximately equal times.

The key comparison is that the loaded car shows a smaller change in motion for a similar applied force.

### Feasibility

- The activity fits entirely on fixed laboratory benches; cars are not released onto the floor.
- The teacher can prepare seven taped lanes and place one car, masses, ruler and stopwatch at each bench before class.
- The listed spring scales are the only measuring instruments required. Groups can share a lane if necessary, with one group pulling while the other times and checks the scale reading.
- The roles keep all students occupied and reduce crowding.
- Loads are taped to cars so they cannot fall onto the floor or into sinks.
- The final two minutes are sufficient to return cars, scales and masses to labelled locations.

### Failure risk and mitigation

**Likely failure:** Students pull harder on the loaded car and conclude that the heavier car “needs more force to move,” rather than comparing its response to the same force.

**Mitigation:** Require a target spring-scale reading and reject trials in which the reading is substantially different. Students repeat trials and compare time intervals rather than simply stating which car moved. The teacher also asks, “Was the applied force the same, and did the motion change by the same amount?”

A second risk is that students interpret the slowing car as evidence that motion always requires a force. The class table must label friction as the unbalanced force acting during the slowing trial.

# 2. Sudden support-removal test

### Mechanism name
**Sudden support-removal inertia test**

### Physical student action

Each group uses a plastic cup, a flat cardboard strip, tape, a ruler, masses and a stopwatch.

1. Students place the cardboard strip on the bench and put the empty cup near its centre.
2. They mark the cup’s starting position on the bench with tape.
3. A cardboard pull-tab is taped to one end of the strip.
4. One student pulls the cardboard horizontally through 20 cm:
   - once slowly, taking approximately four seconds;
   - once quickly, taking less than one second.
5. Students measure how far the cup moved from its starting mark after the cardboard had been removed.
6. They repeat the quick and slow pulls with a 100 g mass and then a 500 g mass inside the cup.
7. Students predict the result before each trial and record whether the cup stayed near its original position or was carried along by the cardboard.
8. They sketch the forces on the cup before, during and after the pull.

Suggested timing:

- 4 minutes: set up and make predictions
- 18 minutes: quick and slow pulls with three mass conditions
- 7 minutes: measure displacement and complete the table
- 3 minutes: draw the force-and-motion sequence
- 3 minutes: clean up

### Science payoff

Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- The cup initially at rest does not automatically acquire the motion of the cardboard.
- During a rapid pull, the horizontal frictional force on the cup acts for a short time and produces little change in the cup’s motion. The cup therefore remains approximately at rest.
- During a slow pull, friction has more opportunity to accelerate the cup, so the cup moves with the cardboard.
- Adding mass makes the cup harder to accelerate: greater mass means greater inertia.
- The cardboard receives a large unbalanced force from the student, while the cup receives only a relatively small horizontal frictional force.

The vertical forces on the cup—its weight and the bench’s support force—are balanced throughout, while the important horizontal force is friction.

### Evidence

Students record:

| Cup load | Pull speed | Cardboard travel | Cup displacement | Did the cup remain near its starting point? |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| Empty | Slow | 20 cm | | |
| Empty | Fast | 20 cm | | |
| 100 g | Slow | 20 cm | | |
| 100 g | Fast | 20 cm | | |
| 500 g | Slow | 20 cm | | |
| 500 g | Fast | 20 cm | | |

They also produce a three-stage sketch:

1. cup and cardboard initially at rest;
2. cardboard moving rapidly while the cup remains nearly stationary;
3. cardboard removed, cup still near its original location.

The recordable evidence is the difference in cup displacement between a rapid and a slow pull.

### Feasibility

- The apparatus is small and stays on the bench.
- The teacher can prepare the cardboard strips and pull-tabs before the lesson.
- Each group needs only one cup, one strip and a small selection of masses.
- A quick teacher demonstration at the start can establish the correct horizontal pulling direction; groups then work independently.
- Students do not need to hold the cup, reducing the chance of accidentally moving it.
- Cardboard, cups and masses can be returned to labelled kits in three minutes.

### Failure risk and mitigation

**Likely failure:** Students pull the cardboard upward or at an angle, tip the cup, or pull so slowly in both trials that friction carries the cup along.

**Mitigation:** Tape a pull-tab to the cardboard, mark a horizontal path, and require the quick pull to be completed over 20 cm in less than one second. The teacher demonstrates one correct trial before groups begin. Students repeat a trial if the cup tips or the cardboard catches.

A further risk is that students claim the cup is unaffected by force. The teacher should ask them to identify friction and explain that the cup is not force-free; it experiences a small horizontal force that produces only a small change in motion during the rapid pull.

# 3. Collision prediction and before–during–after investigation

### Mechanism name
**Marble–target collision investigation**

### Physical student action

Each group uses a marble, cardboard, tape, a book, a plastic cup, masses and a ruler.

1. Students fold or tape two pieces of cardboard into a narrow channel on the bench. One end is raised slightly with a book to form a short ramp; the rest of the channel is level.
2. They mark a common marble release point and place an empty plastic cup at a marked target position.
3. Before releasing the marble, students predict:
   - what will happen to the stationary cup before contact;
   - what will happen to the marble and cup during contact;
   - how far the cup will move afterwards.
4. One student releases the marble without pushing it. Another observes the collision, while a third marks the cup’s furthest position and a fourth records the result.
5. They repeat the collision with 100 g and 500 g masses inside the cup.
6. They perform a no-target control run, observing the marble’s motion along the channel before it reaches the empty target area.
7. For each trial, students make a three-part sketch labelled **before contact**, **during contact** and **after contact**.

Suggested timing:

- 5 minutes: assemble tracks and make predictions
- 5 minutes: no-target control and one practice collision
- 15 minutes: empty, 100 g and 500 g target trials
- 7 minutes: measure displacement and complete sketches
- 3 minutes: return marbles, cups and masses

### Science payoff

Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- The stationary cup remains stationary until the moving marble contacts it. Before contact, there is no significant horizontal unbalanced force on the cup.
- During the collision, the contact force from the marble is unbalanced, so the cup changes from rest to motion.
- The marble also changes speed or direction because the cup exerts a force on it during contact.
- A heavier target cup changes its motion less in response to a comparable collision. This provides a physical example of greater mass producing greater inertia.
- The marble’s motion after the collision is affected by friction. The frictional slowing is itself an unbalanced force; without such a force, an object in motion would continue at constant velocity.

### Evidence

For each trial, students record:

| Target load | Cup position before contact | Cup furthest position after contact | Marble after contact | Force event identified |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| Empty | | | | |
| 100 g | | | | |
| 500 g | | | | |

They also sketch:

- the cup at rest before the collision;
- the marble applying a contact force during the collision;
- the cup moving and the marble changing motion afterwards.

The central evidence is that the cup’s position does not change before contact, changes during or immediately after contact, and changes by a smaller amount when more mass is added.

### Feasibility

- The cardboard channels keep marbles on the bench and prevent them rolling towards sinks or across the room.
- The teacher can tape seven identical channels and mark release and target positions before the lesson.
- Each group uses one marble, so there is little equipment movement or searching.
- The ramp is made from cardboard and a book; no specialised apparatus is required.
- A four-person role system—releaser, collision observer, measurer and recorder—allows groups to work without teacher intervention at every trial.
- The teacher can circulate to check that students release rather than push the marble and that masses are not placed where they can fall.
- All marbles and masses are returned to a labelled tray during the final three minutes.

### Failure risk and mitigation

**Likely failure:** The marble misses the cup, or groups use different release speeds and treat the target’s travel distance as a precise measure of force.

**Mitigation:** Use a narrow taped channel, a wide cup target and a fixed release mark. Require two trials for each target mass. Students use the distance only as qualitative evidence of a smaller motion change in the heavier target; they are not asked to calculate force or momentum.

Another risk is that students say the cup was “already moving because the marble was moving.” The before–during–after sketch and the pre-release prediction specifically separate the marble’s motion from the cup’s state of rest.
