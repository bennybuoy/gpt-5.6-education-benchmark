## 1. Controlled tow trials

### Physical student action
Groups of 3–4 make a short straight track on a bench using tape and a ruler. They attach a string to a toy car and use a spring scale to pull it.

Students complete four trials:

1. Pull an unloaded car with approximately the same spring-scale reading each time.
2. Add a 100 g mass and repeat.
3. Add a 500 g mass and repeat.
4. Hold the car stationary while another student pulls gently in the opposite direction. When the pulls are equal, the car remains stationary; when one pull is greater, it moves.

One student pulls, one reads the scale, one times the car over a marked distance, and one records results. Groups rotate roles. If there are fewer spring scales than groups, groups can share them at two or three clearly marked bench stations while other students prepare loads and predict results.

### Science payoff
Students should arrive at the idea that:

- A car changes its motion only when the forces on it are unbalanced.
- Equal opposing forces produce no change in motion.
- With approximately the same applied force, the car with greater mass takes longer to speed up or covers less distance in the same time because greater mass means greater inertia.
- Once pulling stops, friction is an unbalanced force that causes the car to slow down; the car does not stop because “motion disappears” by itself.

### Evidence
Students record a table containing:

| Load | Approximate pulling force | Time over marked distance | Observation after string is released |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| None |  |  |  |
| 100 g |  |  |  |
| 500 g |  |  |  |

They also sketch force arrows for:

- a stationary car with equal pulls;
- a car with one larger pull;
- a car slowing after the pull stops.

The expected pattern is that the heavier car takes longer to travel the same distance under a similar pull.

### Feasibility
Before the lesson, the teacher can place tape tracks, cut string, and put masses and cars into group trays. This takes approximately 5–8 minutes before students enter. During the 35-minute block:

- 5 minutes: distribute equipment and demonstrate reading the spring scale;
- 20 minutes: conduct and repeat the trials;
- 10 minutes: complete the table and force-arrow sketches.

The activity remains on fixed benches and uses only cars, string, tape, rulers, stopwatches, masses, and spring scales. Groups have defined roles, so the teacher can check spring-scale use and move between benches without operating the experiment for students. Equipment is returned to trays during the final two minutes.

### Failure risk
The main failure is that students pull harder on the heavier car, so they conclude that heavier objects move more slowly because they were given less force.

**Mitigation:** Require a target spring-scale reading, such as approximately 1 N, and reject trials that differ substantially. Students should repeat each trial and compare the average times. The teacher should also explicitly identify friction as an additional force rather than treating the car as a perfect frictionless system.

## 2. Sudden-support removal

### Physical student action
Each group places an upside-down plastic cup on the bench. They balance a piece of cardboard over the cup and place a 100 g mass on the cardboard directly above the cup.

Students first predict what will happen if the cardboard is pulled sharply sideways. They then:

1. Pull the cardboard slowly and observe that the mass tends to move with it.
2. Replace the cardboard and flick or pull it rapidly sideways.
3. Observe that the mass remains almost in its original horizontal position and drops into the cup.
4. Repeat with a 200 g and then a 500 g mass.
5. Repeat several trials using different pull speeds, recording whether the mass lands inside the cup and how far it shifts sideways.

The student pulling the cardboard sits or stands directly at the side of the bench, with all other hands clear of the mass.

### Science payoff
Students should arrive at the idea that:

- An object at rest remains at rest unless an unbalanced force acts on it.
- The sideways force is mainly applied to the cardboard, not directly to the mass.
- The mass resists the change in its motion, so it does not immediately move sideways with the cardboard.
- A larger mass has greater inertia and is more resistant to a change in motion.

The demonstration also separates the object receiving the force from the nearby object that appears to “come along” with it.

### Evidence
Students record:

- whether the mass lands in the cup;
- the mass used;
- whether the cardboard was pulled slowly or rapidly;
- the estimated sideways displacement of the mass from its starting position;
- a before-and-after sketch showing the cardboard moving sideways while the mass initially remains above the cup.

A sample record might state: “500 g mass, rapid pull, mass moved only about 1 cm sideways before falling; slow pull caused the mass to move with the cardboard.”

### Feasibility
The equipment can be placed in seven group trays: one cup, one piece of cardboard, and a set of masses per group. No floor space or movement around the laboratory is required.

A workable timing sequence is:

- 5 minutes: teacher demonstration and safety instruction;
- 8 minutes: predictions and slow-pull trials;
- 12 minutes: rapid-pull trials with different masses;
- 10 minutes: repeat trials, measurements, and sketches.

The setup is very quick and the cleanup consists of returning masses and cups to trays. The teacher needs mainly to monitor that students pull sideways rather than upward and that masses are not thrown.

### Failure risk
The most likely failure is that students pull the cardboard too slowly, causing friction to drag the mass along. They may then conclude that inertia is absent or that the mass “sticks” to the cardboard.

**Mitigation:** Demonstrate the difference between a slow pull and a rapid, level pull before group work. Require students to make both observations and explain that friction between the cardboard and mass is an unbalanced force; reducing the time available for that force to act makes the inertia effect more visible.

## 3. Predict-and-test car collisions

### Physical student action
Groups create a straight collision lane on a bench using tape and a ruler. One toy car is placed stationary at a marked line. A second car is rolled from a fixed starting line so that it collides with the stationary car.

Before each trial, students predict:

1. Whether the stationary car will remain still or begin moving.
2. Whether the moving car will speed up, slow down, or change direction.
3. Which car will move farther after the collision.

They then conduct and repeat trials:

- unloaded moving car into unloaded stationary car;
- unloaded moving car into a stationary car carrying a 500 g mass;
- loaded moving car into an unloaded stationary car;
- a glancing collision, if the bench space permits, to produce a change in direction.

Students mark the starting and final positions with tape or pencil marks. One student releases the car, one observes the collision, one measures final distances with a ruler, and one records the prediction and result.

### Science payoff
Students should arrive at the idea that:

- The stationary car remains stationary only until the collision force acts on it.
- During contact, the cars exert forces on each other, changing their motion.
- A collision can make an object speed up, slow down, or change direction.
- Adding mass makes a car more resistant to a change in motion.
- A car that slows after the collision is experiencing an unbalanced force; it does not stop simply because it was moving.

This provides a concrete contrast between a balanced situation before contact and an unbalanced interaction during contact.

### Evidence
For every trial, students record:

| Trial | Prediction | Moving car result | Stationary car result | Final distances |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unloaded into unloaded |  |  |  |  |
| Unloaded into loaded |  |  |  |  |
| Loaded into unloaded |  |  |  |  |

They mark the cars’ positions before and after the collision and draw arrows showing the direction of motion. For the glancing collision, they sketch the change in direction. They then write one evidence-based statement such as: “The stationary car began moving only after the collision, showing that the contact force was unbalanced.”

### Feasibility
The teacher can prepare taped lanes and starting marks before class. Cars and masses can be shared at stations if there are not enough cars for every group. Groups can rotate through three lanes while waiting groups make predictions, label diagrams, and measure the previous trial.

A 35-minute allocation is:

- 5 minutes: demonstrate a collision and explain the fixed starting line;
- 20 minutes: conduct the prediction-and-test trials;
- 7 minutes: repeat the most informative comparison;
- 3 minutes: return cars and remove tape.

The activity uses bench space only. Cars are released by hand, so no ramp or special apparatus is needed. The teacher can control safety by requiring cars to remain on the taped lanes and by stopping glancing collisions if cars leave the bench.

### Failure risk
The main failure is inconsistent release speed. If students push the moving car harder in different trials, differences caused by release speed may be confused with differences caused by mass.

**Mitigation:** Use the same starting line, release the car without an extra push, repeat each condition at least three times, and compare patterns rather than one result. The teacher should model the release and have students discard trials in which the car is visibly pushed or leaves the lane.
