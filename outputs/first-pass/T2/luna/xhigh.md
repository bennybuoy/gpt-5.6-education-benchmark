1. Central decision

Ask: does the available evidence support formation of a new substance? Students classify each case as physical, chemical, or “insufficient evidence”—not a third type of change—and attach a reason and limitation.

2. Learning intention and three success criteria

Learning intention: Use observations and comparisons to justify a classification and communicate uncertainty.

Success criteria:

- I separate an observation from an inference.
- I justify a physical or chemical classification with evidence about whether a new substance formed, including that dissolving can be physical.
- I identify a limitation or useful additional observation and apply the class reasoning tool.

3. Lesson sequence

- **0–5 minutes—Entry:** Project two paper case records. Individually choose P, C or ? and write one observation; students hold up responses.
- **5–13 minutes—Teacher model:** Compare: (A) “blue pieces became smaller when crushed”; (B) “after mixing, bubbles and a product unlike either starting material were recorded.” On a two-column display, label observations, then model the inferences: “physical is best supported” and “chemical is best supported.” Students challenge one label.
- **13–25 minutes—Construct:** Groups of four receive a blank A3 sheet, case cards and markers. They build the Change-Evidence Mat: **Observation → evidence for/no new substance → P/C/? → limitation**.
- **25–37 minutes—Test:** Groups apply the mat to five records: cutting, dissolving with recovery, colour-only change, gas-only change, and a product comparison. A card-mover places each case; an evidence-checker demands a reason.
- **37–45 minutes—Debate and revise:** Groups swap one ambiguous card, challenge the classification, and revise wording that treats a sign as proof.
- **45–54 minutes—Individual check:** Students complete the written response below while the teacher checks observation/inference errors.
- **54–60 minutes—Bridge:** Share one qualified claim, collect checks, and store mats labelled for A16. **Total: 60 minutes.**

4. Student-built artifact

The mat is a student-designed, reusable decision model, not a fill-in worksheet. Its final four boxes contain a factual observation, comparison/evidence, a P/C/? claim, and a limitation or next useful observation. Groups may add arrows or icons, but must test and revise the model against the cards. It becomes their recording protocol in A16.

5. Teacher explanation

A physical change alters state, shape, size or arrangement without forming a new substance. A chemical change forms one or more new substances. “The pieces became smaller” is an observation; “physical” is an inference. Colour, gas, temperature, precipitate, light or odour can support a chemical interpretation, but none proves it in every context. Dissolving can be physical. Reversibility or irreversibility alone is not a definition.

6. Individual exam-style check and marking points

**Prompt (9 minutes):** “Two clear liquids are mixed. Records show bubbles, warming and a solid. A comparison record says the solid is a different material, not merely a different state, from either starting liquid. Classify as physical, chemical or insufficient evidence. Give two observations, your inference and one limitation. Then classify and justify: a solid dissolves in water and is recovered unchanged when the water is removed.”

**Marking, 4 points:**

- 1: Separates recorded observations from the inference.
- 1: Classifies the first case as chemical, or chemical as best supported, because product evidence supports a new substance.
- 1: States that bubbles or warming alone are not proof and identifies a limit.
- 1: Classifies the second case as physical, explaining that dissolving and recovery support no new substance.

Do not award the chemical point for signs alone.

7. ADHD-supportive access and deep extension

Use heterogeneous groups, fixed four roles—reader, mover, sceptic and recorder/reporter—and rotate once. Three-minute card rounds, a visible timer, pre-cut packs and required physical placement reduce waiting and make the next action clear; movement is purposeful while the evidence-and-limit demand preserves depth. Provide large-print cards, read-aloud support, icons and the stem, “I observed…; therefore…; however…”. Early finishers receive a gas-, colour- or odour-only card and must propose evidence that would discriminate, then rebut a peer.

8. Forward link and operational risks

At A16, students use the stored mat for every practical case: record observations first, classify physical or chemical, justify with evidence, and state confidence or limits before moving on. AH17 uses only paper cards, A3 sheets, markers and boards—no liquids, powders, heating, flames, tasting or uncontrolled substances. Pre-cut materials and role cards protect timing. The teacher must stop claims that one sign proves chemistry and preserve the individual check if discussion overruns.
