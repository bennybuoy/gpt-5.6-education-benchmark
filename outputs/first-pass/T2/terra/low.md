## 1. Central decision

Students will decide: **“Does the available evidence justify claiming that a new substance formed?”** Rather than sorting changes by superficial signs or reversibility, they will build and test an evidence-based classification tool. Their conclusion may be physical change, chemical change, or **“insufficient evidence to be certain.”**

## 2. Learning intention and three success criteria

**Learning intention:** Use observations and scientific reasoning to distinguish physical and chemical changes.

**Success criteria:**
1. I can separate an observation (“bubbles appeared”) from an inference (“a gas formed”).
2. I can use evidence to justify whether a change is physical or chemical.
3. I can explain why one sign, such as colour change or dissolving, is not automatic proof of a chemical change.

## 3. Lesson sequence

**0–5 min — Entry decision:** Display: “Ice melts”; “A tablet dissolves in water”; “A mixture produces bubbles.” Students stand or point to **physical / chemical / need more evidence**. No answers yet. Teacher records disagreement visibly.

**5–13 min — Teacher-modelled comparison:** Use two projected case descriptions.

- Case A: “A white solid is added to water. It is no longer visible. The liquid remains clear.”
- Case B: “Two clear liquids are mixed. A cloudy solid appears.”

Model a three-column reasoning routine: **Observation / inference / justified conclusion**.  
For A, “solid is no longer visible” is observation; “it dissolved” is an inference; this may be a physical change, but more evidence is needed to establish whether a new substance formed.  
For B, “cloudy solid appears” is observation; “a precipitate formed” is an inference; this is evidence consistent with chemical change, but the claim should be justified by considering whether a new substance is likely.

**13–18 min — Build the tool:** In groups of three, students construct an “Evidence Ladder” on A3 paper:

1. Record only what was observed.  
2. Identify possible inferences.  
3. Ask: could state, shape, size or arrangement alone explain this?  
4. Ask: is there evidence that one or more new substances formed?  
5. Conclude physical, chemical, or insufficient evidence; state the evidence limit.

**18–33 min — Test with case cards:** Groups apply their ladder to six dry, teacher-provided scenario cards, for example: ice melting; paper torn; sugar disappearing in water; a glow stick giving light; metal changing colour over time; two solutions producing a solid. Each group must place one card in the “insufficient evidence” category and defend why.

**33–43 min — Debate and revision:** Pair groups compare one disputed classification. Speakers must use the sentence frame: “Our observation is ___. Our inference is ___. This supports ___ because ___. However, the limit is ___.” Groups revise their ladder after challenge.

**43–50 min — Whole-class calibration:** Teacher samples conclusions, correcting the rule “irreversible means chemical” and “dissolving means chemical.” Class agrees that signs such as gas, colour, temperature, light, odour and precipitate are evidence to investigate, not automatic proof.

**50–58 min — Individual exam-style check:** Silent written response (Section 6).

**58–60 min — A16 launch:** Students keep their Evidence Ladder and identify one observation they will need to record carefully in tomorrow’s practical.

## 4. Student-built artifact

Each group produces an **Evidence Ladder**: a reusable decision-and-justification model with observation/inference/conclusion sections and an explicit “limits of evidence” prompt. It is revised after debate, then taken to A16 as the group’s practical reasoning guide.

## 5. Teacher explanation

A physical change does not form a new substance; state, shape, size or arrangement can change. A chemical change forms one or more new substances. Observable events are not themselves conclusions: bubbles may suggest gas production, but students must distinguish what they saw from what they infer. Likewise, dissolving can be physical, and reversibility alone does not define either type of change. Scientists make conclusions proportional to the evidence available.

## 6. Individual exam-style check and marking points

**Question:** A student mixes two clear liquids. The mixture becomes cloudy and a solid collects at the bottom. Classify the change as physical, chemical, or insufficient evidence. Justify your answer using an observation, an inference, and one limitation of the evidence.

**Marking points (4):**
- Identifies observations: clear liquids became cloudy; solid collected (1).
- Separates inference from observation: infers a precipitate/possible new substance (1).
- Classifies as chemical change, justified by evidence consistent with formation of a new substance (1).
- States that a precipitate alone is not automatic proof; further testing could strengthen the conclusion (1).

## 7. ADHD-supportive access and deep extension

The lesson alternates short whole-class inputs with timed, active decisions, visible roles (reader, recorder, challenger) and a physical A3 model. This reduces sustained passive listening and gives clear re-entry points after distraction. A projected countdown and one task per card limit working-memory load.

Depth is preserved because the challenger must identify alternative explanations and evidence limits. Extension students compare competing explanations for a case and propose what additional observation or test in A16 would most strengthen a claim about new substances.

## 8. Forward link and operational risks

In A16, students will use their Evidence Ladder while observing changes, recording observations before deciding whether changes are physical or chemical. They will classify and justify conclusions, including uncertainty where evidence is limited.

AH17 uses only projected descriptions, paper cards, pens and posters: no wet chemistry, heating, flames, tasting or uncontrolled substances. Keep movement bounded to group tables, distribute one card set per group, and use clear stop signals during debate to prevent card-based activity becoming unfocused.
