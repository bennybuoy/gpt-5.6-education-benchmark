## 1. Central decision
Students build, test and revise an “Evidence-to-verdict protocol,” not a list of signs. It forces an observation–inference–limit distinction before a physical, chemical or “insufficient evidence to classify” verdict.

## 2. Learning intention and three success criteria
Learning intention: Use evidence and its limits to make and justify physical or chemical claims.

1. Separate direct observations from inferences/claims.  
2. Classify physical where evidence justifies no new substance, and chemical where it justifies one or more new substances; do not decide from dissolving, reversibility or irreversibility alone.  
3. State a verdict with linked evidence and a limitation or needed further evidence.

## 3. Lesson sequence
- **0–5 (5 min):** Silent sort: label “colour changed” and “it is chemical” as observation or inference; reveal together.  
- **5–13 (8 min):** Teacher models an Observation–Inference–Verdict table: state/colour changes are observations; new-substance and physical/chemical conclusions are inferences. P: state changed; file establishes no new substance formed; physical. Q: colour change observed; further evidence establishes one or more new substances; chemical. Remove Q’s final evidence: colour alone is consistent with chemical change, not proof, so more evidence is needed.  
- **13–20 (7 min):** Six teams of five create protocol version 1 on blank A3, using arrows and their own wording.  
- **20–34 (14 min):** Test paper files: a no-new-substance state/shape/size/arrangement case; a new-substances-established case; dissolving alone; reversibility/irreversibility alone; and single- or mixed-sign cases using colour, gas production, temperature, precipitate, light or odour. Add a claim–evidence–limit tag to each.  
- **34–44 (10 min):** Swap two files. A challenger rejects a verdict only by locating an unsupported rule; teams revise one rule.  
- **44–52 (8 min):** Gallery calibration: individual response-card vote, team explanation of disputed cases, teacher confirms non-negotiables; label tool for A16.  
- **52–60 (8 min):** Independently complete the case verdict in section 6.  

Total: **60 minutes**.

## 4. Student-built artifact
Each blank A3 has four linked panels: Observation (seen/measured); possible inference; “Does available evidence justify no new substance, one or more new substances, or not enough?”; and Verdict—because evidence—however limit/next evidence. Its branches are: justified no-new-substance conclusion → physical; justified one-or-more-new-substances conclusion → chemical; neither → insufficient evidence to classify. Red flags state: “Dissolving can be physical”; “reversibility/irreversibility alone and one sign do not decide.”

## 5. Teacher explanation
Say: “An observation is what was seen or measured. ‘A new substance formed’ and ‘chemical change’ are inferences unless a file explicitly establishes them. A physical change forms no new substance, though state, shape, size or arrangement may change; a chemical change forms one or more new substances. Colour, gas production, temperature change, precipitate, light and odour are consistent clues; no single one is automatic proof in every context. Dissolving can be physical. Neither reversibility nor irreversibility alone is a definition. ‘Insufficient evidence’ is a rigorous limit, not a third change type.”

## 6. Individual exam-style check and marking points
Individually, with protocols face down (6 marks):

File A: A sample is reshaped. The investigation establishes no new substance formed.  
File B: Gas production is observed. Further evidence establishes one or more new substances formed.

Classify A and B. For B, identify the direct observation and classification inference. State what can be concluded if B’s second sentence is removed.

Marking: A physical (1); reshaping plus no new substance (1); B chemical (1); further evidence of one or more new substances (1); gas production observation versus chemical/new-substance inference (1); without further evidence, gas is consistent with chemical change but one sign cannot prove it, so evidence is insufficient to classify (1).

## 7. ADHD-supportive access and deep extension
Visible countdowns, one displayed next action and one file at a time externalise sequencing and shorten waiting. Rotating reader, evidence-checker, challenger, recorder and card-runner roles channel talk, card handling and movement into an evidence function. Individual response cards precede debate, requiring a personal claim. Four visual labels and oral rehearsal lower language/working-memory demand without an answer key. Deep extension: design a challenge file from the listed evidence types that breaks “one sign proves chemical” or a two-outcome tool, then defend the strongest warranted claim and needed evidence.

## 8. Forward link and operational risks
Same practical groups take labelled A3 tools to A16. For each wet-lab change, they record observations before making a new-substance inference and physical/chemical claim with a limit; revise if evidence changes. AH17 uses printed files, A3 and stationery only: no wet chemistry, substances, heating, flames or tasting. Pre-check wording so a single sign is never presented as proof; use one authorised card swap, a timer and a return-to-seat signal; store tools in named folders.
