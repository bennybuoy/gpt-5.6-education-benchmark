## Marking criteria sheet — 8 marks

### Indicative calculations and interpretation

Using \(N=\frac{M\times C}{R}\):

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Change, Year 1–4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A — control** | \(\frac{100(120)}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{110(130)}{13}=1100\) | \(\frac{120(140)}{14}=1200\) | \(\frac{125(150)}{15}=1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%\) |
| **Lake B — predator** | \(\frac{100(120)}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{90(110)}{11}=900\) | \(\frac{70(100)}{14}=500\) | \(\frac{50(80)}{16}=250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\) |

Relevant relationships include:

- Both lakes began with the same estimated population of 1000.
- Following the predator’s introduction at the end of Year 1, Lake B declined each year, reaching 250 in Year 4.
- Lake A increased from 1000 to 1250 over the same period.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH were broadly stable and similar between lakes, providing no clear abiotic explanation from the measured variables.
- The contrasting temporal patterns are consistent with predation or predator-induced changes in catchability.
- The evidence supports the hypothesis, but it does not establish causation conclusively because there is only one treatment lake and one control lake and because observational and mark-recapture limitations remain.

### Application of NSW NESA glossary terms

- **Analyse:** Identify components and the relationship between them; draw out and relate implications. For this task, students should calculate and break down trends by lake, year and abiotic variable, then identify relationships among predator introduction, population change and environmental conditions.
- **Evaluate:** Make a judgement based on criteria; determine the value of the evidence. For this task, students should judge the strength of support for the hypothesis using criteria such as the timing and magnitude of the decline, comparison with the control, abiotic evidence, study design and reliability of the population estimates.

### What distinguishes a high-band response from a mid-band response?

A **mid-band** response may correctly calculate most values, describe that Lake B declined and state that the predator probably caused the decline. It may also list limitations. However, description and listing alone are not sufficient for the highest band.

A **high-band** response must:

1. **Analyse**, not merely describe: it breaks the evidence into components and identifies relationships—for example, that both populations began at 1000, Lake B declined only after predator introduction, Lake A simultaneously increased, and measured abiotic variables remained similar.
2. **Evaluate**, not merely conclude: it uses explicit criteria such as temporal sequence, control comparison, magnitude of change, alternative explanations and design reliability to make and justify a qualified judgement.
3. Explain how limitations affect the strength of inference, rather than simply naming them.
4. Distinguish strong support for a hypothesis from proof of causation.

## Indicative limitations

Credit should be given for any two or more well-explained relevant limitations, including:

- **Lack of treatment replication:** There was only one predator lake and one control lake. Lake-specific differences could therefore be confounded with predator presence, limiting generalisation and causal inference.
- **Non-random predator introduction:** The study is a natural or quasi-experiment rather than a randomised experiment, so unmeasured differences or events in Lake B may have contributed to the decline.
- **Limited abiotic measurements:** Only dissolved oxygen, surface temperature and pH were recorded. Other factors—habitat change, food availability, disease, pollution, fishing, rainfall or recruitment—were not measured.
- **Mark-recapture assumptions:** Predation, births, deaths, migration, mark loss, altered behaviour or unequal catchability could violate Schnabel assumptions and bias estimates.
- **Predator-induced catchability:** Rainbowfish may avoid the littoral zone or sampling gear when cichlids are present. A lower estimate could partly reflect behavioural change rather than only a lower true population.
- **Short time series:** One pre-introduction year provides limited evidence about normal variation or whether the lakes had parallel trends before the introduction.
- **No direct predation data:** No cichlid diet, abundance, attack-rate or rainbowfish mortality data are supplied, so the mechanism is inferred rather than directly demonstrated.
- **Abiotic sampling limitations:** Three littoral sites sampled annually may not represent temporal fluctuations or whole-lake conditions.

## Common-error diagnosis

### 1. Numerical error: misapplying the formula or percentage-change baseline

**Example:** A student calculates Year 3 for Lake B as  
\[
N=\frac{70\times14}{100}=9.8
\]
by confusing \(C\) and \(R\), instead of:
\[
N=\frac{70\times100}{14}=500.
\]

Another error is calculating the decline as:
\[
\frac{1000-250}{250}\times100=300\%
\]
rather than using Year 1 as the baseline:
\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%.
\]

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Incorrect estimates distort the trends and undermine all later analysis and evaluation. A wrong denominator can also make the magnitude of change scientifically misleading.

**How to avoid it:**  
Write the formula and label every substituted value:
\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}.
\]
For percentage change, use:
\[
\%\text{ change}=\frac{\text{Year 4}-\text{Year 1}}{\text{Year 1}}\times100.
\]
Check whether the sign agrees with the observed direction: positive for Lake A and negative for Lake B.

---

### 2. Conceptual error: misinterpreting the predator’s ecological role

**Example:** A student describes the cichlid as a producer or primary consumer, claims it causes **bottom-up** control, or argues that it competes with rainbowfish for sunlight. The cichlid is a carnivorous consumer at a higher trophic level that feeds on small fish. Its hypothesised effect is principally **top-down control** through predation.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
This misidentifies the proposed mechanism and weakens the biological explanation linking introduction to rainbowfish decline. It also prevents the student from correctly relating predator presence to prey abundance.

**How to avoid it:**  
Identify the feeding relationship before interpreting the trend:

- rainbowfish = prey;
- cichlid = higher-level consumer/predator;
- predator reducing prey abundance = top-down effect.

Students should still recognise that the table alone does not directly measure predation rate or diet.

---

### 3. Causal-overclaim error: treating correlation as proof

**Example:** A student states, “The results prove that cichlids caused the 75% decline because the decline occurred after their introduction,” while ignoring the lack of replicate treatment lakes and possible unmeasured variables.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Temporal association, a control comparison and stable measured abiotic variables strengthen the hypothesis, but they do not eliminate every alternative explanation. With only one lake in each condition, predator presence is confounded with any unique feature or event affecting Lake B. Stable oxygen, temperature and pH also rule out only those measured factors, not all abiotic or biotic alternatives.

**How to avoid it:**  
Use qualified causal language, such as:

> “The data strongly support, but do not conclusively prove, the hypothesis.”

Then justify this judgement with both:

- **supporting evidence:** the post-introduction decline, the contrasting control trend and stable measured abiotic variables; and
- **qualifications:** no treatment replication, limited pre-introduction data, possible mark-recapture bias and unmeasured confounding factors.
