# Marking Criteria Sheet — 8 marks

## Expected calculations and indicative analysis

Using \(N=\dfrac{M\times C}{R}\):

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Change, Year 1–4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A: control** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((110\times130)/13=1100\) | \((120\times140)/14=1200\) | \((125\times150)/15=1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%\) |
| **Lake B: predator introduced** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((90\times110)/11=900\) | \((70\times100)/14=500\) | \((50\times80)/16=250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\) |

### Indicative interpretation

- Both lakes began with an estimated population of 1000 rainbowfish.
- Lake A increased steadily from 1000 to 1250, whereas Lake B declined from 1000 to 250 after the predator was introduced at the end of Year 1.
- The contrasting trajectories are consistent with a predator effect. The cichlid feeds on small-bodied littoral fish, providing a plausible biological mechanism for top-down population control.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH remained broadly similar and stable in both lakes. These measured variables therefore do not provide an obvious abiotic explanation for the decline in Lake B.
- The evidence supports the hypothesis, but does not prove causation. A conclusion of **“supports”** or **“partially supports”** may receive full credit if it is justified using appropriate evidence and limitations.

## Application of NSW NESA command verbs

- **Analyse:** Break the data into components, identify trends and relationships, and connect their implications. In this task, this includes calculating each population estimate, comparing changes over time and between lakes, and relating population trends to predator timing and abiotic measurements.
- **Evaluate:** Make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement with evidence. Relevant criteria include the strength of the temporal and control-lake comparison, biological plausibility, whether abiotic alternatives are supported, and the limitations of the study design and mark-recapture method.

A high-band response therefore does more than list calculations or describe a decline. It integrates the population, control, timing and abiotic evidence and then makes a qualified, evidence-based judgement. A mid-band response may calculate and describe the main trends but provides less analysis of relationships or a less fully justified evaluation.

## Banded performance descriptors

### 7–8 marks

The response:

- correctly applies the Schnabel formula and provides all or nearly all eight population estimates:
  - Lake A: 1000, 1100, 1200, 1250
  - Lake B: 1000, 900, 500, 250
- correctly calculates the Year 1–Year 4 percentage changes as:
  - Lake A: **25% increase**
  - Lake B: **75% decrease**
- **analyses** the evidence by breaking it into relevant components and identifying relationships, including:
  - the different trends in the control and predator lakes
  - the timing of the Lake B decline relative to predator introduction
  - the stability and similarity of the measured abiotic variables
  - the ecological plausibility of predation by a visual predator of small littoral fish
- discusses at least two valid limitations and explains how they affect confidence in the conclusion
- **evaluates** the hypothesis by making a clear judgement—support, partial support, or no support—using explicit criteria and relevant evidence
- recognises that the data are consistent with a predator effect but do not conclusively establish causation.

**Distinguishing feature:** The response integrates multiple lines of evidence and limitations into a justified judgement. It does not merely state trends.

### 5–6 marks

The response:

- correctly calculates most population estimates and percentage changes, with only minor numerical or transcription errors
- identifies the major contrast: Lake A increased while Lake B declined following predator introduction
- makes some use of the abiotic data, generally recognising that they remained stable or similar
- discusses at least two limitations, although their effects on the conclusion may be explained only briefly
- makes a relevant judgement about the hypothesis and gives some supporting evidence.

**Distinguishing feature:** The response shows sound analysis but may describe rather than fully relate all components. Its evaluation is reasonable but less developed: the criteria for the judgement, alternative explanations, or implications of the limitations are not fully integrated.

### 3–4 marks

The response:

- demonstrates partial use of the mark-recapture formula, but includes several errors or omissions
- identifies that Lake B declined and/or Lake A increased, but provides limited comparison between the lakes
- may mention the predator, abiotic data or study limitations without clearly explaining their relationship to the population trends
- identifies at least one valid limitation, or lists two limitations with little explanation
- provides a conclusion about the hypothesis, but the judgement is asserted rather than adequately justified.

**Distinguishing feature:** The response mainly describes isolated results. It shows limited **analysis** of relationships and limited **evaluation** based on stated criteria and evidence.

### 1–2 marks

The response:

- provides one or more relevant calculations, observations or ecological statements, but with substantial errors or omissions
- may state that Lake B declined or that the predator could eat rainbowfish
- gives little or no meaningful comparison with Lake A or the abiotic data
- gives an unsupported or scientifically overconfident conclusion
- provides no valid limitation, or names a limitation without relevant explanation.

### 0 marks

The response:

- provides no relevant calculations, analysis or evaluation.

## Acceptable study limitations

Responses may discuss limitations including, but not limited to:

1. **Lack of replication:** There is only one control lake and one predator lake. Differences could be due to lake-specific factors rather than predator presence.
2. **No random assignment or controlled introduction:** This is a natural field comparison following an illegal introduction, so treatment conditions were not experimentally assigned.
3. **Unmeasured confounding variables:** Food availability, disease, habitat change, fishing, competition, recruitment or other predators were not measured.
4. **Mark-recapture assumptions may have been violated:** Births, deaths, immigration or emigration during sampling would violate closure.
5. **Unequal catchability:** Predator presence could alter rainbowfish behaviour or habitat use, making them harder to catch and causing an apparent decline.
6. **Effects of marking or mark loss:** Marks may affect survival or behaviour, or may be lost, altering recapture numbers and population estimates.
7. **Limited abiotic measurements:** Only three abiotic variables and three littoral sites were sampled. Other environmental changes, spatial variation or short-term fluctuations may have been missed.
8. **No uncertainty estimates or statistical testing:** Confidence intervals and tests of differences are absent, making it difficult to assess precision and statistical significance.
9. **Limited duration:** Four years may be insufficient to distinguish a sustained predator effect from short-term population fluctuations.

# Common-error diagnosis

## 1. Numerical error: incorrect formula or percentage-change baseline

### Example error
A student adds \(M\), \(C\) and \(R\), or divides by the wrong quantity, instead of using:

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}.
\]

Alternatively, the student calculates Lake B’s percentage change using Year 4 as the denominator:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{250}\times100=-300\%
\]

instead of using the initial Year 1 population:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%.
\]

### Why it reduces response quality
Incorrect estimates undermine the trend comparison and may produce an invalid evaluation of the hypothesis. Percentage change must be measured relative to the baseline population.

### How to avoid it
- Write the formula before substituting values.
- Calculate each year separately.
- Use:

\[
\%\text{ change}=\frac{\text{final}-\text{initial}}{\text{initial}}\times100.
\]

- Check whether the sign and magnitude are reasonable: Lake A should show an increase and Lake B a substantial decline.

---

## 2. Conceptual error: misidentifying the trophic effect of the cichlid

### Example error
A student describes the cichlid as a producer or primary consumer, or states that the pattern demonstrates bottom-up control because the cichlid reduces the rainbowfish population.

### Why it reduces response quality
The cichlid is a predatory consumer that feeds on small-bodied fish. A predator reducing prey abundance is an example of **top-down control**. Bottom-up control would involve resources or abiotic conditions—such as food or nutrients—limiting organisms at higher trophic levels. Misidentifying the relationship weakens the biological mechanism used to support the hypothesis.

### How to avoid it
Identify the feeding relationship before naming the type of control:

- cichlid = predatory consumer
- rainbowfish = prey
- predator affecting prey abundance = top-down control.

The student should also connect this mechanism to the cichlid’s littoral feeding behaviour and the habitat occupied by small rainbowfish.

---

## 3. Causal-inference error: claiming that the study proves predation caused the decline

### Example error
A student concludes, “The data prove that the introduced cichlid caused the 75% decline,” without discussing alternative explanations or study-design limitations.

### Why it reduces response quality
The population decline occurred after predator introduction and was not observed in the control lake, so the evidence is consistent with the hypothesis. However, there is only one treatment lake and one control lake, no random assignment, and no replicate lakes. Unmeasured factors such as disease, recruitment failure, habitat change or altered catchability could also have contributed. Therefore, the field data support an association and a plausible causal explanation but do not prove causation.

### How to avoid it
Use qualified language such as:

> “The contrasting population trends, timing of the decline and stable measured abiotic conditions support the hypothesis that the cichlid contributed to the decline. However, the lack of replicate lakes and possible unmeasured confounding variables mean that causation cannot be established conclusively.”

This makes an evidence-based evaluation while acknowledging the limits of the study.
