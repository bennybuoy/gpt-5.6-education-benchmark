## Expected calculations and interpretation

The Schnabel population estimate is:

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}
\]

| Lake | Year | Calculation | Estimated population, \(N\) |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| A | 1 | \((100\times120)/12\) | 1000 |
| A | 2 | \((110\times130)/13\) | 1100 |
| A | 3 | \((120\times140)/14\) | 1200 |
| A | 4 | \((125\times150)/15\) | 1250 |
| B | 1 | \((100\times120)/12\) | 1000 |
| B | 2 | \((90\times110)/11\) | 900 |
| B | 3 | \((70\times100)/14\) | 500 |
| B | 4 | \((50\times80)/16\) | 250 |

Percentage change:

\[
\%\text{ change}=\frac{N_{\text{Year 4}}-N_{\text{Year 1}}}{N_{\text{Year 1}}}\times100
\]

- **Lake A:**  
  \[
  \frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%
  \]
  The estimated population increased by 25%.

- **Lake B:**  
  \[
  \frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
  \]
  The estimated population decreased by 75%.

Expected relationships identified in the analysis include:

- Both lakes began with the same estimated population of 1000 fish.
- Lake A increased steadily from 1000 to 1250.
- Following the introduction at the end of Year 1, Lake B declined from 1000 to 250.
- The lakes had broadly similar, stable dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH values, so the measured abiotic variables do not readily explain the contrasting trends.
- The timing, control-lake comparison and feeding ecology of the visual fish predator are consistent with a predation effect.
- However, the study is correlational and has only one control lake and one affected lake, so it cannot establish causation conclusively.

An appropriate overall judgement is that the data **partially support, or strongly support but do not prove**, the hypothesis. The exact label should be accepted where the judgement is justified using both supporting evidence and limitations.

## Application of NSW NESA command verbs

- **Analyse:** identify the components of the data and the relationships between them, then draw out and relate their implications. In this task, this requires more than listing estimates: students should compare years, compare lakes, relate the decline to the timing of predator introduction, and consider the abiotic data.
- **Evaluate:** make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement with evidence. Relevant criteria include the size and timing of the decline, the control-lake trend, stability of measured abiotic factors, biological plausibility, study design and reliability of the population estimates.

### Distinguishing a high-band response from a mid-band response

A **high-band response** does not simply calculate and describe the results. It:

1. **Analyses** by separating the evidence into components—population estimates, temporal trends, control-versus-affected lake comparison, timing of introduction and abiotic variables—and identifying relationships among them.
2. Draws out implications, such as the fact that the divergence after Year 1 is consistent with a predator effect and is not readily explained by the three measured abiotic factors.
3. **Evaluates** by applying explicit criteria to make a judgement, including both evidence supporting the hypothesis and evidence or design features that weaken causal confidence.
4. Explains how limitations affect validity, reliability or generalisability.

A **mid-band response** generally identifies the main trends and gives a reasonable conclusion, but may remain partly descriptive. It may state that Lake B declined after the introduction and that abiotic variables stayed stable without fully relating these observations. Its evaluation may be based mainly on the observed association, with limited discussion of alternative explanations, methodological assumptions or the lack of lake-level replication.

### Guidance for marks within each band

Award the upper mark in a band when the response:

- is quantitatively accurate;
- integrates several lines of evidence;
- explains rather than merely lists limitations; and
- provides a clearly justified judgement.

Award the lower mark when the response:

- contains calculation errors or omissions;
- describes trends without fully analysing relationships;
- lists limitations without explaining their consequences; or
- gives a judgement with limited supporting evidence.

# Common-error diagnosis

## 1. Numerical error: misapplying the formula or using the wrong percentage baseline

**Example error:** A student calculates Lake B Year 4 as:

\[
N=\frac{M\times R}{C}=\frac{50\times16}{80}=10
\]

instead of:

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}=\frac{50\times80}{16}=250
\]

Another possible error is calculating the percentage decline relative to the Year 4 value rather than the Year 1 baseline, producing:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{250}\times100=-300\%
\]

The correct calculation is:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
\]

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Incorrect estimates distort the size and possibly the direction of the population trend. This weakens all subsequent comparisons and makes the final evaluation unreliable.

**How to avoid it:**  
Write the formula before substituting values, label \(M\), \(C\) and \(R\), and perform a reasonableness check. For percentage change, use the initial value—Year 1—as the denominator:

\[
\frac{\text{final}-\text{initial}}{\text{initial}}\times100
\]

## 2. Conceptual error: describing the predator effect as bottom-up control or assigning the cichlid the wrong ecological role

**Example error:** A student states that the cichlid causes “bottom-up control” of the rainbowfish or identifies the cichlid as a producer or lower-trophic-level organism.

**Why this is incorrect:**  
The cichlid is a consumer and visual pursuit predator that feeds on small-bodied fish. A predator reducing the abundance of prey is a **top-down** effect because regulation occurs from a higher consumer trophic level toward organisms below it. Bottom-up control would involve changes in resources or lower trophic levels, such as nutrients, primary productivity or food availability affecting consumer abundance.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
It misrepresents the proposed biological mechanism and weakens the connection between the cichlid’s feeding ecology and the observed rainbowfish decline.

**How to avoid it:**  
Identify the direction of the interaction before naming the process:

\[
\text{predator abundance or activity} \rightarrow \text{increased prey mortality} \rightarrow \text{lower prey population}
\]

This is top-down control. Avoid assigning an exact trophic level unless the diets of all relevant organisms are known; it is sufficient to identify the cichlid as a predatory consumer at a higher trophic position than its fish prey.

---

## 3. Causal-inference error: claiming the results prove that the cichlid caused the decline

**Example error:** A student concludes, “The experiment proves that the cichlid caused the 75% decline because Lake B declined after the cichlid was introduced.”

**Why this is an overclaim:**  
The timing, large decline, stable measured abiotic variables and contrasting control-lake trend provide evidence consistent with the hypothesis. However, there was only one affected lake and one control lake. Consequently, predator presence is confounded with other differences unique to Lake B. The three sampling sites within a lake do not constitute independent treatment-lake replicates. Only one year of pre-introduction population data is also available, and unmeasured factors such as disease, habitat change, fishing, food availability or other water-quality variables may have contributed.

In addition, mark-recapture estimates depend on assumptions such as population closure and equal catchability. Predator presence could alter rainbowfish behaviour, habitat use, survival or capture probability, potentially biasing the estimates.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
It fails to distinguish correlation and temporal association from causation and does not evaluate the study against appropriate criteria. It also ignores limitations affecting validity and generalisability.

**How to avoid it:**  
Use appropriately qualified language, for example:

> “The data partially or strongly support the hypothesis because Lake B declined by 75% after predator introduction while Lake A increased by 25% and measured abiotic conditions remained similar. However, the unreplicated field design and possible unmeasured variables mean the study does not conclusively demonstrate that the cichlid alone caused the decline.”
