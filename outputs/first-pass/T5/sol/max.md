# Marking criteria sheet — 8 marks

## Application of NSW NESA glossary terms

- **Analyse:** identify the components of the data and the relationships between them, and draw out their implications. In this task, this includes calculating the population estimates, examining changes over time, comparing the control and predator lakes, and relating the population trends to the timing of the introduction and the abiotic data.
- **Evaluate:** make a judgement based on criteria. Relevant criteria include temporal sequence, the treatment–control comparison, biological plausibility, alternative explanations and the reliability of the study design. The judgement must be justified with evidence.
- **Justify:** support an argument or conclusion with data and scientific reasoning.
- **Discuss:** identify relevant issues and explain their implications. Merely listing limitations does not constitute a developed discussion.

## Indicative quantitative content

Using \(N=(M\times C)/R\):

| Year | Lake A population estimate | Lake B population estimate |
|---|---:|---:|
| 1 | \((100\times120)/12=\mathbf{1000}\) | \((100\times120)/12=\mathbf{1000}\) |
| 2 | \((110\times130)/13=\mathbf{1100}\) | \((90\times110)/11=\mathbf{900}\) |
| 3 | \((120\times140)/14=\mathbf{1200}\) | \((70\times100)/14=\mathbf{500}\) |
| 4 | \((125\times150)/15=\mathbf{1250}\) | \((50\times80)/16=\mathbf{250}\) |

Percentage change:

\[
\%\text{ change}=\frac{N_{\text{Year 4}}-N_{\text{Year 1}}}{N_{\text{Year 1}}}\times100
\]

- **Lake A:** \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=\mathbf{+25\%}\), a 25% increase.
- **Lake B:** \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=\mathbf{-75\%}\), a 75% decrease. Lake B fell **to** 25% of its initial population, which is a decline **by** 75%.

## Indicative analysis and evaluation

A strong response would identify that:

- Both lakes began with an estimated population of 1000 fish.
- Following the cichlid introduction at the end of Year 1, Lake B declined successively to 900, 500 and 250 fish.
- Over the same period, the control population increased to 1250 fish.
- The measured abiotic variables remained broadly similar:
  - DO ranges: Lake A 7.9–8.1 mg/L; Lake B 7.8–8.0 mg/L.
  - Temperature ranges: Lake A 22.4–22.7°C; Lake B 22.5–22.7°C.
  - pH ranges: both approximately 7.2–7.3.
- Thus, the measured abiotic variables do not show a Lake B-specific change corresponding to the 75% decline.
- Predation provides a biologically plausible **top-down** mechanism because the cichlid is a higher-trophic-level predator of small littoral fish.

A well-supported judgement is that the data **support the hypothesis, but do not conclusively establish causation**. A conclusion of **“partially supports”** is also acceptable where the student uses that term to recognise the strong population pattern but limited causal design. Marks should be awarded for the quality of the evidence-based judgement rather than for the choice between those two labels.

## Acceptable limitations

Students must discuss at least two. Suitable limitations include:

- **No lake-level replication:** there is only one predator lake and one control lake. Predator presence is therefore confounded with other unique differences or events in Lake B. The three littoral sampling sites are subsamples, not three independent treatment lakes.
- **Limited pre-introduction baseline:** only Year 1 represents the population before introduction, so normal interannual variation was not well established.
- **Non-randomised field study:** the illegal introduction was not an experimentally assigned treatment, so unmeasured factors such as disease, fishing, habitat change or food availability could have contributed.
- **Mark–recapture assumptions:** deaths or movement during a sampling window, altered habitat use or catchability, mark loss, or effects of marking could bias the estimates. Population change between annual sampling periods does not by itself violate closure; closure applies to each sampling window.
- **Limited environmental monitoring:** only three abiotic variables were measured annually at three sites. This could miss short-term events, deeper-water conditions and other environmental or biotic changes.
- **Unknown estimate precision:** no confidence intervals or measures of sampling error are provided, and recapture numbers are relatively small.
- **No direct predator-effect measurement:** cichlid abundance, diet and actual predation rates were not measured, so a dose–response relationship or direct consumption of rainbowfish was not demonstrated.

## Explicit high-band versus mid-band distinction

| Command verb | High-band response | Mid-band response |
|---|---|---|
| **Analyse** | Breaks the evidence into components—\(M,C,R\) and estimated \(N\), annual trends, treatment versus control, pre/post-introduction timing, abiotic patterns and methodological factors—and identifies relationships and their implications. It explains why the combined pattern matters. | Usually calculates and describes that Lake B decreased while Lake A increased, and may mention stable abiotic conditions, but links only some components or gives limited implications. Correct description alone is not full analysis. |
| **Evaluate** | Applies clear causal criteria such as temporal order, control contrast, mechanistic plausibility, exclusion of measured alternatives, replication and method reliability. It weighs strengths and weaknesses before giving a justified, qualified judgement. | Selects “supports” or “partially supports” and cites some data, but the judgement is based on a narrower range of evidence. Limitations may be listed without being used to modify the conclusion. A conclusion alone is not evaluation. |

# Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces response quality | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Numerical error: calculating percentage change from the wrong value or baseline**. For example, using raw captures gives Lake B \((80-120)/120=-33.3\%\), or dividing by the Year 4 estimate gives \((250-1000)/250=-300\%\). | The task asks for change in the **estimated population**, not change in the number captured, and percentage change must be relative to the initial Year 1 population. The error misrepresents the magnitude of the decline and weakens all subsequent analysis. | First calculate each \(N\) using \(N=(M\times C)/R\). Then use \((N_4-N_1)/N_1\times100\). Check wording carefully: Lake B fell **to 25%** of its original population but declined **by 75%**. |
| **2. Conceptual error: describing cichlid predation as bottom-up control or identifying the cichlid as a producer/primary consumer.** | This reverses the ecological mechanism and demonstrates an incorrect understanding of trophic relationships. The cichlid is a higher-trophic-level consumer acting on prey, so its proposed effect is top-down. Bottom-up control would arise from changes in resources or productivity at lower trophic levels. | Sketch the feeding relationship before assigning terminology: predatory cichlid \(\rightarrow\) mortality of rainbowfish. Describe this as **top-down predation pressure** and the cichlid as a higher-order consumer. |
| **3. Causal overclaim: stating that the results “prove” the cichlid caused the decline.** | The temporal association, control comparison and similar measured abiotic conditions strengthen the hypothesis, but the study has only one treatment lake, one control lake and one pre-introduction year. The introduction was not randomised, and other factors or mark–recapture bias cannot be excluded. Treating the three sites as independent treatment replicates would also be pseudoreplication. Such an overclaim fails to evaluate the evidence against causal criteria. | Use qualified language such as “supports,” “is consistent with,” or “partially supports, but does not prove.” Explicitly weigh the evidence against the design limitations. Suggest replicated treatment and control lakes, multiple pre- and post-introduction years, predator abundance or diet measurements, and uncertainty estimates for a stronger causal test. |
