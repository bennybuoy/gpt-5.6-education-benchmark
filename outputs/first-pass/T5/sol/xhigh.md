# Marking criteria sheet — 8 marks

## NSW NESA command verbs

- **Analyse:** identify the components of the data and the relationships between them, and draw out their implications. In this task, students should break the evidence into population estimates, temporal trends, differences between lakes, timing of predator introduction and abiotic patterns, then relate these components to the hypothesis.
- **Evaluate:** make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement with evidence. Relevant criteria include temporal sequence, comparison with the control lake, magnitude and consistency of change, abiotic evidence, biological plausibility and limitations of the design.
- A conclusion alone is not an evaluation. The judgement must be supported and qualified using the data and study limitations.

## Indicative calculations

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}
\]

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((110\times130)/13=1100\) | \((120\times140)/14=1200\) | \((125\times150)/15=1250\) |
| **Lake B** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((90\times110)/11=900\) | \((70\times100)/14=500\) | \((50\times80)/16=250\) |

Percentage change must be calculated relative to Year 1:

\[
\%\text{ change}=\frac{N_{\text{Year 4}}-N_{\text{Year 1}}}{N_{\text{Year 1}}}\times100
\]

- **Lake A:**  
  \[
  \frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%
  \]
  A 25% increase.

- **Lake B:**  
  \[
  \frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
  \]
  A 75% decline.

## Indicative analysis and evaluation

A strong response should recognise that:

- Both lakes began with the same estimated rainbowfish population of 1000.
- Following the predator’s introduction at the end of Year 1, Lake B declined from 1000 to 900, 500 and then 250.
- In contrast, Lake A increased from 1000 to 1250.
- By Year 4, Lake B had only 20% of the population estimated for Lake A.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH remained broadly similar and changed little in both lakes. Therefore, the three measured abiotic variables do not provide an obvious explanation for the contrasting population trends.
- The timing, control-lake contrast and cichlid’s predatory ecology provide evidence consistent with a top-down effect.
- However, the study does not establish causation conclusively. There was only one predator lake and one control lake, the introduction was not randomly assigned, and other Lake B-specific factors may have contributed.

A well-supported overall judgement would be that the data **strongly support the cichlid hypothesis as a plausible explanation but only partially support a definitive causal claim**. Responses using the label “support” may also receive full credit if they clearly state that the evidence does not prove causation.

### What distinguishes a high-band response from a mid-band response?

- A **high-band analysis** does more than list values. It breaks the data into components and relates them: for example, it explains that the lakes began at the same estimated population, diverged only after predator introduction, and did so despite similar measured abiotic conditions.
- A **mid-band analysis** generally reports that Lake B declined and Lake A increased, but may not fully explain why the timing, control comparison and abiotic similarity strengthen the hypothesis.
- A **high-band evaluation** makes a judgement using stated or evident criteria and weighs both strengths and weaknesses. It distinguishes evidence that is consistent with causation from proof of causation.
- A **mid-band evaluation** gives a conclusion and some supporting evidence but may merely list limitations rather than explaining how they affect the judgement, or may not adequately qualify the causal claim.

## Acceptable limitations

At least two should be explained rather than merely named. Examples include:

1. **No replicate treatment or control lakes:** there was only one lake in each condition. The three littoral sampling sites are subsamples, not independent lake-level replicates. Lake-specific factors could therefore be confounded with predator presence.

2. **Observational and non-randomised introduction:** the illegal introduction was not an experimentally assigned treatment, reducing the ability to attribute the decline exclusively to the cichlid.

3. **Limited baseline and study duration:** there was only one pre-introduction year, so normal long-term population variation is not well characterised.

4. **Limited abiotic measurements:** only three variables were measured annually at littoral sites. Short-term fluctuations, deeper-water conditions, habitat change, pollutants, food availability, disease or fishing were not assessed.

5. **Mark-recapture assumptions:** predator presence could alter rainbowfish behaviour and catchability, marks could be lost, or deaths and movement could occur during a sampling window. Any such violation could bias the estimates. Deaths or births between annual sampling periods do not themselves violate closure; closure applies during each sampling window.

6. **Low recapture numbers and no uncertainty estimates:** recapture counts were relatively small, and confidence intervals or statistical tests were not supplied, making the precision and statistical significance of the estimates unclear.

7. **No direct measure of predation:** cichlid abundance, diet and rainbowfish mortality from predation were not directly measured, so the proposed mechanism is plausible but not demonstrated.

# Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces response quality | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Numerical error: using the wrong baseline for percentage change.** For example, calculating Lake B’s change as \((250-1000)/250\times100=-300\%\), using Year 4 as the denominator. | The task asks for change from Year 1 to Year 4, so Year 1 must be the reference value. The wrong denominator greatly exaggerates the decline and undermines the subsequent evaluation. | Write the general formula first: \((\text{final}-\text{initial})/\text{initial}\times100\). Thus Lake B is \((250-1000)/1000\times100=-75\%\), while Lake A is \(+25\%\). |
| **2. Conceptual error: describing the cichlid’s effect as bottom-up control or placing it at a lower trophic level than its fish prey.** | Predation by a higher-level consumer reducing prey abundance is **top-down control**. Bottom-up control begins with changes in nutrients, producers or resource availability. Confusing the two misrepresents the proposed biological mechanism. | Trace the feeding relationship explicitly: cichlid predator \(\rightarrow\) consumes small-bodied fish \(\rightarrow\) possible reduction in rainbowfish abundance. Describe this as a plausible top-down effect from a higher-level consumer. |
| **3. Causal overclaim: stating that the results “prove” the cichlid caused the decline or that stable abiotic data rule out all other causes.** | The data are correlational field evidence from one treatment lake and one control lake. Unmeasured differences such as disease, fishing, habitat change or food availability could affect Lake B. Without replicate lakes, random assignment or direct predation measurements, predator presence is confounded with other Lake B-specific factors. | Use calibrated language such as “supports,” “is consistent with,” or “partially supports.” Weigh the timing, control comparison and stable measured abiotic variables against the lack of replication and other possible explanations. State that replicated before-after-control-impact studies or direct predation evidence would strengthen causal inference. |
