# Marking Criteria Sheet — 8 marks

## NSW NESA glossary interpretation

- **Analyse:** Identify the components of the data and the relationships between them, then draw out and relate their implications. In this task, students should separate the data by lake, year, predator presence and abiotic variable; calculate the population estimates; compare trends; and explain relationships between predator introduction and population change.
- **Evaluate:** Make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement with evidence. Relevant criteria include:
  - whether the decline occurred after predator introduction;
  - whether Lake B differed from the control lake;
  - whether measured abiotic changes provide an alternative explanation;
  - whether the study design and mark-recapture assumptions permit a strong causal conclusion.

A high-band response must do more than list values or state that one population declined. It must relate the trends to one another and weigh supporting evidence against methodological limitations before reaching a justified judgement.

## Indicative analysis and evaluation

A strong response may identify that:

- Both lakes began with an estimated population of 1,000 in Year 1.
- Following the introduction of the cichlid at the end of Year 1, Lake B declined from 1,000 to 900, then 500 and finally 250.
- Over the same period, the control population in Lake A increased from 1,000 to 1,250.
- Thus, Lake A increased by 25%, whereas Lake B declined by 75%, producing a strong treatment-control divergence.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH remained relatively stable and similar between lakes:
  - dissolved oxygen remained approximately 7.8–8.1 mg/L;
  - temperature remained approximately 22.4–22.7 °C;
  - pH remained 7.2–7.3.
- The decline therefore does not correspond with a major change in any of the three measured abiotic variables.
- There is a plausible biological mechanism: the cichlid is a visual pursuit predator that consumes small-bodied littoral fish, so it could cause a **top-down** reduction in the rainbowfish population.

Suitable limitations include:

1. **Lack of treatment replication:** There was only one predator lake and one control lake. Lake-specific differences are therefore confounded with predator presence, and the three sites within a lake are not independent replicate lakes.
2. **Limited baseline data:** There was only one year of data before the introduction, so natural longer-term differences in population trends are not well established.
3. **Mark-recapture assumptions:** Predation during the sampling window could violate closure, while predator-induced changes in rainbowfish behaviour or habitat use could cause unequal catchability.
4. **Unmeasured alternative explanations:** Only three abiotic variables were recorded. Disease, food availability, fishing, habitat change, competitors or other pollutants were not assessed.
5. **Limited environmental sampling:** Annual measurements at three littoral sites may not represent short-term fluctuations or conditions throughout each lake.
6. **No direct measurement of predator abundance or predation rate:** The study does not demonstrate that predation intensity increased in parallel with rainbowfish decline.

An appropriate judgement is that the results **support the hypothesis, but do not prove causation**. The temporal sequence, control-lake comparison, stable measured abiotic conditions and plausible predation mechanism all support the hypothesis. However, the absence of replicated treatment lakes and the possibility of unmeasured confounding variables weaken causal certainty. A conclusion of **“partially support”** is also acceptable if the student uses that term to emphasise these design limitations and justifies it with evidence.

### Key distinction between high- and mid-band responses

- A **mid-band response** generally calculates most values, recognises the major trend and gives a plausible conclusion. However, it may mainly **describe** the data and list limitations.
- A **high-band response analyses** by connecting the timing of predator introduction, the opposing lake trends and the stable abiotic measurements, then drawing out the implications of those relationships. It **evaluates** by applying criteria for causal inference, weighing the strength of the evidence against design limitations, and justifying a qualified judgement.

# Common-Error Diagnosis

## 1. Numerical error: misapplying the mark-recapture formula or percentage-change baseline

**Example error:**  
A student calculates Lake B Year 4 as:

\[
N=\frac{M\times R}{C}
\]

instead of:

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}
\]

Alternatively, the student calculates the percentage decline using Year 4 as the denominator:

\[
\frac{1000-250}{250}\times100=300\%
\]

rather than using the Year 1 population as the baseline.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Incorrect estimates distort the magnitude and possibly the direction of the trends. This weakens all subsequent analysis and makes the final evaluation unsupported by the actual data.

**How to avoid it:**  
Write the formula before substituting values and check that \(R\) is the denominator. For percentage change, always use:

\[
\frac{\text{final}-\text{initial}}{\text{initial}}\times100
\]

Thus, Lake B’s change is:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
\]

The negative sign indicates a decline.

---

## 2. Conceptual error: describing predator effects as bottom-up control or assigning the cichlid an incorrect trophic role

**Example error:**  
A student states that the cichlid causes “bottom-up control” of the rainbowfish, or identifies the cichlid as a producer or primary consumer.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
The proposed mechanism is predation by a higher-level consumer on a prey population. This is a **top-down** effect because mortality imposed by the predator regulates organisms at a lower trophic position. Misidentifying the mechanism demonstrates weak ecological understanding and undermines the explanation of why the hypothesis is biologically plausible.

**How to avoid it:**  
Construct the relevant feeding relationship:

\[
\text{rainbowfish} \rightarrow \text{predatory cichlid}
\]

The arrow represents energy transfer from prey to predator. The cichlid is a higher-level consumer, and its effect on rainbowfish is top-down control. Bottom-up control would instead involve factors such as nutrient or food limitation affecting populations from lower trophic levels upward.

---

## 3. Causal-inference error: claiming that the field data prove the cichlid caused the decline

**Example error:**  
“The cichlid definitely caused the 75% decline because the decline occurred after it was introduced.”

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
A temporal association is evidence for the hypothesis, but it does not by itself establish causation. There is only one treatment lake and one control lake, so predator presence is confounded with any other difference specific to Lake B. Other biological or environmental factors may not have been measured, and mark-recapture assumptions may have been violated. An unqualified claim of proof therefore fails to evaluate the strength and limits of the evidence.

**How to avoid it:**  
Use appropriately qualified language, such as:

> “The data support the hypothesis because the decline followed predator introduction, did not occur in the control lake, and was not associated with changes in the measured abiotic variables. However, the study does not prove causation because treatment lakes were not replicated and other factors may have differed between the lakes.”

This both recognises the strength of the observed relationship and accurately communicates the limits of the field design.
