## Marking criteria sheet: 8-mark response

### Expected data calculations

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Change, Year 1 to Year 4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A (control)** | \((100 \times 120)/12 = 1000\) | \((110 \times 130)/13 = 1100\) | \((120 \times 140)/14 = 1200\) | \((125 \times 150)/15 = 1250\) | \((1250-1000)/1000 \times100 = +25\%\) |
| **Lake B (cichlid introduced)** | \((100 \times 120)/12 = 1000\) | \((90 \times 110)/11 = 900\) | \((70 \times 100)/14 = 500\) | \((50 \times 80)/16 = 250\) | \((250-1000)/1000 \times100 = -75\%\) |

### Key scientific points
- Before introduction, both lakes had the same estimated eastern rainbowfish population: **1000 fish in Year 1**.
- After the cichlid was introduced to Lake B, rainbowfish estimates declined from **1000 to 250**, whereas Lake A increased from **1000 to 1250**.
- Lake B had a **75% decline**, compared with a **25% increase** in the control lake.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH changed only slightly and similarly across lakes; therefore, the recorded abiotic variables do not provide a strong alternative explanation for the large Lake B decline.
- The cichlid is a predatory fish feeding on small fish, so a **top-down effect** on rainbowfish is biologically plausible.
- The evidence **partially supports** the hypothesis: the temporal pattern, control comparison and stable recorded abiotic conditions are consistent with cichlid predation, but the study cannot prove causation.

## Distinguishing high-band from mid-band responses

A **high-band response** does more than state that Lake B declined. It:

- **Analyses** by separating the evidence into components and identifying relationships:
  - calculates and compares both lakes’ trends;
  - links the decline in Lake B to the timing of predator introduction;
  - compares Lake B against the control rather than considering Lake B alone;
  - considers whether the abiotic data provide an alternative explanation.
- **Evaluates** by making a judgement against stated criteria and justifying it with evidence:
  - judges the evidence as **partially supportive**, rather than claiming proof;
  - weighs evidence supporting the hypothesis against limitations;
  - explains why a single treatment lake, possible mark-recapture bias and unmeasured variables limit causal inference.

A **mid-band response** usually identifies the main pattern and may correctly conclude that the cichlid is associated with the decline, but it tends to:
- describe results rather than identify relationships among the control, treatment, time and abiotic variables;
- provide a conclusion with less explicit evidence;
- mention limitations without explaining how they weaken the causal conclusion; or
- use “supports” without qualifying the strength of the evidence.

## Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces response quality | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Incorrect mark-recapture or percentage-change calculation** — for example, calculating Lake B Year 4 as \(50 + 80 - 16\), or calculating percentage decline using Year 4 rather than Year 1 as the denominator. | This produces incorrect population estimates and may lead to an incorrect interpretation of the trend. The required formula is \(N = (M \times C)/R\). Percentage change must be calculated relative to the original population, Year 1. | Substitute values carefully and show working. For Lake B Year 4: \((50 \times 80)/16 = 250\). Then calculate change from Year 1 as \((250-1000)/1000 \times 100 = -75\%\). |
| **2. Confusing trophic relationships or top-down control** — for example, describing the cichlid as a primary consumer, or stating that rainbowfish decline is caused by bottom-up control without evidence of reduced resources. | The cichlid is a fish-eating predator and therefore occupies a higher trophic level than the rainbowfish. Its predation on small-bodied fish is a potential **top-down** effect. Misidentifying this relationship weakens the ecological explanation. | Identify the cichlid as a predator of small fish. Explain that an increase in predator pressure can reduce prey abundance through top-down control. Do not infer bottom-up control unless evidence of changes in food, nutrients or primary productivity is provided. |
| **3. Overclaiming causation from the field comparison** — for example, writing that the results “prove the cichlid caused the decline” or ignoring that only one treatment lake was studied. | The data show a strong association consistent with cichlid predation, but they do not eliminate all alternative explanations. There is only one lake with the introduced predator, limited pre-introduction data, possible unmeasured environmental or biological differences, and no direct evidence of cichlid diet or predation rate. | Use scientifically justified language such as “the data partially support the hypothesis” or “the decline is consistent with a top-down effect of cichlid predation.” Then explain limitations, including lack of replicate treatment lakes and possible mark-recapture or unmeasured-variable effects. |
