## Marking Criteria Sheet: Introduced Predator and Rainbowfish Population Decline (8 marks)

### Expected calculations

Population estimate:  
\[
N=\frac{M \times C}{R}
\]

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((110\times130)/13=1100\) | \((120\times140)/14=1200\) | \((125\times150)/15=1250\) |
| **Lake B** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((90\times110)/11=900\) | \((70\times100)/14=500\) | \((50\times80)/16=250\) |

Percentage change from Year 1 to Year 4:

- **Lake A:**  
\[
\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%
\]

- **Lake B:**  
\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
\]

Indicative judgement: the evidence **partially supports** the hypothesis. Lake B declined substantially after cichlid introduction while Lake A increased, and abiotic conditions were similar between lakes and over time. However, the study is observational, has only one treatment lake, and cannot exclude other biotic or unmeasured environmental explanations.

## Performance Descriptors

### 7–8 marks
- **Analyses** the data by accurately calculating all or nearly all population estimates and percentage changes, and breaking the results into relevant components: changes over time, differences between Lake A and Lake B, timing of predator introduction, and abiotic measurements.
- Clearly identifies relationships in the data, including that Lake B declined from approximately 1000 to 250 fish (75% decline) after introduction, whereas Lake A increased from 1000 to 1250 fish (25% increase).
- Uses the control lake and relatively stable dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH data to make a reasoned comparison and identify that measured abiotic conditions are unlikely to explain the contrasting trends.
- **Evaluates** the hypothesis by making a clear, qualified judgement—most appropriately, that the data partially support the hypothesis—and justifying that judgement with relevant evidence and limitations.
- Discusses at least two relevant limitations, such as lack of replicate treatment lakes, correlational/observational design, unmeasured variables, possible migration or recruitment, small sample size, or possible violation of Schnabel assumptions.
- Distinguishes association from proof of causation and explains why the evidence is suggestive but not conclusive.

**High-band distinction:** A high-band response does more than describe a decline. It **analyses** by identifying and linking patterns across lakes and years, and **evaluates** by applying criteria for causal inference—control comparison, timing, alternative explanations and methodological limitations—to reach a justified, qualified judgement.

---

### 5–6 marks
- Correctly calculates most population estimates and percentage changes, with minor arithmetic or presentation errors.
- Identifies the overall pattern: Lake B declines after cichlid introduction while Lake A remains stable or increases.
- Refers to the control lake and/or abiotic data, though the relationship between these data and the hypothesis may not be fully developed.
- Makes a judgement that the hypothesis is supported or partially supported, with some evidence-based justification.
- Describes at least two limitations, although these may be brief or not clearly linked to the strength of the conclusion.
- Recognises, at least generally, that the results do not fully prove causation.

**Mid-band distinction:** A mid-band response generally identifies the main trend and gives a plausible conclusion, but may mainly **describe** results rather than fully analyse relationships among the predator introduction, control lake, abiotic data and population estimates. Its evaluation is less explicit, less balanced, or less well justified than a high-band response.

---

### 3–4 marks
- Calculates some population estimates correctly or shows a generally correct method.
- Identifies that rainbowfish numbers declined in Lake B, but may provide limited comparison with Lake A or limited use of the timing of cichlid introduction.
- May calculate or state one percentage change correctly.
- Gives a simple conclusion, such as that cichlids may have affected rainbowfish.
- Identifies one or two limitations, but these may be vague, inaccurate or not linked to the conclusion.
- Uses limited evidence to support the judgement.

---

### 1–2 marks
- Provides limited, incomplete or substantially inaccurate calculations.
- Makes a general statement about population decline or predators without adequately using the supplied data.
- May confuse the lakes, years or direction of population change.
- Gives an unsupported conclusion or claims the data prove causation.
- Identifies few or no relevant limitations.

## Guidance on Command Verbs

### Analyse
To **analyse**, students should break the data into components and identify relationships. Relevant components include:

- population estimates for each lake and year
- percentage change from Year 1 to Year 4
- the timing of cichlid introduction
- comparison of treatment and control lakes
- comparison of dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH
- assumptions of the Schnabel method.

A strong analysis explains the relationship: Lake B’s population fell after predator introduction, while Lake A increased despite similar measured abiotic conditions.

### Evaluate
To **evaluate**, students should make a judgement based on criteria and justify it with evidence. Relevant criteria include:

- whether decline occurred after cichlid introduction
- whether the control lake showed the same trend
- whether measured abiotic conditions differed enough to explain the decline
- whether alternative explanations and study limitations have been considered
- whether the design permits a causal conclusion.

A strong evaluation concludes that the data **partially support** the hypothesis because the patterns are consistent with top-down predation, but the evidence does not establish causation conclusively.

# Common-Error Diagnosis

## 1. Numerical error: incorrect use of the Schnabel formula or incorrect percentage-change baseline

**Example error:** Calculating Lake B Year 4 as  
\[
\frac{50 \times 80}{16}=4000
\]
instead of 250, or calculating percentage decline using the Year 4 population as the denominator.

Correct calculation:
\[
N=\frac{50\times80}{16}=250
\]

Correct percentage change:
\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
\]

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Incorrect calculations undermine the analysis because the conclusion must be based on valid population estimates. Using the wrong baseline for percentage change also gives a misleading magnitude of population decline.

**How to avoid it:**  
Write the formula before substituting values, check whether multiplication occurs before division, and use the initial population, Year 1, as the denominator when calculating change from Year 1 to Year 4.

---

## 2. Conceptual error: confusing trophic relationships or top-down and bottom-up control

**Example error:** Stating that the cichlid is a primary consumer, or that the decline in rainbowfish is evidence of bottom-up control.

**Correct concept:**  
The speckled peacock cichlid is a predatory fish that feeds on small-bodied fish, including rainbowfish. It occupies a higher trophic level than the rainbowfish. Its effect would be a potential example of **top-down control**, where a predator reduces the abundance of prey.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Misidentifying the cichlid’s trophic role prevents accurate interpretation of the ecological mechanism proposed in the hypothesis. Bottom-up control would involve changes in resources such as nutrients, primary productivity or food availability, rather than increased predation.

**How to avoid it:**  
Identify what each organism eats. Since the cichlid consumes fish, it is a consumer at a higher trophic level than its prey. Link predation to top-down control and resource limitation to bottom-up control.

---

## 3. Overclaiming causation from field correlation

**Example error:** Writing, “The data prove that cichlids caused the rainbowfish decline.”

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Although the decline in Lake B occurred after the cichlid introduction and did not occur in Lake A, the study has only one treatment lake and one control lake. Other unmeasured factors could differ between lakes, such as habitat structure, food supply, disease, fishing pressure, other predators, recruitment, immigration or emigration. The measured abiotic variables were stable, but they do not represent every possible environmental factor. In addition, mark-recapture estimates depend on assumptions that may not be fully met.

**How to avoid it:**  
Use qualified scientific language, such as:

- “The data are consistent with the hypothesis.”
- “The results support a possible top-down effect of the introduced cichlid.”
- “The evidence partially supports the hypothesis, but does not prove causation.”
- “Further replicated treatment and control lakes, or experimental predator-exclusion studies, would strengthen causal inference.”
