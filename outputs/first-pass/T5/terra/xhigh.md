## Marking criteria sheet — 8 marks

### NESA command verbs applied
- **Analyse**: *identify components and the relationship between them; draw out and relate implications.*  
  In this task, students must break the data into population estimates, temporal trends, lake-to-lake comparisons, predator timing and abiotic patterns, then relate these to the hypothesis.
- **Evaluate**: *make a judgement based on criteria; determine the value of.*  
  In this task, students must make a justified judgement about the causal hypothesis using criteria such as temporal sequence, comparison with the control, exclusion of alternative explanations and limitations of the evidence.

### Expected evaluative judgement
The data **partially support** the hypothesis. Lake B declined by 75% after cichlid introduction, whereas Lake A increased by 25%. The introduction preceded the decline, and the reported dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH values were relatively stable and similar between lakes. However, the study cannot prove that cichlids caused the decline because there was only one treatment lake, only selected abiotic variables were measured, cichlid abundance/predation was not quantified and mark-recapture assumptions may have been violated.

### What distinguishes a high-band from a mid-band response

A **high-band response** does more than state that “Lake B declined after the cichlid was introduced.” It **analyses** by separating and linking the relevant components: the Year 1 baseline, post-introduction Lake B decline, Lake A control increase, and stability of the measured abiotic variables. It then **evaluates** by applying causal criteria and weighing the evidence. For example, it recognises that temporal sequence and the control comparison support the hypothesis, but limited replication and possible sampling bias prevent a definitive causal conclusion.

A **mid-band response** generally identifies the broad trend and may calculate most values, but is more descriptive. It may state that the predator “caused” the decline without clearly considering alternative explanations, or list limitations without explaining how they reduce confidence in the causal claim.

## Common-error diagnosis

### 1. Numerical error: incorrect use of the Schnabel formula
**Common error:** A student may multiply or divide the values in the wrong order, for example calculating Lake B Year 3 as \(70 \times 14 / 100\), rather than:

\[
N = \frac{M \times C}{R} = \frac{70 \times 100}{14} = 500
\]

They may also fail to show working, making an arithmetic error difficult to identify.

**Why it reduces quality:** Incorrect population estimates produce incorrect trends and undermine the conclusion about the predator’s impact.

**How to avoid it:** Write the formula first, substitute each value into the correct position and calculate in steps. A student should also check that a larger recapture number, all else equal, produces a lower estimate because more recaptures suggest a smaller population.

---

### 2. Conceptual error: confusing top-down and bottom-up control
**Common error:** A student may describe the cichlid’s effect as **bottom-up control**, or classify it as a producer or low-level consumer.

**Why it reduces quality:** The cichlid is a visual predator that eats small-bodied fish. It is therefore a higher-trophic-level consumer relative to the rainbowfish and could impose **top-down control** through predation. Bottom-up control would involve effects of resources at lower trophic levels, such as food availability, nutrients or primary productivity.

**How to avoid it:** Link the explanation directly to the feeding relationship: predator consuming prey = top-down pressure. Students should refer to the cichlid as a predatory, higher-trophic-level consumer rather than assigning an unsupported fixed trophic level.

---

### 3. Causation error: claiming the data prove that cichlids caused the decline
**Common error:** A student may write that the results “prove” the introduced cichlid caused the rainbowfish decline because Lake B decreased after introduction.

**Why it reduces quality:** The study provides a strong temporal association and a useful control comparison, but it is still field-based evidence from only one treatment lake and one control lake. Other unmeasured factors—such as habitat change, food availability, disease, other predators or differences in capture probability—could have contributed. The three measured abiotic variables do not exclude all alternatives.

**How to avoid it:** Use qualified scientific language such as “the data are consistent with,” “the data support,” or “the data partially support the hypothesis.” Explain both the supporting evidence and why the lack of independent treatment-lake replication and potential mark-recapture limitations prevent proof of causation.
