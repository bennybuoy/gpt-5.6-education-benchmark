# Marking criteria sheet: introduced cichlid and rainbowfish populations (8 marks)

## Glossary-aligned command-verb expectations

- **Analyse**: identify the components of the data and the relationships between them, then draw out implications.  
  In this task, students should separate and connect: mark-recapture estimates, population trends over time, timing of predator introduction, the control-lake comparison, abiotic data and predator ecology.

- **Evaluate**: make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement with evidence.  
  In this task, students should judge the causal hypothesis against criteria such as temporal sequence, comparison with the control, biological plausibility, alternative explanations, replication and reliability of the mark-recapture estimates.

- **Justify**: support the conclusion using relevant numerical and scientific evidence.

### Percentage change from Year 1 to Year 4

\[
\text{Percentage change}=\frac{\text{Year 4 population}-\text{Year 1 population}}{\text{Year 1 population}}\times 100
\]

- **Lake A**:

\[
\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%
\]

Lake A increased by **25%**.

- **Lake B**:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
\]

Lake B decreased by **75%**.

## Indicative scientific interpretation

A strong response should establish that:

- Both lakes had the same estimated rainbowfish population in Year 1: **1000 fish**.
- After cichlids were introduced at the end of Year 1, Lake B declined from **1000 to 250 fish**, a **75% decrease**.
- Over the same period, the control lake increased from **1000 to 1250 fish**, a **25% increase**.
- The divergent trends are temporally consistent with a cichlid effect: Lake B declined after introduction, whereas Lake A did not.
- The cichlid is a predatory fish that feeds on small-bodied fish, making predation a biologically plausible top-down explanation.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH were relatively stable and similar between lakes. For example, DO remained approximately 7.8–8.1 mg/L, temperature approximately 22.4–22.7 °C, and pH 7.2–7.3. This weakens, but does not eliminate, an abiotic explanation.

Relevant limitations include:

1. **Only one treatment lake and one control lake were studied.** Lake-specific factors, rather than cichlids alone, could explain the difference. There is no replication of the predator treatment.

2. **Mark-recapture assumptions may not have been met.** Births, deaths, immigration, emigration, unequal catchability, mark loss or effects of handling may bias estimates. These assumptions need apply within each sampling window; annual population change itself is not automatically a violation.

3. **Abiotic monitoring was limited.** Only three variables were measured annually at three littoral sites. Other factors, such as food availability, vegetation, habitat change, disease, fishing pressure or contaminants, were not assessed.

4. **No direct evidence of cichlid predation was collected.** The study did not report cichlid abundance, rainbowfish in cichlid diets or direct observations of predation.

### Expected overall judgement

The evidence **partially supports** the hypothesis. The timing of the decline, control comparison, stable measured abiotic conditions and predator ecology are consistent with cichlids contributing to the decline. However, the lack of replicate treatment lakes, possible mark-recapture bias and unmeasured environmental factors mean the data do not prove that cichlids were the sole cause.

## Banded performance descriptors

| Mark range | Performance descriptor |
|---|---|
| **7–8** | Accurately calculates all, or almost all, population estimates and both percentage changes. **Analyses** the data by breaking it into relevant components—population trends, predator introduction timing, control comparison, abiotic conditions and predator ecology—and clearly identifies relationships among them. **Evaluates** the causal hypothesis using explicit or clearly implied criteria, such as temporal sequence, biological plausibility, comparison with the control and exclusion of alternatives. Discusses at least two specific limitations and explains how they weaken causal certainty. Reaches and justifies a well-qualified judgement, most appropriately that the data **partially support** the hypothesis. |
| **5–6** | Calculates most estimates and percentage changes correctly, with only minor errors or omissions. Identifies the main pattern that Lake B declined after cichlid introduction while Lake A increased, and refers to stable abiotic conditions and/or predator ecology. Makes a reasonable, evidence-based conclusion and identifies at least two limitations, although the limitations may be briefly explained or not fully integrated into the final judgement. Analysis is sound but less comprehensive or less connected than in the high band. |
| **3–4** | Shows some correct calculations or identifies a general population decline in Lake B. Describes some relevant data, such as Lake B decreasing and Lake A increasing, but gives limited analysis of the relationship between the control, timing of introduction and abiotic variables. Provides a simple conclusion with limited justification. May state one relevant limitation, or list limitations without explaining their significance. |
| **1–2** | Provides limited, inaccurate or incomplete calculations and/or makes unsupported statements about the hypothesis. May identify that Lake B declined but does not effectively compare it with Lake A or link the trend to the predator introduction. Demonstrates little analysis or evaluation, with absent, irrelevant or incorrect limitations. |
| **0** | No relevant response or no assessable evidence. |

## Distinguishing a high-band response from a mid-band response

| Command verb | High band: 7–8 | Mid band: 5–6 |
|---|---|---|
| **Analyse** | Breaks the evidence into components and connects them. For example: the post-introduction decline in Lake B is compared quantitatively with the increase in Lake A; stable measured abiotic conditions are related to alternative explanations; predator diet is related to biological plausibility. | Often describes the main trend, such as “Lake B decreased and Lake A increased,” but does not fully explain the relationship between the population data, control lake, abiotic results and predator ecology. |
| **Evaluate** | Makes a judgement against causal criteria and weighs supporting and limiting evidence. The response explains why the data are consistent with cichlid predation but why they cannot establish causation conclusively. | Gives a generally reasonable conclusion, often supported by selected evidence, but may treat limitations as a separate list rather than using them to qualify the judgement. |

A response that merely states “the cichlids caused the decline because Lake B decreased” is descriptive and does not fully satisfy either **analyse** or **evaluate**.

# Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces response quality | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Calculating percentage change from the wrong baseline.** For example, using Year 4 as the denominator for Lake B: \((250-1000) \div 250 \times 100=-300\%\). | The question asks for change from Year 1 to Year 4, so Year 1 must be the baseline. Using Year 4 exaggerates the decline and gives an invalid interpretation of the data. | Use: \(\frac{\text{final} - \text{initial}}{\text{initial}} \times 100\). For Lake B: \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\). |
| **2. Confusing top-down and bottom-up control, or misidentifying the cichlid’s role.** For example, calling the cichlid a bottom-up factor or a producer-level organism. | The cichlid is a predatory consumer that may reduce rainbowfish through **top-down** control. Misidentifying its role leads to an incorrect explanation of the proposed mechanism. | State the relationship clearly: cichlid predation may increase rainbowfish mortality, causing a top-down effect. Avoid assigning an exact numbered trophic level unless the full food web is known. |
| **3. Claiming that the data “prove” cichlids caused the decline.** | The pattern is consistent with causation, but there was only one treatment lake, limited abiotic monitoring, no direct diet or predation evidence and possible mark-recapture bias. Other lake-specific factors cannot be fully excluded. | Use qualified language such as “the data partially support,” “are consistent with,” or “suggest that cichlids contributed to the decline.” Explain what additional evidence, such as replicated lakes or diet data, would strengthen causal inference. |
