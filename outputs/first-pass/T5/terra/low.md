## Marking criteria sheet — 8-mark extended response

### Expected calculations and key evidence

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Percentage change, Year 1 to Year 4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A (control)** | \(100 \times 120 \div 12 = 1000\) | \(110 \times 130 \div 13 = 1100\) | \(120 \times 140 \div 14 = 1200\) | \(125 \times 150 \div 15 = 1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100 = +25\%\) |
| **Lake B (cichlid introduced)** | \(100 \times 120 \div 12 = 1000\) | \(90 \times 110 \div 11 = 900\) | \(70 \times 100 \div 14 = 500\) | \(50 \times 80 \div 16 = 250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100 = -75\%\) |

A well-supported conclusion is that the data **partially support** the hypothesis. Lake B declined by 75% after cichlid introduction while Lake A increased by 25%, and measured abiotic variables were relatively stable and similar between lakes. However, the field data do not prove that cichlids caused the decline because there was only one treatment lake, other variables were not controlled or measured, and mark-recapture estimates depend on assumptions that may not have been met.

### Performance descriptors

| Mark range | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **7–8** | **Thoroughly analyses and evaluates** the data. Correctly calculates all or almost all population estimates and percentage changes, and clearly compares trends between Lake A and Lake B. Breaks the data into relevant components, including population trends before and after introduction, the control-versus-treatment comparison, and abiotic measurements, and identifies relationships between these components. Makes a well-justified judgement that the hypothesis is **partially supported** (or another defensible qualified conclusion), using numerical evidence. Explains at least two relevant limitations, such as lack of replicate treatment lakes, possible unmeasured confounding variables, and limitations/assumptions of Schnabel mark-recapture. |
| **5–6** | **Analyses and evaluates** the data with generally correct calculations and a clear comparison of the lakes. Identifies that Lake B declines after predator introduction while Lake A increases or remains comparatively stable, and refers to broadly stable abiotic data. Makes a reasoned judgement, usually that the hypothesis is supported but cannot be proven, with some supporting evidence. Identifies at least two limitations, although explanation or linkage to the conclusion may be limited. May contain a minor calculation, interpretation, or detail omission. |
| **3–4** | Provides a **basic analysis** of some data. Calculates some population estimates and/or percentage changes correctly, and identifies a general decline in Lake B. May mention the control lake or abiotic variables but does not effectively compare relationships among the data. Gives a conclusion with limited justification, such as stating that cichlids “may have” caused the decline without adequately weighing evidence and limitations. Identifies one limitation, or lists limitations without explaining their significance. |
| **1–2** | Provides a limited response. May state that Lake B declined or attempt one calculation, but shows little accurate use of the mark-recapture data. Makes little or no comparison with Lake A, does not meaningfully address abiotic factors or study limitations, and provides an unsupported conclusion. May overstate that the data prove causation. |
| **0** | Does not meet the criteria described above. |

### Distinguishing high-band from mid-band responses: command verbs

In line with the NSW NESA glossary:

- **Analyse** requires students to break information into components and identify relationships.  
  - A **high-band** response separates the evidence into population estimates, percentage changes, timing of cichlid introduction, control-lake comparison and abiotic data. It then identifies the relationship: Lake B declined markedly after introduction, whereas Lake A increased despite similar dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH.
  - A **mid-band** response generally identifies the decline in Lake B and may compare it with Lake A, but gives less detailed or less integrated analysis of the relationships among all data sets.

- **Evaluate** requires students to make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement with evidence.  
  - A **high-band** response judges the strength of the causal claim using criteria such as the temporal pattern, control comparison, consistency with predator–prey ecology, stability of measured abiotic variables, replication, alternative explanations and methodological assumptions. It reaches a qualified conclusion, such as **partially supported rather than proven**.
  - A **mid-band** response gives a broadly reasonable conclusion and some evidence, but may not clearly weigh the strengths against the limitations of the evidence.

## Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces response quality | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Incorrect calculation of population estimates or percentage change** — for example, calculating Lake B Year 4 as \(50 \times 80 \div 16 = 4000\), rather than 250; or calculating percentage decline using Year 4 rather than Year 1 as the baseline. | This produces inaccurate conclusions about the magnitude of population change. The required percentage change from Year 1 to Year 4 must use the Year 1 population as the original baseline. | Write the formula first: \(N = \frac{M \times C}{R}\). Then use: \(\frac{\text{final} - \text{initial}}{\text{initial}}\times100\). For Lake B: \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100 = -75\%\). |
| **2. Confusing trophic relationships or top-down and bottom-up control** — for example, stating that the cichlid is a primary consumer, or that the decline is due to bottom-up control by the predator. | The cichlid is a predatory fish that eats small-bodied fish, including rainbowfish; it is therefore at a higher trophic level than its prey. Predation by a higher trophic level is an example of potential **top-down control**. Bottom-up control would involve resource availability at lower trophic levels, such as nutrients, primary producers or food supply. | Identify the feeding relationship before explaining the trend: cichlid → consumes rainbowfish. Use “top-down predation pressure” for predator effects, while recognising that the study does not directly measure predation rates. |
| **3. Overclaiming causation from the field data** — for example, writing that the cichlid data “prove” or “definitely caused” the rainbowfish decline. | Although the timing, control lake and stable measured abiotic variables are consistent with a predator effect, this is not a fully replicated controlled experiment. There is only one treatment lake, other biotic or abiotic factors may not have been measured, and mark-recapture assumptions may have been violated. Therefore, correlation and temporal association alone cannot establish certainty. | Use qualified scientific language: “the data support,” “are consistent with,” or “partially support the hypothesis.” Explain both the supporting evidence and alternative explanations or methodological limitations. |
