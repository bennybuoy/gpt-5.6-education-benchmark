## Marking criteria sheet (8 marks)

### Expected calculations
Using \(N=\frac{M\times C}{R}\):

| Year | Lake A estimate | Lake B estimate |
|---|---:|---:|
| 1 | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) |
| 2 | \(\frac{110\times130}{13}=1100\) | \(\frac{90\times110}{11}=900\) |
| 3 | \(\frac{120\times140}{14}=1200\) | \(\frac{70\times100}{14}=500\) |
| 4 | \(\frac{125\times150}{15}=1250\) | \(\frac{50\times80}{16}=250\) |

Percentage change from Year 1 to Year 4:

- **Lake A:** \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%\)
- **Lake B:** \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\)

Abiotic conditions changed only slightly and similarly in both lakes, so they provide some support for a predator-related explanation. However, the study has limitations, including only one control and one predator lake, possible violations of mark-recapture assumptions, and the absence of direct measurements of predator abundance or predation rates.

### High-band versus mid-band distinction

A high-band response does more than state that Lake B declined. It **analyses** the data by separating and comparing the lakes, years, calculated population sizes and abiotic measurements, then identifying the relationships among these components. It **evaluates** the hypothesis by making a qualified judgement—such as “the data support, but do not conclusively establish, the hypothesis”—and justifying that judgement with the 75% decline in Lake B, the 25% increase in Lake A, the timing of introduction and the relatively stable abiotic conditions, while considering study limitations.

A mid-band response generally identifies the main trend and may calculate most values, but provides less developed analysis and evaluation. It may describe the decline without fully examining relationships, or claim support for the hypothesis without sufficiently discussing alternative explanations, assumptions or the limitations of having only one lake in each treatment.

## Common-error diagnosis

### 1. Numerical error: incorrect formula or percentage baseline

A student may calculate Lake B Year 3 as \(70\times100/14=500\) incorrectly, omit division by \(R\), or calculate percentage change using Year 4 as the denominator. The correct percentage change is relative to **Year 1**:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%.
\]

This reduces quality because inaccurate calculations undermine the evidence used to evaluate the hypothesis. Students should substitute each value carefully into the stated formula, show working, and use Year 1 as the baseline for percentage change.

### 2. Conceptual error: misidentifying the ecological relationship

A student may describe the cichlid as a bottom-up factor or as a primary producer, rather than recognising it as a **predator** and higher trophic-level consumer exerting potential **top-down control** on small-bodied fish.

This reduces quality because the biological mechanism is misunderstood. The cichlid feeds directly on rainbowfish in the littoral zone, so a plausible mechanism is increased predation causing reduced rainbowfish abundance. Students should identify the cichlid’s trophic role and link it to the observed population trend without assuming that every decline must be caused by predation.

### 3. Overclaiming causation from correlational field data

A student may state that the results “prove” the cichlid caused the decline. The decline occurred after introduction and is consistent with predation, but the study has only one predator lake and one control lake, no replication, and possible uncertainty in the mark-recapture estimates and assumptions.

This reduces quality because temporal association and comparison with one control do not eliminate all alternative explanations. Students should use qualified language such as “supports” or “is consistent with,” discuss the similar abiotic conditions, and acknowledge limitations including lack of replicate lakes, possible movement or demographic changes, unequal catchability, mark loss, or effects of marking.
