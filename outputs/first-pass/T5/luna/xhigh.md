### Percentage change from Year 1 to Year 4

\[
\text{Percentage change}=\frac{\text{Year 4}-\text{Year 1}}{\text{Year 1}}\times100
\]

**Lake A:**

\[
\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=25\%
\]

Lake A increased by **25%**.

**Lake B:**

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
\]

Lake B decreased by **75%**.

### Analysis of the patterns

Both lakes had an estimated population of 1000 fish in Year 1. Because the cichlid was introduced at the end of Year 1, this provides a useful baseline before the predator could have had a substantial effect.

After the introduction, Lake B showed a progressive decline:

- 1000 fish in Year 1
- 900 fish in Year 2
- 500 fish in Year 3
- 250 fish in Year 4

In contrast, the control lake increased from 1000 to 1250 fish over the same period. Thus, the two lakes showed contrasting population trends: Lake B declined by 75%, whereas Lake A increased by 25%.

The abiotic measurements were relatively stable and similar between the lakes. Temperatures remained approximately \(22.4-22.7^\circ\text{C}\), pH remained between 7.2 and 7.3, and dissolved oxygen changed only slightly. Therefore, the recorded abiotic variables do not provide an obvious explanation for the large decline in Lake B. The decline is also ecologically plausible because the cichlid is a visual predator that feeds on small-bodied fish in the littoral zone, where it could consume eastern rainbowfish.

### Limitations

1. **Lack of replication:** There was only one predator lake and one control lake. Any unmeasured difference between the lakes could be responsible for some or all of the decline. The study therefore cannot clearly separate the effect of the cichlid from lake-specific factors.

2. **Mark-recapture assumptions may not be met:** The method assumes a closed population and equal catchability of marked and unmarked fish. Rainbowfish may have moved, died, recruited into the population, or changed their behaviour and habitat use in response to the predator. These factors could bias the estimates, rather than reflecting true changes in population size.

3. **Limited abiotic sampling:** Measurements were taken at only three littoral sites annually and included only three variables. Other factors, such as food availability, habitat change, disease, fishing, pollution or other predators, were not assessed.

4. **Short pre-introduction record:** There was only one baseline year before the introduction. More pre-introduction years would be needed to establish whether the Lake B population was already declining.

### Judgement

The data **partially support** the hypothesis that the introduced speckled peacock cichlid caused the decline. The timing of the decline, the contrasting increase in the predator-free control lake, the stability of the measured abiotic variables and the known predatory behaviour of the cichlid all support a predator-related effect. However, the absence of replicate treatment lakes, the short baseline and possible violations of mark-recapture assumptions mean that the data demonstrate a strong association rather than proving that the cichlid was the sole cause.

### High-band versus mid-band distinction

A **high-band** response does more than report that Lake B declined: it breaks the data into population trends, control comparison, timing and abiotic measurements, then identifies how these components are related. It also **evaluates** the evidence against criteria such as temporal association, control comparison and study reliability, leading to a justified and qualified judgement.

A **mid-band** response generally reports the main trends and may state that the cichlid is associated with the decline, but it makes fewer explicit connections between the data components and gives a less developed judgement, with limited discussion of why the study design affects the strength of the conclusion.

## 3. Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces the quality of the response | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **Numerical error:** Applying the mark-recapture formula incorrectly, such as adding rather than multiplying \(M\) and \(C\), dividing by the wrong value, or calculating percentage change using Year 4 as the baseline. For example, Lake B Year 4 should be \((50\times80)/16=250\), and the percentage change must be calculated relative to Year 1: \((250-1000)/1000\times100=-75\%\). | Incorrect estimates undermine the analysis and can lead to an incorrect conclusion about the size or direction of the population change. | Substitute the values into the formula step by step and use Year 1 as the denominator for the requested change: \((\text{Year 4}-\text{Year 1})/\text{Year 1}\times100\). State whether the result is an increase or decrease. |
| **Conceptual error:** Describing the cichlid as a bottom-up factor, producer or herbivore, or failing to recognise its predator-prey relationship. Because it eats small fish, the cichlid represents potential **top-down control** of the eastern rainbowfish population. | This misidentifies the ecological mechanism and weakens the explanation of why the predator could reduce rainbowfish abundance. | Trace the direction of the interaction: introduced fish predator \(\rightarrow\) increased predation on native fish \(\rightarrow\) possible decline in rainbowfish. Distinguish this from bottom-up effects, where resources or abiotic conditions affect organisms higher in the food web. |
| **Causal overclaim:** Stating that the results “prove” the cichlid caused the decline, while ignoring that there is only one predator lake and one control lake and that the study is observational. | The pattern is consistent with predation, but alternative lake-specific factors, sampling bias and unmeasured environmental changes have not been excluded. Overclaiming shows that the student has not evaluated the strength of the evidence or the study design. | Use qualified language such as “the data support” or “are consistent with” a predator effect. Mention the contrasting control trend and stable measured abiotic variables as evidence, but also explain the lack of replicate treatment lakes, the short baseline and possible violations of mark-recapture assumptions. |
