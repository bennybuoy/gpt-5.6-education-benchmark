# Marking criteria sheet — 8 marks

## Command-verb expectations

Using the NSW NESA glossary:

- **Calculate**: determine a number or amount using mathematical methods.
- **Analyse**: identify the components of the data and the relationships between them, and draw out their implications.
- **Evaluate**: make a judgement based on criteria and determine the significance or value of the evidence.
- **Justify**: support a conclusion with relevant evidence and reasoning.

A high-quality response must therefore do more than describe that Lake B declined. It must **break the data into population estimates, percentage changes, lake comparisons, time trends and abiotic measurements**, then **weigh the evidence and limitations before making a justified judgement about causation**.

## Indicative calculations and evidence

Using \(N = \frac{M \times C}{R}\):

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Percentage change, Year 1–Year 4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Lake A | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{110\times130}{13}=1100\) | \(\frac{120\times140}{14}=1200\) | \(\frac{125\times150}{15}=1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%\) |
| Lake B | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{90\times110}{11}=900\) | \(\frac{70\times100}{14}=500\) | \(\frac{50\times80}{16}=250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\) |

Relevant analytical points include:

- Lake A increased steadily from 1000 to 1250 fish, a **25% increase**.
- Lake B decreased from 1000 to 250 fish, a **75% decrease**.
- The decline in Lake B occurred after the cichlid was introduced at the end of Year 1.
- Lake A, which had no introduced predator, showed the opposite trend.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH were broadly similar and changed only slightly in both lakes, so the measured abiotic variables do not provide an obvious alternative explanation.
- The cichlid is a visual pursuit predator that feeds on small fish in the littoral zone, providing a plausible **top-down predation mechanism**.
- The evidence is best judged as **partially supporting the hypothesis**: the pattern is consistent with predation causing the decline, but the study does not prove causation.

Relevant limitations include:

- There was only one predator lake and one control lake, so there was no replication at the lake level. Differences between the lakes or other unmeasured factors could have contributed to the decline.
- The cichlid was introduced illegally rather than assigned randomly, making this an observational field comparison.
- Other biotic factors, such as food availability, disease, competition, habitat change or other predators, were not fully measured.
- Mark–recapture estimates depend on assumptions such as a closed population, equal catchability, marks not being lost and marking not affecting behaviour or survival. These assumptions may not be fully met.
- Only three abiotic variables were measured annually, so short-term or unmeasured environmental changes could have been missed.
- No uncertainty measures or confidence intervals are provided for the population estimates.

### Distinguishing high-band from mid-band responses

A **high-band** response does not simply state that “Lake B went down after the predator was introduced.” It:

1. separates and compares the population estimates for each lake;
2. identifies the different trends and their relationship to the timing of the introduction;
3. considers whether the abiotic data offer an alternative explanation;
4. uses criteria such as temporal order, the control comparison, biological plausibility and study limitations; and
5. makes a qualified judgement supported by evidence.

A **mid-band** response may correctly identify the decline and calculate some values, but is more descriptive. It may mention that the predator was introduced before the decline without fully relating the control-lake trend, abiotic measurements and study limitations to the strength of the causal conclusion. It may state a conclusion but provide only limited justification.

# Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces response quality | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Numerical error: incorrect formula or percentage baseline**. For example, treating \(M\) or \(C\) as the population estimate, or calculating Lake B’s change as \((250-1000)/250 \times 100\) rather than using Year 1 as the baseline. | Incorrect calculations undermine the evidence used in the analysis and can lead to an incorrect conclusion about the size of the decline. The required percentage change is calculated relative to Year 1: \(\frac{\text{Year 4} - \text{Year 1}}{\text{Year 1}}\times100\). | Substitute the values carefully into \(N=\frac{M\times C}{R}\). Check each result. Use Year 1 as the denominator for both percentage changes. Report Lake A as a **25% increase** and Lake B as a **75% decrease**. |
| **2. Conceptual error: misidentifying the trophic interaction**. For example, describing the cichlid as a producer, competitor or bottom-up factor, rather than as an introduced fish predator. | This shows misunderstanding of the mechanism. The cichlid consumes small-bodied fish and can exert **top-down control** on the eastern rainbowfish population. A mistaken trophic explanation weakens the analysis and evaluation. | Identify the cichlid as a higher-trophic-level visual predator. Explain that predation could reduce rainbowfish abundance directly, particularly because both predator and prey use the littoral zone. |
| **3. Overclaiming causation from correlational field data**. For example, stating that the results “prove” the cichlid caused the decline. | The timing and control comparison support the hypothesis, but the study has only one treatment lake and one control lake, no random assignment and incomplete measurement of possible confounding factors. Therefore, the data cannot establish causation with certainty. | Use qualified language such as “the results support” or “are consistent with” the hypothesis. State that the hypothesis is **partially supported**, then justify this with the contrasting lake trends, similar measured abiotic conditions and plausible predation mechanism, while acknowledging the lack of replication and other limitations. |
