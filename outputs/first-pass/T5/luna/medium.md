## Marking criteria sheet — 8 marks

### Expected calculations and evidence

Using \(N = \frac{M \times C}{R}\):

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Percentage change, Year 1–Year 4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Lake A | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{110\times130}{13}=1100\) | \(\frac{120\times140}{14}=1200\) | \(\frac{125\times150}{15}=1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%\) |
| Lake B | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{90\times110}{11}=900\) | \(\frac{70\times100}{14}=500\) | \(\frac{50\times80}{16}=250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\) |

Relevant interpretation:

- Lake A increased by 25%, whereas Lake B declined by 75%.
- The Lake B decline occurred after the cichlid was introduced at the end of Year 1.
- Abiotic variables were similar and showed only minor annual variation between lakes, so they provide little evidence that dissolved oxygen, temperature or pH caused the contrasting trends.
- The pattern is consistent with top-down predation: the introduced cichlid is a predator that consumes small-bodied littoral fish.
- The data support, but do not prove, the hypothesis. A cautious judgement is that the hypothesis is **partially supported**, because the temporal pattern and control-lake comparison are consistent with predation, but there is only one treatment lake and the study is observational.

Relevant limitations may include:

- only one predator-introduction lake and one control lake, so lake-level effects cannot be separated from the predator effect;
- no random assignment or replicated treatment lakes;
- possible confounding variables not measured, such as food availability, habitat change, disease, fishing or other predators;
- Schnabel assumptions may not be met, including population closure, equal catchability, mark retention and no behavioural effects of marking;
- annual estimates may not represent a closed population during the sampling period if fish move, die or recruit.

### Distinction between high- and mid-band responses

A **high-band** response does more than list values or state that Lake B declined. In the NESA glossary sense, it **analyses** by breaking the dataset into components—years, lakes, population estimates, percentage changes, predator timing and abiotic measurements—and identifying their relationships. It also **evaluates** by making a justified judgement against explicit criteria, such as whether the decline follows the introduction, whether it differs from the control, and whether alternative explanations have been excluded.

A **mid-band** response generally identifies the main trend and may calculate most values, but it is more descriptive. It may say that the predator “probably caused” the decline without fully weighing the control comparison, stable abiotic conditions, study limitations and the difference between evidence consistent with causation and proof of causation.

## Common-error diagnosis

### 1. Numerical error: incorrect formula or percentage baseline

A student may calculate Lake B Year 4 as \(50 \times 80 \times 16\), divide by the wrong quantity, or calculate the percentage change using Year 4 as the denominator rather than Year 1. The correct calculation is:

\[
N=\frac{50\times80}{16}=250
\]

and

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%.
\]

This reduces the quality of the response because an incorrect population estimate can reverse or distort the apparent strength of the trend. Students should write the formula for every year, check that \(R\) is the denominator, and use Year 1 as the baseline for percentage change.

### 2. Conceptual error: misunderstanding trophic interactions

A student may describe the cichlid as a competitor or as a bottom-up influence, rather than recognising it as a visual predator that eats small-bodied fish. The cichlid represents a **top-down** pressure on the eastern rainbowfish population.

This reduces the quality of the analysis because it misinterprets the proposed mechanism and weakens the explanation of why the rainbowfish declined. Students should identify the cichlid’s ecological role from the information provided: it is an introduced consumer at a higher trophic level, and the rainbowfish is its prey.

### 3. Overclaiming causation from correlational field data

A student may state that the results “prove” the cichlid caused the decline, ignoring that there is only one predator lake, no replicated treatment lakes and no random introduction. Other unmeasured factors could have contributed.

This reduces the quality of the evaluation because a temporal association is not conclusive evidence of causation. The data show that the decline followed the introduction and differed from the control-lake trend, which supports the hypothesis, but they do not eliminate all alternative explanations. Students should use qualified language such as “the data support” or “are consistent with,” discuss the missing replication and confounders, and justify a conclusion of **partial support rather than proof**.
