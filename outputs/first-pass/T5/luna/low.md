# Marking criteria sheet — 8 marks

## Expected calculations

Using \(N = \frac{M \times C}{R}\):

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Percentage change, Year 1–4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Lake A | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{110\times130}{13}=1100\) | \(\frac{120\times140}{14}=1200\) | \(\frac{125\times150}{15}=1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%\) |
| Lake B | \(\frac{100\times120}{12}=1000\) | \(\frac{90\times110}{11}=900\) | \(\frac{70\times100}{14}=500\) | \(\frac{50\times80}{16}=250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\) |

A strong response should recognise that Lake A increased by 25%, whereas Lake B declined by 75%, with the decline occurring after the cichlid introduction. Abiotic conditions were broadly similar and showed only minor annual variation.

## Performance bands

### 7–8 marks — Extensive and well-supported response

The response:

- accurately calculates, or correctly reports, population estimates for both lakes across all four years;
- correctly calculates the percentage change for each lake, including the correct Year 1 baseline;
- **analyses** the data by breaking the results into relevant components, such as changes over time, differences between lakes, the timing of predator introduction and abiotic measurements, and identifies relationships among these components;
- **evaluates** the hypothesis by making a clear, qualified judgement based on criteria such as temporal sequence, comparison with the control lake, consistency of abiotic conditions and strength of experimental evidence;
- explains that the results support, but do not conclusively prove, the hypothesis that predation contributed to the Lake B decline;
- discusses at least two relevant limitations, such as lack of replicate predator lakes, assumptions of mark-recapture, possible unmeasured environmental variables, or lack of direct measurement of cichlid abundance;
- uses numerical evidence appropriately, for example, Lake B declined from 1000 to 250 fish, while Lake A increased from 1000 to 1250 fish.

**High-band distinction:** The response goes beyond describing the trend. In the sense of the NESA glossary, it **analyses** by separating the data into components and identifying relationships, then **evaluates** by weighing the evidence and limitations before justifying a conclusion. It does not claim that the predator’s effect has been proven beyond doubt.

### 5–6 marks — Sound response

The response:

- gives mostly correct population estimates and percentage changes, with only minor calculation or presentation errors;
- identifies the contrasting trends in the two lakes and links the Lake B decline to the timing of predator introduction;
- makes a generally appropriate judgement that the data support or partially support the hypothesis;
- refers to the relatively stable abiotic measurements;
- discusses at least two limitations, although explanations may be brief or general;
- demonstrates some **analysis** by comparing years or lakes and identifying a relationship, but may not fully break down all relevant components;
- demonstrates some **evaluation** by giving a conclusion, although the judgement may not be fully weighed against alternative explanations or limitations.

### 3–4 marks — Basic response

The response:

- calculates some population estimates correctly but contains substantial numerical errors or omissions;
- identifies that Lake B declined and Lake A increased, but provides limited comparison or trend analysis;
- may state that the predator caused the decline without adequately considering the control lake, abiotic data or other explanations;
- provides one relevant limitation, or lists limitations without explaining their significance;
- mainly describes the data rather than analysing relationships between variables;
- gives a conclusion, but the evaluation is weak, unsupported or insufficiently qualified.

At this level, **analyse** is usually shown as simple description of results rather than breaking the data into components. **Evaluate** is usually a conclusion without a well-supported judgement based on evidence and criteria.

### 1–2 marks — Limited response

The response:

- provides few correct calculations or an incorrect application of the formula;
- gives a vague or inaccurate description of the population trends;
- makes little or no connection between the predator introduction and the Lake B data;
- provides little relevant discussion of limitations;
- includes an unsupported conclusion or no conclusion.

### 0 marks

No relevant response, or the response does not address the task.

## Suggested allocation within the 8 marks

- Population estimates for both lakes: **2 marks**
- Percentage changes: **1 mark**
- Analysis of temporal, lake-to-lake and abiotic patterns: **2 marks**
- Evaluation and justified conclusion: **2 marks**
- Limitations and recognition of causal uncertainty: **1 mark**

# Common-error diagnosis

## 1. Numerical error: incorrect use of the mark-recapture formula or percentage baseline

A student may calculate Lake B Year 4 as \(50 \times 80 \times 16\), divide by the wrong value, or calculate the percentage change using Year 4 as the denominator. The correct calculation is:

\[
N=\frac{50\times80}{16}=250
\]

and the percentage change is:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%.
\]

The Year 1 estimate must be the baseline. This error reduces the quality of the response because incorrect values can reverse or exaggerate the apparent population trend and weaken the evidence-based conclusion. Students can avoid it by writing the formula for every year, checking that \(R\) is the denominator, and using:

\[
\frac{\text{final value}-\text{initial value}}{\text{initial value}}\times100.
\]

## 2. Conceptual error: misidentifying the ecological relationship

A student may describe the cichlid as a competitor, herbivore or bottom-up environmental factor rather than a predatory fish. The cichlid is a visual pursuit predator that feeds on small fish, so its likely effect is **top-down control** of the eastern rainbowfish population through direct predation.

This error reduces the quality of the response because it shows misunderstanding of the mechanism being tested and prevents the student from linking the predator’s trophic role to the observed decline. Students can avoid it by identifying the cichlid’s feeding behaviour and trophic level before explaining the population pattern.

## 3. Overclaiming causation from correlational field data

A student may write that the results “prove” the cichlid caused the decline. Although Lake B declined by 75% after introduction while the control Lake A increased by 25%, the study has only one predator lake and one control lake. Other unmeasured factors, sampling issues or violations of mark-recapture assumptions could have contributed.

This error reduces the quality of the response because a temporal association and control comparison provide support for causation but do not eliminate all alternative explanations. Students should use a qualified judgement such as: *the data support, or strongly suggest, that the cichlid contributed to the decline, but do not conclusively prove that it was the sole cause*. They should also mention limitations such as the absence of replicate lakes, possible unequal catchability, mark loss, movement, births or deaths during sampling, and the lack of direct cichlid-abundance data.
