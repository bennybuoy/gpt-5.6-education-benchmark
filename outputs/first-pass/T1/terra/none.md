## Year 8 Science Unit Plan: Electricity Models and Circuits

### 1. Lesson-by-lesson sequence

| Lesson | Scheduled day/period | Content statement(s) | Type | Brief activity description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Monday P2 | C1 | Theory | Introduce models as simplified representations through teacher examples and students identify what a simple circuit diagram includes and omits. |
| 2 | Tuesday P4 | C2 | Theory | Students classify examples of energy transfer as heat, light, motion or electric current and trace transfers in familiar electrical devices. |
| 3 | Wednesday P1 | C3 | Practical | In six bench groups, students build and troubleshoot simple circuits to determine that a continuous path, source and load are required. |
| 4 | Thursday P3 | C3, C4 | Formative | Students complete a 15-minute circuit-reading and current-checkpoint task, receive marked feedback, then practise describing current as flow of charge measured in amperes. |
| 5 | Monday P2 | C5 | Theory | Use diagrams and worked examples to distinguish voltage from current and identify voltage as energy transferred per unit charge, measured in volts. |
| 6 | Tuesday P4 | C6 | Theory | Students investigate resistance conceptually using conductor comparisons and explain how material, length, thickness and temperature affect resistance. |
| 7 | Wednesday P1 | C6 | Formative | Students complete a 15-minute resistance-explanation checkpoint, receive same-lesson feedback, then correct and improve one response. |
| 8 | Thursday P3 | C7, C8 | Practical | Groups construct series and parallel circuits, measure voltage and current where available, and use simple measurements to model relationships using V = IR. |
| 9 | Monday P2 | C8, C9 | Theory | Students use V = IR in simple resistive circuit examples and identify assumptions and limits of circuit models. |
| 10 | Tuesday P4 | C7, C8, C9 | Summative | Students complete a maximum 30-minute task comparing circuit types, applying V = IR, and explaining a limitation of a circuit model. |

### 2. Prerequisite chain diagram

```text
C1 Models simplify systems to explain or predict behaviour
  ↓
C2 Energy can be transferred between systems
  ↓
C3 A circuit requires a continuous path, source and load
  ↓
C4 Current is flow of charge, measured in amperes
  ↓
C5 Voltage is energy transferred per unit charge, measured in volts
  ↓
C6 Resistance opposes current and depends on material, length, thickness and temperature
  ↓
C7 Series circuits have one path; parallel circuits have multiple paths
  ↓
C8 Energy transfer can be modelled quantitatively using V = IR
  ↓
C9 Models have limits when assumptions break down
```

### 3. Practical placement justification

#### Practical 1: Lesson 3 — Wednesday P1, C3
**Why placed here:** This practical follows C1 and C2 so students can apply the idea that a circuit is a model of an energy-transfer system. It directly supports C3 before the first formative checkpoint in Lesson 4 and establishes the prerequisite for C4.

**Equipment and grouping:** The class is organised into six groups of four or five students at the six fixed bench stations. Each group uses one basic-glassware-equivalent equipment set only if required for components storage; the circuit activity relies on available circuit components and does not exceed the six power supplies. Safety goggles are available as a class set if required by the school procedure.

**Usable bench time:** Equipment is pre-set at each bench before the lesson. A 5-minute safety and task briefing is followed by 40 minutes of circuit construction, testing and troubleshooting, with 5 minutes for pack-up. This provides the required minimum of 40 minutes usable bench time.

#### Practical 2: Lesson 8 — Thursday P3, C7 and C8
**Why placed here:** This practical occurs after students have learned current, voltage and resistance, allowing them to compare series and parallel paths and apply simple V = IR reasoning. It provides evidence and experience needed before C9 and the final summative task.

**Equipment and grouping:** Six groups use the six fixed benches and up to six power supplies, one per group. Four digital multimeters are rotated between groups using measurement roles and a timed station sequence; groups without a multimeter complete circuit construction, prediction and observation while waiting. The task does not require more than eight basic glassware sets, 12 hand lenses or 30 safety goggles.

**Usable bench time:** Circuit kits and instruction cards are prepared before class. After a 5-minute briefing, students have 40 minutes for constructing series and parallel circuits, collecting measurements and completing V = IR calculations, followed by a 5-minute pack-up. Thursday P3 avoids Friday P5 early-dismissal risk and does not rely on technician support after 1:00 pm.

### 5. Deferrals and risks

#### Deferrals if a practical is unavailable
- If **Lesson 3 practical** is unavailable, the hands-on construction component is deferred to the next available Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday or Thursday period. C4 should be delayed until students have completed either the practical or a teacher-led circuit demonstration using the same C3 requirements.
- If **Lesson 8 practical** is unavailable, C7 may be introduced through diagrams, but the quantitative application of C8 and the Lesson 10 summative task should be deferred until students have completed a practical comparison of series and parallel circuits.
- If both practicals cannot occur in the planned week sequence, retain theory lessons only through C6 and reschedule C7–C9 and the summative task after practical access is restored.

#### Timetable conflicts
- No practical is scheduled on Friday P5 because early dismissal could reduce usable bench time.
- Practical lessons are scheduled on Wednesday P1 and Thursday P3, meeting the limit of no more than two practical lessons per week.
- Thursday P3 practical preparation must be completed before the lesson because there is no dedicated technician after 1:00 pm.

#### Named risks and mitigation
1. **Early dismissal risk:** Friday P5 is excluded from practical scheduling.  
2. **Equipment shortage risk:** Six groups align with six bench stations and six power supplies; four multimeters are managed through rotation roles.  
3. **Circuit/component failure risk:** Prepare spare leads, cells and lamps in labelled bench kits before each practical.  
4. **Electrical safety risk:** Use low-voltage supplies, provide a safety briefing, require teacher checking before power is connected, and supervise all benches.  
5. **Pack-up time risk:** Use labelled trays and assign group roles, including equipment manager and pack-up checker.  
6. **Processing-time risk:** Provide printed, stepwise instructions and allow additional completion time through reduced written quantity rather than reduced practical participation.

### 6. Adjustments

#### Practical lessons: Lessons 3 and 8
- Provide both students with printed instructions containing numbered steps, labelled circuit diagrams, safety reminders and a short word bank.
- Give instructions before equipment is handled and allow extra processing time before each task transition.
- Place each student in a supportive group with a clear role, such as circuit checker, diagram recorder, measurement reader or results verifier.
- Check understanding privately using one-step prompts before the group begins the next stage.
- Provide a partially completed results table and reduce copying demands while retaining the same C3, C7 and C8 learning goals.

#### Formative checkpoints: Lessons 4 and 7
- Provide printed copies in clear spacing with one question per section and key command words highlighted.
- Allow extra processing time within the lesson, including beginning promptly after instructions are issued.
- Read or clarify task wording without giving answers and offer a word bank for terms such as source, load, current, resistance, voltage and circuit.
- Return feedback using brief, specific written prompts and a short teacher check-in so each student can complete the correction activity.
