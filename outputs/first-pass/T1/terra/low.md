## Unit plan: Year 8 Science — Circuits, Energy Transfer and Models

| Lesson | Scheduled day/period | Content statement addressed | Lesson type | Brief activity description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Week 1 Monday P2 | C1 | Theory | Students distinguish models from real systems and identify how a simple circuit diagram can simplify a physical circuit. |
| 2 | Week 1 Tuesday P4 | C2 | Theory | Students classify examples of energy transfer as heat, light, motion or electric current using teacher-provided circuit contexts. |
| 3 | Week 1 Wednesday P1 | C3 → C4 | Practical | In groups, students construct and troubleshoot simple circuits, then use shared multimeters to observe and record current in amperes. |
| 4 | Week 1 Thursday P3 | C3, C4 | Formative | Students complete a 15-minute circuit-path and current checkpoint, followed by immediate whole-class feedback and corrections. |
| 5 | Week 1 Friday P5 | C5 | Theory | Students interpret voltage as energy transferred per unit charge and practise identifying voltage readings in volts. |
| 6 | Week 2 Monday P2 | C6 | Theory | Students investigate through diagrams and examples how material, length, thickness and temperature affect resistance. |
| 7 | Week 2 Tuesday P4 | C6 | Formative | Students complete a 15-minute resistance-prediction checkpoint and receive immediate feedback through worked comparisons. |
| 8 | Week 2 Wednesday P1 | C7 → C8 | Practical | Students build series and parallel circuits, collect voltage and current data in rotations, and use \(V = IR\) for simple resistive circuits. |
| 9 | Week 2 Thursday P3 | C9; assesses C7, C8, C9 | Summative | Students receive a brief lesson on model limits, then complete a maximum 30-minute circuit-model task involving series/parallel reasoning, \(V = IR\), and limitations of the model. |

## Prerequisite chain

```text
C1: Models simplify systems to explain or predict behaviour
  ↓
C2: Energy can be transferred between systems
  ↓
C3: A circuit requires a continuous path, source and load
  ↓
C4: Current is flow of charge, measured in amperes
  ↓
C5: Voltage is energy transferred per unit charge, measured in volts
  ↓
C6: Resistance opposes current and depends on stated factors
  ↓
C7: Series circuits have one path; parallel circuits have multiple paths
  ↓
C8: Simple resistive circuits can be modelled using V = IR
  ↓
C9: Models have limits when their assumptions break down
```

## Practical placement justification

### Lesson 3 practical: Continuous paths and current
- **Placement:** This practical occurs after C1 and C2 so students can use a circuit model to represent energy transfer, then learn C3 before applying C4 through measurement. It occurs on Wednesday P1, avoiding Friday P5 and not requiring after-1:00pm technician support.
- **Equipment management:** The class works in six bench groups of 4–5 students. Each group uses one of six power supplies and one circuit set. Four digital multimeters are used by four groups first, then passed to the remaining two groups during the second measurement rotation. No more than six power supplies or four multimeters are used. All 28 students wear the available class set of 30 safety goggles.
- **Bench-time allocation:** Minutes 0–5 are for safety directions, printed instructions and equipment issue; minutes 5–45 provide 40 minutes of usable bench time for construction, fault-finding and current measurements; minutes 45–50 are for pack-up.

### Lesson 8 practical: Series, parallel and \(V = IR\)
- **Placement:** This practical follows explicit teaching of C5 and C6, enabling students to use voltage, current and resistance concepts before comparing C7 circuit arrangements and applying C8. It is scheduled in Week 2 Wednesday P1, keeping practicals to one per week and avoiding Friday P5.
- **Equipment management:** Six groups each use one power supply and a circuit set with known-resistance loads. Four multimeters are allocated to four groups for the first 20-minute measurement rotation; the other two groups construct circuits, predict results and prepare tables. Groups then exchange roles for the second 20-minute rotation. This remains within the limits of six power supplies and four multimeters. All students use safety goggles.
- **Bench-time allocation:** Minutes 0–5 are for safety directions, printed instructions and issue of equipment; minutes 5–45 provide 40 minutes of usable bench time for building, measuring, comparing and calculating; minutes 45–50 are reserved for pack-up.

## Formative cadence

| Checkpoint | Lesson | Content checked | Same-lesson feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| Circuit-path and current check | Lesson 4 | C3 and C4: identify whether a circuit has a source, load and continuous path; interpret a current value in amperes | Students complete a short 10–12 minute printed response, then compare answers against projected worked examples and correct one response in a different colour. |
| Resistance-prediction check | Lesson 7 | C6: predict resistance changes from material, length, thickness and temperature | Students complete a short 10–12 minute prediction table, then receive a projected answer sequence and teacher verbal feedback before revising their table. |

Both checkpoints are low-stakes and occupy no more than 15 minutes including feedback.

## Deferrals and risks

### Deferrals if a practical is unavailable
- If the Lesson 3 practical cannot proceed, defer the practical measurement and application component of **C4** until a replacement practical is available; retain only introductory C3 diagram work and reschedule the C3 formative checkpoint if students have not demonstrated continuous-path understanding.
- If the Lesson 8 practical cannot proceed, defer **C7**, **C8**, and therefore **C9** and the final summative task, because students need the series/parallel evidence and simple \(V = IR\) application before evaluating model limits.
- The final summative task cannot occur until C7, C8 and C9 have all been taught.

### Timetable conflicts
- No practical is scheduled on Friday P5.
- Only one practical occurs in each week, meeting the maximum of two practicals per week.
- Practical lessons are scheduled Wednesday P1 and do not rely on dedicated technician support after 1:00pm.
- The fume cupboard is not required.

### Risks and mitigation
1. **Risk: Limited multimeters delay data collection.**  
   **Mitigation:** Use planned two-stage measurement rotations, with non-meter groups constructing circuits, predicting outcomes and completing data tables while waiting.

2. **Risk: Circuits do not work, reducing usable investigation time.**  
   **Mitigation:** Provide a fault-finding sequence on printed instructions: check source, continuous path, connections and load before requesting teacher assistance.

3. **Risk: Setup or pack-up reduces the required 40-minute bench time.**  
   **Mitigation:** Pre-sort equipment by bench, assign group equipment managers, and use a fixed 5-minute setup and 5-minute pack-up routine.

4. **Risk: Electrical or equipment misuse.**  
   **Mitigation:** Require goggles, give a brief safety reminder before equipment issue, and require power supplies to be switched off before circuit changes.

5. **Risk: Friday early dismissal interrupts learning.**  
   **Mitigation:** Friday P5 is used only for theory; no practical or assessment dependent on full-period completion is scheduled then.

## Adjustments

### Practical lessons: Lessons 3 and 8
- Provide both students with printed, numbered instructions, labelled circuit diagrams, a short equipment checklist and a clearly marked data table before the practical begins.
- Give instructions verbally and allow additional processing time before equipment is collected; group equipment managers begin setup while the students review the steps.
- Allocate each student a predictable role, such as diagram checker, connection checker, recorder or data-table manager, with the option to change roles after understanding the task.
- Check understanding privately at the start of each measurement rotation and provide a simplified fault-finding prompt if needed.
- Allow additional time to complete recording during pack-up or immediately after the practical where required.

### Formative checkpoints: Lessons 4 and 7
- Provide printed checkpoint questions with key words highlighted and one question per section.
- Give the two students the printed task at the start of the lesson so they have additional processing time before the 15-minute checkpoint window.
- Allow up to 12 minutes for their responses while other students use 10 minutes, followed by the same immediate feedback process.
- Provide verbal clarification of command words without changing the assessed content, and allow corrections during the same-lesson feedback stage.
