## 1. Lesson-by-lesson unit sequence

| Lesson | Scheduled day/period | Content statement addressed | Lesson type | Brief activity description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Week 1 Monday P2 | C1 | Theory | Students distinguish a system from its simplified representation and construct a labelled model that explains or predicts one behaviour. |
| 2 | Week 1 Tuesday P4 | C2 | Theory | Students use diagrams to trace energy transferred between systems as heat, light, motion or electric current, followed by logistical and safety preparation for Lesson 3. |
| 3 | Week 1 Wednesday P1 | C3 | Practical | In six bench groups, students build, break and repair simple circuits to identify the continuous path, source and load required for operation. |
| 4 | Week 1 Thursday P3 | C3, then C4 | Formative/Theory | Students complete and receive feedback on a 15-minute C3 checkpoint before using circuit diagrams to define current and its measurement in amperes. |
| 5 | Week 1 Friday P5 | C5 | Theory | Students compare current and voltage using circuit representations and explain voltage as energy transferred per unit charge, measured in volts. |
| 6 | Week 2 Monday P2 | C6 | Theory/Formative | Students examine how material, length, thickness and temperature affect resistance, then complete a 15-minute checkpoint with immediate feedback. |
| 7 | Week 2 Tuesday P4 | C7 | Theory | Students construct and compare series and parallel circuit diagrams, identifying one path in series and multiple paths in parallel. |
| 8 | Week 2 Wednesday P1 | C8, supported by C7 | Practical | Groups compare series and parallel arrangements and collect voltage and current measurements to calculate resistance using \(V=IR\). |
| 9 | Week 2 Thursday P3 | C9 | Theory | Students test circuit-model assumptions against examples and identify circumstances in which a simplified model may fail. |
| 10 | Week 2 Friday P5 | C7, C8, C9 | Summative | Students complete a 25-minute written task comparing circuit paths, applying \(V=IR\), and explaining a limitation of the model used. |

## 2. Prerequisite chain

```text
C1: Models are simplified representations
  ↓
C2: Energy can be transferred between systems
  ↓
C3: Circuits require a continuous path, source and load
  ↓
C4: Current is charge flow measured in amperes
  ↓
C5: Voltage is energy transferred per unit charge, measured in volts
  ↓
C6: Resistance opposes current and depends on specified factors
  ↓
C7: Series circuits have one path; parallel circuits have multiple paths
  ↓
C8: Simple resistive circuits can be modelled using V = IR
  ↓
C9: Models have limits and may fail when assumptions break down
```

## 3. Practical placement justification

### Practical 1 — Lesson 3: Circuit requirements

- **Placement:** C1 and C2 have already established models and energy transfer. The practical introduces C3 before current, voltage and resistance are taught, preserving the prerequisite order.
- **Grouping and equipment:** The 28 students work in six bench groups—four groups of five and two groups of four. Each group uses one of the six power supplies as its source, with a simple load and connecting circuit components. No multimeters are required, and all 28 students use the available class set of 30 goggles.
- **Usable bench time:** Safety and procedural instructions are printed and previewed in Lesson 2. In Lesson 3, five minutes are allocated to entry, goggles and station readiness; 40 minutes are reserved for circuit construction, testing and recording; and five minutes are allocated to shutdown and pack-up.
- **Timetable suitability:** Wednesday P1 avoids Friday early-dismissal risk and reduces dependence on technician support after 1:00 pm.

### Practical 2 — Lesson 8: Series, parallel and \(V=IR\)

- **Placement:** Students have already learned current, voltage, resistance and series/parallel paths in C4–C7, so they can collect and interpret measurements when applying C8.
- **Grouping and equipment:** The same six groups each use one power supply. Four digital multimeters rotate between groups in planned measurement windows: four groups measure while two construct and diagram their circuits, then groups exchange tasks. No more than four meters or six power supplies are used at once, and all students wear goggles.
- **Usable bench time:** Printed procedures and circuit diagrams are distributed in Lesson 7. Lesson 8 provides five minutes for goggles and station checks, 40 minutes of construction, comparison, measurement and calculation, and five minutes for power-down and pack-up.
- **Timetable suitability:** Wednesday P1 permits pre-staging in the shared laboratory and avoids the lack of a dedicated technician after 1:00 pm.

## 4. Formative cadence

| Checkpoint | Lesson | Content checked | Same-lesson feedback |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Circuit requirements checkpoint | 4 | C3: continuous path, source and load | Students complete a 10-minute diagram-based check and spend five minutes marking against projected answers; the teacher immediately corrects common errors before C4 begins. |
| Resistance checkpoint | 6 | C6: meaning of resistance and effects of material, length, thickness and temperature | After C6 instruction, students complete a 10-minute classification and prediction task, followed by five minutes of teacher-led correction using model responses. |

Both checkpoints are low-stakes and take no more than 15 minutes, including feedback.

## 5. Deferrals and risks

### Deferrals if practical access is unavailable

- If **Lesson 3** cannot occur, C3 and the dependent sequence C4–C9 must be deferred until the C3 practical is rescheduled in an available L4 period that can provide 40 minutes of bench time.
- If **Lesson 8** cannot occur, C8, C9 and the summative task must be deferred so that the required practical support for C7/C8 occurs before final assessment.
- A Friday P5 practical is not used as a replacement because practical work is prohibited in that period.

### Timetable conflicts

- Tuesday P4 and Thursday P3 may occur after technician support has ended, so neither is used for practical work.
- Friday P5 has early-dismissal risk; it contains only theory or a 25-minute written task, not practical work.
- Each practical occupies the full 50-minute period with only five minutes before and after the required 40 minutes of usable bench time.

### Named risks and mitigation

1. **Shared-laboratory access risk:** L4 may not be ready or equipment may have been moved.  
   **Mitigation:** Confirm the booking in advance, use labelled station trays and check all six stations before Wednesday P1.

2. **Multimeter bottleneck:** Four meters are insufficient for six groups simultaneously.  
   **Mitigation:** Use scheduled rotation windows, with two groups completing circuit construction and diagram analysis while four groups measure.

3. **Technician-availability risk:** There is no dedicated technician after 1:00 pm.  
   **Mitigation:** Schedule both practicals Wednesday P1 and pre-check and stage equipment before the lesson.

4. **Electrical safety or equipment-failure risk:** Incorrect connections could interrupt the practical or damage equipment.  
   **Mitigation:** Use teacher-checked low-voltage setups, require power-off before altering circuits, provide printed diagrams and keep one verified demonstration circuit ready.

5. **Friday early-dismissal risk:** Lesson 10 may be shortened.  
   **Mitigation:** Limit the summative response time to 25 minutes, distribute papers immediately and keep the remaining time for collection and administrative disruption.

## 6. Adjustments

### Practical 1 — Lesson 3

- Provide the two students with printed, numbered instructions and labelled circuit diagrams during Lesson 2 for advance processing.
- Use one instruction per step, a completion checklist and a clearly assigned group role.
- Conduct a brief individual comprehension check before power is connected and avoid reliance on oral instructions alone.

### Practical 2 — Lesson 8

- Provide printed procedures, a meter-use sequence, a worked calculation layout and circuit diagrams in Lesson 7.
- Seat each student in a stable group with a defined measurement or recording role.
- Highlight the group’s multimeter rotation time so additional processing does not reduce access to measurement equipment.

### Formative checkpoint 1 — Lesson 4

- Provide an uncluttered printed copy with short instructions and labelled diagrams.
- Allow the two students up to 12 minutes while the rest of the class completes the task in 10 minutes.
- Give them direct teacher feedback during the final three minutes using the same answer criteria.

### Formative checkpoint 2 — Lesson 6

- Provide a printed, chunked task with one resistance factor addressed at a time and key units already displayed.
- Allow up to 12 minutes rather than 10 and reduce unnecessary copying while retaining all C6 concepts.
- Return immediate verbal and annotated feedback during the final three minutes of the checkpoint.
