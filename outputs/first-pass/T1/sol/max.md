## 1. Lesson-by-lesson sequence

| Lesson | Scheduled day/period | Content statement | Lesson type | Brief activity description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Week 1 Monday P2 | C1 | Theory | Students compare a detailed system description with a simplified representation, identifying what the model omits and what it can explain or predict. |
| 2 | Week 1 Tuesday P4 | C2 | Theory | Students construct box-and-arrow models showing energy transferred between systems as heat, light, motion or electric current. |
| 3 | Week 1 Wednesday P1 | C3 | Theory/formative | After learning that circuits require a continuous path, source and load, students analyse circuit representations and complete Formative Checkpoint 1. |
| 4 | Week 1 Thursday P3 | C3–C4 | Practical | Six groups assemble complete and broken source–path–load arrangements, rotate multimeters, and relate readings in amperes to current as charge flow. |
| 5 | Week 1 Friday P5 | C5 | Theory | Students use printed circuit representations to explain voltage as energy transferred per unit charge and identify volts as its unit. |
| 6 | Week 2 Monday P2 | C6 | Theory/formative | Students examine resistance as opposition to current and its dependence on material, length, thickness and temperature before completing Formative Checkpoint 2. |
| 7 | Week 2 Tuesday P4 | C7 | Theory | Students classify and redraw circuit models as series circuits with one path or parallel circuits with multiple paths. |
| 8 | Week 2 Wednesday P1 | C7–C8 | Practical | Groups compare one-path and multiple-path arrangements, collect voltage and current readings, and use \(V = IR\) for simple resistive circuits. |
| 9 | Week 2 Thursday P3 | C9 | Theory | Students critique circuit and \(V = IR\) models by identifying their assumptions, limits and possible failure when assumptions break down. |
| 10 | Week 2 Friday P5 | C7–C9 | Summative | Students complete an individual printed task of no more than 30 minutes that integrates series/parallel classification, a \(V = IR\) calculation and explanation of a model limitation. |

## 2. Prerequisite chain

**C1 Models** → **C2 Energy transfer** → **C3 Circuit requirements** → **C4 Current** → **C5 Voltage** → **C6 Resistance** → **C7 Series and parallel circuits** → **C8 Quantitative \(V = IR\) model** → **C9 Model limits and failure**

## 3. Practical placement justification

### Practical 1 — Lesson 4, Week 1 Thursday P3
- **Placement:** It follows the teaching and checkpoint for C3, so students already understand source, load and continuous path; it then provides the first direct treatment of C4 before C5 is introduced.
- **Grouping and equipment:** The class forms six groups of 5, 5, 5, 5, 4 and 4 at the six fixed benches. Each group uses one of the six power supplies. Four multimeters are rotated: four groups take readings first, followed by the remaining two while earlier groups complete observations and repeat continuity checks. Twenty-eight of the 30 goggles are used; glassware and hand lenses are not required.
- **Bench time:** Station materials are pre-positioned before the bell. Minutes 0–5 cover printed instructions, goggles and setup; minutes 5–45 provide 40 minutes of usable bench work; minutes 45–50 are used for disconnection, checking and pack-up.

### Practical 2 — Lesson 8, Week 2 Wednesday P1
- **Placement:** It occurs after C7 has been taught and uses the distinction between one and multiple paths as the basis for introducing and applying C8; C9 is deliberately left until after students have experience of the quantitative model.
- **Grouping and equipment:** The same six groups use one power supply each. No more than four multimeters are in use: four groups collect voltage/current readings in the first rotation while two construct, compare and predict, then the meters rotate and the first groups calculate with \(V = IR\). Twenty-eight goggles are available from the class set.
- **Bench time:** Equipment and printed data tables are prepared before class; a five-minute setup and briefing is followed by 40 minutes of construction, measurement and bench calculation, with the final five minutes reserved for pack-up.

## 4. Formative cadence

- **Checkpoint 1 — Lesson 3, after C3:** A low-stakes printed task asks students to identify the source, load and continuous path and correct an incomplete circuit representation. The core task is designed for 10 minutes, with a maximum of 15 minutes. Students then self-correct using a projected and printed annotated key while the teacher gives immediate verbal correction and short reteaching in the same lesson.
- **Checkpoint 2 — Lesson 6, after C6:** A low-stakes printed task checks that resistance opposes current and that students can identify material, length, thickness and temperature as relevant variables. It is designed for 10 minutes, with a maximum of 15 minutes, followed by same-lesson correction using a printed/projected key and teacher feedback on common errors.

## 5. Deferrals and risks

### Deferrals
- If Practical 1 is unavailable, C3 remains completed, but C4 and consequently C5–C9 must be deferred because Lesson 4 is the planned first teaching of C4; the practical is moved to the next eligible non-Friday period before the chain continues.
- If Practical 2 is unavailable, C7 can be consolidated, but C8, C9 and the summative task must be deferred until the practical can occur.
- Rescheduling must retain no more than two practicals in any week and cannot use Friday P5.

### Timetable conflicts and named risks
- **Timetable compliance:** All lessons use listed L4 periods; there is one practical in each week and neither is on Friday. No fixed room conflict is created by the sequence.
- **After-1:00pm technician risk:** The exact clock time of Thursday P3 is not supplied, and no dedicated technician is available after 1:00pm. Lesson 4 equipment is therefore booked, checked and pre-positioned before 1:00pm so the teacher can run the lesson independently; otherwise the deferral rule applies.
- **Circuit-component availability risk:** Quantities of connecting-path and load components required by C3 and C8 are not supplied. Six usable station arrangements must be confirmed before the unit; if they cannot be provided, the corresponding practical and dependent content are deferred.
- **Multimeter bottleneck or failure:** Only four meters are available for six groups. All four are checked beforehand and used through a written rotation; if insufficient meters are operational to complete readings within 40 minutes, the quantitative practical is rescheduled.
- **Shared-lab setup risk:** L4 and all six benches are booked in advance, with station materials grouped and counted before class to protect the 40-minute bench allocation.
- **Friday early-dismissal risk:** Lesson 10 begins with the summative task so its maximum 30 minutes occurs first; if an early dismissal is announced in advance, the task moves to the next Monday P2.

## 6. Adjustments

Printed versions of all projected directions are provided throughout the unit, with additional pause and processing time.

- **Practical 1:** Each student receives the step-by-step method at the end of Lesson 3 for advance preview, is placed in a four-person group, receives a printed role/checklist, and has a teacher check before starting; their groups receive meters in the first rotation to reduce time pressure.
- **Practical 2:** Each receives the method in Lesson 7, including printed path representations, a data table and a \(V = IR\) calculation layout; they work in four-person groups, receive the first meter rotation and are given processing pauses before each stage.
- **Checkpoint 1:** Instructions and diagrams are supplied in an uncluttered printed format; the two students may use the full 15 minutes while others complete quiet correction preparation, then receive a printed feedback key and individual teacher check.
- **Checkpoint 2:** The students receive printed one-step prompts and the four C6 variables in print, may use the full 15 minutes without speed being assessed, and receive printed corrections plus an individual same-lesson feedback check.
- **Summative:** Directions are printed, and the task is designed for approximately 25 minutes so the two students can use the full 30-minute limit as extra processing time.
