## 3. Short explanation — 15 minutes

Write a 6–8 sentence explanation using your own data and graph. Do not just list the three terms.

Use these reasoning prompts first:

- **Claim and evidence:** Which design cooled more slowly? Quote temperatures or temperature drops.
- **Conduction:** On your sketch, trace a possible path through parts that touch. How might your material or layer choice have changed this path?
- **Convection:** Mark places where air or water could move. Did your revision make movement more or less likely? How could that affect cooling?
- **Radiation:** Circle an exposed outer surface. Could energy transfer be involved across a gap where objects do not touch? How might the surface choice matter?

# TEACHER KEY

## Practical timing and data interpretation

Keep the approved practical within its 35-minute block:

- **0–4 min:** initial build and individual sketch  
- **4–14 min:** Trial 1, readings at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 min  
- **14–18 min:** individual analysis, mandatory revision rationale and revised sketch  
- **18–28 min:** Trial 2  
- **28–35 min:** individual comparison and graph  

Expected data pattern: temperatures should generally decline over time. For comparable starting conditions, a jacket with a smaller temperature decrease,

\[
\Delta T = T_{0\text{ min}} - T_{10\text{ min}},
\]

has slowed cooling more effectively. Do not judge designs using only the final temperature if starting temperatures differed.

Do not require identical class results. Jacket thickness, fit, gaps, compression, probe depth, water volume, starting temperature and test location can affect results.

## Correct scientific explanations: authoritative wording and application

**P1.** *Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.*  
Application: conduction can occur through the beaker wall and through jacket layers that touch it or each other. A solid jacket does not need to move for conduction to occur.

**P2.** *Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.*  
Application: aluminium foil is the metal in this investigation. A foil layer in direct contact with the beaker should not be treated as the main insulating barrier.

**P3.** *Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.*  
Application: cotton wool, bubble wrap, foam and suitably arranged cardboard can reduce conduction when their structure retains pockets of still air.

**P4.** *Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.*  
Application: water in the beaker and air around it are fluids. Open channels, large gaps and exposed spaces may allow fluid movement; trapped air spaces reduce this effect.

**P5.** *Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.*  
Application: radiation can occur across gaps and from exposed surfaces. A reflective foil surface can affect this route.

**P6.** *Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).*  
Application: the strongest designs may combine an insulating, air-trapping layer with a reflective exposed surface. The temperature test measures the combined effect, not one isolated transfer mode.

## Expected observations and material links

### Teacher demonstration

A likely pattern for neatly wrapped beakers is:

1. **Cotton wool:** slowest cooling; smallest temperature decrease.  
2. **Aluminium foil:** often intermediate.  
3. **Bare beaker:** fastest cooling; greatest temperature decrease.

The foil-versus-bare comparison can vary with wrap fit, air gaps and surface condition. The more robust expected comparison is that cotton wool should generally slow cooling more effectively than a thin foil layer directly against the beaker.

| Demonstration beaker | Expected observation | Relevant propositions |
|---|---|---|
| Bare beaker | Greatest overall temperature drop and steepest downward trend, because there is no added insulating, air-trapping or reflective jacket. | P1, P4, P5; no additional P6 strategies are present. |
| Cotton wool wrap | Smallest temperature drop, especially if the wool is loose, continuous and not compressed. | P3 and P6. Heat loss is reduced, not eliminated. |
| Aluminium foil wrap | Usually a greater temperature drop than cotton wool. It may be intermediate between cotton wool and bare, but results can vary. | P1 and P2 explain why direct foil contact is not a strong insulating barrier. P5 and P6 explain why a reflective foil surface can still reduce radiation. |

### Student jacket materials

| Jacket material or feature | Expected observation in comparable trials | Relevant propositions and interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Bubble wrap | An intact, continuous layer should usually produce a smaller temperature drop than a bare beaker. Flattened bubbles or large joins may reduce the effect. | P3 and P6. P1 still occurs through materials in contact; P4 may contribute if air can move through gaps. |
| Cotton wool | A loose, reasonably thick layer should usually give a small temperature drop. Compressed or sparse wool may be less effective. | P3 and P6. |
| Foam pieces | A fitted foam layer should generally slow cooling compared with no jacket. Gaps between pieces may reduce the effect. | P3 and P6; P4 is relevant where air can move through gaps. |
| Cardboard | A continuous cardboard layer can reduce cooling compared with bare glass, particularly when its arrangement retains air spaces. Its effect may be more variable than cotton wool, bubble wrap or foam. | P1, P3 and P6. |
| Aluminium foil | A thin foil layer directly touching the beaker is usually less effective than cotton wool, bubble wrap or foam. Foil used with an air-trapping layer and presented as a reflective surface may improve a design. | P1, P2, P5 and P6. |
| Masking tape | Tape is mainly a fastening material, not a reliable stand-alone insulating layer. Excess tape can compress cotton wool or bubble wrap, change gaps, or alter contact between layers. | P1; P3 and P6 are relevant if tape changes trapped air spaces; P4 is relevant if it changes gaps or channels. |
| Large gaps/open channels | A jacket with open pathways may cool faster than a similarly thick jacket that holds its layers in place. | P4 and P6. Do not equate all air spaces with effective insulation; distinguish trapped spaces from spaces that permit movement. |
| Layered design: insulating material plus exposed foil | May show a smaller temperature drop than foil alone if the air-trapping insulating layer remains effective and the foil acts as a reflective surface. | P1–P6 may all be relevant. Do not claim the data isolate one mode. |

### Marking the final explanation

A scientifically sound explanation should:

- use actual Trial 1 and Trial 2 data;
- identify whether the revision lowered, increased or did not clearly change the cooling rate;
- connect touching solid layers to conduction;
- connect possible water or air movement to convection;
- connect exposed surfaces and foil to radiation;
- recognise that more than one transfer mode can occur at the same time;
- describe a limitation if the comparison was not fully controlled.

Do not require students to reproduce the proposition wording exactly. Their explanation should be conceptually consistent with it and supported by their own evidence.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Common misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| “Foil is always the best insulator because it is shiny.” | Contrast P2 with P6. Ask students to distinguish foil directly touching the beaker from foil used as a reflective outer surface with an insulating layer beneath it. |
| “Metal cannot insulate at all, so foil can never help.” | Explain that P2 and P6 can both apply. Foil is a good conductor, but a reflective surface can minimise radiation. Its placement matters. |
| “Any air gap insulates.” | Use P3, P4 and P6. Ask whether the air is trapped or whether it has space to move. |
| “Convection is heat rising.” | Revisit P4. The moving substance is a fluid, liquid or gas, due to density differences caused by heating. Heat transfer can also occur by conduction and radiation. |
| “Convection only happens in liquids.” | Point to the “liquid or gas” wording in P4. Air around the beaker is also a fluid. |
| “Conduction means the whole material moves.” | Revisit P1: conduction occurs through direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving. |
| “Radiation needs air” or “radiation only comes from the Sun.” | Revisit P5. Ask students to consider whether energy transfer can occur across a gap. |
| “Insulation stops heat completely” or “insulation makes heat.” | Use the temperature graph: the water should still cool, but a successful jacket reduces the rate of cooling. |
| “The beaker with the highest final temperature must be best.” | Require comparison of temperature decrease and starting temperatures, not endpoint temperatures alone. |
| “More tape, more layers or more changes automatically makes a fairer test.” | Return to the one-main-change revision rule. Excess tape may compress air pockets; multiple changes make cause-and-effect conclusions weaker. |
| “One lower graph proves one heat-transfer mode caused the result.” | Explain that the investigation measures total cooling. Students should use the modes as reasoned explanations, not claim that one was independently measured. |
