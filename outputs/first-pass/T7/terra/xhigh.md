## Practical implementation and expected evidence

### Suggested timing within the existing 35-minute practical
- **0–4 min:** Students plan and build Jacket 1.
- **4–14 min:** Test 1; readings at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 minutes.
- **14–18 min:** Students calculate temperature decrease, identify a weakness and make a required revision.
- **18–28 min:** Test 2 with reset water conditions.
- **28–35 min:** Students calculate, compare and select evidence for the final written explanation.

For a fair retest, the teacher should provide a similar water volume and starting temperature for both tests. Keep the beaker, location, probe depth and test duration consistent. Students should not handle or pour hot water.

A class-wide decision should be made about the beaker opening: for example, side jackets only, or a permitted loose cover with a probe opening. Do not allow sealed hot containers.

## Authoritative science propositions

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

## Interpreting the teacher demonstration

### Likely observations

| Beaker | Expected observation | Relevant propositions and explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Bare beaker | Usually shows the greatest temperature decrease because there is no jacket to reduce heat loss from the sides. | Thermal energy can move through the beaker material by conduction (P1), be transferred to moving air or water by convection (P4), and leave from surfaces by radiation (P5). The bare beaker does not use the approaches described in P6. |
| Cotton wool | Usually cools more slowly than the bare beaker and is likely to be the strongest performer of the three demonstration materials. | Cotton wool contains many air spaces. P3 applies: **“Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.”** It can also support the reduction of convection by limiting moving air, as described in P6. |
| Aluminium foil | May cool more slowly than bare glass, but results can be variable. A tightly wrapped foil layer may be less effective than cotton wool because the foil is in direct contact with the beaker. | P2 applies because aluminium is a metal: **“Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.”** If the shiny foil surface faces outward, it can help minimise radiation, consistent with P5 and P6. Students should recognise that foil has competing effects rather than assuming it is always best or always worst. |

Do not require one fixed ranking between foil and bare if construction, surface condition or starting conditions differ. Cotton wool should generally be expected to outperform a tightly wrapped foil-only jacket.

## Expected observations for available materials

These are expected patterns, not fixed numerical outcomes. Temperature data represent the combined effect of several heat-transfer pathways, construction quality and measurement consistency.

| Material or configuration | Likely observation in a jacket test | Science links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Greater temperature decrease than a well-built insulating jacket. | P1, P4, P5 and P6. Heat loss can occur by several pathways; no deliberate reduction is provided. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually a smaller temperature decrease than bare glass, especially when dry, thick and not tightly compressed. Compressed cotton or large gaps may reduce effectiveness. | P3 and P6. Cotton wool can trap pockets of still air. |
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually slows cooling compared with bare glass if the bubble layer covers the sides well and is not crushed. Flattened bubbles, gaps or loose joins reduce performance. | P3 and P6. The bubbles create pockets of air; still air is important. |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually a smaller temperature decrease than bare glass when fitted closely without leaving large gaps. Thickness and coverage affect results. | P3 and P6. Foam contains trapped air spaces and can reduce conduction and convection pathways. |
| **Cardboard** | May slow cooling compared with a bare beaker, but often less effectively than a thick foam, cotton wool or bubble-wrap jacket of similar coverage. Results depend on thickness, gaps and whether the cardboard becomes compressed or damp. | P1, P3 and P6. Cardboard is a solid layer but may also contain some air spaces. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Shiny outer foil may improve performance relative to bare glass, but foil pressed directly onto the beaker may not perform as well as air-trapping materials. Crumpled, dull or inward-facing foil may give a different result. | P2, P5 and P6. Foil is metal and conducts well, but a reflective outward surface can reduce radiation. |
| **Masking tape** | Should mainly hold layers in place. Small amounts should not determine the result. Excess tape may compress air-containing layers; carefully placed tape can reduce gaps between layers. | P1 and P6. It is not the main insulating material; its main effect is through construction quality and gaps. |
| **Layered jacket: cotton, bubble wrap or foam with outward-facing foil** | May produce a smaller temperature decrease than a single layer if it has good coverage and does not crush trapped air. There is no guaranteed result; students must use their data. | P3 and P6 for air-trapping layers; P5 and P6 for an outward reflective surface; P2 reminds students that foil directly against the beaker can also conduct energy rapidly. |

### Core explanation for student designs

A beaker jacket does not eliminate heat loss; it can reduce the rate of heat transfer by different pathways.

- Heat can move through the glass and jacket materials by conduction. Apply P1.
- Aluminium foil has a special role because P2 applies to metals.
- Air pockets in cotton wool, foam and bubble wrap are relevant to P3.
- Convection may occur in the water, in air around the beaker and near an uncovered opening. Apply P4.
- A shiny foil surface can reduce radiation from the outer surface. Apply P5 and P6.
- The strongest explanation links a student’s design feature and data to P6: **“Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).”**

Students should phrase conclusions cautiously. Their temperature results show total cooling, not a separate measured value for conduction, convection or radiation.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Common student idea | Teaching response |
|---|---|
| “Cold moves into the beaker.” | Reframe: the water is warmer than its surroundings, so thermal energy is transferred away from the water. Ask students to identify where that transfer could occur. |
| “Cotton wool makes heat” or “foam keeps heat in because it is warm.” | Explain that these materials do not create thermal energy. Direct students to P3 and ask what is present in the spaces within the material. |
| “Air gaps are always good, even if air can move around.” | Distinguish still air from moving air. Ask whether a large gap could allow air circulation. Link to P3, P4 and P6. |
| “Convection happens in solids.” | Return to P4: convection involves movement of a fluid, which is a liquid or gas. |
| “Conduction means the hot material moves through the jacket.” | Use P1: conduction occurs by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving. |
| “Foil is the best insulator because it is shiny.” | Foil can reduce radiation when reflective, but aluminium is also a metal. Use P2, P5 and P6 to explore its competing effects. |
| “Foil is useless because metal conducts.” | Acknowledge P2, then ask what changes when foil is shiny and faces outward. Link to radiation and P6. |
| “Radiation needs air” or “radiation means radioactivity.” | Use P5 exactly: radiation is transfer by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium. |
| “The design with the highest final temperature definitely worked best.” | Direct students to compare temperature decrease over the same time and check whether starting temperatures were similar. If not, identify this as a limitation. |
| “More layers always work better.” | Ask students whether layers were compressed, whether gaps increased, whether the opening changed, and whether the revision altered more than one variable. The evidence, not a rule, should support the conclusion. |
| “A failed revision means the investigation was wrong.” | An unexpected result is valid evidence. Students should report it, identify a possible design or measurement limitation, and propose a controlled next revision. |
