# TEACHER KEY  
## Heat Transfer and Insulation Design

## Purpose of the practical
Students design, test, revise and retest an insulating jacket for a 250 mL beaker. The key evidence is the temperature decrease over 10 minutes, compared between the initial and revised designs. Lower temperature drop indicates slower cooling under comparable conditions.

The practical is not intended to produce one identical “best” design. Results will vary with starting temperature, thickness of materials, coverage of the base and top, gaps, compression of materials, thermometer placement and timing. Students should use evidence to justify design decisions.

## Correct scientific explanations

Use the following wording when explicitly teaching, correcting or assessing the concepts.

### Conduction
**P1. Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.**

For this investigation, heat can transfer through the glass beaker and through materials touching it. Students may identify that materials such as foam, cotton wool and bubble wrap can slow this transfer compared with a metal layer directly against the beaker.

### Metals and aluminium foil
**P2. Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.**

Aluminium foil is therefore not automatically an effective insulating layer simply because it is wrapped around a beaker. Its value in this context is mainly as a reflective surface when used appropriately, often in combination with insulating material.

### Insulators and trapped air
**P3. Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.**

Cotton wool, foam and bubble wrap can be useful because of the still air they trap. Students should connect performance to air pockets, thickness, incomplete coverage and whether a material has been compressed.

### Convection
**P4. Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.**

Warm air near the beaker may rise, and cooler air can move in to replace it. A design that reduces gaps and traps air can reduce this movement. Water and air are fluids; solids such as foil, cotton wool and cardboard do not convect.

### Radiation
**P5. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.**

Students may discuss radiation from the warm beaker and the possible role of a reflective foil surface. Do not overstate the evidence: the class test measures total cooling and does not isolate radiation from conduction and convection.

### Combined design principle
**P6. Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

A strong student explanation links a specific design feature to one or more pathways:
- foam/cotton wool/bubble wrap: insulating material and trapped still air;
- reduced gaps/partial top coverage: less movement of warm air;
- reflective foil layer: reduced radiation;
- multiple layers: potentially more trapped still air, if not compressed.

## Interpreting student data

### Expected pattern
- Both Test 1 and Test 2 should show temperatures decreasing over time.
- A revised design that better covers the beaker, adds an air-trapping layer, reduces gaps, or rearranges foil as an outer layer may show a smaller 10-minute temperature drop.
- Some groups will obtain no improvement or a worse result. This is still useful evidence if students identify plausible variables and make a defensible explanation.

### Fair-test reminders
Encourage students to keep as consistent as possible:
- beaker size;
- volume of water;
- approximate starting temperature;
- measurement interval;
- total testing time;
- thermometer placement and depth;
- room location and drafts;
- amount of material used, unless material amount is the intended change.

The only deliberate difference between Test 1 and Test 2 should ideally be the stated revision. In practice, acknowledge limitations rather than requiring perfect control.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Teacher response |
|---|---|
| “Foil is an insulator because it keeps food warm.” | Distinguish between foil’s reflective surface and its metallic conduction. Use **P2**: “Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.” Ask students whether foil was directly touching the beaker and whether an insulating air-trapping layer was also present. |
| “Cotton wool makes heat.” | Clarify that the material does not produce heat; it slows heat transfer. Ask, “Where did the thermal energy come from?” Link to **P3**. |
| “Thicker always means better.” | Ask whether the extra thickness contains trapped still air, whether it has been compressed, and whether it creates gaps. Thickness can help, but material and design matter. |
| “Heat rises.” | Rephrase carefully: warm fluids can rise because heating causes density differences. Heat itself can transfer in different directions by conduction, convection and radiation. Link to **P4**. |
| “Convection happens in solids.” | Reinforce that **P4** refers to movement of a fluid: liquid or gas. A solid jacket does not convect, though it can conduct. |
| “A lid stops all heat loss.” | A cover may reduce convection from the top, but heat can still transfer by conduction through materials and radiation from surfaces. |
| “If the outside feels warm, the insulation is not working.” | A warm outer surface shows some energy has transferred outward. Compare the rate of cooling rather than expecting zero heat transfer. |
| “The best result proves one heat-transfer mode only.” | Cooling involves several pathways at once. The test measures total temperature change; it does not isolate conduction, convection or radiation. |
| “The temperature went down because cold entered the beaker.” | Use energy-transfer language: thermal energy moves from the warmer water/beaker toward cooler surroundings. |
| “Shiny foil reflects convection.” | Convection involves moving fluids; a reflective surface relates to radiation. Foil may also conduct because it is metal. |
| “Still air and moving air work the same way.” | Draw attention to gaps, escaping steam and drafts. Link still air to **P3** and fluid movement to **P4**. |

## Suggested assessment of student explanations

A satisfactory Year 7 response should:
1. state whether Test 2 had a smaller or larger temperature drop than Test 1;
2. support this claim with numerical data;
3. describe at least one design change;
4. connect the design change to a plausible heat-transfer pathway;
5. recognise a limitation or fair-test issue if results were unexpected.

A stronger response distinguishes the three modes. For example:

> “Our revised jacket cooled more slowly because the temperature dropped 8 °C instead of 12 °C. We added cotton wool and bubble wrap around the sides and reduced gaps near the top. The cotton wool and bubble wrap trapped still air, so less energy was transferred through the jacket and less warm air could move away. We also put foil on the outside, which may have reduced radiation. Our test did not measure each type of transfer separately.”

## Individual-evidence verification checklist

Use this checklist while circulating and collecting work. Every student should have their own completed resource, even when students work in groups.

### Student identification and participation
- [ ] Student name and group are recorded.
- [ ] Student has completed an individual first-design sketch with labelled materials.
- [ ] Student has written an individual prediction/justification for the initial design.
- [ ] Student has recorded Test 1 temperatures at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 minutes.
- [ ] Student has calculated or recorded the Test 1 temperature drop.
- [ ] Student has included at least one Test 1 observation.

### Revision and retest
- [ ] Student identifies evidence from Test 1.
- [ ] Student states a specific design change; “we made it better” is insufficient.
- [ ] Student provides a revised-design sketch or clearly labels the revision.
- [ ] Student gives a predicted effect or rationale for the change.
- [ ] Student has recorded Test 2 temperatures at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 minutes.
- [ ] Student has calculated or recorded the Test 2 temperature drop.
- [ ] Student compares Test 1 and Test 2 using data.

### Scientific reasoning
- [ ] Student attempts a conduction explanation connected to materials in the design.
- [ ] Student attempts a convection explanation connected to air, gaps, steam or a cover.
- [ ] Student attempts a radiation explanation connected to surface properties, particularly foil if used.
- [ ] Student writes a final claim supported by observations or temperature data.
- [ ] Student identifies a possible limitation if the result was unexpected.

For students requiring support, accept annotated diagrams, sentence starters, oral conferencing recorded by the teacher, or partially completed data tables—but retain evidence that the student personally contributed observations, data and reasoning.
