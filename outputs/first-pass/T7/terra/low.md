# TEACHER KEY  
## Heat Transfer and Insulation Design

## Authoritative science propositions

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.  

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.  

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.  

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.  

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.  

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

---

## Expected demonstration observations

The exact temperatures will vary with starting temperature, wrapping thickness, gaps, room conditions and whether a cover is used. Focus on **relative cooling rate**, not a required numerical result.

| Beaker/material | Expected observation | Scientific explanation and links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually cools fastest, or has the greatest temperature decrease. Its glass sides and exposed top have no additional insulating layer. | Heat can be transferred through the glass by conduction. Warm air above and around the beaker can transfer energy by convection. The surface also transfers energy by radiation. This is consistent with P1, P4 and P5. It lacks the strategies in P6. |
| **Cotton-wool-wrapped beaker** | Usually cools more slowly than the bare beaker. Thick, fluffy cotton wool is expected to be effective if it is dry, wrapped with few gaps and not compressed heavily. | Cotton wool contains trapped pockets of still air. **Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.** (P3) It also reduces air movement near the beaker, helping reduce convection. This applies P1, P3, P4 and P6. |
| **Aluminium-foil-wrapped beaker** | May cool more slowly than bare, especially if the shiny surface is exposed and reflects thermal radiation. However, it may be less effective than a thick cotton-wool layer when used alone; results can vary. | **Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.** (P2) Therefore thin foil is not automatically a good insulating layer against conduction. A shiny reflective surface can help minimise radiation, consistent with P5 and P6. Foil works best as part of a layered design with an insulating, air-trapping layer. |

### Important interpretation of foil results
Do not require a fixed ranking such as “cotton always first, foil always second, bare always last.” Cotton wool is commonly expected to outperform bare glass. Foil’s result depends strongly on:
- whether the shiny side is exposed;
- whether it is tightly in contact with the beaker;
- whether there is an air-trapping layer beneath it;
- gaps, top exposure and handling;
- thickness and arrangement of the wrap.

Students should be rewarded for using data and identifying a fair-test limitation, even if their ranking differs from another group’s.

## Expected observations for available jacket materials

| Material | Likely observation when used in a jacket | Relevant propositions |
|---|---|---|
| **Cotton wool** | Usually reduces cooling well when loosely layered because it traps air. Compressing it reduces trapped-air spaces and may reduce effectiveness. | P1, P3, P4, P6 |
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually reduces cooling because the sealed bubbles trap air. Multiple layers may improve performance, provided gaps are limited. | P1, P3, P4, P6 |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually effective because foam contains many air spaces. A close-fitting foam layer can reduce heat transfer through the sides. | P1, P3, P4, P6 |
| **Cardboard** | Can provide some insulation, particularly in layers or with air spaces, but is often less effective than thick foam/cotton wool/bubble wrap. | P1, P3, P6 |
| **Aluminium foil** | Can help reduce radiation when used as a reflective outer surface. Used directly against the beaker and alone, it may not reduce conductive heat loss effectively because it is metal. | P1, P2, P5, P6 |
| **Masking tape** | Mainly holds layers in place. Excess tape or tightly compressed wrapping can reduce air spaces and create gaps elsewhere. | P3, P6 |

## Expected explanation of heat transfer in the investigation

A strong Year 7 explanation should identify that heat can leave the hot water/beaker system in more than one way:

1. **Conduction through the beaker and jacket**  
   **Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.** (P1)  
   Insulating materials reduce this transfer because **insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.** (P3)

2. **Convection from exposed surfaces and gaps**  
   **Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.** (P4)  
   An uncovered top, gaps in a jacket and loose openings allow warmed air to move away and be replaced by cooler air. Air-trapping layers reduce air movement close to the beaker.

3. **Radiation from surfaces**  
   **Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.** (P5)  
   A shiny foil outer surface may help reduce radiation loss because reflective surfaces are included in P6.

4. **Overall design principle**  
   **Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).** (P6)

## What successful student designs may look like

There is no single required design. Effective designs commonly include:

- a thick layer of cotton wool, foam or bubble wrap around the sides;
- limited gaps between layers;
- an outer foil layer with the shiny surface exposed;
- a partial top cover or collar that reduces air movement while leaving safe access for the probe thermometer;
- tape used sparingly so that insulating layers are not heavily compressed.

A student may reasonably find that a simpler design performs better than a bulky one if the simpler design has fewer gaps, is less compressed, or better reduces exposed surfaces.

## Data interpretation guidance

### Temperature decrease calculation
\[
\text{Temperature decrease} = \text{starting temperature} - \text{temperature at 10 minutes}
\]

- **Smaller decrease:** better insulation under that group’s conditions.
- **Larger decrease:** more heat was transferred to the surroundings during the test.

### Fair comparison prompts
Students should compare:
- the same water volume;
- similar starting temperatures;
- the same beaker;
- the same thermometer/probe position;
- equal test times;
- similar room location and conditions;
- the revised design as the main intentional change.

Acknowledge that temperature readings may not be perfectly matched. Students can still compare cooling trends if they identify limitations.

## Common misconceptions and teaching responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| “Cold moves into the beaker.” | Reframe: thermal energy moves from the warmer water and beaker to cooler surroundings. Ask students to describe what happened to the water temperature rather than treating “cold” as a substance. |
| “Foil is an insulator because it keeps food warm.” | Distinguish the foil’s reflective role from its metal properties. Use P2: **Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.** Explain that foil can be useful for radiation but is strongest when combined with an air-trapping insulator. |
| “All shiny materials stop heat transfer.” | Explain that reflective surfaces can reduce radiation, but heat may still transfer by conduction and convection. Ask which other pathways remain open. |
| “Thicker is always better.” | Thickness can help if it adds trapped still air, but tightly compressing cotton wool or adding poorly arranged layers can reduce effectiveness. Ask students to use their data rather than assume. |
| “Convection means any heat moving upward.” | Return to P4: **Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.** Emphasise movement of air or water, not simply “heat rising.” |
| “Conduction only happens in metals.” | P1 applies to energy transfer through a material generally. Metals conduct particularly well because of P2; other materials can conduct less effectively. |
| “Radiation needs air.” | Use P5: **Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.** Contrast this with convection, which requires a liquid or gas. |
| “The jacket completely stops heat transfer.” | Use “reduces” or “slows” heat loss. Every real design still transfers some energy, as shown by the temperature decrease. |
| “One group’s result proves the best material for all situations.” | Encourage cautious claims: “Under our test conditions…” Discuss gaps, layer thickness, starting temperature and measurement variation. |

## Quick feedback prompts during the practical

Use questions rather than immediately supplying answers:

- “What does a smaller temperature decrease tell you?”
- “Where is the most exposed part of your beaker?”
- “What changed between Test 1 and Test 2?”
- “How will your change reduce one possible pathway for heat transfer?”
- “If foil is metal, why might it still be included in a design?”
- “Have you trapped air, or compressed the material so tightly that there is little air left?”
- “Is your retest a fair comparison with your first test?”
