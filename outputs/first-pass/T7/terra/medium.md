# TEACHER KEY  
## Heat Transfer and Insulation Design — Year 7

## Authoritative science propositions

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.  

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.  

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.  

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.  

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.  

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).  

---

## Expected demonstration observations

Conditions should be kept as consistent as practical: same beaker type, water volume, starting temperature, location and timing.

| Demonstration beaker | Expected observation | Scientific explanation and proposition links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually the largest temperature decrease because there is no added insulating jacket. Heat can be lost through the glass and from the exposed surfaces. | P1 applies to energy transfer through the glass by direct particle contact. The exposed beaker can also lose energy by radiation (P5), while air movement near the beaker and open top contributes to convection (P4). |
| **Cotton-wool-wrapped beaker** | Usually cools slowest, or shows a substantially smaller temperature decrease than the bare beaker. Thick, loosely arranged cotton wool is generally more effective than compressed cotton wool. | Cotton wool contains many air spaces. **P3:** “Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.” The trapped still air also helps limit air circulation close to the beaker, supporting P6’s focus on minimising convection. |
| **Aluminium-foil-wrapped beaker** | May cool more slowly than a bare beaker, particularly if its shiny surface faces outward, but is commonly less effective than a thick cotton-wool jacket when the foil is tightly against the glass. Results can vary with tightness, folds and surface orientation. | Aluminium is a metal. **P2:** “Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.” Therefore foil alone is not a reliable conduction insulator. A shiny, reflective outward foil surface can reduce radiative loss: P5 and P6 apply. If foil is crumpled or separated from the beaker by air-containing layers, its performance may differ. |

Do not require a fixed ranking of foil versus every other configuration. The defensible expected pattern is generally: **cotton wool has a lower temperature decrease than bare; foil performance depends on whether it is acting mainly as a reflective outer surface or as a metal layer in direct contact with the beaker.**

| **Bubble wrap** | Usually effective when bubbles remain intact and the wrap is not tightly flattened. Multiple layers may improve performance. | Air in the bubbles and between layers provides still-air spaces. Apply P3 and P6. Flattened bubbles reduce the amount of trapped still air. |

| **Cardboard** | Can reduce heat loss, particularly in multiple layers or with air gaps; usually less effective if wet, very thin or tightly pressed against the beaker without air spaces. | Cardboard is a poor conductor compared with metal and may include air spaces. | **Aluminium foil** | A shiny outer layer may improve a design, but foil placed directly against the beaker may not be as effective as insulating layers. Crumpled foil can create air gaps, but this must be controlled carefully. | Apply P2 for the metal’s high conductivity. Apply P5 and P6 where a reflective outer surface reduces radiation. Foil is most sensibly used as an outer reflective layer over cotton wool, foam or bubble wrap rather than as the only jacket material. |

| **Glass beaker** | The bare beaker feels warm and the water cools over time. | **P1:** “Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.” Energy passes through the glass; other pathways also operate at exposed surfaces. |

## Expected result pattern for the investigation

A successful revised design should generally show:

- a **smaller temperature decrease** over 10 minutes than the first design;
- a shallower temperature-versus-time graph;
- fewer gaps, less compressed insulation, or a better combination of insulating layers and a reflective outer surface.

However, a revised design may not improve because of:
- different starting temperatures;
- different water volume;
- thermometer depth or position changing;
- jacket placement changing;
- delays in starting the timer;
- compressed cotton/bubble wrap;
- a loose or gapped jacket;
- inconsistent foil orientation;
- a draft or different bench location.

Students should be credited for evidence-based evaluation even if Test 2 does not improve, provided they identify likely control-variable or design reasons.

## Model explanation structure

A strong Year 7 response should make a claim, use temperature evidence, and link each mode appropriately. For example:

> Our revised jacket had a smaller temperature decrease than our first jacket, so it reduced heat loss more effectively. The cotton wool and bubble wrap reduced conduction because they contained trapped pockets of still air. This links to P3: “Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.” We reduced gaps in the jacket so less air could circulate around the beaker. P4 explains that convection involves movement in a liquid or gas caused by density differences from heating. We placed shiny foil on the outside because radiation can transfer by electromagnetic waves without a medium, as stated in P5. Overall, our design used insulating material, trapped still air and a reflective surface, consistent with P6.

Accept equivalent wording from students; they do not need to reproduce propositions verbatim.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| “Foil is the best insulator because it is shiny.” | Separate radiation from conduction. Ask students what happens when a metal spoon is placed in hot liquid. Reinforce P2: metal transfers energy rapidly. Explain that foil can be useful as a **reflective outer surface**, but it is not necessarily the best layer directly against the beaker. |
| “All heat rises.” | Clarify that heated **fluids** can move because of density differences; this is convection. Heat transfer through a solid jacket is not “rising”; it is conduction. Use P1 and P4. |
| “Cold moves into the beaker.” | Use energy language: thermal energy transfers from the warmer water and beaker to cooler surroundings. “Cold” is not a substance moving in. |
| “Air is always an insulator.” | Distinguish still air from moving air. Trapped still air helps insulation; moving air can transfer energy through convection. Use P3 and P4. |
| “More material always means better insulation.” | Ask whether the material is metal, whether it is compressed, and whether it creates gaps or air movement. Thickness can help, but material type and arrangement matter. |
| “A jacket stops heat transfer completely.” | Emphasise that insulation reduces the **rate** of heat transfer; it does not eliminate all heat loss. |
| “The temperature at 10 minutes alone proves the design was better.” | Require comparison of temperature decrease and starting temperature. If starting temperatures differ, discuss fair testing and/or compare cooling rate cautiously. |
| “The foil makes heat stay in because it reflects heat back, so it must always be inside.” | Explain that the foil’s useful role is linked to radiation at a surface. If it is directly against the beaker, metal conduction may be important. Outer placement over insulating layers is generally more appropriate. |
