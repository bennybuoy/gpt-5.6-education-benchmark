# TEACHER KEY  
## Heat Transfer and Insulation Design

### Purpose of the practical
Students compare the cooling of hot water in an initially designed beaker jacket and a revised jacket. The evidence of learning is not simply the “best” insulation result; it is the student’s use of controlled observations and temperature data to justify a revision and explain heat transfer by conduction, convection and radiation.

---

## Authoritative science propositions

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.  

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.  

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.  

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.  

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.  

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).  

---

## Expected observations and explanations by material

Actual rankings will vary with thickness, compression, gaps, starting temperature, jacket fit and whether foil is used with an air gap. Mark explanations based on evidence and reasoning, not on a single required ranking.

### Bare beaker
**Likely observation**
- Usually the greatest temperature decrease over 10 minutes.
- The glass is exposed to surrounding air; warm air may move around the beaker and above the open top.

**Explanation**
- Thermal energy can transfer through the glass by conduction: **P1. Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.**
- Air around the beaker and water inside the beaker can move as they are heated or cooled: **P4. Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.**
- The exposed beaker can also transfer thermal energy by radiation: **P5. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.**

### Cotton wool
**Likely observation**
- Usually a relatively small temperature decrease if the cotton wool is thick and not tightly compressed.
- Cotton wool may perform less well if compressed flat, wet, or full of gaps.
- In the demonstration, cotton wool is expected to cool more slowly than the bare beaker and is commonly slower than foil used alone.

**Explanation**
- Cotton wool holds many small spaces of still air. **P3. Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.**
- The trapped air also makes it harder for air to circulate next to the beaker, reducing convection.
- This applies P6: **P6. Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

### Bubble wrap
**Likely observation**
- Often produces a smaller temperature decrease than a bare beaker.
- Intact bubbles generally perform better than flattened or punctured bubbles.
- Gaps at seams or around the base reduce effectiveness.

**Explanation**
- Bubble wrap traps air in bubbles. **P3. Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.**
- The separated, enclosed air spaces also reduce movement of air close to the beaker, limiting convection.
- A student who identifies both reduced conduction and reduced convection is applying P6 appropriately.

### Foam pieces
**Likely observation**
- Foam is often one of the better available choices, particularly if it fits closely around the sides without being crushed.
- Thick foam or layered foam may result in a low temperature change.
- Large unsealed gaps can still allow air circulation.

**Explanation**
- Foam contains many trapped air pockets. **P3. Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.**
- Foam reduces the direct pathway for energy to move from hot glass to the outside, consistent with P1 and P6.
- If students note that still air reduces movement in the jacket, connect this to P4 and P6.

### Cardboard
**Likely observation**
- Usually provides some insulation, often less than thick foam, cotton wool or intact bubble wrap.
- Corrugated cardboard or multiple loose layers may work better than thin, tightly pressed cardboard.
- Cardboard can leave gaps and is less effective if it does not fit around the beaker.

**Explanation**
- Cardboard contains air spaces and can reduce heat transfer compared with a bare glass surface. The relevant statement is: **P3. Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.**
- Corrugations and layers can trap still air, helping reduce both conduction and convection.
- This is an application of P6.

### Aluminium foil
**Likely observation**
- Foil used alone and tightly against the beaker may not be an effective insulating jacket. It may cool faster than cotton wool, foam or bubble wrap.
- A shiny foil layer facing outward, especially when combined with an insulating layer or an air gap, may improve a design.
- In the demonstration, foil may perform differently from class expectations; it should not automatically be assumed to be the best insulator.

**Explanation**
- Aluminium is a metal. **P2. Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.**
- Therefore, foil pressed directly onto the hot beaker can provide a rapid conduction pathway.
- However, a shiny surface can reduce radiant heat transfer. **P5. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.**
- A foil outer layer is most defensible when students explain that it is intended to reduce radiation while another material or air gap reduces conduction and convection. This applies P6: **P6. Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

### Masking tape
**Likely observation**
- Tape mainly holds the jacket together. Excess tape can compress cotton wool or bubble wrap and reduce the air spaces that make these materials effective.
- Tape itself should not be claimed as the major insulator unless students have evidence and a justified mechanism.

**Explanation**
- Direct students back to whether the tape changed trapped air spaces, material contact or gaps rather than treating it as a separate “best” insulation material.

## Expected demonstration discussion

A typical prediction/result pattern is:

| Beaker treatment | Expected relative cooling | Useful discussion |
|---|---|---|
| Bare | Fastest or among fastest | No jacket; exposed surface and moving air. |
| Cotton wool | Slowest or among slowest | Trapped still air limits heat loss. |
| Aluminium foil | Variable; often between bare and cotton wool when used alone | Metal conducts well, but shiny foil can affect radiation. |

Do not insist that every class obtains exactly this ranking. Emphasise that a fair comparison requires equal water volumes, starting temperatures, container types and timing. The key conceptual contrast is that “shiny” and “insulating” are not identical properties.

---

## What strong student reasoning may include

### Conduction
Accept statements such as:
- “Foam/cotton/bubble wrap reduced the temperature change because the beaker was not directly touching a good conductor.”
- “The foil was touching the glass, so it may have transferred energy through the metal.”
- “The material between the hot glass and outside made the pathway harder.”

Link to P1, P2 and P3 as appropriate.

### Convection
Accept statements such as:
- “The jacket trapped air, so there was less air movement near the beaker.”
- “Large gaps let warm air move away.”
- “The water may circulate inside the beaker, and air can circulate outside it.”

Link to P4 and P6. Note that the open top of the beaker remains a route for convection; students do not need to eliminate all heat loss to show sound reasoning.

### Radiation
Accept statements such as:
- “The shiny foil faced outward and may have reduced heat transfer by radiation.”
- “Foil works best as part of a design, not necessarily pressed against the beaker.”

Link to P5 and P6. Students should not need to use the term “electromagnetic waves” independently at Year 7 level if their evidence-based explanation is otherwise sound; introduce or reinforce it during feedback.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Teacher response |
|---|---|
| “Metal foil is the best insulator because it keeps food warm.” | Ask what foil is made of and whether it was touching the beaker. Contrast a foil-only design with foil used outside foam/cotton wool or with an air gap. Use P2 and P6. |
| “Foil always makes heat stay in.” | Explain that foil can have different effects on conduction and radiation. Its shiny surface can be useful for radiation, while the metal can conduct energy rapidly. |
| “Cold moves into the beaker.” | Rephrase in terms of thermal energy moving from the hotter water/beaker to cooler surroundings. Ask students to identify the hotter and cooler objects. |
| “Insulators stop heat completely.” | Point to the temperature data: even a good jacket usually cools. Insulators reduce the rate of heat transfer; they do not eliminate all pathways. |
| “Trapped air works because air is warm.” | Focus on whether the air is moving and on the small pockets of air. Still air is the relevant feature, not simply “warm air.” |
| “Convection only happens in air.” | Ask whether water is also able to flow. Reinforce that convection occurs in fluids: liquids and gases. |
| “Convection means heat rises.” | Clarify that heated, less dense fluid rises and cooler, denser fluid sinks; it is the movement of the fluid that transfers energy. |
| “Radiation needs air.” | Ask how energy from the Sun reaches Earth through space. Reinforce P5. |
| “A higher final temperature automatically proves the jacket was better.” | Direct students to compare starting temperatures and calculate temperature change. A smaller decrease is the relevant measure when starts are comparable. |
| “More layers are always better.” | Ask whether extra tape or tight wrapping compressed air spaces, created gaps or placed foil directly against the beaker. Design quality matters, not only quantity. |
| “My group’s result is wrong because it differs from another group’s.” | Discuss uncontrolled variables: thickness, fit, start temperature, water volume, gaps, compression and thermometer placement. Require an evidence-based interpretation rather than a matching result. |
