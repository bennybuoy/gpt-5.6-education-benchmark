Use this structure:

2. **Evidence:** Include starting and finishing temperatures, both temperature decreases and one relevant observation.

4. **Convection reasoning:** Identify where water or air could move and explain how gaps, air spaces or the opening affected the result.
5. **Radiation reasoning:** Discuss the surfaces facing the hot beaker or room and how your surface choice may have affected the result.

# TEACHER KEY

## 1. Authoritative scientific explanations

Use the following wording when explaining or correcting the science:

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

Exact temperature rankings will depend on material thickness, coverage, seams, initial temperature, probe placement and whether the top is covered. Assess the quality of students’ evidence and reasoning rather than requiring a particular numerical result.

## 2. Expected demonstration observations

Under reasonably controlled conditions:

1. **Bare beaker:** Usually shows the greatest temperature decrease.
2. **Cotton-wool beaker:** Usually shows the smallest temperature decrease if the cotton wool is thick, dry and not tightly compressed.
3. **Aluminium-foil beaker:** Often performs between the bare and cotton-wool beakers, but results can vary substantially. Foil can reduce radiation, but a tight foil layer is also a metal layer in direct contact with the beaker.

### Explanation

- Energy moves through the beaker and touching jacket layers by conduction: **P1**.
- Aluminium is a metal and therefore a good conductor: **P2**.
- Cotton wool traps pockets of still air, reducing conduction: **P3**.
- Air movement around gaps and the open top contributes to convection: **P4**.
- Every warm surface can transfer energy by radiation; a reflective foil surface can reduce this pathway: **P5–P6**.
- No material stops heat transfer completely. Effective insulation slows the rate of transfer.

## 3. Expected observations by material

| Material or condition | Expected observation | Scientific interpretation and proposition links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually cools fastest. Temperature falls quickly at first and then more slowly as the water approaches room temperature. | There is no added layer to reduce transfer. Thermal energy conducts through the glass (**P1**), fluid movement occurs in the water and surrounding air (**P4**), and energy is transferred from exposed surfaces by radiation (**P5**). It lacks the combined strategies in **P6**. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually produces a relatively small temperature decrease when thick, dry and fluffy. Performance may worsen if compressed, wet or left with large uncovered areas. | Cotton wool acts as an insulator by trapping pockets of still air (**P3**). Keeping air relatively still also limits convective movement (**P4**), supporting the conduction and convection strategies in **P6**. |
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually provides good insulation if bubbles remain intact and the beaker is well covered. Popped or flattened bubbles and open seams reduce performance. | The bubbles contain pockets of air, linking to **P3**. The enclosed air is less able to circulate, linking to **P4**. This supports the conduction and convection strategies in **P6**. |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually provide good insulation when arranged as a continuous layer. Poorly joined pieces may leave gaps that reduce effectiveness. | Foam contains many small air pockets, linking to **P3**. Its cellular structure restricts air movement, linking to **P4**. It supports the insulating-material and trapped-air strategies in **P6**. |
| **Cardboard** | Usually gives moderate insulation. Several layers or corrugated sections generally perform better than one thin, tightly flattened layer. Gaps at seams may allow greater loss. | Cardboard is a poorer conductor than metal, and layered or corrugated cardboard can hold air pockets (**P3**). Air movement through open seams is explained by **P4**. The result depends on whether the design applies **P6** effectively. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Results are variable. A shiny exposed surface or a reflective surface facing an air space may reduce cooling. Foil tightly touching the beaker may provide limited insulation and can become warm rapidly. | Heat can move through touching solids by **P1**. Aluminium is a metal, so **P2** applies. Its reflective surface can reduce transfer by radiation, linking to **P5** and the reflective-surface strategy in **P6**. Foil should not be described simply as “the best insulator.” |
| **Masking tape** | Tape alone provides little bulk insulation. It may improve a design by closing seams and holding layers in place. Excessive tape may compress cotton wool or bubble wrap. | Closing gaps can reduce air exchange and convection (**P4**, **P6**). Compressing an air-trapping material can reduce the benefit described in **P3**. Continuous direct-contact paths also allow conduction as described in **P1**. |
| **Layered combination** | A well-built combination often performs better than a single thin material. Likely successful designs include an air-trapping layer, sealed seams and a reflective surface. | The design can address all three pathways in **P6**: insulating materials reduce conduction, trapped still air reduces convection and a reflective surface reduces radiation. The detailed mechanisms are explained by **P1–P5**. |
| **Open top, large gaps or uncovered base** | These areas may remain major routes of energy transfer even if the sides are well insulated. | Moving warm air contributes to convection (**P4**). Exposed surfaces also transfer energy by radiation (**P5**). A complete design should apply **P6** rather than focusing on only one pathway. |

### Useful observational patterns

- All trials should show an overall temperature decrease.
- Cooling is often faster early in a test because the temperature difference between the water and surroundings is greater.
- A revised design is considered better if it has a smaller 10-minute temperature decrease under comparable conditions.
- Identical group readings are expected because students share a thermometer. Individual recording, graphing, calculations and reasoning are still required.
- If starting temperatures differ substantially, comparisons are less reliable. Comparing temperature decreases is better than comparing final temperatures alone, but it does not fully correct an unfair starting-temperature difference.

## 4. Expected analysis and calculations

Students should calculate:

\[
\text{Temperature decrease}=T_0-T_{10}
\]

\[
\text{Average cooling rate}=\frac{T_0-T_{10}}{10\text{ min}}
\]

\[
\text{Improvement}=\text{initial decrease}-\text{revised decrease}
\]

A positive improvement means that the revised design lost fewer degrees over the test.

### Example only

- Jacket 1: \(80^\circ\text{C}\rightarrow65^\circ\text{C}\)  
  Decrease \(=15^\circ\text{C}\); average cooling rate \(=1.5^\circ\text{C/min}\)

- Revised jacket: \(79^\circ\text{C}\rightarrow69^\circ\text{C}\)  
  Decrease \(=10^\circ\text{C}\); average cooling rate \(=1.0^\circ\text{C/min}\)

- Improvement: \(15-10=5^\circ\text{C}\)

Do not require students’ results to match these example values.

## 5. Model final explanation

A suitable response should use the student’s actual data. For example:

> The revised jacket slowed cooling more because its temperature decreased by 10°C, compared with 15°C for the first jacket. The added foam layer and sealed seams reduced the routes through which thermal energy could leave. **Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.** The foam and cotton-wool layers reduced this transfer because **insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.** Closing the gaps also reduced moving air. **Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.** The foil surface may also have reduced energy transfer from the exposed surface because **radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.** Overall, the revised jacket used several strategies because **reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

Where aluminium is discussed, add:

> **Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.**

Students do not need to reproduce this full wording exactly, but their explanation should be scientifically consistent with it.

## 6. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| **“Cold entered the beaker.”** | Ask students to track the direction of thermal energy. The hot water transferred energy to the cooler surroundings; “cold” is not a substance moving inward. |
| **“Insulation creates heat.”** | Compare the starting and finishing temperatures. The jacket did not add energy; it reduced the rate at which energy was transferred away. |
| **“A material keeps water hot because it feels warm.”** | Feeling warm only shows that some energy reached that surface. Judge performance using controlled temperature data, not touch. Students should not touch hot apparatus. |
| **“Conduction means particles travel through the solid.”** | Reuse **P1**. Energy is transferred by direct contact between particles; the material itself does not move through the jacket. |
| **“Convection happens in cardboard or foil.”** | Reuse **P4** and identify the moving fluid. Convection occurs in liquids and gases, such as the water or air, not in a solid layer. |
| **“All air spaces improve insulation.”** | Distinguish small, enclosed air pockets from large open gaps. Enclosed still air supports **P3**; large connected gaps can allow air movement described by **P4**. |
| **“More layers must always be better.”** | Ask whether the extra layer trapped air, sealed a gap, reflected radiation or instead compressed the insulation and created more contact. Performance depends on arrangement, not just layer count. |
| **“Foil must be the best insulator because it is shiny.”** | Apply both **P2** and **P5–P6**. Foil can reduce radiation, but aluminium is also a good conductor. Its effect depends on contact, air gaps and surface arrangement. |
| **“Foil cannot help because metal is a conductor.”** | Explain that one material can affect different transfer modes differently. Aluminium conducts well, but a reflective surface can reduce radiation. |
| **“Radiation only comes from the Sun or flames.”** | Reuse **P5**. Any object above absolute zero emits electromagnetic radiation; warm beakers and water transfer some energy this way. |
| **“Radiation needs air.”** | Reuse **P5**: it does not require a medium. |
| **“The design with the highest final temperature is automatically best.”** | Check the initial temperatures first. Students should compare temperature decreases and cooling rates under controlled conditions. |
| **“One unusual reading proves the material failed.”** | Check probe depth, timing, accidental movement, water volume and transcription. Use the overall trend and repeat testing where possible. |
| **“The water does not move because the beaker stays still.”** | Use **P4**. Water can circulate within a stationary beaker because heating and cooling create density differences. |
