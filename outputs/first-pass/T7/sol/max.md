# TEACHER KEY

## Heat Transfer and Insulation Design

### 1. Use within the approved lesson sequence

Maintain the three approved phases:

1. **10 minutes:** demonstration and prediction;
2. **35 minutes:** mandatory build–test–revise–retest cycle;
3. **15 minutes:** individual explanation.

Within the practical block, do not allow groups to treat revision or Test 2 as an extension activity. Before a group rebuilds, quickly verify that every member has completed the Design 1 calculation, diagnosis, proposed change and revised rationale.

The teacher should:

- operate kettles and dispense all hot water;
- use a consistent class-set water volume and filling procedure;
- make hot equipment safe before students remove or alter jackets;
- standardise probe depth, beaker location, timing and treatment of the opening;
- aim for similar initial temperatures in both tests; and
- have every student record each live reading rather than relying on a group master sheet.

Identical raw values within a group are expected because students share an apparatus. Individual evidence is demonstrated through real-time recording, individual sketches, calculations, observations, rationale and explanation.

---

## 2. Authoritative science propositions

Use the following wording when teaching, correcting or assessing scientific explanations.

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

---

## 3. Expected demonstration result

Assuming equal water volumes, similar starting temperatures and comparable coverage:

1. **Cotton wool:** usually cools slowest and has the smallest temperature decrease.
2. **Aluminium foil:** often produces an intermediate result, but its result is highly dependent on fit and placement.
3. **Bare beaker:** usually cools fastest and has the largest temperature decrease.

This ranking should not be treated as an absolute marking rule. A loose cotton layer, a large exposed opening, different starting temperatures or a well-positioned reflective foil surface may alter the order. Students should report the measured result rather than change data to match the expected order.

## 4. Expected observations and material links

There are no fixed expected temperatures because starting temperature, room conditions, layer thickness and fit will vary. Compare each material by its **10-minute temperature fall** under otherwise similar conditions.

| Material/setup | Expected observation | Scientific explanation and proposition links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually the largest temperature fall and steepest early cooling trend. The open top and exposed sides remain major heat-loss routes. | Thermal energy passes through the glass according to **P1**. Movement within the water and around the outer surface is explained by **P4**. The warm beaker also transfers energy by **P5**. It lacks the combined measures described in **P6**. |
| **Cotton wool** | A dry, loose, continuous layer normally gives a much smaller fall than the bare beaker and is likely to be the strongest material in the demonstration. Its performance decreases if it is compressed, wet, very thin or full of open gaps. | Cotton wool holds many small air pockets, linking directly to **P3**. Restricting air movement links to **P4**, while reducing conductive and convective loss is part of **P6**. Energy still moves through the connected materials as described in **P1**. |
| **Bubble wrap** | Intact bubbles and continuous coverage normally produce a smaller temperature fall than the bare beaker. Popped or heavily compressed bubbles, open seams and uncovered regions reduce its effectiveness. | The bubbles provide pockets of air, so **P3** applies. Small enclosed spaces restrict bulk fluid movement associated with **P4**. Its role in the overall design links to **P6**, while any solid contact pathway remains subject to **P1**. |
| **Foam pieces** | A continuous, well-fitted foam layer generally gives a small temperature fall. Separate pieces with gaps are less effective; thicker coverage may help only if it remains stable and does not leave exposed routes. | Foam contains many small air spaces, linking to **P3**. These spaces also limit air movement described by **P4**. The design reduces conduction and convection in accordance with **P6**. Heat can still conduct through solid foam or gaps according to **P1**. |
| **Cardboard** | Usually provides moderate insulation. Folded or air-containing cardboard generally performs better than a thin, tightly pressed single sheet. Wet cardboard or large joins reduce performance. | Cardboard and spaces formed by its structure can retain air, linking to **P3**. Enclosing gaps can restrict the fluid movement in **P4**. Its insulating use is covered by **P6**, while conduction through the solid structure follows **P1**. |
| **Aluminium foil** | A single tight layer often gives less insulation than cotton wool, bubble wrap or foam and may be close to the bare result. An exposed reflective foil surface, especially when combined with an air-rich layer, can reduce cooling. Results are configuration-dependent. | Aluminium is governed by **P2**, so foil does not provide a thick, poor-conducting barrier. If it forms a conductive bridge to an exposed area or support, **P1** and **P2** are important. Its reflective surface can reduce the transfer described by **P5**, as specified in **P6**. |
| **Masking tape** | Tape alone should cause little measurable change because it is thin. It can improve a jacket by closing joins and holding layers in position. Excessive tape may compress cotton wool or bubble wrap and reduce their air spaces. | Tape can alter direct contact pathways under **P1**. When it seals a join and helps retain air, **P3**, **P4** and **P6** are relevant. It should be described primarily as a fastening and sealing material, not the main insulating layer. |
| **Layered combinations** | A continuous cotton, foam or bubble-wrap layer with well-sealed joins and a suitably exposed foil surface is likely to outperform a single poorly fitted layer. A gap or uncovered region may dominate the result. | This is the clearest application of **P6**: different features can target conduction, convection and radiation at the same time. Apply **P1–P5** to the particular materials and arrangement. |

### Important qualifications

- Do not insist that “more material” must always work better. Fit, compression, gaps and the location of layers matter.
- A jacket around only the sides may produce modest differences if the top remains open. Keep treatment of the top consistent between tests.
- If Design 2 performs worse, the result is still valid. Students should identify why the change may have opened a gap, compressed an air-rich layer, created extra contact or changed an uncontrolled variable.
- A probe measures **temperature**, not heat flow directly. With equal water volumes and similar conditions, temperature decrease per minute is a useful comparative indicator of cooling rate.

## 5. Expected data analysis

### Calculations

For each test:

\[
\text{Temperature fall}=T_0-T_{10}
\]

\[
\text{Average cooling rate}=\frac{T_0-T_{10}}{10\text{ min}}
\]

A successful redesign should normally have:

- a smaller 10-minute temperature fall;
- a lower average cooling rate; and
- higher temperatures at matched elapsed times, provided the starting temperatures were similar.

If the starting temperatures differ substantially, students should identify this as a limitation. A higher final temperature alone does not establish a better design if that test also started much hotter.

### Expected pathway reasoning

**Conduction**

A suitable pathway is:

hot water → beaker glass → touching jacket materials or base → outer surface/support/surroundings.

Students should connect this to **P1**. If foil is present, they should also apply **P2**. Air-rich materials should be connected to **P3**.

**Convection**

Students may identify:

- movement of water within the beaker;
- movement of air above the opening;
- movement of air through large or connected gaps; and
- reduced movement within small enclosed spaces.

These ideas should be linked to **P4**. A side jacket does not stop convection inside the water, but it can reduce overall heat loss and restrict movement in air spaces around the beaker.

**Radiation**

Students should recognise that a warm beaker or jacket can transfer energy even without direct contact with the surroundings. This must be explained using **P5**. An exposed reflective foil surface can target this component under **P6**. The simple practical does not isolate radiation from conduction and convection, so students should use language such as “supports” or “is consistent with” rather than “proves.”

### Model scientific explanation

A scientifically complete response could read:

> Design 2 was more effective if its temperature fall, **[Design 2 value] °C**, was smaller than the **[Design 1 value] °C** fall for Design 1. **Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.** The continuous foam, cotton wool, bubble wrap or cardboard layer interrupted the contact pathway from the beaker to the surroundings. **Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.** **Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.** Enclosing small gaps limited air movement, although movement could still occur in the water and near an open top. **Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.** A suitably exposed reflective foil surface can reduce this part of the energy transfer. If aluminium foil formed a contact pathway, **metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.** Overall, **reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

Students do not need to use the model’s exact structure, but their science should be consistent with these statements and supported by their own results.

## 6. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| **“The insulation makes heat” or “cotton wool warms the water.”** | Point to the falling temperature in both tests. The jacket reduces the rate of transfer; it does not create thermal energy. Use **P6**. |
| **“Cold gets into the beaker.”** | Ask students to trace thermal energy from the hotter water to the cooler surroundings through the pathways in **P1**, **P4** and **P5**. |
| **“An insulator stops all heat transfer.”** | Emphasise that temperatures still fall. **P6** says “reducing heat loss,” not eliminating it. |
| **“Only metals conduct.”** | Use **P1**: conduction can occur through a material. Use **P2** to explain why metals transfer energy rapidly, not why they are the only conductors. |
| **“Particles travel through the solid from the beaker to the room.”** | Revisit **P1**, particularly “without the material itself moving.” Energy is transferred through interactions; the solid does not flow outwards. |
| **“Convection means heat rises.”** | Use the complete wording of **P4**. It is movement of a fluid due to heating-related density differences, not a substance called “heat” floating upwards. |
| **“Convection happens through the cardboard or glass.”** | Distinguish bulk movement of a liquid or gas under **P4** from transfer through a stationary material under **P1**. |
| **“Air is always an insulator.”** | Emphasise the word **still** in **P3**. Air moving through large open gaps can transfer energy by **P4**. |
| **“Radiation only comes from the Sun or a flame.”** | Use **P5**. Warm classroom objects can transfer thermal energy by electromagnetic waves. |
| **“Radiation needs air.”** | Use the final clause of **P5**: it “does not require a medium.” |
| **“Foil must always be the best because it is shiny.”** | Contrast **P2** with **P5–P6**. Foil can reduce radiation, but it is also a metal and does not by itself trap a thick air layer. Placement determines the overall result. |
| **“Foil must always be the worst because metal conducts.”** | Acknowledge **P2**, then apply **P5–P6**. A reflective outer surface can still improve a layered design by reducing radiation. |
| **“Compressing cotton wool as tightly as possible improves it.”** | Compression can remove the pockets central to **P3**. Compare loose and compressed structures visually without adding another test phase. |
| **“The design with the highest final temperature is automatically best.”** | Require comparison of starting temperature, temperature fall and average cooling rate. |
| **“A worse redesign means the investigation failed.”** | Treat it as evidence. Ask which intended feature changed and whether a gap, compression, contact pathway or unfair starting condition explains the result. |
| **“One mixed-material test proves which transfer mode caused the result.”** | Explain that several modes operate simultaneously. Students should connect plausible mechanisms to evidence and acknowledge that the modes were not isolated. |
| **“The probe measures heat.”** | Clarify that it measures temperature. Under controlled conditions, temperature decrease is used to compare cooling rates. |

- [ ] Units are included.
- [ ] At least two specific Test 1 observations are recorded.
- [ ] The 10-minute temperature fall is calculated correctly.
- [ ] The average cooling rate is calculated correctly.

### Mandatory revision evidence

- [ ] The student uses at least one number and one observation to diagnose Design 1.
- [ ] One main design change is stated precisely.
- [ ] A fair-test feature that will remain unchanged is identified.
- [ ] The revised rationale predicts an outcome and explains why.
- [ ] A separate revised sketch is present, with changed features clearly marked.
- [ ] This evidence was completed before the group began Test 2.

### Test 2 and comparison evidence

- [ ] A second set of six live readings is recorded individually.
- [ ] At least two Test 2 observations are recorded.
- [ ] Design 2 temperature fall and average cooling rate are calculated.
- [ ] The comparison uses numerical evidence from both tests.
- [ ] The student acknowledges significantly different starting temperatures or another relevant limitation.
- [ ] A worse or unchanged result is reported honestly rather than altered to match expectations.

### Final explanation and authenticity

- [ ] The explanation is individually written.
- [ ] It includes conduction, convection and radiation.
- [ ] It links at least one design feature to each relevant heat-transfer pathway.
- [ ] It cites the student’s own temperature results.
- [ ] It evaluates the revision and includes a limitation.
- [ ] The teacher observed the student recording at least some readings in real time, or the student can answer a quick verification question such as: “What was your 6-minute reading?” or “Why did your group make that revision?”

A group’s identical raw readings are acceptable; a single shared sheet, copied calculations or one shared explanation are not sufficient evidence for individual participation.
