# TEACHER KEY

## 1. Authoritative science propositions

Use the following wording when providing or marking scientific explanations.

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

---

## 2. Expected demonstration observations

All beakers should decrease in temperature over time because thermal energy is transferred from the warmer water and beaker to the cooler surroundings.

Under typical classroom conditions:

- The **cotton-wool-wrapped beaker** should generally cool slowest.
- The **bare beaker** should generally cool fastest.
- The result for **aluminium foil** may be intermediate or close to the bare beaker, depending on how tightly it is wrapped, which side faces outward, the number of layers and whether an air space is present.

Do not treat the exact order of foil and bare as guaranteed. Use the measured data and discuss the competing effects of conduction and radiation.

### Cotton wool

Expected observation:

- Smaller temperature drop and lower cooling rate than the bare beaker.
- The result is usually stronger if the cotton wool remains thick and contains many air spaces.
- Compressed or wet cotton wool is likely to be less effective.

Relevant propositions:

- **P3:** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.
- **P6:** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

### Aluminium foil

Expected observation:

- Foil alone may provide only a modest improvement over the bare beaker.
- A reflective foil surface can reduce radiation.
- If foil is tightly wrapped against the beaker, it provides little resistance to conduction and may spread thermal energy rapidly through the foil.
- Foil is often more effective as the reflective part of a layered design than as the only layer.

Relevant propositions:

- **P2:** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.
- **P5:** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.
- **P6:** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

### Bare beaker

Expected observation:

- Usually shows a larger temperature drop than a well-insulated beaker.
- Thermal energy can move through the glass, be transferred to moving air around the beaker, and leave by radiation.
- Convection also occurs within the water and from the exposed water surface.

Relevant propositions:

- **P1:** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.
- **P4:** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.
- **P5:** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

## 3. Expected observations for practical materials

Actual rankings will depend on thickness, coverage, compression, gaps, starting temperatures and construction quality. Accept conclusions supported by valid student data.

| Material | Expected observation | Scientific link |
|---|---|---|
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually produces a smaller temperature drop than a bare beaker. Multiple intact layers are often more effective than one layer. Flattened or punctured bubbles reduce effectiveness. | The trapped air makes the material a poor conductor and restricts air movement: **P3, P6**. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually a strong insulator when thick, dry and not highly compressed. Loose fibres trap air. | Trapped still air reduces conduction and convection: **P3, P6**. |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually reduce cooling well, particularly when gaps between pieces are minimised. Thick or continuous coverage should outperform scattered pieces. | Foam contains trapped air and is a poor conductor: **P3, P6**. |
| **Cardboard** | Usually provides moderate insulation. Layered or corrugated cardboard should outperform a thin, single layer. Gaps at joins can weaken the design. | Cardboard and its air spaces reduce conduction; enclosed air spaces can reduce convection: **P3, P6**. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Variable when used alone. It may reduce radiant heat loss, but tight contact does not provide a strong conductive barrier. It is often useful as a reflective outer layer combined with an air-trapping material. | Metal conduction: **P2**. Radiation and reflective surfaces: **P5, P6**. |
| **Masking tape** | Small amounts should have little effect. Large areas may compress insulation, close useful air pockets or form gaps when they loosen. It is mainly a joining material rather than the main insulation. | Its effect depends on whether it helps retain still-air layers or creates direct heat pathways: **P1, P3, P6**. |
| **Bare glass** | Usually cools more rapidly than effective layered jackets. | Heat can be transferred by conduction, convection and radiation: **P1, P4, P5, P6**. |

### Likely effective combinations

A successful design may include:

- cotton wool, bubble wrap or foam to provide insulating layers;
- intact, enclosed pockets of still air;
- limited compression of air-trapping materials;
- continuous coverage with few gaps;
- secure joins that do not greatly compress the insulation;
- aluminium foil positioned as a reflective surface;
- more than one layer addressing different heat-transfer pathways.

The scientific synthesis is **P6:** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

## 4. Expected data analysis

Students should calculate:

\[
\text{temperature drop}=\text{starting temperature}-\text{temperature at 10 min}
\]

\[
\text{average cooling rate}=\frac{\text{temperature drop}}{10\text{ min}}
\]

The more effective design should normally have:

- a smaller temperature drop;
- a lower average cooling rate; and
- a higher final temperature, provided the starting conditions were sufficiently similar.

Students should not rely only on final temperature if the two tests began at different temperatures. They should compare temperature drops or cooling rates and acknowledge any major difference in starting temperature.

A revised design is not guaranteed to perform better. A valid response may conclude that it was less effective if the conclusion is supported by data and a plausible explanation, such as:

- insulation was compressed;
- a useful air gap was removed;
- a new gap exposed part of the beaker;
- the foil orientation or position was ineffective;
- probe depth changed;
- water amounts or starting temperatures differed;
- timing began late;
- the jacket became wet or loose.

## 5. Model scientific explanation

A high-quality response should communicate the following ideas using the evidence from the student’s own tests:

> Design 2 was more effective because its temperature drop was smaller and its average cooling rate was lower. **Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.** The insulating layers reduced this pathway through the sides of the beaker. **Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.** The trapped air also limited moving air within the jacket. **Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.** Some convection could still occur in the water and near the uncovered top. **Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.** A reflective foil surface could reduce this pathway. Overall, **reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

Where students discuss foil as a metal, the correct explanation is:

> **Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.**

Students do not need to reproduce every proposition word for word, but their explanations should be scientifically consistent with them.

## 6. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| “Insulation makes heat” or “cotton adds heat.” | Point out that the water still cooled. The insulation reduced the rate of energy transfer; it did not create thermal energy. Use **P3** and **P6**. |
| “Cold moved into the water.” | Ask students to compare the temperatures of the water and surroundings and trace the energy pathway. Thermal energy was transferred from the warmer system to cooler surroundings. |
| “Thicker is always better.” | Thickness can help only if the design retains useful air spaces and coverage. Compression, gaps, wet materials or poor joins may reduce performance. Use **P3**. |
| “Any air gap is good.” | Distinguish a small pocket of still air from a large open gap in which air can circulate. Use **P3**, **P4** and **P6**. |
| “Convection happens in solids.” | Reinforce **P4:** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating. In a solid jacket, energy transfer through the material is described using conduction. |
| “Conduction means the material moves.” | Use **P1:** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving. |
| “Foil must be the best because it is shiny.” | Explain that foil has competing properties. It can reduce radiation when used as a reflective surface, but it is also metal. Use **P2**, **P5** and **P6**. |
| “Metal attracts heat” or “metal creates cold.” | Use **P2:** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly. Metal transfers energy quickly; it does not attract heat or create cold. |
| “Radiation needs air.” | Use **P5:** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium. |
| “Radiation only comes from the Sun.” | Explain that objects emit and absorb thermal radiation. Use **P5**. |
| “The highest final temperature automatically means the best design.” | Check whether starting temperatures and water amounts were comparable. Have students calculate temperature drop and cooling rate. |
| “Our revision failed, so the experiment was wrong.” | A less effective revision is still valid evidence. Ask what design change or testing variable could explain the measured result. |
| “The three heat-transfer modes occur separately.” | Use **P6** to show that several pathways can operate at the same time and that a layered design may reduce more than one pathway. |
