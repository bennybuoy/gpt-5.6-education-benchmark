# TEACHER KEY

## 1. Expected observations and proposition links

Actual rankings may vary because of layer thickness, compression, gaps, surface orientation, starting temperature and measurement error. Reward evidence-based interpretations rather than treating one class result as universally definitive.

### Demonstration

| Material/configuration | Expected observation | Scientific interpretation and proposition links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually has the greatest or a relatively large temperature decrease. The exposed glass and water surface allow several pathways of heat loss. Warm air may be observed or inferred to rise near the beaker. | **P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving. Energy conducts through the glass and into objects or air in contact with it. **P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating. Air warmed near the beaker rises and is replaced by cooler air; convection also occurs within the water. **P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium. The warm beaker also transfers energy by radiation. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually cools more slowly than the bare beaker, particularly when dry, reasonably thick and not tightly compressed. Its outside may feel less warm. Gaps or compressed areas reduce performance. | **P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor. Cotton fibres create many air pockets. This reduces conduction and limits bulk air movement. Links also apply to **P1**, **P4** and **P6**. |
| **Aluminium foil alone** | The result can be mixed. A shiny outward-facing surface may reduce radiant heat loss, but a thin foil layer in close contact with glass provides little resistance to conduction and may cool faster than thick cotton wool. Loose or layered foil that traps air may perform differently. | **P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly. Therefore, foil should not automatically be described as a good conduction insulator. **P5** applies to energy transferred by radiation. A reflective surface can reduce radiant exchange. **P6** applies because successful designs may combine reflective foil with insulating, air-trapping layers. |
| **Cotton wool plus foil or another combined design** | Often performs better than a single thin material if the cotton or foam traps still air, gaps are minimised and the reflective surface is appropriately positioned. | **P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces). |

Do not promise that foil must beat the bare beaker. Thin foil touching the glass may conduct energy readily, while its reflective surface can reduce radiation. Experimental construction determines the net result.

### Practical materials

| Material | Expected observation | Relevant propositions |
|---|---|---|
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually slows cooling because sealed bubbles trap air. Punctured, flattened or tightly taped bubble wrap may be less effective. Keep it away from direct high-temperature hazards. | **P3**, **P4**, **P6**; conduction through contacting solids relates to **P1**. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually effective when thick, dry and loosely packed. Compression removes some air pockets and can increase solid-to-solid contact. | **P1**, **P3**, **P4**, **P6**. |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually effective because the material structure contains many small air pockets. Gaps between pieces can allow warm air movement and create heat-loss paths. | **P1**, **P3**, **P4**, **P6**. |
| **Cardboard** | Provides moderate insulation, especially in multiple layers or corrugated form. Usually less effective than thick foam or cotton wool of comparable coverage. Wet cardboard performs poorly. | **P1**, **P3**, **P6**. Corrugated cardboard may trap still air, linking to **P4**. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Useful chiefly as a reflective surface in this task. It is not expected to provide strong conduction insulation as a single thin, tightly contacting layer. Crumpled or layered foil may also trap air, complicating interpretation. | **P2**, **P5**, **P6**. |
| **Masking tape** | Holds the design in place but may compress insulation or form thermal bridges. Excess tape can reduce trapped air and increase direct contact paths. | **P1**, **P3**, **P6**. |
| **Open top/exposed water surface** | Often a major source of cooling through moving warm air and evaporation. Students may identify it as a weakness, but the probe must remain usable and all designs must follow the same agreed testing conditions. | **P4**, **P6**. Radiation from exposed surfaces relates to **P5**. |
| **Gaps and loose joins** | May increase cooling by allowing warmed air to escape and cooler air to replace it. | **P4**, **P6**. |
| **Beaker base touching the bench** | Provides a direct contact pathway. A foam or cardboard base layer may reduce this loss if allowed and used consistently and safely. | **P1**, **P3**, **P6**. |

## 2. Authoritative scientific explanations

Use the following exact wording when consolidating student findings or marking the final explanation:

- **P1. Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.**
- **P2. Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.**
- **P3. Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.**
- **P4. Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.**
- **P5. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.**
- **P6. Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

### Expected application to a successful design

A strong response should explain that:

1. Thick cotton wool, bubble wrap, foam or corrugated cardboard can reduce energy transfer through the jacket because they trap air and provide poor-conducting layers (**P1, P3**).
2. Small, enclosed air pockets are useful, whereas open gaps may allow air to circulate. Reducing air movement around the beaker reduces convection (**P4, P6**).
3. Aluminium foil is metal and conducts readily, so it should not be credited as a strong conduction insulator by itself (**P2**). Its shiny surface may reduce radiation when used as a reflective layer (**P5, P6**).
4. Combining insulating layers, trapped still air and a reflective surface can address all three heat-transfer modes (**P6**).
5. The better design is the one with the smaller temperature decrease over a comparable ten-minute interval, subject to measurement uncertainty and fair-test conditions.

### Sample evidence-based explanation

Jacket 2 was more effective because its temperature decreased by less than Jacket 1 over ten minutes. The added foam and loosely packed cotton wool contained air pockets, reducing conduction and limiting the movement of air around the beaker. Gaps were taped without heavily compressing the insulating layer, reducing convection. The outward-facing foil provided a reflective surface that could reduce radiation. This design therefore addressed conduction, convection and radiation. If initial temperatures differed, the conclusion should be treated cautiously and supported by the shapes or rates shown on the graph.

## 3. Interpreting student data

### Suitable calculations

- Temperature decrease:  
  \[
  \Delta T = T_{\text{initial}}-T_{\text{final}}
  \]
- For tests with reasonably similar starting temperatures, the smaller decrease generally indicates better insulation.
- Students may compare the steepness of the two graph lines: a less steep downward trend indicates a slower cooling rate.

### Fair-test variables

Acceptable controlled variables include:

- water volume;
- beaker size and type;
- test duration;
- two-minute measurement intervals;
- probe depth and position;
- not allowing the probe to touch the glass;
- location and room conditions;
- extent of top coverage;
- timing procedure;
- comparable initial temperatures;
- same jacket testing criteria.

If starting temperatures differ substantially, students should not rely only on final temperature. They should compare temperature changes and recognise the limitation. A perfectly fair comparison may require a repeat, although the approved lesson sequence only requires the two stated tests.

### Likely sources of error

- delayed start of timing;
- different initial temperatures;
- thermometer probe touching the glass;
- inconsistent probe depth;
- reading or transcription error;
- heat loss while the teacher pours water;
- different water volumes;
- jacket shifting between readings;
- changing room airflow;
- compressed, wet or damaged materials;
- inconsistent top openings;
- students holding the beaker or probe differently.

## 4. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| **“Cold moved into the beaker.”** | Ask students to track the thermal energy instead. The hotter water transfers energy to cooler surroundings; describe the direction of energy transfer rather than “cold entering.” |
| **“Insulation makes heat” or “adds warmth.”** | Point out that no heater is inside the jacket. Insulation reduces the rate of energy transfer; it does not create thermal energy. Compare both cooling curves. |
| **“Foil is always the best insulator because it looks shiny.”** | Separate conduction from radiation. Use **P2** to explain the metal and **P5–P6** to explain the possible benefit of a reflective surface. A thin contacting foil layer may still conduct energy readily. |
| **“All trapped air causes convection.”** | Distinguish small pockets of still air from a large open air space. Restricted air movement can reduce convection; open gaps can permit circulation. Reinforce **P3, P4 and P6**. |
| **“Convection occurs in solids.”** | Ask what bulk material is moving. Use **P4**: convection involves a fluid—liquid or gas. Energy transfer through a stationary solid is addressed by **P1**. |
| **“Radiation only comes from the Sun” or “only flames radiate.”** | Explain with **P5** that warm objects transfer thermal energy by electromagnetic waves; no direct contact is required. |
| **“Shiny surfaces stop all heat transfer.”** | Clarify that reflective surfaces target radiation, not all energy-transfer pathways. Conduction through contact and convection in fluids may still occur. Apply **P6**. |
| **“A warm outside surface proves the jacket is the best.”** | A warm outside may indicate that energy has transferred through the jacket. Direct students to compare measured temperature decreases and graph gradients. Touch is qualitative and must not replace probe data. |
| **“More layers must always be better.”** | Ask whether layers are compressed, wet, gapped or creating extra contact. Design quality and trapped still air matter, not simply layer count. |
| **“The highest final temperature automatically wins.”** | Check whether the tests began at the same temperature. Compare temperature decrease, test duration and curve shape, and note uncertainty. |
| **“Energy disappears when the water cools.”** | Ask where the energy went. It transferred to the beaker, jacket, air, bench and wider surroundings. |
| **“Conduction means the material itself travels outward.”** | Reinforce **P1**: energy is transferred by direct contact between particles without the material itself moving. |
| **“Evaporation and convection are exactly the same.”** | Explain that both can contribute to cooling at an open surface, but evaporation involves higher-energy particles escaping the liquid, whereas **P4** describes energy transfer by bulk fluid movement due to density differences. |
