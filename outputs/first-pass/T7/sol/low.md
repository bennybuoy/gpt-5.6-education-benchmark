# TEACHER KEY

## 1. Expected demonstration observations

Actual rankings may vary with layer thickness, tightness, exposed surfaces, starting temperatures and measurement error. Accept conclusions that are supported by reliable data.

| Condition | Expected observation and interpretation | Relevant propositions |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually cools fastest because there is no added insulating layer or reflective barrier. Heat can transfer through the glass and exposed surfaces; warmed air can move away from the beaker; thermal radiation can leave the surface. | P1, P4, P5, P6 |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually cools slowest when it is dry, thick and not compressed. Its fibres trap pockets of still air, reducing conduction and limiting air movement within the layer. | P3, P4, P6 |
| **Aluminium foil** | May cool more slowly than the bare beaker because a shiny surface can reduce heat loss by radiation. However, foil is a metal and conducts thermal energy well. If it is tightly wrapped against the beaker, conduction may reduce its overall effectiveness. Its performance depends on orientation, gaps and whether it is combined with an air-trapping layer. | P1, P2, P5, P6 |

Do not insist on a predetermined ranking between cotton wool and foil if student data differ. Check whether the comparison was fair and whether the explanation accounts for the construction.

## 2. Expected observations for available jacket materials

### Bubble wrap

**Expected observation:** Bubble wrap should reduce cooling because its sealed bubbles trap air. Multiple layers generally work better than one layer, provided the wrap remains intact and gaps are limited. Students may notice that the outside remains less warm than a thin, tightly contacting conductive layer.

**Links:** P3, P4 and P6.

### Cotton wool

**Expected observation:** Loose, dry cotton wool should reduce cooling effectively because it contains many small air pockets. Compressing or wetting it is likely to make it less effective. Uncovered gaps around the top can still allow heat loss.

**Links:** P3, P4 and P6.

### Aluminium foil

**Expected observation:** Aluminium foil can reduce heat transfer by radiation when its reflective surface is appropriately positioned. However, foil alone may not provide strong insulation because aluminium conducts energy rapidly. Foil combined with bubble wrap, cotton wool or an air gap may perform better than foil tightly touching the beaker.

**Links:** P1, P2, P5 and P6.

### Cardboard

**Expected observation:** Corrugated cardboard should insulate better than thin, dense cardboard because its structure traps air. Cardboard may also provide structural support and reduce gaps. Its effect on radiation is limited unless combined with a reflective layer.

**Links:** P3, P4 and P6.

### Foam pieces

**Expected observation:** Foam should reduce cooling because its structure contains many trapped air pockets. Performance improves when pieces fit closely enough to reduce large gaps and air circulation, while retaining the foam’s air-filled structure.

**Links:** P3, P4 and P6.

### Masking tape

**Expected observation:** Tape is mainly useful for holding layers in place and sealing gaps. It is not expected to be the main insulating material. Excessive tape or compressed insulation can reduce trapped air spaces.

**Links:** P3, P4 and P6.

### Exposed top and gaps

**Expected observation:** Designs with a large uncovered opening or loose joins may cool more rapidly because warmed air above the water can move away and be replaced by cooler air. The probe opening must remain accessible, so students should minimise—but not completely block—the opening.

**Links:** P4 and P6.

## 3. Authoritative scientific explanations

Use the following exact wording when consolidating students’ explanations:

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

### Features of a strong student conclusion

A strong response should:

- identify the better design using temperature decrease or average cooling rate;
- cite specific data from both tests;
- connect direct contact and material choice to conduction;
- connect trapped air, gaps and movement of warm air to convection;
- connect aluminium foil or another reflective surface to radiation;
- explain how the revision responded to evidence from Test 1;
- recognise uncertainty where starting temperatures or other variables differed.

## 4. Data interpretation

Calculate:

\[
\text{Temperature decrease}=\text{starting temperature}-\text{final temperature}
\]

\[
\text{Average cooling rate}=\frac{\text{temperature decrease}}{10\text{ minutes}}
\]

The more effective design normally has the **smaller temperature decrease** and **lower average cooling rate**.

If Test 2 begins at a different temperature, students may still make a cautious comparison using the temperature decrease or cooling rate. Prompt them to acknowledge that unequal starting temperatures weaken the comparison. A graph of temperature against time may also reveal whether one design cooled more slowly.

## 5. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Teacher response |
|---|---|
| “Cold entered the beaker.” | Ask students to identify the warmer and cooler objects and trace the direction in which thermal energy was transferred. Emphasise that the investigation measures thermal energy leaving the warmer water and beaker. |
| “Cotton wool makes heat.” | Ask whether the cotton wool has an energy source. Clarify that it slows the transfer of thermal energy rather than producing energy. Use P3. |
| “Thicker materials are always better.” | Compare thick metal with air-filled foam. Effectiveness depends on conductivity, trapped air, gaps, compression and surface properties, not thickness alone. |
| “Aluminium foil is an insulator because it keeps food warm.” | Distinguish its reflective effect from its conductivity. Use P2 and P5. Foil can reduce radiation but aluminium itself is a good conductor. |
| “Shiny foil stops all heat transfer.” | Explain that radiation is only one transfer mode. Conduction and convection can continue. Use P6. |
| “Air is always a good insulator.” | Clarify that **still, trapped air** is useful. Air moving through large gaps can transfer thermal energy by convection. Use P3 and P4. |
| “Convection occurs in solids.” | Ask whether the material is flowing. Use P4 to distinguish fluid movement from conduction through a solid. |
| “Radiation needs air.” | Use P5 and note that radiation can travel without a medium. |
| “The design with the lowest final temperature is automatically worst.” | Check starting temperatures. Compare temperature decrease and cooling rate, not final temperature alone. |
| “One unexpected reading proves the design failed.” | Check probe depth, timing, reading technique, loose layers and recording errors. Look for the overall trend rather than relying on one value. |
| “A higher starting temperature makes a design better.” | Explain that starting temperature is a controlled variable, not evidence of insulation quality. Compare change over the same duration. |
| “Compressing cotton wool or foam makes it better.” | Point out that compression can remove air pockets. Use P3 and compare a loose sample with a compressed sample. |
