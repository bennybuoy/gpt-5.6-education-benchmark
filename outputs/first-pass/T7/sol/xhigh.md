# TEACHER KEY

## 1. Authoritative science propositions

Use the following exact wording when teaching, correcting or marking:

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

---

## 2. Expected observations and material explanations

Exact rankings will depend on layer thickness, coverage, starting temperature and construction quality. Treat measured evidence as primary; do not mark a student wrong merely because an uncontrolled test produces a different ranking.

### Teacher demonstration

Under comparable conditions, the likely pattern is:

1. **Cotton wool:** slowest cooling or smallest temperature drop.
2. **Aluminium foil:** intermediate or sometimes similar to the bare beaker.
3. **Bare beaker:** fastest cooling or largest temperature drop.

The foil result can vary substantially. Foil directly against the glass is not a thick insulating layer, but its reflective surface can reduce radiation. If it encloses an air gap or reduces air movement, it may perform better than expected. Because all three beakers remain open, heat loss from the water surface is common to all conditions and can reduce the size of the differences.

### Material-by-material key

| Material or condition | Expected observation | Scientific interpretation and proposition links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually has one of the largest temperature drops and steepest cooling curves. | Energy transfers through the glass and from the exposed surfaces. Conduction through contacting materials is described by **P1**. Moving water and surrounding air can transfer energy by convection as in **P4**. The exposed beaker also transfers energy by radiation as in **P5**. It has few of the heat-loss-reduction features described in **P6**. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually produces a smaller temperature drop than bare glass. Loose, dry cotton generally performs better than heavily compressed or wet cotton. | Its fibres create many small pockets of still air. This is consistent with **P3**. Restricting large-scale air movement also reduces convection, linking to **P4** and **P6**. Compressing or wetting the cotton removes or replaces some air pockets and can reduce its effectiveness. |
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually gives a small temperature drop if bubbles are intact and coverage is continuous. | The sealed bubbles provide pockets of still air, linking to **P3**. The small enclosed spaces restrict air circulation, linking to **P4** and **P6**. Popped bubbles, large uncovered gaps or a very thin layer reduce performance. |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually insulate well when they form continuous coverage without large open gaps. | Foam contains many small air-filled spaces, linking to **P3**. Its cellular structure limits fluid movement, linking to **P4** and **P6**. Loosely arranged pieces with open channels can allow air circulation and perform less well. |
| **Cardboard** | A single dense sheet usually gives modest insulation. Layered or corrugated cardboard generally performs better. | Energy can conduct through the solid material according to **P1**, but layered or corrugated cardboard also contains air spaces, linking to **P3** and **P6**. Large open channels are less useful than enclosed spaces because air may circulate. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Results are variable. One layer directly touching the beaker may be only slightly better than bare glass; foil used as an outer reflective layer over another insulator may improve performance. | Aluminium is a metal, so **P2** applies. It should not be described as a good conduction insulator. Its separate benefit is that a reflective surface can reduce radiation, linking to **P5** and **P6**. Students should distinguish its conducting property from its reflective property. |
| **Masking tape** | Tape alone generally produces little insulation because it is thin. Used sparingly, it can improve a jacket by holding layers in place or closing gaps. Excessive tape can compress cotton, foam or bubble wrap. | Thermal energy can transfer through touching solid layers according to **P1**. Sealing unwanted gaps may reduce air movement under **P4** and **P6**, but compressing air-trapping materials reduces the benefit described by **P3**. Tape amount should be kept similar except where it is the planned revision. |
| **Combined design** | A continuous layer of cotton, bubble wrap or foam, possibly with a reflective outer surface, will often produce one of the smallest temperature drops. | Such a design can address all three pathways in **P6**: insulating materials reduce conduction, enclosed air spaces reduce convection, and a reflective outer surface reduces radiation. Apply **P1–P5** to the particular materials and arrangement used. |

### Other expected data patterns

- All beakers should decrease in temperature toward room temperature.
- Cooling is often fastest near the beginning, so the graph may curve rather than form a perfect straight line.
- A better design has a smaller \(T_0-T_{10}\) and a less steep cooling curve under fair-test conditions.
- A successful revision should reduce the 10-minute temperature drop.
- An unsuccessful revision is still valid evidence. Possible causes include compressed insulation, new gaps, wet materials, changed coverage, delayed timing, a changed probe position or different starting conditions.
- Final temperatures alone should not be compared if starting temperatures differ. Students should primarily compare temperature drops and identify unequal starts as a limitation.

## 3. Expected scientific explanation

A strong explanation should apply the propositions rather than simply state that a material “kept the heat in.”

### Conduction

Students should identify contact pathways through the beaker and jacket and use **P1**:

> Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

For cotton wool, bubble wrap, foam or layered cardboard, apply **P3**:

> Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

If aluminium is discussed, apply **P2**:

> Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

Students should therefore avoid claiming that foil is effective because aluminium is a poor conductor.

### Convection

Students should identify moving water within the beaker, surrounding air and any air able to circulate through large jacket gaps. Use **P4**:

> Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

Small enclosed air spaces limit circulation; large open gaps can permit it.

### Radiation

Students should recognise that radiant transfer occurs even without direct contact. Use **P5**:

> Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

A reflective foil surface may reduce this pathway. A student who did not compare surface types should appropriately state that their own test did not isolate the radiation effect.

### Integrated design conclusion

Use **P6**:

> Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

A complete response should support the explanation with the student’s actual Test 1 and Test 2 temperature drops. It is acceptable for a student to conclude that a revision was unsuccessful, provided the conclusion matches the data and discusses plausible reasons.

## 4. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| **“Insulation makes heat.”** | Point out that there is no energy source in the jacket and that every graph still trends toward room temperature. Insulation reduces the rate of heat loss; it does not create thermal energy. Revisit **P6**. |
| **“Cold gets into the beaker.”** | Draw an energy-transfer arrow from the warmer water toward the cooler surroundings. Ask which object initially has the higher temperature. Describe the observed change as thermal energy leaving the warmer water. |
| **“Heat rises.”** | Distinguish thermal energy from the movement of a fluid. Warmed, less-dense fluid may rise while cooler, denser fluid moves down, creating a current. Use the exact wording of **P4**. |
| **“Convection happens through cardboard or metal.”** | Ask whether the solid material is flowing. Apply **P1** to transfer through solids and **P4** only where a liquid or gas moves. |
| **“All air gaps are good insulation.”** | Compare a small enclosed pocket with a large open channel. Small enclosed pockets can remain relatively still, while air can circulate through a large connected gap. Apply **P3**, **P4** and **P6**. |
| **“Packing cotton as tightly as possible makes it better.”** | Compare loose and compressed samples or examine the students’ designs. Compression removes some trapped air spaces. Revisit **P3**. |
| **“Foil must be the best because it is shiny.”** | Separate its two properties. Aluminium is a metal and **P2** applies, while its reflective surface can affect radiation under **P5** and **P6**. Suggest comparing foil directly on glass with foil outside an air-trapping layer. |
| **“Foil is an insulator because it kept the water warm.”** | Explain that a result may come from reflection, reduced air movement or an enclosed air gap—not because aluminium is a poor conductor. Use **P2** and **P6**. |
| **“Radiation only comes from the Sun” or “radiation needs air.”** | State **P5** exactly. Ask whether radiant transfer could occur across an airless space, then connect the answer to reflective surfaces in **P6**. |
| **“Metal feels colder, so it must have a lower temperature.”** | Explain that materials in the same room can be at the same temperature but transfer energy to or from the hand at different rates. Connect the rapid transfer by metal to **P2**. |
| **“The highest final temperature automatically proves the best design.”** | Check the two starting temperatures. Require comparison of \(T_0-T_{10}\), graph shape and fair-test conditions rather than final temperature alone. |
| **“Insulation stops heat transfer completely.”** | Point to the continuing downward trend in the insulated beaker’s graph. Use the word **minimising** from **P6**, not “stopping.” |
| **“If the revision performed worse, the investigation failed.”** | Reinforce that an evidence-supported negative result is valid. Ask the student to identify design changes and uncontrolled variables that could explain the result. |

## 5. Individual evidence verification checklist

Numerical readings may legitimately be identical for students using the same beaker. However, every student must make a contemporaneous individual record and complete their own reasoning. **A single group sheet is not acceptable.**

### Before Test 1

- ☐ Student’s name is on their own resource.
- ☐ Individual demonstration prediction and reason recorded.
- ☐ Materials, layers and locations recorded.
- ☐ Initial annotated design sketch completed.
- ☐ Sketch identifies contact points, air spaces, exposed areas and outer surface.
- ☐ Teacher observed the student contributing to planning or construction.

### After Test 1

- ☐ All six readings—0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 minutes—entered on the student’s own sheet with °C.
- ☐ At least two individual observations recorded with times.
- ☐ Temperature drop calculated correctly.
- ☐ Average temperature drop per minute calculated correctly.

### Mandatory revision checkpoint

- ☐ Student identified a weakness using Test 1 evidence.
- ☐ Student stated one main design change.
- ☐ Student stated what would be kept the same.
- ☐ Revised design rationale is written in the student’s own words.
- ☐ Revised sketch clearly shows and labels the change.
- ☐ Teacher observed the student contributing to the physical revision.
- ☐ Revision was completed before Test 2; it was not treated as optional.

### After Test 2

- ☐ All six retest readings recorded on the student’s own sheet.
- ☐ At least two retest observations recorded.
- ☐ Test 2 drop and average drop per minute calculated.
- ☐ Improvement value calculated and interpreted.
- ☐ Student’s own two-series graph has title, axes, units, scale and key.
- ☐ Conclusion cites at least two numerical results.
- ☐ Any significant difference in starting temperature is identified as a limitation.

### Final explanation

- ☐ Conduction reasoning is connected to the actual materials or contact pathways.
- ☐ Convection reasoning identifies movement or restriction of a fluid.
- ☐ Radiation reasoning considers the exposed outer surface.
- ☐ All three heat-transfer modes are included.
- ☐ Claim is consistent with the student’s results.
- ☐ Explanation includes a limitation or further improvement.
- ☐ Teacher has evidence that the student personally read, timed, built, revised, graphed or explained—not merely copied the team’s work.
