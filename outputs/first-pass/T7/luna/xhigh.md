# TEACHER KEY

## Lesson use

This key follows the approved sequence:

1. Demonstration and prediction — 10 minutes  
2. Build, test, analyse, revise and retest — 35 minutes  
3. Written explanation — 15 minutes  

Temperature values will vary. Mark the **pattern, quality of evidence and reasoning**, not a particular numerical result.

---

## Authoritative science propositions

Use these exact statements when modelling, questioning or marking.

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

## 1. Expected demonstration observations

Assuming similar water volumes, starting temperatures, beaker positions and wrapping coverage, the usual pattern is:

**cotton wool = slowest cooling; aluminium foil = often intermediate; bare beaker = fastest cooling.**

The exact order may vary if the foil is tightly pressed against the beaker, if the foil is highly reflective, if the cotton is compressed, or if the top and bottom are exposed. Students should record the actual observation rather than being told to change their result.

| Beaker | Expected observation | Scientific link |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare** | Usually the largest temperature decrease and steepest cooling graph. It has no added jacket to reduce heat loss. | P1 applies to energy transfer through the beaker and other touching materials. P4 applies to movement of surrounding air. P5 applies to energy exchanged at exposed surfaces. P6 explains that the main heat-loss pathways have not been deliberately minimised. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually the smallest temperature decrease and the highest temperature after the same time. Fluffy, uncompressed cotton generally performs better than compressed cotton. | P3: **“Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.”** P4 is relevant because the structure can restrict air movement. P6 links insulating material and trapped air to reduced heat loss. Cotton is not a highly reflective surface, so radiation may still occur under P5. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Usually cools more slowly than the bare beaker, but may cool faster than cotton wool when used as a tight single layer. A shiny outer surface can improve performance. | P2: **“Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.”** Therefore foil is not automatically a good insulator. P5 and P6 explain why a reflective outer surface can reduce radiant heat loss. P1 is relevant to energy transfer through touching materials. |

### Appropriate student conclusions

Accept conclusions such as:

- “Cotton wool kept the water warmest because its structure contains many small air spaces.”
- “The foil reduced cooling compared with the bare beaker, especially with the shiny side facing out.”
- “Foil was not automatically the best material because aluminium conducts energy rapidly.”
- “My result differed from the prediction because the jackets did not have equal coverage or one design had more gaps.”

Do not require students to reproduce the usual ranking if their observations are reliable and their explanation addresses test conditions.

## 2. Expected practical observations

A good insulating jacket generally produces:

- a smaller temperature decrease over 10 minutes;
- a higher temperature at later time points;
- a less steep temperature–time graph;
- a design with few gaps and consistent coverage;
- a clear difference between the initial and revised designs.

There are no fixed expected temperatures because kettle temperature, room temperature, probe placement and construction will vary.

| Available material | Likely observation or design effect | Relevant propositions |
|---|---|---|
| **Bubble wrap** | Often performs well when the bubbles remain intact and the material is not crushed. Punctures, compression or large gaps can reduce its effect. | P3, P4 and P6 |
| **Cotton wool** | Often performs well when it remains fluffy. Compression reduces the spaces within the material. | P3, P4 and P6 |
| **Foam pieces** | Often perform well when arranged to surround the beaker. Gaps between separate pieces may allow more movement of air. | P3, P4 and P6 |
| **Cardboard** | May provide moderate insulation, especially in layers or when it creates separation from the beaker. A single thin sheet may be less effective than a thicker air-containing structure. | P1, P3 and P6 |
| **Aluminium foil** | Can help when the shiny surface faces outward and the foil is used as part of a larger design. A tight foil layer alone may not insulate well because aluminium conducts energy rapidly. | P1, P2, P5 and P6 |
| **Masking tape** | Holds layers in place and closes gaps. Excessive tape can create additional contact paths or compress insulating layers. | P1 and P6 |

A student may identify more than one mode for a material. For example, bubble wrap can affect both conduction and convection, while foil can affect conduction and radiation.

## 3. Expected responses to the three-mode prompts

### Conduction

Students should identify touching parts such as:

- hot water and the inside of the glass;
- glass and the jacket;
- touching layers of the jacket;
- foil, tape or cardboard that creates a continuous contact path.

A strong response uses P1:

**“Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.”**

For aluminium foil, students may also use P2:

**“Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.”**

Address the misconception that conduction requires the whole material to move. The material does not move as a whole, but energy can be transferred through contact between particles.

### Convection

Students should consider whether air in and around the jacket can move easily, or whether the design limits that movement. They may refer to:

- gaps between layers;
- an open top or bottom;
- compressed or loose material;
- bubbles or cellular foam;
- air moving around the outside of the beaker.

The expected scientific statement is P4:

**“Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.”**

P3 and P6 are also relevant when students explain why small, contained air spaces can improve an insulating design.

### Radiation

Students should notice shiny and dull surfaces and may explain that a shiny foil surface can reduce radiant heat loss.

Use P5:

**“Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.”**

Use P6 to connect the design feature to heat loss:

**“Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).”**

Students should not claim that foil stops all heat transfer. A reflective surface addresses one pathway, while contact and air movement may continue.

## 4. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Response or teaching move |
|---|---|
| **“Aluminium foil is always the best insulator because it is shiny.”** | Ask students to compare a foil-only design with a fluffy or cellular design. Use P2 to explain that metals conduct energy rapidly, while P5 and P6 explain the benefit of a reflective surface. |
| **“An insulator stops heat completely.”** | Emphasise that the investigation measures a rate of cooling. A successful jacket slows heat loss; it does not make the temperature remain unchanged. |
| **“Cold enters the beaker.”** | Rephrase the observation as thermal energy leaving the warmer water and moving to the surroundings. Ask which design reduced the temperature decrease. |
| **“Air is always an insulator.”** | Distinguish between air held in small spaces and air that can move. Use P3 and P4. |
| **“Convection means heat rises.”** | Explain that convection involves movement of a fluid caused by density differences during heating. Rising warm fluid is one part of a circulation pattern, not the whole definition. Use P4. |
| **“Convection only happens in liquids.”** | P4 explicitly includes liquids and gases. Ask students to identify the fluid around or between the jacket layers. |
| **“Radiation needs air or another substance to travel.”** | Use P5: radiation does not require a medium. Ask why a reflective outer surface may change the result. |
| **“If the final temperature is higher, the design is definitely better.”** | Check starting temperatures first. Compare temperature decreases and graph slopes, not final temperatures alone. |
| **“More layers or thicker material must always be better.”** | Ask whether the material was compressed, whether gaps remained, and whether the added layer changed the contact or surface conditions. Require evidence from both tests. |
| **“A group result is my individual evidence.”** | Acknowledge that group members may obtain similar apparatus readings, but each student must personally record the readings, observations, sketches, calculations and reasoning on an individual resource. |
