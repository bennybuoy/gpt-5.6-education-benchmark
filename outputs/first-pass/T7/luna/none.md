# TEACHER KEY

## Demonstration: expected observations and explanations

With equal water volumes and comparable starting temperatures, the **bare beaker is expected to cool fastest**. The **cotton-wool-wrapped beaker is generally expected to cool slowest**, while the aluminium-foil result may depend on how tightly and completely it is wrapped, the exposed surfaces and experimental conditions.

### Bare beaker
- Expected observation: greatest temperature decrease over the demonstration period.
- Explanation: The bare beaker has no added insulating layer. Heat can leave through contact with the beaker and surrounding air, moving air currents, and emission from the surface.
- Relevant propositions: **P1, P3, P4, P5 and P6**.

### Cotton wool
- Expected observation: usually the smallest temperature decrease.
- Explanation: Cotton wool contains many pockets of trapped air and reduces contact with the surrounding air and surfaces.
- Relevant propositions: **P3 and P6**, with heat-transfer pathways also interpreted using **P1, P4 and P5**.

Use the exact proposition wording where applicable:  
**P3:** “Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.”

### Aluminium foil
- Expected observation: may cool more slowly than the bare beaker, but results can vary. A reflective foil surface can reduce radiative heat loss; foil alone is not necessarily a strong insulator if it is thin, tightly wrapped or provides little trapped air.
- Relevant propositions: **P2, P5 and P6**.
- Exact wording:
  - **P2:** “Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.”
  - **P5:** “Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.”
  - **P6:** “Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).”

Accept variation caused by foil thickness, gaps, coverage, probe placement, starting temperature and handling.

## Practical investigation: expected reasoning

Students should:
- Record their own temperature readings at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 minutes for both tests.
- Produce an initial design, a revised design and a reason for the change.
- Use graphs or calculations to compare temperature decrease.
- Recognise that a more effective jacket generally produces a smaller temperature decrease, assuming comparable starting temperatures and conditions.
- Identify that layered materials, trapped air, reduced gaps and/or a reflective outer surface may reduce heat loss.
- Recognise that the lid/top, exposed beaker, probe opening, tape gaps and direct contact with surrounding surfaces can affect results.

### Scientific explanations using P1–P6

**Conduction — P1:**  
“Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.”

**Metals — P2:**  
“Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.”

This explains why aluminium foil should not automatically be described as a good insulator. Its reflective surface can be useful, especially when combined with an air gap or another insulating layer.

**Insulation and still air — P3:**  
“Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.”

This supports explanations involving cotton wool, bubble wrap and foam. Cardboard may also provide some insulation, particularly where it creates air spaces.

**Convection — P4:**  
“Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.”

Students may refer to warm air moving away and cooler air moving in. They should not describe convection as movement of a solid jacket.

**Radiation — P5:**  
“Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.”

A reflective foil surface can reduce radiative heat loss, particularly when its shiny surface faces an appropriate air space.

**Combined design — P6:**  
“Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).”

Strong explanations connect specific design features to more than one pathway and support claims with measured data.

## Suggested analysis responses

Accept scientifically justified responses supported by data. Strong responses may include:

- The best design has the smallest temperature decrease, not necessarily the highest final temperature if starting temperatures differ substantially.
- A thick, continuous layer of cotton wool, foam or bubble wrap may reduce conduction and trap air.
- Compressing cotton wool too tightly may reduce air spaces and make it less effective.
- Gaps or an exposed beaker section may allow increased heat transfer.
- A foil outer layer may reduce radiation, but foil alone may conduct heat readily.
- A loose or poorly sealed jacket may allow air movement, increasing convection.
- Differences between tests may result from starting temperature, probe position, water volume, timing, room conditions or construction differences.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Response |
|---|---|
| “Insulation creates heat.” | Ask students whether the jacket increased the water’s thermal energy. Emphasise that it reduces the rate of energy leaving. |
| “The thickest material is always the best insulator.” | Compare material type, trapped air, compression, gaps and data rather than thickness alone. |
| “Aluminium foil is always the best insulator because it feels cold/warm.” | Distinguish temperature sensation from heat-transfer performance. Explain that metals conduct well, while reflective foil can reduce radiation. Use P2, P5 and P6. |
| “Convection happens in solids.” | Ask what is moving. Convection involves movement of a fluid—liquid or gas. Use P4. |
| “Radiation needs air or direct contact.” | Use sunlight as an example of energy transfer through space. Use P5. |
| “Air always transfers heat quickly.” | Distinguish moving air from still air. Still air is a poor conductor; moving air can transfer energy by convection. Use P3 and P4. |
| “A higher final temperature proves the design is best.” | Check starting temperatures and compare temperature decreases or graph slopes. |
| “The group’s results are enough for everyone.” | Require every student to record personal readings, observations, sketches and reasoning. |
| “A failed revision is useless.” | Treat it as evidence. Students should explain what the result suggests and propose a better next change. |
