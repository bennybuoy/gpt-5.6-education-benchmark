## 3. Explain your results — 15 minutes

Write **one connected explanation of about 6–8 sentences**, not three separate word definitions. Name **conduction, convection and radiation** and connect each one to an observation or design feature.

Use these questions to plan your explanation:

- **Conduction:** At places where parts of the setup touch, what might be happening at the particles? How could your material or arrangement change this route?
- **Convection:** Where could a liquid or gas move in or around your jacket? How might heating change that movement? What did your layers, spaces or openings do?
- **Radiation:** What might happen to energy travelling from a warm surface to another surface when they are not touching? What did the foil or another outer surface suggest?
- Which design performed better? Include at least one temperature or temperature drop from **each** test.
- What would you change in a third design, and why?

# TEACHER KEY

## Lesson alignment

Use this key within the approved sequence:

1. Demonstration and prediction — **10 minutes**
2. Build, Test 1, analyse, revise and Test 2 — **35 minutes**
3. Written explanation — **15 minutes**

The revision and retest are compulsory parts of the 35-minute practical block. No additional lesson phase is required.

## Required science wording

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

## Expected observations and explanations

### General interpretation

- With the same volume, starting temperature, probe position and timing, the water temperature should generally decrease.
- A higher temperature at the same time, or a smaller value for `starting temperature − final temperature`, indicates slower cooling during that test.
- Do not require particular numerical temperatures. Kettle temperature, room temperature, jacket thickness, probe placement and gaps will vary.
- A successful revision will usually produce a smaller temperature drop, but an unsuccessful revision is valid evidence if the student recognises and explains it.

### Demonstration materials

| Material or condition | Expected observation | Relevant propositions and explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually the fastest cooling and the largest temperature drop. It provides the baseline with no jacket. | The bare beaker has no design feature deliberately reducing the three heat-transfer routes. Link to **P1, P4, P5 and P6**. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually the slowest cooling, particularly when dry, fluffy and covering the beaker well. Compressed cotton, gaps or damp cotton may be less effective. | Cotton wool contains many spaces. Link to **P1, P3, P4 and P6**. Radiation can still occur, but cotton wool is not a reflective outer surface, so **P5** may be discussed as a remaining route. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Often gives an intermediate result: generally better than bare foil under some conditions, but often less effective than thick cotton wool or foam when used alone. A loose wrap or air space may perform differently from foil pressed tightly against the beaker. | Aluminium is a metal, so direct-contact transfer through it can be rapid: **P1 and P2**. Its shiny surface can reduce radiative loss: **P5 and P6**. The arrangement determines whether it also creates useful spaces. |

### Construction materials

| Material | Expected observation in a jacket | Relevant propositions and explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually reduces cooling when the bubbles remain intact and the jacket covers the beaker. Crushed bubbles, holes or uncovered sections reduce its effectiveness. | The bubbles provide pockets of air and can limit air movement. Link to **P1, P3, P4 and P6**. |
| **Foam pieces** | Often one of the more effective materials, especially when thick pieces join to cover the beaker. Gaps and thin coverage allow greater cooling. | Link to **P1, P3, P4 and P6**. |
| **Cardboard** | A single thin layer usually gives moderate protection. Several layers or a carefully arranged jacket may perform better than one layer. | Fibres and spaces can provide insulating structure. Link to **P1, P3, P4 and P6**. |
| **Masking tape** | Mainly holds layers together or closes openings; a small amount is not expected to act as the main insulating layer. Its effect depends on how it changes the coverage and spaces in the jacket. | Link mainly to **P4 and P6** when it helps maintain an arrangement or limit air movement. **P1** may be relevant when students discuss direct contact through any material. |
| **250 mL beaker** | The same beaker should be used for both tests where possible. It is not the design variable; its condition must be kept constant. | Energy can be transferred through the beaker by direct contact and from its surfaces by the other routes. Link to **P1, P4 and P5**. |

### Common pattern in the practical data

A typical graph will show:

- a downward trend for both tests;
- a flatter line and higher final temperature for the more effective jacket;
- a smaller temperature drop after a successful revision;
- possible irregular readings caused by probe movement, timing, mixing, gaps, or changing starting temperatures.

Students should compare **temperature drops or rates**, not simply compare final temperatures when the two tests started at different temperatures.

## Expected responses to the guided questions

### Conduction

Look for students identifying a transfer route at places where particles or materials touch and recognising that the material does not move as a whole. The exact explanation is **P1**. For aluminium foil, students may also use **P2** to explain why foil can transfer energy rapidly through the metal.

### Convection

Look for students identifying a moving liquid or gas and linking heating with changes in density and movement. The exact explanation is **P4**. Students may connect spaces in bubble wrap, foam or cotton wool with **P3** and **P6**, especially when explaining how an arrangement limits movement of air.

### Radiation

Look for students recognising that thermal energy can travel from a warm surface without direct contact. The exact explanation is **P5**. Students should connect a shiny or reflective foil surface with the radiation part of **P6**.

### Overall design explanation

A strong response:

1. states whether the revised design cooled more slowly, more quickly or about the same;
2. uses at least one numerical comparison;
3. refers to a specific observation or design change;
4. discusses conduction, convection and radiation;
5. explains that the design affects the **rate** of heat loss rather than creating heat;
6. identifies a limitation or a possible next improvement.

There is no required single winning design. A student may conclude that a different arrangement worked best if the conclusion is supported by their own data.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Response |
|---|---|
| **“Foil is automatically the best insulator because it is shiny.”** | Separate surface appearance from metal behaviour. Use **P2** for aluminium’s conductivity and **P5–P6** for the effect of a reflective surface. Ask which heat-transfer mode the shiny surface may affect and whether the foil also made a direct-contact path. |
| **“Cotton wool, foam or bubble wrap makes heat.”** | Explain that the material is not a heat source. It changes the rate at which the water loses thermal energy. Use **P3 and P6** and compare the temperature drops. |
| **“Any air gap is always helpful.”** | Ask whether the air is held in place or able to move through an opening. Use **P3** for still air and **P4** for movement of a fluid. The size, continuity and coverage of spaces matter. |
| **“Conduction means the whole material moves.”** | Return to **P1**, especially the phrase “without the material itself moving.” Contrast direct contact with the movement of a fluid in convection. |
| **“Convection is just heat rising” or “convection happens in solids.”** | Use **P4**: convection involves a fluid—liquid or gas—and movement caused by density differences during heating. |
| **“Radiation needs air” or “radiation only comes from the Sun.”** | Use **P5**. Ask students to consider energy travelling between warm surfaces when they are not touching. |
| **“Insulation stops all heat transfer” or “a good jacket should show no temperature change.”** | Use **P6**, which describes reducing heat loss. A slower temperature decrease is evidence of reduced transfer, not proof that transfer has stopped. |
| **“More layers or more tape must always improve the design.”** | Require evidence from the two tests. More material may be poorly arranged, compressed or leave openings. Students should explain the result rather than assume it. |
| **“The final temperature alone proves which test was better.”** | Check starting temperatures and compare `T0 − T10` or the rate of change. Also check probe position and timing. |
| **“A group data sheet counts as each student’s evidence.”** | Group members may share an apparatus and obtain matching readings, but every student must record the readings, observations, sketches and reasoning independently. |
