# TEACHER KEY

## Demonstration: expected observations and explanations

The exact order may vary with wrapping thickness, gaps, foil position and starting temperatures. With equal volumes and similar starting temperatures, the usual pattern is:

1. **Bare beaker:** cools fastest and has the largest temperature decrease.  
2. **Aluminium foil:** often cools more slowly than the bare beaker, particularly when the foil is clean, reflective and separated from the beaker by an air gap. However, foil can conduct heat readily if it is in direct contact with the beaker.  
3. **Cotton wool:** usually cools most slowly because its fibres trap many pockets of air and reduce movement of air around the beaker.

Students should compare temperature changes rather than simply ranking materials. Results that differ from the expected pattern can be scientifically acceptable if the student identifies relevant design features and supports the conclusion with data.

### Links to the propositions

- **Conduction:** Heat can pass through the beaker and jacket where materials touch.  
  **P1.** “Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.”
- **Aluminium foil:** Aluminium is a metal and can transfer energy through direct contact.  
  **P2.** “Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.”
- **Cotton wool, foam and bubble wrap:** These materials contain or trap air, especially when layers are not compressed.  
  **P3.** “Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.”
- **Convection:** Air around and within an incomplete or loose jacket may move as it warms and cools.  
  **P4.** “Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.”
- **Radiation:** A warm beaker emits thermal energy to its surroundings; a reflective foil surface can affect this transfer.  
  **P5.** “Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.”
- **Overall design:** A successful jacket combines appropriate layers, trapped air and, where suitable, a reflective surface.  
  **P6.** “Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).”

## Expected student reasoning

Strong responses are likely to include:

- A smaller temperature decrease and a higher final temperature indicate less heat loss.
- Cotton wool, foam, bubble wrap and cardboard can reduce heat transfer when they trap still air.
- Compressing cotton wool or bubble wrap may reduce the useful air spaces.
- Gaps between layers can reduce direct contact and may help trap air, but large openings can allow air movement.
- Aluminium foil may reduce radiant heat loss when its reflective surface faces appropriately, but aluminium itself is not a good insulating material if it is tightly touching the beaker.
- The revised design must be based on initial-test evidence, not merely on a preference for a different material.
- Students should acknowledge uncontrolled variables or unusual results where relevant.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Teacher response |
|---|---|
| “The thickest material is automatically the best insulator.” | Ask students to compare temperature changes and consider whether the material traps air or has been compressed. |
| “Aluminium foil is always an insulator because it is used for wrapping.” | Ask whether the foil is reflective and separated from the beaker, or directly touching it. Discuss its metal properties and the different heat-transfer modes. |
| “Cold moves into the hot water.” | Reframe the investigation as thermal energy leaving the warmer water and moving to cooler surroundings. |
| “Convection only happens in liquids.” | Ask students what happens to air near a warm beaker. Convection can occur in gases as well as liquids. |
| “Radiation requires air.” | Ask how thermal energy can cross space from the Sun to Earth. |
| “Still air and moving air have the same effect.” | Compare a sealed or trapped air pocket with an open gap where air can circulate. |
| “A higher final temperature proves the material is better.” | Check that starting temperatures, water volume, timing and thermometer position were comparable. |
| “One reading is enough.” | Emphasise the complete time series and the graph’s pattern. |
| “The group’s data is my data.” | Require each student to record their own readings, observations, sketch and explanation. Group discussion is permitted, but copied or shared sheets do not provide individual evidence. |
| “A redesign means decorating or adding more material.” | Require a specific change linked to evidence from the first test and a prediction for the retest. |

## Practical checking points

Before testing, check that students:

- have selected materials and explained their intended roles;
- have drawn and labelled an initial jacket;
- can access the probe thermometer;
- have recorded their own starting temperature.

During the initial test, check that students:

- record readings at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 minutes;
- record observations rather than relying only on the group recorder;
- keep water volume, beaker type, timing and thermometer placement as consistent as possible.

Before the retest, check that students:

- have plotted or begun plotting their first data;
- have identified a weakness using evidence;
- have made a visible design change;
- have drawn the revised design;
- have written a rationale and prediction.

After the retest, check that students:

- have a second complete set of readings;
- have compared temperature changes and graph patterns;
- have stated whether the revision improved performance;
- have linked observations to conduction, convection and radiation.
