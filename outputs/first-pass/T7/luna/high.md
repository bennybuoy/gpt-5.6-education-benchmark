# TEACHER KEY

## Intended practical outcome

Students should see that a jacket containing materials that create insulating layers or spaces generally slows the fall in temperature. Results will vary with thickness, fit, compression, gaps, starting temperature and probe placement. Students should compare **temperature drops or cooling rates**, not only final temperatures, when starting temperatures differ.

---

## 1. Demonstration: expected observations

All beakers should begin at approximately the same temperature. In a typical setup, the expected ranking from **slowest cooling to fastest cooling** is:

1. **Cotton wool** — usually retains the most heat because its fluffy structure contains many air spaces.  
2. **Aluminium foil** — may cool more slowly than the bare beaker because its outer surface can reduce radiant loss, but it is thin and may conduct energy if it is in direct contact with the beaker.  
3. **Bare beaker** — usually shows the greatest temperature decrease because there is no additional insulating jacket.

The precise foil/cotton ranking may vary. The important evidence is the measured temperature change and a scientifically justified explanation.

### Demonstration material links

| Observation | Relevant propositions and explanation |
|---|---|
| The bare beaker cools fastest. | Heat can leave through the glass and exposed outer surface. **P1** applies to transfer through solids by contact; **P4** applies to movement in the water and surrounding air; **P5** applies to energy transferred from exposed surfaces. |
| Cotton wool slows cooling. | Cotton wool contains many spaces between fibres. These spaces reduce pathways through the solid material and restrict movement of the air within the covering. This supports **P3** and the design guidance in **P6**. |
| Aluminium foil may slow cooling compared with the bare beaker. | The shiny surface can reduce one pathway for energy leaving the container, as described in **P5** and **P6**. However, foil is a metal, so direct contact can provide a pathway through the foil; this is linked to **P1** and **P2**. |
| Foil is not automatically the best insulator. | Its reflective surface and its ability to conduct energy are different effects. A thin foil layer with no insulating air space may perform less well than a thick layer of cotton, foam or bubble wrap. |

---

## 2. Expected behaviour of available materials

These are general expectations, not fixed rankings. The construction of the jacket must be considered.

| Material | Expected observation in a jacket | Relevant propositions |
|---|---|---|
| **Bubble wrap** | The bubbles provide many enclosed air spaces. Thick, uncompressed bubble wrap will generally slow cooling effectively. Crushing the bubbles or leaving large openings may reduce its performance. | **P3**, **P4**, **P6** |
| **Cotton wool** | A thick, dry layer generally slows cooling well. It works less effectively when compressed, wet or held tightly against the beaker with few air spaces. | **P3**, **P4**, **P6** |
| **Foam pieces** | Foam generally performs well because its structure contains small air spaces and separates the beaker from the outside. Gaps between pieces can reduce performance. | **P3**, **P4**, **P6** |
| **Cardboard** | Cardboard can provide some separation and may contain small spaces within or between layers. A single thin layer is usually less effective than thick foam, cotton wool or bubble wrap. | **P1**, **P3**, **P6** |
| **Aluminium foil** | A shiny foil surface facing an air space can reduce radiant loss. Foil alone does not provide many trapped air spaces and, because it is metal, direct contact can allow rapid transfer through the foil. | **P1**, **P2**, **P5**, **P6** |

A strong design may combine a thick insulating layer with a reflective outer surface, provided the foil is not used as the only insulating layer. Students should not be required to produce the same best design; they must justify their design with evidence.

---

## 3. Authoritative science propositions

Use the following wording in explanations and feedback.

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

### Expected links in student explanations

- **Conduction:** Students should identify direct contact through the beaker, jacket layers or foil. A suitable response may use **P1**. If students discuss aluminium foil, **P2** should also be used.
- **Convection:** Students should connect movement of heated air or water with temperature differences and density differences. A suitable response should use **P4**.
- **Radiation:** Students should connect exposed or reflective surfaces with energy transfer by waves. A suitable response should use **P5**.
- **Design conclusion:** Students should explain that insulation works by addressing more than one pathway. The most complete response uses **P6**, supported by **P1–P5** where relevant.

## 4. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Teacher response |
|---|---|
| “Insulation creates heat.” | Insulation does not create heat. It changes the rate at which thermal energy is transferred. Ask students to compare the temperature drop rather than claim that the jacket adds energy. |
| “The material with the highest final temperature is always best.” | Check starting temperatures first. Compare the temperature drop or cooling rate over the same interval. |
| “All metals are good insulators because foil is used for wrapping food.” | Distinguish the effects. Aluminium is a metal and conducts well. Its shiny surface can reduce radiant transfer, while an insulating layer or air space addresses other pathways. Use **P2**, **P5** and **P6**. |
| “Air does not transfer thermal energy.” | Ask students to consider movement of heated air. Air can be involved in convection, while trapped still air can reduce transfer. Use **P3** and **P4**. |
| “Convection only happens in water.” | Emphasise that a fluid can be a liquid or a gas. Use **P4**. |
| “Radiation needs air.” | Radiation is transferred by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium. Use **P5**. |
| “A bigger gap is always better.” | A large open gap may allow air movement. Ask whether the design traps air or allows it to circulate. Use **P3**, **P4** and **P6**. |
| “More layers automatically make a better jacket.” | Layers can be compressed, poorly fitted or contain gaps. Students should use their data to evaluate the actual design. |
| “Bubble wrap works because plastic is always a poor conductor.” | Direct students to examine the air spaces in the bubbles and whether the bubbles were compressed. Use **P3**. |
| “The probe reading is not part of the observation.” | A temperature reading is quantitative evidence. Students should combine readings with observations about fit, gaps, surfaces and changes between designs. |
