# TEACHER KEY

## Expected demonstration observations

The exact order may vary with wrapping thickness, gaps, starting temperatures and contact with the beakers. The expected general pattern is:

- **Bare beaker:** usually cools fastest because its surface is directly exposed to the surrounding air and thermal energy can leave readily.
- **Cotton wool:** usually cools slowest or among the slowest because its fibres hold pockets of air and provide a substantial barrier around the beaker.
- **Aluminium foil:** may cool more slowly than the bare beaker, particularly when it forms a reflective outer surface, but a single thin foil layer is not necessarily as effective as thick cotton wool or foam. Gaps, contact with the beaker and the number of layers affect results.

Students should record actual observations rather than being marked incorrect for a different order if their evidence is sound and they identify limitations.

## Scientific explanations using the authoritative propositions

### Conduction
Use P1:

> **P1. Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.**

Use P2 where metals are discussed:

> **P2. Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.**

Application: Aluminium foil is a metal and can conduct thermal energy through direct contact. A thin foil layer may therefore provide limited insulation against conduction unless it is combined with air spaces or other insulating layers. Students may also identify direct contact between the beaker, jacket materials, tape or bench as possible conduction pathways.

### Insulating materials and still air
Use P3:

> **P3. Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.**

Application: Cotton wool, bubble wrap and foam can reduce heat transfer when they contain trapped air. Compressing cotton wool or creating large gaps may reduce its effectiveness. Cardboard can also provide a barrier, especially when it creates air spaces, but its performance depends on thickness and construction.

### Convection
Use P4:

> **P4. Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.**

Application: Warm air near the beaker can move and be replaced by cooler air. A well-fitted jacket that traps still air can reduce this movement. Students may also discuss movement within the water, although the practical design mainly targets air movement around the beaker.

### Radiation
Use P5:

> **P5. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.**

Use P6 for the design connection:

> **P6. Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

Application: Aluminium foil can provide a reflective surface that reduces radiative heat loss, especially when it faces an air gap rather than being pressed tightly against the beaker. Foil is not automatically a good insulator: its reflective function and its ability to conduct heat must be considered separately.

## Expected practical investigation outcomes

Students should generally:

1. Produce an initial labelled design.
2. Record six individual temperature readings at 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and 10 minutes.
3. Record observations during Test 1.
4. Calculate or describe temperature change and identify a pattern.
5. Link observations to conduction, convection and radiation through reasoning.
6. Identify a specific weakness or possible heat-loss pathway.
7. Produce a revised, labelled design with a stated reason for the change.
8. Record a second complete set of individual readings.
9. Compare final temperatures or cooling changes and make an evidence-based conclusion.

A successful revision may include:
- adding or improving foam, bubble wrap or cotton wool layers;
- trapping air without excessively compressing the insulating material;
- closing gaps around the sides or base;
- adding foil as an outer reflective layer with an air gap;
- reducing exposed areas while maintaining safe probe access.

The most effective design is not determined by material name alone. Thickness, trapped air, gaps, contact, exposed surface area and construction quality all matter.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Teacher response |
|---|---|
| “Foil is an insulator because it stops all heat.” | Ask students to distinguish a material’s conduction from the effect of a reflective surface. Refer to P2, P5 and P6. |
| “Cotton wool produces cold.” | Explain that it does not cool the water; it slows transfer of thermal energy away from it. Use P3. |
| “Insulation creates heat.” | Ask students to compare temperature change and identify that the jacket reduces heat loss rather than adding energy. |
| “Conduction means heat moving through the air.” | Ask what particles are in direct contact and whether the material itself is moving. Use P1. |
| “Convection happens only in liquids.” | Prompt students to consider moving air around the beaker. Use P4, which includes liquids and gases. |
| “Radiation needs air.” | Ask how energy from the Sun reaches Earth. Use P5. |
| “A thicker or heavier jacket is always better.” | Ask students to inspect trapped air, gaps, compression, reflective surfaces and exposed areas; require evidence from the tests. |
| “A single group result is my data.” | Require every student to copy or independently record each reading, observation, sketch and conclusion. |
| “The lowest final temperature always proves the design is worst.” | Check starting temperatures and compare temperature change, not only final temperature. |
