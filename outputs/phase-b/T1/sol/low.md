## Unit sequence: Electric Circuits and Models

| Lesson | Scheduled day/period | Content statement addressed | Lesson type | Brief activity description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Week 1 Monday P2 | C1 | Theory | Students distinguish models from real systems and explain how a simple circuit diagram can simplify a physical system. |
| 2 | Week 1 Tuesday P4 | C2 | Theory | Students classify examples of energy transfer as heat, light, motion, or electric current and trace transfers between systems. |
| 3 | Week 1 Wednesday P1 | C3 | Practical | In six bench groups, students construct and systematically break simple circuits to determine that a continuous path, source, and load are all required. |
| 4 | Week 1 Thursday P3 | C3 checkpoint; C4 | Formative/theory | Students complete a 15-minute circuit-path checkpoint, receive immediate feedback, and then learn that current is charge flow measured in amperes. |
| 5 | Week 1 Friday P5 | C5 | Theory | Students use representations and worked examples to explain voltage as energy transferred per unit charge, measured in volts. |
| 6 | Week 2 Monday P2 | C6 | Theory/formative | Students examine how material, length, thickness, and temperature affect resistance, then complete a 15-minute checkpoint with same-lesson feedback. |
| 7 | Week 2 Tuesday P4 | C7 | Theory | Students compare circuit representations to identify series circuits as one-path systems and parallel circuits as multiple-path systems. |
| 8 | Week 2 Wednesday P1 | C8, reinforcing C7 | Practical | Groups collect voltage and current measurements from simple resistive circuits, use \(V=IR\), and compare measurements in one-path and multiple-path arrangements. |
| 9 | Week 2 Thursday P3 | C9, consolidating C7–C8 | Theory | Students identify assumptions in simple circuit models and explain when circuit diagrams or \(V=IR\) predictions may fail. |
| 10 | Week 2 Friday P5 | C7, C8, C9 | Summative | Students complete a 30-minute task comparing series and parallel circuits, applying \(V=IR\), and evaluating the limits of the model used. |

## Practical placement justification

### Practical 1: Lesson 3 — Requirements for a circuit

- **Placement:** C1 and C2 have already established models and energy transfer. The practical introduces C3 before current, voltage, and resistance, allowing students to establish the necessary circuit structure first.
- **Grouping:** Six groups of four or five students fit the six fixed bench stations.
- **Equipment:** Each group uses one power supply, remaining within the six-supply limit. No multimeters or glassware are required, and all 28 students can use the class set of 30 goggles.
- **Timing:** Instructions, roles, and circuit diagrams are issued before students move to benches. The lesson uses five minutes for safety/setup, 40 minutes of usable bench investigation, and five minutes for shutdown and pack-up.
- **Technician constraint:** Wednesday P1 avoids reliance on a technician after 1:00 pm; the teacher can check and place equipment before the lesson.

### Practical 2: Lesson 8 — Quantitative circuit modelling

- **Placement:** It follows C7, so students can distinguish one-path and multiple-path arrangements before collecting measurements. It directly teaches and supports C8 through application of \(V=IR\), while reinforcing C7.
- **Grouping:** Students work in the same six groups of four or five at the fixed stations.
- **Equipment:** Each station uses one power supply, meeting the six-supply limit. The four digital multimeters rotate between groups according to a printed schedule; groups without a meter complete circuit construction, prediction, or calculation while waiting. No more than four meters are used at once, and the 30 goggles cover the whole class.
- **Timing:** Circuits and printed station instructions are pre-positioned. Five minutes are allocated to safety/setup, 40 minutes to bench work and meter rotation, and five minutes to power-down and pack-up.
- **Technician constraint:** Wednesday P1 permits advance checking and does not depend on technician availability after 1:00 pm.

## Formative cadence

1. **Checkpoint 1 — Lesson 4**
   - **Checks:** C3: identification of a continuous path, source, and load; diagnosis of incomplete circuits.
   - **Format:** A low-stakes, 15-minute printed task using circuit representations and short explanations.
   - **Feedback:** The teacher displays worked answers immediately, students self-correct in a different colour, and the teacher gives brief targeted feedback to students with incorrect path, source, or load identifications before C4 begins.

2. **Checkpoint 2 — Lesson 6**
   - **Checks:** C6: resistance and its relationship to material, length, thickness, and temperature.
   - **Format:** A low-stakes, 15-minute printed prediction-and-explanation task.
   - **Feedback:** Students compare responses with displayed model answers; the teacher conducts a short correction conference with identified students and provides annotated corrections before the lesson ends.

## Deferrals and risks

### Deferrals if a practical is unavailable

- If **Practical 1** is unavailable, physical testing of the continuous-path, source, and load requirements must be deferred to the next eligible non-Friday lab period. C3 may be introduced through circuit representations, but its practical evidence remains deferred.
- If **Practical 2** is unavailable, quantitative measurement supporting C8 must be deferred. Students may practise \(V=IR\) with teacher-supplied values, but measurement and comparison of physical circuits must be rescheduled.
- Any replacement practical must remain within the limit of two practicals per week and cannot be placed on Friday P5.

### Timetable conflicts

- Friday P5 is used only for theory or assessment because practical work is prohibited due to early-dismissal risk.
- Tuesday P4 and Thursday P3 may be affected by the lack of a dedicated technician after 1:00 pm, so both practicals are placed on Wednesday P1.
- The summative task uses only 30 minutes of Lesson 10, complying with the in-class limit.

### Named risks and mitigation

1. **Multimeter bottleneck:** Only four meters are available for six groups.  
   **Mitigation:** Use a timed rotation, printed group schedule, and parallel prediction/calculation tasks so no group is inactive.

2. **Shared-lab availability or equipment displacement:** Equipment may be moved by another class.  
   **Mitigation:** Confirm L4 booking, check equipment before each practical, and place labelled station trays in advance.

3. **Insufficient bench time:** Slow instructions or setup could reduce usable practical time below 40 minutes.  
   **Mitigation:** Provide printed instructions beforehand, pre-position equipment, assign group roles, and enforce a five-minute setup/five-minute pack-up structure.

4. **Unsafe or incorrect circuit connections:** Students may connect components incorrectly or alter powered circuits.  
   **Mitigation:** Require teacher approval before power is switched on, keep supplies off during changes, use goggles, and stop the class immediately if unsafe connections occur.

5. **Early dismissal on Friday:** Lesson time may be shortened unexpectedly.  
   **Mitigation:** No practical is scheduled Friday; the 30-minute summative begins immediately after instructions and is collected before any early release.

6. **Absence during a practical:** A student may miss required evidence.  
   **Mitigation:** Retain group data and printed procedures, then provide a supervised catch-up using the next eligible lab period or a supplied data set for interim learning.

## Adjustments

### Practical 1

- The two students receive printed, numbered instructions with a circuit diagram, safety steps, and a completion checklist.
- Instructions are provided before movement to benches to allow extra processing time.
- Each student is assigned a stable role, such as component checker or results recorder, and the teacher checks understanding privately before power is used.
- They are not required to complete additional circuit variations if extra processing time is needed; the essential C3 observations are prioritised.

### Practical 2

- The two students receive a printed meter-use sequence, labelled circuit representations, a \(V=IR\) calculation scaffold, and the multimeter rotation timetable.
- Their groups are scheduled later in the meter rotation, providing additional time to process instructions and construct the circuit before measuring.
- The teacher checks the first calculation and connection before independent work continues.
- Reduced repetition is permitted while retaining one valid measurement, one \(V=IR\) calculation, and one C7 comparison.

### Formative checkpoint 1

- Printed instructions and uncluttered circuit representations are supplied.
- The students receive the full 15 minutes without pressure to move early to the next activity.
- Feedback is given individually using the same source–load–continuous path language as the printed task.

### Formative checkpoint 2

- The students receive a printed comparison scaffold covering material, length, thickness, and temperature.
- Extra processing time is protected within the 15-minute checkpoint by reducing non-essential writing.
- The teacher provides immediate verbal and annotated feedback before the end of Lesson 6.
