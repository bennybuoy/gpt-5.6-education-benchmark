## 1. Lesson-by-lesson unit plan

| Lesson | Scheduled day/period | Content statement addressed | Lesson type | Brief activity description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Week 1 Monday P2 | C1 | Theory | Students examine several simplified representations and identify how a model can explain or predict a system while omitting detail. |
| 2 | Week 1 Tuesday P4 | C2, then C3 | Theory + formative | After learning forms of energy transfer and the source–path–load requirements of a circuit, students complete the 15-minute C3 checkpoint and self-correct from immediate feedback. |
| 3 | Week 1 Wednesday P1 | C3 | Practical | In six bench groups, students construct and alter simple source–path–load circuits to determine why opening any part of the continuous path stops the circuit. |
| 4 | Week 1 Thursday P3 | C4 | Theory | Students use a flow model to explain current as flow of charge and practise identifying current values expressed in amperes. |
| 5 | Week 1 Friday P5 | C5 | Theory | Students distinguish voltage from current by interpreting voltage as energy transferred per unit charge and identifying volts as its unit. |
| 6 | Week 2 Monday P2 | C6 | Theory + formative | Students predict how material, length, thickness and temperature affect resistance, then complete a 15-minute checkpoint followed by immediate correction. |
| 7 | Week 2 Tuesday P4 | C7, then C8 | Theory | Students first compare one-path series and multiple-path parallel circuits, then apply \(V=IR\) to simple resistive circuit examples. |
| 8 | Week 2 Wednesday P1 | C7, C8 | Practical | Groups compare series and parallel paths and rotate four multimeters to collect voltage and current readings for testing \(V=IR\). |
| 9 | Week 2 Thursday P3 | C9 | Theory | Students evaluate where the simple circuit models used in the unit are useful and identify how their assumptions can limit or invalidate predictions. |
| 10 | Week 2 Friday P5 | C7, C8, C9 | Summative | Students complete a 30-minute printed task that identifies series and parallel arrangements, applies \(V=IR\), and evaluates the limits of the model used. |

## 3. Practical placement justification

### Practical 1 — Lesson 3: Continuous circuits

- **Placement:** It follows the explicit teaching and formative check of C3, allowing students to test the source–path–load model before progressing to current in C4.
- **Grouping:** The 28 students form four groups of five and two groups of four across the six fixed benches.
- **Equipment:** Each group uses one of the six power supplies and a pre-checked simple source–path–load circuit; all 28 students can use the class set of 30 goggles, and no multimeters, glassware or hand lenses are required.
- **Timing:** Equipment is pre-staged before the bell; minutes 0–5 are for the safety and task briefing, minutes 5–45 provide 40 minutes of usable bench work, and minutes 45–50 are for pack-up.
- **Inventory condition:** The ordinary connectors and simple loads required for the circuits must be confirmed before the lesson because their availability is not specified in the supplied equipment list.

### Practical 2 — Lesson 8: Series, parallel and \(V=IR\)

- **Placement:** It occurs after C7 and C8 have been taught in order, so students can apply both path models and the quantitative relationship rather than encounter them without prerequisites.
- **Grouping:** The same six groups use one power supply per bench, staying within the limit of six power supplies.
- **Equipment:** Four groups initially use the four multimeters while two groups construct, compare and predict circuit behaviour; two meters are then transferred so every group collects readings, and the 30 goggles cover the whole class.
- **Timing:** Pre-staging and a printed procedure permit a five-minute briefing, 40 minutes of bench work and five minutes of pack-up within the 50-minute period.
- **Inventory condition:** Simple resistive loads and connectors must be confirmed in advance; no fume cupboard, glassware, hand lenses or mobile devices are needed.

Both practicals are on Wednesday P1, avoiding Friday P5 and reducing dependence on technician support after 1:00 pm. There is only one practical in each week.

## 4. Formative cadence

### Checkpoint 1 — Lesson 2

- **Checks:** C3: whether a circuit contains a source, load and continuous path.
- **Format:** A 15-minute printed task in which students label circuit elements, identify broken paths and correct one incomplete model.
- **Feedback:** During the final ten minutes, model answers are projected and explained; students correct their papers in a different colour while the teacher checks common errors.

### Checkpoint 2 — Lesson 6

- **Checks:** C6: resistance as opposition to current and the effects of material, length, thickness and temperature.
- **Format:** A 15-minute printed prediction-and-explanation task.
- **Feedback:** The teacher projects an annotated answer set immediately afterwards, addresses each factor orally and checks students’ corrections before the lesson ends.

## 5. Deferrals and risks

### Deferrals

- If Practical 1 is unavailable, no new authoritative content must be deferred because C3 has already been taught, but its required hands-on application must move to the next eligible non-Friday lab period.
- If Practical 2 cannot run on Wednesday, it should move to Thursday P3; C9 can then be taught before the 30-minute summative task on Friday.
- If Practical 2 cannot be recovered that week, C9 and the summative task should be deferred until the required C7/C8 practical support has occurred.
- If the necessary loads or connectors cannot be confirmed, the affected practical must be deferred rather than replaced by unsupported equipment.

### Timetable conflicts and risks

1. **Circuit-component availability risk:** Loads and connectors are not listed as confirmed stock; inspect and reserve them before the unit, with Thursday P3 retained as the first recovery slot.
2. **Forty-minute bench-time risk:** Shared-room turnover or late setup could reduce usable time; pre-stage six labelled trays, assign group roles and use a visible pack-up alarm.
3. **Multimeter bottleneck risk:** Only four multimeters serve six groups; use a timed rotation, with non-meter groups completing construction and predictions while waiting.
4. **After-1:00-pm staffing risk:** Technician support is unavailable after 1:00 pm; both planned practicals are Wednesday P1 and all equipment is pre-checked beforehand.
5. **Friday early-dismissal risk:** No practical is scheduled on Friday, and the summative task begins at the start of P5 so its 30 minutes are protected.
6. **Electrical-safety risk:** The teacher pre-checks all source–load combinations, students wear goggles and power is disconnected before paths are changed.

## 6. Adjustments

- **Practical 1:** Both students receive the numbered printed procedure and circuit diagram in Lesson 2 for advance processing; at the bench they have a consistent partner, a clearly assigned role, teacher check-ins and fewer repeated trials if additional processing time is needed.
- **Practical 2:** Both receive the printed procedure, meter-rotation schedule and results table in Lesson 7; they retain the same group and may collect fewer repeated readings while still obtaining the evidence needed to test \(V=IR\).
- **Checkpoint 1:** Both receive printed instructions and the question layout five minutes before the common task window, allowing extra processing while the core task remains completable in 15 minutes; the teacher privately checks understanding of directions.
- **Checkpoint 2:** Both receive the printed factor list and response format five minutes early, with additional wait time during instructions and a reduced-writing response format; they participate in the same immediate feedback and correction process.
