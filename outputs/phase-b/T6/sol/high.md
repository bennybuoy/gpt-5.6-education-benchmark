## 2. Worksheet repairs

**Title**  
**Evidence for Chemical Change**

**Learning intention**  
Distinguish observations from inferences and use multiple observations to decide whether evidence supports formation of a new substance.

**Success criteria**
1. Label statements as observation or inference.  
2. Use at least two relevant observations before claiming a chemical change.  
3. State when evidence is insufficient.

**Worked example**  
Fizzing tablet in water:  
- **Observation:** Bubbles and temperature decrease.  
- **Inference:** Gas may have formed.  
- **Decision:** These observations alone do not identify the gas or prove a new substance; more evidence is needed.

**Group task, 18–33 minutes**  
Build an **Evidence–Inference–Decision** tool. Include rules for:
- labelling observations and inferences;
- using at least two relevant observations before claiming a chemical change;
- stating when evidence is insufficient.

**Group task, 33–43 minutes**  
Test your tool against the two case cards. Revise one rule.

**Individual check, 43–54 minutes — 5 marks**
1. Record two relevant observations. **(2)**  
2. Distinguish an observation from an inference. **(1)**  
3. Make an evidence-based decision. **(1)**  
4. State a limitation or further evidence needed. **(1)**

**Exit ticket, 54–60 minutes**  
“One observation is ___; one inference is ___; my decision is ___ because ___; evidence still needed is ___.”

**Teacher note**  
Mark allocation: two observations **(2)**; observation/inference distinction **(1)**; evidence-based decision **(1)**; limitation or further evidence **(1)**. Total: **5 marks**.
