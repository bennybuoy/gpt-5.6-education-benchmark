## Marking criteria sheet: 8-mark extended response

### Expected data calculations

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Change, Year 1 to Year 4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A** | \((100 \times 120)/12 = 1000\) | \((110 \times 130)/13 = 1100\) | \((120 \times 140)/14 = 1200\) | \((125 \times 150)/15 = 1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100 = +25\%\) |
| **Lake B** | \((100 \times 120)/12 = 1000\) | \((90 \times 110)/11 = 900\) | \((70 \times 100)/14 = 500\) | \((50 \times 80)/16 = 250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100 = -75\%\) |

### Key evidence available for analysis and evaluation

- Before cichlid introduction, both lakes had the same estimated rainbowfish population: **1000 fish in Year 1**.
- After cichlid introduction at the end of Year 1:
  - Lake A increased from **1000 to 1250 fish** (+25%).
  - Lake B decreased from **1000 to 250 fish** (−75%).
- The decline in Lake B became progressively larger across Years 2–4.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH were similar between lakes and changed little over time, so these measured abiotic variables do not provide a strong alternative explanation for the decline.
- A justified overall judgement is that the data **partially support** the hypothesis: they show a strong association consistent with top-down predation by the cichlid, but cannot prove that the cichlid alone caused the decline.

## Distinguishing high-band from mid-band responses

### High-band response: “analyse”
A high-band response does more than state that Lake B declined. It **breaks the evidence into components and identifies relationships**:

- compares Year 1 populations with Years 2–4;
- compares Lake B with the control Lake A;
- identifies the different population trends: Lake A increased by 25%, whereas Lake B decreased by 75%;
- considers whether abiotic factors show a corresponding change;
- links the predator’s role as a visual pursuit predator of small fish to a plausible **top-down** explanation.

For example:  
“Following cichlid introduction, Lake B declined from 1000 to 250 fish, whereas Lake A increased from 1000 to 1250 fish. Because dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH remained similar between lakes, the measured abiotic variables are unlikely to explain the contrasting trends.”

### Mid-band response: “analyse”
A mid-band response may identify the decline and calculate results, but tends to describe rather than relate the evidence:

- “Lake B decreased by 75% and Lake A increased.”
- “The cichlid probably ate the rainbowfish.”

This identifies some relevant facts but does not fully analyse the pre-/post-introduction pattern, control comparison and abiotic evidence together.

### High-band response: “evaluate”
A high-band response **makes a judgement based on criteria and justifies it with evidence**. It should state that the data **partially support** the hypothesis because:

1. the temporal pattern is consistent with cichlid predation;
2. Lake A provides a comparison showing no equivalent decline;
3. the measured abiotic variables were broadly stable and similar;
4. however, one treatment lake and one control lake do not exclude other differences between lakes;
5. mark-recapture assumptions may be affected by predation, movement or differing catchability.

### Mid-band response: “evaluate”
A mid-band response gives a broadly appropriate conclusion but with weaker criteria or justification, for example:

- “The data support the hypothesis because Lake B decreased after the predator was introduced.”
- “There could be other factors.”

This is relevant, but it does not adequately weigh the strength of the evidence against the study limitations or explain why the conclusion should be qualified as partial support rather than proof.

## Common-error diagnosis

| Common error | Why it reduces response quality | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Applying the Schnabel formula incorrectly or calculating percentage change from the wrong baseline.** For example, calculating Lake B Year 4 as \(16/(50 \times 80)\), or calculating the decline using Year 4 rather than Year 1 as the denominator. | Incorrect calculations undermine all later analysis. The correct Lake B Year 4 estimate is \((50 \times 80)/16 = 250\), and the percentage change is \((250-1000)/1000 \times 100 = -75\%\). Using the final population as the denominator gives an incorrect percentage and exaggerates the decline. | Write the formula before substituting values: \(N = MC/R\). For percentage change, always use: \(\frac{\text{final} - \text{initial}}{\text{initial}}\times100\). Check whether the final value is logically lower or higher than the initial value. |
| **2. Confusing top-down and bottom-up control, or misidentifying the cichlid as a primary consumer.** | The cichlid is a fish-eating predator and therefore a higher-level consumer. Its possible effect on rainbowfish is a **top-down** effect through predation. Bottom-up control would involve changes in resources such as nutrients, primary productivity or food availability. Confusing these concepts weakens the biological explanation. | Link the mechanism to the information supplied: the cichlid is a visual pursuit predator that feeds mainly on small-bodied fish. State that increased predation pressure could reduce rainbowfish abundance through top-down control. Avoid assigning an exact trophic level unless the prey’s diet is known; describe it accurately as a higher-level piscivorous consumer. |
| **3. Overclaiming causation from the field data, for example stating that the study “proves” the cichlid caused the decline.** | The temporal association and control comparison provide evidence consistent with causation, but there is only one treatment lake and one control lake. Unmeasured environmental differences, disease, habitat change, fishing pressure, immigration/emigration or changes in catchability could also contribute. In addition, predation and changes in fish behaviour may affect Schnabel assumptions. | Use cautious scientific language: “the data support” or “partially support the hypothesis,” “are consistent with,” or “suggest that.” Explain that replication of treatment and control lakes, more environmental measures, and repeated sampling would strengthen causal inference. |
