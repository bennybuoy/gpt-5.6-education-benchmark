# Marking Criteria Sheet — 8 marks

## NSW NESA command verbs

- **Analyse:** Identify the components of the data and the relationships between them, then draw out and relate their implications. In this task, this involves separating the results by lake and year, comparing trends before and after predator introduction, and relating these trends to the control lake and abiotic data.
- **Evaluate:** Make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement with evidence. Relevant criteria include temporal sequence, the difference between the treatment and control lakes, stability of abiotic variables, reliability of the mark-recapture estimates, and limitations of the experimental design.

## Expected calculations

The population estimate is:

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}
\]

| Lake | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Change, Year 1–4 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Lake A** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((110\times130)/13=1100\) | \((120\times140)/14=1200\) | \((125\times150)/15=1250\) | \(\frac{1250-1000}{1000}\times100=+25\%\) |
| **Lake B** | \((100\times120)/12=1000\) | \((90\times110)/11=900\) | \((70\times100)/14=500\) | \((50\times80)/16=250\) | \(\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%\) |

A strong response should identify that:

- Lake A increased from 1,000 to 1,250 fish, a **25% increase**.
- Lake B declined from 1,000 to 250 fish, a **75% decline**.
- The decline in Lake B occurred after the cichlid was introduced at the end of Year 1.
- Lake A did not show a similar decline.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH were broadly stable and similar between lakes, so these measured variables do not provide an obvious alternative explanation.
- The results support a top-down predation explanation, but do not prove causation because the study lacks replication and may be affected by other uncontrolled factors.

## Banded performance descriptors

### 7–8 marks — Thorough analysis and well-justified evaluation

- Correctly calculates all or nearly all eight population estimates and both percentage changes.
- **Analyses** the evidence by breaking it into relevant components: annual trends in each lake, the timing of predator introduction, the contrast between Lake A and Lake B, and the abiotic measurements.
- Clearly identifies relationships and implications, such as the decline in Lake B beginning after introduction while Lake A increased and measured abiotic conditions remained relatively stable.
- Discusses at least two relevant limitations and explains how they affect confidence in the conclusion. These may include:
  - only one control and one treatment lake, resulting in a lack of replication and possible lake-specific confounding;
  - possible violations of mark-recapture assumptions;
  - limited temporal and spatial sampling of abiotic variables;
  - unmeasured biotic or environmental factors;
  - lack of direct measurements of cichlid abundance or predation rates;
  - absence of uncertainty estimates or statistical testing.
- **Evaluates** the hypothesis by applying explicit or clearly implied criteria, weighing supporting evidence against limitations, and making a justified judgement.
- Concludes appropriately that the data **support**, or strongly but not conclusively support, the hypothesis while recognising that they do not prove causation.

### 5–6 marks — Sound analysis and supported evaluation

- Correctly calculates most population estimates and percentage changes, with only minor numerical errors or omissions.
- Identifies the main trends: Lake B declined after predator introduction, whereas Lake A was stable or increased.
- Refers appropriately to the generally stable abiotic measurements.
- Discusses at least two limitations, although their effects on the interpretation may not be fully developed.
- Makes a reasonable judgement about the hypothesis and supports it with relevant evidence.
- Demonstrates some **analysis**, but may mainly describe trends rather than fully connect the timing, control comparison, abiotic evidence and implications.
- Demonstrates **evaluation**, but the criteria for the judgement may be implicit, or limitations may be listed without being fully weighed against the supporting evidence.

### 3–4 marks — Basic interpretation with limited analysis or evaluation

- Calculates some population estimates correctly but includes several errors or omissions.
- May calculate one percentage change correctly or identify the approximate direction and magnitude of change.
- Describes the decline in Lake B and/or the increase in Lake A but makes limited comparison between the lakes.
- Makes a general reference to abiotic data or identifies one or two limitations, with little explanation of their significance.
- Gives a conclusion about the hypothesis, but justification is incomplete, weak or largely assertive.
- Shows limited **analysis** because components are identified without clearly explaining their relationships.
- Shows limited **evaluation** because the judgement is not based on clearly applied criteria or balanced consideration of evidence and limitations.

### 1–2 marks — Elementary response

- Provides few correct calculations or makes major errors in applying the mark-recapture formula.
- Identifies only a simple trend, such as stating that the Lake B population decreased.
- Makes little or no meaningful use of the control lake, abiotic data or timing of introduction.
- Identifies no valid limitation, or provides a limitation without explanation.
- Gives an unsupported, absolute or scientifically inappropriate conclusion.
- Demonstrates minimal analysis and does not make a justified evaluation.

### 0 marks

- Provides no relevant calculation, analysis or judgement.

## What distinguishes a high-band response from a mid-band response?

A **high-band response** does more than calculate and describe. For **analyse**, it breaks the evidence into components and explicitly relates them: the Year 1 baseline, post-introduction changes in Lake B, the contrasting Lake A trend, and the stable abiotic variables. It then draws out the implication that predator introduction is a plausible explanation for the divergent population trends.

For **evaluate**, a high-band response uses criteria such as temporal sequence, control-treatment contrast, exclusion of measured abiotic explanations, replication and methodological reliability. It weighs these criteria and reaches a qualified judgement supported by evidence.

A **mid-band response** generally identifies the main trends and reaches a reasonable conclusion but may remain descriptive—for example, “Lake B declined and Lake A increased.” It may list limitations without explaining how they reduce causal confidence, or conclude that the hypothesis is supported without clearly weighing the strengths and weaknesses of the evidence.

# Common-Error Diagnosis

## 1. Numerical error: applying the formula or percentage-change baseline incorrectly

**Example error:** A student may calculate \(N=(M\times R)/C\), fail to divide by the number of recaptures, or calculate percentage change using Year 4 as the denominator.

For Lake B, the correct Year 4 estimate is:

\[
N=\frac{50\times80}{16}=250
\]

The correct percentage change uses Year 1 as the baseline:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%
\]

This can be expressed as a **75% decline**.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
Incorrect estimates distort the population trend and undermine all later analysis and evaluation. Using the wrong baseline can substantially exaggerate or understate the percentage change.

**How to avoid it:**  
Write the formula before substituting values, calculate each year separately, and use:

\[
\%\text{ change}=\frac{\text{final}-\text{initial}}{\text{initial}}\times100
\]

Check that the direction and size are plausible: a fall from 1,000 to 250 leaves one-quarter of the original population and therefore represents a 75% decline.

## 2. Conceptual error: describing the predator effect as bottom-up control

**Example error:** A student may state that the cichlid caused “bottom-up control” or identify it as a producer or primary consumer.

The cichlid is a carnivorous consumer that feeds on small-bodied fish. A reduction in rainbowfish caused by predation is an example of **top-down control**, because an organism at a higher trophic position affects the abundance of organisms below it.

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
It shows misunderstanding of the ecological mechanism being tested. This weakens the explanation linking cichlid introduction to the rainbowfish decline.

**How to avoid it:**  
Trace the feeding relationship before naming the process:

\[
\text{cichlid predator} \rightarrow \text{consumes rainbowfish prey}
\]

Because the effect moves from predator to prey, it is top-down. Bottom-up control would instead involve changes in resources or abiotic conditions affecting organisms at higher trophic levels.

## 3. Causal-inference error: claiming that the data prove the cichlid caused the decline

**Example error:** A student may conclude, “The experiment proves that the cichlid caused the 75% decline.”

**Why this reduces response quality:**  
The timing, control-lake comparison and stable measured abiotic variables support the hypothesis, but the study has only one predator lake and one control lake. The treatment is therefore not replicated, so an unmeasured difference specific to Lake B could have caused or contributed to the decline. Other factors—such as disease, habitat change, fishing, recruitment failure or changes in prey behaviour and catchability—were not excluded. Mark-recapture assumptions may also have been violated.

**How to avoid it:**  
Use appropriately qualified language, such as:

> “The data support the hypothesis because Lake B declined after cichlid introduction while Lake A increased and measured abiotic conditions remained stable. However, the evidence does not prove causation because the treatment was unreplicated and other variables may have differed between the lakes.”

A student should also suggest improvements such as using multiple treatment and control lakes, measuring cichlid abundance and predation directly, testing mark-recapture assumptions, and collecting more extensive environmental data.
