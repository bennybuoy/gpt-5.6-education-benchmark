### Evidence relevant to the judgement

- Before the introduction, both lakes had an estimated rainbowfish population of 1000.
- After the cichlid was introduced, Lake B declined from 1000 to 900, 500 and 250.
- Over the same period, the control population increased from 1000 to 1250.
- Dissolved oxygen, temperature and pH remained broadly stable and similar between lakes. This makes the measured abiotic variables less likely explanations for the contrasting trends.
- The timing, direction and magnitude of the decline are consistent with predation by a visual pursuit predator that consumes small littoral fish.
- However, the study is correlational and contains only one control lake and one treatment lake. Other differences between the lakes cannot be excluded.
- Appropriate overall judgement: the data **support**, or strongly support, the hypothesis, but do not prove that the cichlid caused the decline.

## Application of NSW NESA command verbs

- **Analyse:** Break the information into components, identify relationships between those components, and draw out their implications. Here, this involves calculating and comparing annual population estimates, identifying trends within each lake, comparing the treatment and control lakes, and relating population trends to predator introduction and abiotic data.
- **Evaluate:** Make a judgement based on criteria and justify that judgement using evidence. Relevant criteria include the timing and magnitude of the decline, the control-lake comparison, the stability of measured abiotic variables, the reliability of the population estimates, and the limitations of the experimental design.

### Key distinction between high- and mid-band responses

A **high-band response** does more than report that Lake B declined. It **analyses** by separating the evidence into annual estimates, percentage changes, control-versus-treatment patterns, timing and abiotic patterns, then identifying relationships among them. It **evaluates** by applying criteria such as temporal association, control comparison, alternative explanations and study reliability to reach a qualified, evidence-based judgement.

A **mid-band response** generally identifies the correct trend and reaches a reasonable conclusion, but may mainly describe the results. Its evaluation may rely heavily on the coincidence between predator introduction and population decline, without fully balancing supporting evidence against limitations or explaining why those limitations weaken causal certainty.

# Common-Error Diagnosis

## 1. Numerical error: incorrect use of the Schnabel formula or percentage-change baseline

**Example error:**  
A student calculates Lake B Year 4 as:

\[
N=\frac{R\times C}{M}=\frac{16\times80}{50}=25.6
\]

rather than:

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}=\frac{50\times80}{16}=250
\]

Alternatively, the student calculates the percentage decline using Year 4 as the denominator:

\[
\frac{1000-250}{250}\times100=300\%
\]

instead of using the Year 1 population as the baseline:

\[
\frac{250-1000}{1000}\times100=-75\%.
\]

**Why it reduces response quality:**  
Incorrect estimates distort the population trend and the apparent magnitude of change. Since the evaluation depends on comparing population trajectories, numerical errors can lead to an unsupported judgement.

**How to avoid it:**  
Write the formula before substituting values:

\[
N=\frac{M\times C}{R}.
\]

For percentage change, use:

\[
\%\text{ change}=\frac{\text{final value}-\text{initial value}}{\text{initial value}}\times100.
\]

Check that the result is biologically and mathematically plausible: Lake B falls from 1000 to 250, so the final population is one-quarter of the original and the decline must be 75%.

## 2. Conceptual error: confusing top-down and bottom-up control or misidentifying trophic roles

**Example error:**  
A student states that the cichlid causes “bottom-up control” because it reduces the rainbowfish population, or describes the cichlid as a producer or primary consumer.

**Why it reduces response quality:**  
The cichlid is a predatory consumer at a higher trophic level than the small fish it eats. A predator reducing prey abundance is an example of **top-down control**. Bottom-up control would occur if rainbowfish abundance were limited by resources or conditions at lower trophic levels, such as reduced primary productivity or food availability. Confusing these concepts weakens the biological explanation linking the introduced predator to the observed decline.

**How to avoid it:**  
Identify the feeding relationship before assigning trophic control:

- cichlid = higher-level predatory consumer;
- rainbowfish = prey;
- predator acting on prey = top-down effect.

A suitable statement would be: “The decline is consistent with top-down control because the introduced cichlid is a visual predator that feeds on small-bodied littoral fish.”

---

## 3. Causal-overclaiming error: stating that the data prove the cichlid caused the decline

**Example error:**  
A student concludes: “The results prove that the introduced cichlid caused the 75% decline because abiotic conditions did not change.”

**Why it reduces response quality:**  
The study provides a strong temporal association and a useful control comparison, but it is still a field study with only one treatment lake and one control lake. The “treatment” is therefore not replicated. Unmeasured differences—such as disease, fishing pressure, habitat change, food availability or other species interactions—could have contributed to the decline. Measuring only dissolved oxygen, surface temperature and pH does not exclude every alternative explanation. Mark-recapture assumptions may also have been violated if predator presence changed rainbowfish behaviour or catchability.

**How to avoid it:**  
Use a qualified judgement that weighs evidence against limitations. For example:

> “The data support the hypothesis because Lake B declined by 75% after predator introduction while the control increased by 25%, and the measured abiotic variables remained stable. However, the evidence does not prove causation because there was no replication of treatment lakes and unmeasured factors or mark-recapture bias could have contributed to the observed decline.”

This wording fulfils **evaluate** by making and justifying a judgement while recognising the limits of the evidence.
