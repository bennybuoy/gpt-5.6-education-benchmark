1. Central decision

Students will not classify by spotting one “chemical sign”. They will build an Evidence-to-Claim protocol: record observations, separate the inference that a new substance formed, make a qualified physical/chemical/insufficient-evidence claim, and state a limitation.

2. Learning intention and three success criteria

Learning intention: Use evidence to distinguish physical and chemical changes without treating visible signs or reversibility as automatic proof.

Success criteria:
- I can label a statement as an observation or an inference.
- I can use evidence about possible new substances to make a justified classification.
- I can explain why my conclusion is limited when the evidence does not establish that a new substance formed.

3. Lesson sequence

- **0–4 min:** Project two brief evidence records. Students make a silent provisional claim on mini-whiteboards: physical, chemical, or insufficient evidence.
- **4–12 min:** Teacher models a comparison. Record A: “a piece becomes smaller”; Record B: “bubbles are visible and a temperature reading rises.” Class identify observable statements versus inferences.
- **12–17 min:** Pairs propose rules for a class Evidence-to-Claim protocol. Teacher combines essential rules visibly.
- **17–31 min:** Six teams construct their own protocol poster/card and apply it to evidence dossiers: state/size change, dissolving, colour-only evidence, bubbles/temperature evidence, light/odour/solid-appearing evidence, and irreversibility-only evidence.
- **31–41 min:** Teams exchange one dossier. The receiving team challenges the original claim: “Which words are observations? What new-substance inference is being made? What is the limit?” Teams revise their tool.
- **41–48 min:** Teams rehearse the A16 practical talk routine: recorder states observations; classifier makes a claim; skeptic identifies an unsupported leap or limitation.
- **48–56 min:** Individual written exam-style check in exercise books.
- **56–60 min:** Students photograph or retain their revised protocol. Teacher previews its required use in A16. Total: **60 minutes**.

4. Student-built artifact

Each team creates a non-preprinted “Evidence-to-Claim” decision tool on A3 paper:

1. What was directly observed?
2. What inference about substances is being made?
3. Does evidence support physical, chemical, or only a provisional/insufficient conclusion?
4. What is the evidence limit?

It must include warnings: “Dissolving can be physical,” “one sign is not proof,” and “reversibility alone does not decide.”

5. Teacher explanation

Say: “A physical change has no new substance; state, shape, size or arrangement may change. A chemical change forms one or more new substances. Classification is therefore about substances, not about how dramatic something looks.”

Model: “Bubbles are visible” and “the temperature reading rose” are observations. “A gas formed,” “a new substance formed,” and “therefore chemical” are inferences. These observations support a chemical-change claim, but neither observation proves it alone. Likewise, a change in size supports a physical interpretation only when there is no defensible reason to infer a new substance.

6. Individual exam-style check and marking points

Prompt:  
“During a change, bubbles are visible, the recorded temperature rises, and a new odour is noticed. A student writes: ‘It is certainly a chemical change: bubbles prove gas formation, and it cannot be reversed.’ In 4–5 sentences, evaluate and improve the student’s claim.”

Marking points:
- Identifies bubbles, temperature rise and odour as observations.  
- Identifies gas formation/new-substance formation/chemical classification as inferences.  
- States that chemical change is the best-supported provisional classification because several observations are consistent with formation of a new substance.  
- States the limit: no sign, and not reversibility alone, proves the conclusion; stronger evidence that a new substance formed would be needed for certainty.

7. ADHD-supportive access and deep extension

Use visible countdowns, one dossier at a time, and short claim–challenge–revision cycles so students do not wait through long explanations. Give roles: evidence reader, observation checker, claim writer, challenger and spokesperson; the card exchange provides purposeful movement.

Depth is retained because the challenger must find where evidence ends and inference begins. Early finishers devise a counterexample to a team rule, such as why colour alone or irreversibility alone fails, then revise the rule without making it vague.

8. Forward link and operational risks

In A16, students will use the same protocol before making each practical classification: first record observations, then identify the new-substance inference, then justify a qualified conclusion. The individual check identifies students needing a teacher prompt during this routine.

AH17 uses only projected records, cards, paper and markers: no wet substances, flames, heating, tasting or uncontrolled materials. Pre-number dossier packs and display the model to prevent lost time. Ensure cards never present “a gas formed” or “chemical change” as an observation; use visible bubbles, odour, light, colour, temperature or solid appearance instead.
