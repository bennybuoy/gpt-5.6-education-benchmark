| ID | Severity | Location / quoted text | Problem | Recommended correction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | **Critical** | Cleanup: “Students should taste a small amount…” | Students must never taste laboratory materials. The mixture may be contaminated, may not be neutral, and tasting cannot establish safety or pH. | Prohibit eating, drinking, and tasting in the laboratory. Determine handling and disposal from known composition and approved school procedures, not student ingestion. |
| F2 | **Critical** | Cleanup: “All leftover liquids and solids … should be poured together down the classroom sink.” | Mixing residues can create incompatible or unidentified waste. Metal scraps, steel wool, rust, and other solids must not be put down a sink; some materials may also still be hot. | Use a written, segregated disposal plan. Keep metal-containing solids, unknown/coded samples, indicator waste, and aqueous waste separate as required by school policy. Send materials to the drain only where specifically authorised. |
| F3 | **Critical** | Station D: “Students cut magnesium ribbon with scissors while holding it directly over a lit Bunsen burner…” | This places hands, scissors, and magnesium close to a flame. It creates avoidable burn and ignition risks and has no clear learning purpose. | Remove this station. Do not ask students to cut or hold materials over a live flame. |
| F4 | **Critical** | Activity 1: “The mass decreases, which demonstrates that mass is destroyed…”; Background Note: “Conservation of mass applies to closed systems…” | Mass is not destroyed. In the open beaker, gaseous products leave the measured system, so the balance reading can fall. Conservation applies to the total system and surroundings; a closed boundary is needed to measure constant mass on a balance. | State that matter is conserved in chemical reactions. Use a school-approved, closed, non-pressurising demonstration or validated data that retains products within the weighed system, and explicitly discuss the system boundary. |
| F5 | **Critical** | Station A: “Melting ice is a chemical change…” | Melting ice is a physical change: solid water and liquid water are the same substance, H₂O. No new substance forms. This directly contradicts a learning objective. | Replace with: melting is a physical state change; the particles and chemical identity remain water. Contrast it with a genuine chemical change that forms new substances. |
| F6 | **Critical** | Station B: “The increase in mass when steel wool burns proves that oxygen gas is produced…” | This reverses the chemistry. When iron/steel wool oxidises, oxygen from the air is incorporated into the solid product; oxygen is consumed, not produced. | Explain that a retained solid product may gain mass because oxygen atoms from the air have joined the iron. Use reliable teacher-provided evidence or data that do not lose solid products. |
| F7 | **Critical** | Plenary: “A reaction that releases heat needs no activation energy to start…” | Exothermic reactions still normally require activation energy to begin. Energy released overall is not the same as the energy barrier for starting the reaction. | Teach that an exothermic profile has an activation-energy peak; after that barrier is overcome, more energy is released than absorbed overall. |
| F8 | **Major** | Whole practical lesson, especially candle, steel wool, indicator sample, Bunsen burner, and cleanup | The plan gives no stated risk assessment, PPE expectations, fire controls, supervision arrangements, or handling instructions for hot materials and solutions. The “unknown solution” may be unknown to pupils, but it must be known and risk-assessed by staff. | Add school risk-assessment controls: appropriate PPE, clear behaviour expectations, teacher supervision, safe handling of hot items, fire precautions, and use only staff-known, low-hazard coded samples. |
| F9 | **Major** | Station E: “Rusting … requires no activation energy because it occurs at room temperature.” | Reactions occurring at room temperature still involve activation energy. Rusting occurs slowly when conditions allow corrosion processes to proceed; it also requires oxygen and water. | Explain that room-temperature particles can sometimes have sufficient energy for reaction steps. Teach rusting as a slow oxidation process requiring oxygen and water, not an activation-energy-free process. |
| F10 | **Major** | Station C: “If universal indicator turns red, this alone confirms … hydrochloric acid.” | Universal indicator indicates an acidic pH range; it cannot identify a particular acid. Many acids can give a red result. | State that red indicates a strongly acidic sample or acidic pH range. Do not claim identification of hydrochloric acid without further appropriate evidence. |
| F11 | **Major** | Starter: “A flame is present, therefore a chemical reaction is occurring”; “flame alone is sufficient evidence…” | In a candle, combustion is chemical, but melting and vaporising wax are physical changes occurring at the same time. Treating a flame as a universal, sole diagnostic rule oversimplifies classification of changes. | Ask pupils to distinguish the physical changes of wax from combustion of wax vapour. Emphasise evidence of formation of new substances rather than a single visual sign. |
| F12 | **Major** | Activity 2: rotating groups; Station B requires teacher to burn steel wool | A live teacher combustion demonstration is poorly suited to a rapid rotation. It divides teacher attention, especially while another station uses a Bunsen burner, and may prevent every group from observing safely. | Make any combustion observation a whole-class, teacher-controlled event, or use validated teacher-provided data, images, or cooled samples at the station. Do not run simultaneous student flame work. |
| F13 | **Major** | Exit ticket: one true/false question worth “100% of the lesson grade”; “No written explanation…” | The keyed answer, **True**, is scientifically acceptable, but one recall question cannot assess the three learning objectives or diagnose misconceptions. Making it the entire grade is unreliable and inequitable. | Use several short, low-stakes formative questions requiring brief reasoning: classify changes, explain apparent mass change in an open system, interpret an energy profile, and interpret indicator evidence. |
| F14 | **Minor** | Activity 2: five stations at “approximately 5 minutes” each, allocated 20 minutes | Five five-minute stations require at least 25 minutes, before transitions, so the lesson exceeds its stated duration. | Reduce the number of stations, shorten and structure rotations, or allocate additional time including transitions. |
| F15 | **Minor** | Cleanup: 5 minutes after multiple stations | Five minutes is likely to encourage rushed handling of waste, wet equipment, and potentially hot materials. | Build in sufficient supervised cleanup time, including cooling time where needed and teacher verification that waste is correctly segregated. |

## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

### Starter
- Use a safe image, video, or teacher-controlled candle observation only if permitted by the school risk assessment. Ask pupils to separate **melting/vaporising wax** from **combustion** rather than treating a flame as a universal test for chemical change. [F8, F11]
- Reinforce that a chemical change forms new substances; a state change does not. [F5]

### Demonstration
- Replace the open-beaker “mass is destroyed” claim with a school-approved closed, non-pressurising conservation-of-mass demonstration or validated class data. Define the system boundary and explain that gas escaping an open container changes the balance reading, not the total mass. [F4]
- Do not use steel-wool combustion as a rotating live activity. If included, use teacher-provided data or a teacher-controlled whole-class observation, explaining that oxygen is incorporated into the solid. [F6, F8, F12]

### Stations
- Use four safe, focused stations rather than five:
  1. **Melting ice:** identify it as a physical change; water remains H₂O. [F5]
  2. **Steel wool oxidation evidence:** interpret reliable mass data as oxygen uptake, not oxygen production. [F6]
  3. **Universal indicator:** infer an acidic pH range only; do not identify a specific acid from colour alone. Use a staff-known, low-hazard coded sample. [F8, F10]
  4. **Rusting:** use nails, images, or condition cards to identify oxygen and water as required conditions and explain that slow room-temperature reactions still have activation energy. [F9]
- Remove the magnesium-and-Bunsen station entirely. [F3]
- Make station prompts questions requiring evidence and explanations, rather than statements pupils are expected to accept. [F5, F6, F9, F10]

### Plenary
- Use the exothermic reaction profile to distinguish:
  - activation energy: the initial energy barrier;
  - overall energy change: more energy released than absorbed.  
  Make clear that exothermic does not mean activation-energy-free. [F7, F9]

### Assessment
- Replace the single high-stakes true/false item with a short formative exit ticket containing several items and brief justifications. [F13]
- Include one item on physical versus chemical change, one on apparent mass loss from an open vessel, one on indicator interpretation, and one on activation energy. [F4, F5, F7, F10]

### Timing
- A workable 60-minute structure is: **5 minutes starter; 10 minutes conservation demonstration; 19 minutes for four stations including transitions; 8 minutes plenary; 8 minutes exit ticket; 10 minutes cleanup.** [F12, F14, F15]
- Four stations can run for about four minutes each with planned movement time; no live student flame work should occur during rotations. [F3, F12]

### Cleanup
- State a strict no-tasting and no-eating rule. [F1]
- Segregate waste according to the school’s disposal procedures; keep metal solids, steel wool/rust, coded samples, and liquids in their designated routes. Do not combine all residues or automatically use the sink. [F2]
- Ensure hot items are cool before handling and have the teacher check cleanup before pupils leave. [F8, F15]
