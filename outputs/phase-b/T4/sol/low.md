## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

### Starter
- Keep the candle only if it is an approved, controlled teacher demonstration; otherwise use an image or video (**F5**).
- Do not state that a flame proves a chemical reaction. Ask students to distinguish observations from interpretations and identify wax melting as physical change and combustion as chemical change (**F7**).
- Establish success criteria: identify whether a new substance forms, account for matter in a defined system, and interpret activation energy.

### Demonstration
- Replace the claim that mass is destroyed with a conservation-of-mass demonstration using an approved closed system or supplied experimental data (**F4, F13**).
- Explicitly define the system boundary: an open beaker appears to lose mass because gas escapes; total matter is still conserved.
- Avoid uncontrolled flames and ensure any teacher demonstration follows the school risk assessment and normal laboratory controls (**F5**).

### Stations
- Use only low-hazard, independently supervised tasks:
  - **Melting ice:** classify it as a physical change because it remains water (**F8**).
  - **Iron oxidation:** preferably use prepared samples, images, or data; explain that oxygen is consumed and added to the iron (**F9**).
  - **Indicator:** use a labelled, teacher-approved low-hazard solution. Conclude only an approximate pH/acidity, not a chemical identity (**F6, F10**).
  - **Magnesium:** remove the cutting-over-flame activity completely; substitute safe observations, video, or data (**F1**).
  - **Rusting:** explain that it is slow but still has an activation-energy barrier (**F11**).
- Do not operate a hazardous teacher demonstration as part of simultaneous group rotations (**F16**).

### Plenary
- Use an energy-profile diagram to distinguish:
  - activation energy—the barrier that must be overcome; and
  - overall energy change—the difference between reactants and products.
- Make clear that exothermic reactions usually still require activation energy (**F12**).
- Revisit the candle and mass demonstrations to correct the central misconceptions from the starter and practical work.

### Assessment
- Replace the single 100%-weighted true/false item with several short questions covering all objectives (**F14, F15**).
- Include:
  1. classification and justification of a physical versus chemical change;
  2. explanation of apparent mass loss in an open system;
  3. interpretation of activation energy on a reaction profile.
- Require brief reasoning and use responses formatively rather than treating one guessed response as complete mastery.

### Timing
- Do not schedule five five-minute stations in a 20-minute block (**F17**).
- A workable 60-minute structure is: brief starter, focused conservation demonstration, three or four low-hazard stations with transition time, plenary, multi-item exit assessment, and protected cleanup time.
- Shorten the original exit-ticket allocation and provide enough time for safe cleanup (**F18**).

### Cleanup
- Ban tasting of all laboratory materials without exception (**F2**).
- Do not combine station waste or automatically pour it into the sink (**F3**).
- Segregate and label liquids, solids, and contaminated materials; dispose of each through the school-approved route.
- The teacher should supervise disposal, confirm that hot items have cooled, inspect benches, and ensure handwashing before dismissal (**F3, F5, F18**).
