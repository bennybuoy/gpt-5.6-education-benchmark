## DELIVERABLE 2 — Corrected Lesson Decisions

### Starter
- Replace the unsupported “flame proves reaction” rule with an observation-and-inference task. Prefer a candle video or image sequence; if a live demonstration is retained, it must be teacher-controlled under school procedures (**F5, F11, F16**).
- Have students distinguish wax melting or solidifying as physical changes from wax combustion as a chemical reaction, and require evidence for each classification (**F10, F11**).

### Demonstration
- Retain the conservation-of-mass focus, but use a pre-risk-assessed system designed to retain the gaseous product safely. Measure the complete apparatus before and after and discuss measurement uncertainty (**F6**).
- Explain that an open-system reading can fall because matter leaves as gas, not because mass is destroyed. Do not tightly seal a gas-generating reaction in unsuitable equipment (**F6**).
- Apply standard school laboratory controls, including eye protection and spill management (**F5**).

### Stations
- Reduce the rotation to three safe, objective-aligned stations (**F15, F17**):
  1. **Physical versus chemical change:** classify ice melting and other prepared examples, with written justification (**F10, F11**).
  2. **Conservation of mass:** analyse prepared steel-wool oxidation data and open-versus-closed-system scenarios. State that oxygen is consumed and incorporated into the product (**F6, F9**).
  3. **Activation energy and rusting:** label an energy profile and interpret safe prepared rusting evidence, including the roles of oxygen and water (**F7, F8, F13**).
- Remove the student magnesium/flame activity entirely; use prepared data or media instead (**F1**).
- Remove the indicator task because it is not aligned with the objectives. If retained for another lesson, use only teacher-known low-hazard samples and infer pH range—not chemical identity—from the colour (**F4, F12, F15**).
- Use predict–observe–explain questions rather than worksheets that announce conclusions (**F16**).

### Plenary
- Correctly distinguish activation energy from overall energy change. On an exothermic profile, products are lower in energy than reactants, but an activation-energy barrier remains (**F7**).
- Ask students to label the barrier and explain why an exothermic reaction or rusting may still need activation energy (**F7, F8, F16**).

### Assessment
- Replace the single 100%-weighted true/false item with several short diagnostic questions covering:
  - classification of a physical and chemical change, with evidence;
  - conservation of mass in an open or closed system;
  - interpretation of activation energy on a reaction profile.
- Require brief explanations and use the results formatively or with a balanced rubric (**F14, F16**).

### Timing
Use a feasible 60-minute allocation, for example:
- Starter: **5 minutes**
- Demonstration and discussion: **10 minutes**
- Three stations, including transitions and shutdown: **22 minutes**
- Plenary: **8 minutes**
- Exit assessment: **8 minutes**
- Final collection and cleanup: **7 minutes**

This resolves the five-stations-in-20-minutes error and includes transition and shutdown time (**F17, F18**).

### Cleanup
- Shut off utilities and secure any demonstration equipment immediately after use; do not leave hot or reactive materials unattended until the end (**F5**).
- Never taste laboratory materials (**F2**).
- Keep waste streams separated and labelled. The teacher or technician should dispose of each material according to school procedures and safety information; only specifically approved material may enter the sink (**F3**).
- Keep appropriate PPE on until equipment and materials have been safely cleared (**F4, F5**).
