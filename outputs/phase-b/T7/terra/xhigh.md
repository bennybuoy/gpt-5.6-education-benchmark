# TEACHER KEY  
## Heat Transfer and Insulation Design — Year 7

## Authoritative science propositions (use verbatim in feedback)

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

---

## Expected observations and explanations

All trials should show a temperature decrease over 10 minutes. A smaller temperature decrease, or a flatter temperature–time graph, indicates slower cooling under comparable conditions.

Do not require an identical ranking between groups. Thickness, compression, gaps, starting temperature, exposed top surface and thermometer placement can affect results.

| Material/setup | Likely observation | Scientific explanation and links |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually the greatest temperature decrease because there is no insulating jacket around the sides. | Energy can leave through the glass and surrounding air by several routes. P1 applies to transfer through the glass. Air and water movement can contribute through P4. Radiation also occurs, consistent with P5. The design has not attempted the measures in P6. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually cools more slowly than a bare beaker and more slowly than a direct foil wrap. Thick, uncompressed cotton wool is generally more effective than tightly compressed cotton. | Cotton wool traps many small air pockets. Use P3: “Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.” The trapped spaces also reduce air circulation around the beaker, linking to P4 and P6. |
| **Bubble wrap** | Often gives a smaller temperature decrease than a bare beaker. It is less effective if bubbles are crushed, gaps are large or the wrap is loose. | The bubbles hold pockets of air, supporting P3. Enclosed air spaces reduce movement of air next to the beaker, linking to P4. This is an example of minimising conduction and convection in P6. |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually provide good insulation when they fit closely without being heavily compressed. A thicker foam layer may reduce cooling further. | Foam contains many air-filled spaces. Apply P3 and P6. It reduces the rate of energy transfer through the jacket and can reduce air movement close to the beaker. |
| **Cardboard** | May improve insulation compared with bare glass, especially if corrugated, layered or used with another air-trapping material. Flat thin cardboard may be less effective than cotton wool, bubble wrap or foam. | Corrugated or layered cardboard can contain air spaces, linking to P3. It can be part of a design that reduces conduction and convection under P6. |
| **Aluminium foil directly against the beaker** | Often cools faster than cotton wool, bubble wrap or foam. It may be similar to, or only slightly different from, the bare beaker. | Aluminium is a metal. Apply P2: “Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.” If it directly contacts the beaker, it creates a conductive path consistent with P1 and P2. |
| **Aluminium foil as an outer layer over insulating material** | May contribute to a better design than foil directly against glass, particularly when combined with cotton wool, foam or bubble wrap. It should not be credited as the only insulating feature. | The inner material can reduce conduction and convection through trapped air (P3, P6). Foil may help reduce radiation when used as a reflective outer surface (P6). Radiation can travel across an air gap because P5 states that it “does not require a medium.” |
| **Masking tape** | Usually has little effect if only small pieces are used to secure the jacket. Excess tape may compress materials or create gaps. | Treat tape mainly as a construction material, not the main insulating material. Students may validly discuss how it altered the fit, compression or air spaces in the jacket. |

### Demonstration interpretation

Typical prediction and result pattern:

- **Cotton wool:** expected to cool slowest of the three, because it traps air pockets.
- **Bare beaker:** expected to cool fastest or close to fastest because the sides are exposed.
- **Foil wrapped directly on the beaker:** should not be automatically treated as the best insulator. Its reflective surface may affect radiation, but its metallic contact provides a rapid conduction pathway.

Accept data that differ from this pattern if measurements are sound and the student explains limitations. Do not mark a student wrong solely because their foil result was slightly better than bare; instead ask them to identify possible variables and distinguish conduction from radiation.

---

## Expected scientific explanation for the final writing task

A strong student explanation should include most of the following:

- Their revised jacket had a smaller temperature decrease than Jacket A, supported by actual temperatures or graph evidence.
- Energy transferred through the glass and any touching material by conduction. Use P1.
- Cotton wool, foam and bubble wrap were effective because they trapped air pockets. Use P3.
- Convection can occur in water and in air around the beaker. Use P4. A jacket can reduce air movement around the sides, but the open top remains a route for heat loss.
- Radiation can occur across an air gap and does not need air. Use P5.
- A combined design, such as air-trapping material near the beaker and foil on the outside, may address more than one heat-transfer route. Use P6.
- Results do not isolate one transfer mode completely; several processes occur at once.

## Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | Teacher response |
|---|---|
| “Foil is always the best insulator because it is shiny.” | Separate foil’s reflective role from its metal conductivity. When directly touching the beaker, aluminium provides a conduction pathway under P2. A reflective outer layer can contribute to reducing radiation under P6, but it does not stop all heat loss. |
| “Metals keep heat in because they feel hot.” | Explain that a metal feels hot because energy reaches the hand rapidly. Revisit P2 and distinguish rapid transfer from good insulation. |
| “Air is an insulator in every situation.” | Ask whether the air is trapped in small pockets or able to circulate. Link still air to P3 and moving fluids to P4. |
| “Heat rises.” | Clarify that heated fluids can rise because of density differences; heat transfer itself can occur in different directions. Use P4 precisely. |
| “Convection happens in solids.” | Revisit that convection involves movement of a liquid or gas. Solids transfer energy mainly by conduction. |
| “Radiation only comes from the Sun” or “radiation needs air.” | Explain that hot objects emit radiation and that P5 states radiation does not require a medium. |
| “The jacket stopped heat transfer.” | Replace “stopped” with “reduced the rate of heat loss.” A real jacket does not eliminate all transfer pathways. |
| “A lower final temperature proves the material was worse.” | Check starting temperatures first. Students should compare temperature decrease only when initial conditions are similar, and acknowledge a limitation if they are not. |
| “My group’s data prove that only one process occurred.” | Reinforce that conduction, convection and radiation can occur at the same time. The design may reduce more than one route. |
