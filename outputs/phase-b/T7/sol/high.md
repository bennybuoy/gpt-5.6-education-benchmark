# TEACHER KEY

## 1. Authoritative science propositions

Use the following exact wording when explaining or assessing the science:

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

---

## 2. Expected demonstration observations

Assuming equal water volumes, similar starting temperatures, identical beakers and the same exposure at the top:

| Beaker | Expected observation | Scientific link |
|---|---|---|
| **Cotton wool** | Usually cools slowest and has the smallest temperature fall. Loosely packed cotton wool is generally more effective than compressed cotton wool. | Cotton wool traps pockets of still air: **P3**. It reduces conduction and limits movement of air near the beaker: **P4, P6**. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Usually cools more slowly than the bare beaker but may not outperform a thick layer of cotton wool. Results may vary with fit, air gaps and exposed area. Foil in direct contact may become warm quickly. | Aluminium is a metal, so **P2** applies. A reflective surface can reduce radiation: **P5, P6**. Foil alone does not provide a thick layer of trapped still air. |
| **Bare beaker** | Usually shows the greatest temperature fall. The surrounding air may warm and rise, and heat is also lost from the uncovered water surface. | Energy passes through touching materials by conduction: **P1**. Moving water and surrounding air transfer energy by convection: **P4**. The beaker and water also transfer energy by radiation: **P5**. **P6** identifies how each route could be reduced. |

### Important interpretation
The precise ranking of foil and bare beakers can vary. The foil’s reflective surface can reduce radiation, but the foil is also a metal and is a good conductor. Cotton wool is expected to provide the clearest insulation effect because it traps still air. Do not treat a single unexpected ranking as proof that the scientific principles are incorrect; first check starting temperatures, coverage, probe placement and other variables.

## 3. Expected observations for available materials

| Material | Expected practical observation | Relevant propositions and explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Bubble wrap** | An intact, closely fitted layer should reduce the temperature fall. Multiple layers may work better than one layer. Crushed or punctured bubbles are less effective. | The bubbles contain pockets of still air, supporting **P3**. Limiting movement within those pockets also helps minimise convection, consistent with **P4 and P6**. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually produces a small temperature fall when thick, dry and loosely packed. Compressing it may reduce its effectiveness. | The spaces between fibres trap still air: **P3**. This minimises conduction and convection as described in **P6**. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Foil alone may give only a small or variable improvement. It can become warm quickly when touching the beaker. It is generally more useful as a reflective outer layer combined with an air-trapping material. | Because it is a metal, **P2** applies. Its reflective surface can reduce transfer by radiation, linking to **P5 and P6**. Warm foil is not evidence that foil is a good insulator. |
| **Cardboard** | Usually gives moderate insulation. Corrugated or layered cardboard is generally more effective than a single compressed sheet. Gaps at joins reduce consistency. | Cardboard layers can trap still air: **P3**. A continuous jacket helps minimise conduction and convection: **P6**. |
| **Foam pieces** | A continuous foam layer should produce a relatively small temperature fall. Gaps between pieces create routes for greater heat loss. | Foam contains many small air pockets, supporting **P3**. It helps minimise conduction and convection: **P6**. |
| **Masking tape** | Tape alone is not expected to provide substantial insulation. It can improve a design by holding layers together and closing gaps. Compression can remove still-air spaces that provide the effect described in **P3**. |
| **No jacket/bare glass** | Expected to show a larger temperature fall than an effective jacket. The outside of the beaker may warm quickly. | Directly contacting materials transfer energy by conduction: **P1**. Surrounding air and water move by convection: **P4**. Radiation also occurs: **P5**. |

### Likely effective revised design
A strong revised design will commonly include:
- a continuous layer of cotton wool, foam or bubble wrap;
- minimal compression of air-trapping materials;
- fewer gaps and exposed areas;
- a reflective outer layer;
- consistent treatment of the top and probe opening according to the class protocol.

This combines the three approaches identified in **P6**. Designs may still differ in effectiveness because of thickness, fit, water temperature and construction quality.

## 4. Data and analysis expectations

### Expected data pattern
- Temperature should generally decrease over the ten-minute period.
- An effective jacket should have a smaller \(T_0-T_{10}\) value than a less effective jacket.
- A successful revision should produce a smaller temperature fall and lower average cooling rate than Test 1, provided starting conditions are similar.
- Minor fluctuations can result from probe response, probe movement, reading errors or changes in room conditions.

### Acceptable analysis
Students should compare **temperature fall**, not simply final temperature. For example, a revised test ending at a higher temperature is not automatically better if it also began at a much higher temperature.

A suitable evidence statement is:

> “Test 1 fell by 12°C, while Test 2 fell by 8°C. The revised design therefore reduced the temperature fall by 4°C over ten minutes.”

If starting temperatures differ substantially, students should acknowledge this as a limitation. They may still compare general cooling curves, but the conclusion should be cautious.

### Unsuccessful revisions
An unsuccessful revision is valid evidence if the student:
- records it accurately;
- identifies a plausible reason;
- does not alter or invent data;
- links the outcome to construction, variables or heat-transfer pathways.

Possible reasons include compressed cotton wool, punctured bubble wrap, gaps between foam pieces, a changed probe position, a different starting temperature or greater exposed area.

## 5. Correct scientific explanation

A complete Year 7 explanation should establish the following:

- **P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.
- In this investigation, thermal energy can be conducted from the hot water to the glass and then through materials touching the glass.
- **P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.
- Therefore, aluminium foil touching the beaker can become warm quickly and should not be described simply as a poor conductor.
- **P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.
- Cotton wool, bubble wrap, foam and layered cardboard can reduce conduction by trapping still air.
- **P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.
- Convection occurs within the water and in the surrounding air. Trapping air and reducing gaps can limit external air movement.
- **P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.
- The beaker, water and jacket surfaces can transfer energy by radiation.
- **P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

### Example teacher model

> The revised jacket cooled more slowly because its temperature fell by less over ten minutes. Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving. The foam and cotton wool reduced conduction because insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor. Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating, so closing gaps reduced the movement of warmed air around the beaker. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium, and the reflective foil surface helped reduce this pathway. Overall, the revised design reduced heat loss by addressing conduction, convection and radiation.

Students do not need to reproduce every sentence word-for-word, but their claims must be scientifically consistent with P1–P6.

## 6. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| **“Cold moved into the water.”** | Reframe the observation as thermal energy transferring from the hotter water to cooler surroundings. Ask students to identify the direction of the temperature difference. |
| **“Insulation makes heat” or “stops all heat transfer.”** | Explain that insulation reduces the rate of energy transfer; it does not create energy or prevent all transfer. Point to the continuing, but slower, temperature decrease. |
| **“Foil must be the best insulator because it is shiny.”** | Separate the mechanisms. Use **P2** to explain the conductivity of metals and **P5–P6** to explain the role of a reflective surface in radiation. Compare foil alone with foil combined with an air-trapping layer. |
| **“If the foil feels warm, it has kept the heat in.”** | A warm outer surface indicates that energy has reached that surface. It does not by itself show a low total cooling rate. Use the temperature data as the main evidence. |
| **“Air gaps are always bad.”** | Distinguish enclosed pockets of still air from open gaps that allow air to circulate or escape. Use **P3** for still air and **P4** for moving fluid. |
| **“Compressed cotton wool works better because there is more material in one place.”** | Compression removes many still-air pockets. Compare loosely packed and compressed samples conceptually using **P3**. |
| **“Convection occurs through the cardboard or foil.”** | Use **P4**: convection requires movement of a fluid, meaning a liquid or gas. Transfer through stationary solid material is discussed as conduction. |
| **“Radiation only comes from the Sun” or “radiation needs air.”** | Use **P5** exactly. All objects can transfer thermal energy by radiation, and radiation does not require a medium. |
| **“The design with the highest final temperature is automatically best.”** | Require comparison of starting temperature, temperature fall and test duration. |
| **“A thicker jacket must always be better.”** | Thickness may help only if the arrangement retains effective insulating layers. Compression, gaps, moisture and poor fit can reduce performance. |
| **“One unexpected result disproves the explanation.”** | Check fair-test variables, probe placement, construction and measurement uncertainty. Encourage conclusions that match the strength of the evidence. |
| **“The thermometer should touch the glass to get a stronger reading.”** | Keep the probe in the water at a consistent depth without touching the base or sides. Contact with the glass can make tests less comparable. |
