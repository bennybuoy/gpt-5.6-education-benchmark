# TEACHER KEY

## 1. Intended implementation

The practical is collaborative in construction and testing, but accountability is individual. Each student records the team’s measured temperatures on their own resource and independently completes predictions, observations, sketches, calculations, revision rationale, graph and final explanation.

A workable 35-minute schedule is:

- **0–5 min:** Build Design 1.
- **5–15 min:** Test Trial 1.
- **During readings:** Complete observations and begin graphing.
- **15–20 min:** Analyse and plan the mandatory revision.
- **20–25 min:** Modify the jacket and prepare the retest.
- **25–35 min:** Test Trial 2 and record results.

The final comparison and scientific explanation continue into the approved 15-minute explanation phase.

For reasonable comparability, use the same water volume and aim for similar starting temperatures. If starting temperatures differ, students should compare the **temperature drop over 10 minutes**, not simply the final temperatures.

Do not allow students to seal the beaker opening around the probe unless this is part of a consistently applied teacher protocol. The supplied task is an insulating **jacket**, so keeping the top uncovered makes comparisons more consistent.

---

## 2. Authoritative scientific explanations

Use the following exact statements when teaching, assessing or correcting explanations:

**P1.** Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.

**P2.** Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.

**P3.** Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.

**P4.** Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.

**P5.** Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.

**P6.** Reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).

Students are not expected to reproduce every statement word-for-word, but their explanations should be scientifically consistent with them.

## 3. Expected observations and material explanations

Actual rankings may vary with thickness, compression, gaps, moisture, foil orientation and construction quality. Reward evidence-based explanations rather than requiring one universal ranking.

| Material/design | Expected observations | Scientific link |
|---|---|---|
| **Bare beaker** | Usually shows a relatively large temperature decrease because there is no added insulating layer. The glass and exposed surface transfer energy to the surroundings. Warm air near the beaker can rise and be replaced by cooler air. | **P1:** Energy can be transferred through the glass and at surfaces in contact. **P4:** Air warmed near the beaker moves due to density differences. **P5:** The beaker also transfers energy by radiation. |
| **Cotton wool** | Usually slows cooling when dry, fluffy and thick. It may perform less effectively if compressed, wet or separated by large gaps from the beaker. | **P3:** “Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.” It also limits bulk air movement, supporting **P4** and **P6**. |
| **Bubble wrap** | Usually slows cooling because sealed bubbles trap air. Multiple intact layers may outperform one thin layer. Punctured or tightly flattened bubbles may reduce effectiveness. | Links strongly to **P3** because the bubbles trap pockets of still air. Trapping air also reduces convection, linking to **P4** and **P6**. |
| **Foam pieces** | Usually reduce cooling when gaps are minimised and the foam creates a continuous jacket. Large gaps or sparse pieces allow more energy transfer. | Foam contains trapped air, so the expected explanation is **P3**. A well-fitted foam layer supports **P6** by minimising conduction and convection. |
| **Cardboard** | Usually provides moderate insulation, especially if corrugated, layered or used to hold an air gap. Thin, wet or tightly compressed cardboard may be less effective. | Cardboard and the air spaces within or between its layers support **P3**. A cardboard air gap can also restrict fluid movement, linking to **P4** and **P6**. |
| **Aluminium foil** | Results depend strongly on placement. A shiny exposed surface or a foil layer facing an air gap may reduce radiation. Foil pressed directly against the beaker may not provide strong conductive insulation and can spread thermal energy through the metal. | **P2:** “Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.” A reflective surface links to **P5** and the radiation component of **P6**. Foil is generally more useful as a reflective layer than as the main trapped-air insulating layer. |
| **Masking tape** | Useful for holding layers in place and closing gaps, but a large amount can compress cotton wool or bubble wrap and reduce trapped air. Tape alone is not expected to be an effective jacket. | Closing gaps can reduce moving air, linking to **P4** and **P6**. Compression can reduce the pockets of still air described in **P3**. |
| **Layered design** | A combination such as cotton wool, bubble wrap or foam plus a reflective outer layer may cool more slowly than a single poorly fitted material. Performance depends on retaining air spaces and avoiding gaps. | A strong explanation integrates **P3**, **P5** and **P6**. |
| **Open top** | The exposed water surface may remain a major route of heat loss. Students may observe warm air above the beaker or rapid initial cooling. | Fluid movement links to **P4**. Students may identify the opening as a design limitation, while still following the instruction to keep it uncovered for a fair comparison. |

### Demonstration expectation

A dry, thick cotton-wool wrapping will commonly cool more slowly than the bare beaker because it traps pockets of still air. Aluminium foil results depend on whether it is tightly touching the beaker, loosely wrapped with an air gap, and which surface faces outward. Do not present “foil always wins” or “cotton always wins” as an absolute rule.

Relevant propositions are **P2, P3, P5 and P6**. The demonstration should be used to generate and test predictions, not to establish a ranking that overrides measured evidence.

## 4. Features of a strong student response

### Data analysis

A strong response:

- records six temperatures for each trial;
- calculates each 10-minute temperature drop correctly;
- identifies the steepest 2-minute decrease using data;
- compares temperature drops rather than final temperatures alone;
- recognises any difference in starting temperature;
- notes anomalies such as a shifted probe, spill, loose jacket or delayed reading;
- avoids claiming that a very small difference is conclusive if measurement uncertainty could explain it.

### Revision rationale

The revision must respond to Trial 1 evidence. Suitable examples include:

- adding a bubble-wrap, cotton-wool or foam layer to trap more still air;
- loosening compressed cotton wool;
- closing large side gaps with tape without compressing the insulating layer;
- changing the order of layers;
- placing foil as a reflective outer layer or next to an air gap;
- replacing wet material;
- improving coverage around the sides and base;
- removing unnecessary direct-contact foil if it is being treated incorrectly as the main conductive insulator.

An acceptable rationale names:

1. an observation or measured result from Trial 1;
2. a specific design change;
3. the predicted effect on one or more heat-transfer pathways.

### Model scientific explanation

> Design 2 was more effective because its temperature decreased by 8°C, while Design 1 decreased by 12°C over the same time. The added bubble wrap trapped pockets of still air. **Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.** The sealed air spaces also reduced the movement of warmed air. **Convection is the transfer of thermal energy by the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) due to density differences caused by heating.** The shiny foil outer layer was intended to reduce energy transfer by radiation. **Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.** Overall, **reducing heat loss from a container can involve minimising conduction (using insulating materials), minimising convection (trapping still air), and minimising radiation (using reflective surfaces).**

If discussing conduction directly, use:

> **Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by direct contact between particles, without the material itself moving.**

If discussing foil as a metal, use:

> **Metals are good thermal conductors because they contain free electrons that transfer energy rapidly.**

## 5. Common misconceptions and responses

| Misconception | How to address it |
|---|---|
| **“Cold moved into the beaker.”** | Reframe the evidence in terms of thermal energy transferring from the warmer water and beaker to the cooler surroundings. Ask: “Which object began at the higher temperature, and in which direction would energy transfer?” |
| **“Insulation creates heat.”** | Point out that no heater is inside the jacket. Insulation changes the rate of energy transfer; it does not create thermal energy. Compare both cooling curves. |
| **“Cotton wool works because cotton is warm.”** | Ask what is held between the fibres. Reinforce **P3**: “Insulators are poor conductors because they trap pockets of still air, and still air is a poor conductor.” |
| **“All air is a good insulator.”** | Distinguish still, trapped air from moving air. Moving air can transfer energy by convection. Use **P4** exactly when correcting the explanation. |
| **“Convection happens in solids.”** | Clarify that convection requires a fluid—a liquid or gas. Use **P4** and contrast fluid movement with the direct-contact particle process in **P1**. |
| **“Metal foil must be the best insulator because it looks shiny.”** | Separate two properties: metal conducts thermal energy readily, while a reflective surface can reduce radiation. Apply **P2**, **P5** and **P6**. Placement and air gaps matter. |
| **“Foil reflects all heat.”** | Explain that radiation is only one transfer mode. Conduction and convection can still occur. Apply **P6**. |
| **“Thicker is always better.”** | Thickness can help if it traps still air, but compression, wetness, gaps and poor fit may reduce effectiveness. Require students to refer to construction and data. |
| **“The design with the lowest final temperature was best.”** | If starting temperatures differed, compare the temperature decreases over the same interval. Also consider whether differences are larger than likely measurement variation. |
| **“A faster-cooling trial proves the material does not insulate.”** | Check fair-test factors and construction. A loose jacket, different starting temperature, altered probe depth or wet material can affect the result. One trial provides evidence, not absolute proof. |
| **“Radiation needs air to travel.”** | Use **P5** exactly: “Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves and does not require a medium.” |
| **“Conduction means particles travel through the material.”** | Use **P1** exactly, emphasising energy transfer by direct contact “without the material itself moving.” |
