The three options use different physical mechanisms:

1. **Measured opposing pulls** — students manipulate known forces and compare resultant force with motion.
2. **Sudden support and carrier changes** — students observe an object retaining its previous motion when another object is abruptly removed or stopped.
3. **Air-force trajectory mapping** — students use balloon air to apply a transient sideways force and measure changes in path and the response of different masses.

Each is a complete alternative for the 35-minute main block.

## 1. Opposing-force cart investigation

### 1. Mechanism name
**Opposing-force cart investigation**

### 2. Physical student action

The class forms seven groups of four. Each group receives a toy car, two spring scales, two lengths of string, tape, a ruler, and a 200 g mass. The teacher has already attached string loops to the front and back of each car and marked a straight 40 cm lane on the bench.

**0–3 minutes: Set roles and apparatus**

- Two students operate the spring scales from opposite ends of the lane.
- One student watches scale readings and the car’s motion.
- One student records results.
- Students keep both strings horizontal and aligned with the lane.

**3–12 minutes: Balanced and unbalanced pulls**

Groups conduct the following trials, returning the car to the start each time:

1. **No horizontal pull:** Record whether the stationary car moves.
2. **Equal opposing pulls:** Both students gradually pull until both scales read approximately 1.0 N. A third student releases the initially held car without pushing it.
3. **Unequal opposing pulls:** One scale is held near 1.0 N and the other near 0.5 N.
4. **Reverse imbalance:** The scale readings are reversed.

Students record both readings, calculate the difference between the measured pulls, and note whether the car remains at rest or moves left or right.

**12–25 minutes: Comparing inertia**

- One string and scale are used to pull the car along the 40 cm lane at approximately 1.0 N.
- Students time how long the unloaded car takes to travel 40 cm from rest.
- They repeat three times.
- They tape the 200 g mass centrally on the same car and repeat the three trials using the same approximate applied force.
- Roles rotate so that one student pulls, one watches the scale and calls “higher” or “lower,” one times, and one records.

**25–30 minutes: Hands-off motion**

- A student gives the unloaded car one brief push and immediately removes their hand.
- The group measures how far the car continues after contact ends.
- They identify rolling resistance and friction as backward, unbalanced forces that eventually slow and stop the car.

**30–35 minutes: Record and pack up**

Groups calculate median travel times for the unloaded and loaded car and write a one-sentence claim connecting force balance, motion change, and mass.

### 3. Science payoff

Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- Equal opposite forces produce zero resultant force, so an object initially at rest has no change in motion.
- Unequal forces produce an unbalanced resultant force, so the car’s velocity changes in the direction of the larger force.
- Under approximately the same applied force, the loaded car changes its motion more slowly than the unloaded car. The greater total mass therefore has greater inertia.
- The car does not require a continuous forward push simply to keep moving. It continues after the hand is removed; it eventually slows because friction and rolling resistance are unbalanced forces.
- “Balanced forces” means **no change in velocity**, not necessarily “no forces” and not necessarily “stationary.”

### 4. Evidence

Each group produces a table such as:

| Left pull | Right pull | Difference between pulls | Motion observed |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| 0 N | 0 N | 0 N | Stayed at rest |
| 1.0 N | 1.0 N | 0 N | Stayed at rest |
| 1.0 N | 0.5 N | 0.5 N left | Moved left |
| 0.5 N | 1.0 N | 0.5 N right | Moved right |

Students also record:

- Three 40 cm travel times for the unloaded car.
- Three travel times for the car with the added 200 g.
- The median time for each condition.
- The distance the car coasted after the brief push ended.
- Force-arrow sketches for one balanced and one unbalanced trial.

The expected pattern is that zero difference between opposing pulls produces no observed change from rest, while a nonzero difference produces motion toward the larger pull. The loaded car should take longer to cover the lane under a similar applied pull.

### 5. Feasibility

- Seven groups of four remain at fixed laboratory benches.
- All equipment is from the permitted list.
- The teacher can pre-place seven trays containing one car, two scales, strings, a ruler, tape, and a mass. Pre-attached string loops reduce setup time.
- Pulls remain below the limits of both 5 N and 10 N scales.
- The 40 cm lane fits on a standard bench and keeps cars away from bench edges.
- The activity includes approximately three minutes for apparatus setup and two minutes for packing away, leaving the separate ten-minute synthesis discussion intact.
- One teacher can monitor the class by checking string alignment, scale readings, and whether masses are taped securely.

### 6. Failure risk

**Most likely failure:** Jerky or poorly aligned pulls make the car move even when scale readings appear equal, leading students to conclude that balanced forces can cause acceleration.

**Mitigation:**

- Students increase both pulls gradually and release the car only when both readings are stable.
- Strings must be horizontal and aligned with the lane.
- Groups repeat anomalous trials rather than discarding the principle after one result.
- The teacher distinguishes the measured opposing pulls from all forces acting on the car, including friction.
- During synthesis, the teacher explicitly states that a brief initial imbalance can start the car moving; later equal pulls do not erase the motion automatically.

## 2. Sudden support and carrier changes

### 1. Mechanism name
**Sudden support and carrier tests**

### 2. Physical student action

Each group receives a plastic cup, two cardboard pieces, a marble, a toy car, a book, tape, a ruler, and a 100 g mass. The mechanism consists of three related sudden-change events.

**0–3 minutes: Set roles**

For each event, students rotate through predictor, operator, observer, and recorder roles. Every trial requires a written prediction before it is performed.

**3–11 minutes: Card, marble, and cup**

- Students place a cardboard square over the mouth of an upright plastic cup.
- They place a marble at the centre of the cardboard.
- First, they pull the cardboard slowly sideways and record what happens.
- They reset the apparatus.
- They then flick the cardboard sharply and horizontally, without striking upward.
- They repeat the sharp flick three times.

With a slow pull, friction has time to exert a horizontal force on the marble, so it tends to move with the cardboard. With a sharp flick, the marble receives little horizontal impulse and retains its horizontal state of rest while the cardboard moves away. Once the support is removed, gravity is unbalanced and the marble falls into the cup.

**11–20 minutes: Sudden stop of a moving carrier**

- Students tape a small flat cardboard platform to the toy car.
- They place an empty plastic cup loosely on the platform as a “passenger.”
- A book is placed across the end of a short cardboard lane as a stop.
- A student rolls the car gently toward the book and removes their hand.
- The car strikes the book and stops, while the loose cup continues forward or topples forward.
- Students repeat three times, measuring the distance from the car’s stopping position to where the cup first touches the cardboard.

The book exerts a large unbalanced force on the car. It does not exert that stopping force directly on the loose passenger, so the passenger retains its forward motion until another force acts on it.

**20–31 minutes: Same collision, different target mass**

- Students form a shallow ramp by resting one end of cardboard on a book.
- A tape line marks a fixed marble-release point.
- The marble is released without a push and rolls into the stationary toy car.
- Students measure how far the empty car moves after the collision.
- They repeat three times.
- They tape the 100 g mass centrally to the car and repeat from the same marble-release point.

The incoming marble supplies a repeatable disturbance. The more massive car should show a smaller change in motion.

**31–35 minutes: Consolidate and pack up**

Groups complete before-and-after motion sketches and write a claim explaining all three events using inertia rather than terms such as “magic” or “the object was thrown.”

### 3. Science payoff

Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- Before the card is moved, the marble remains at rest because gravity downward and the card’s support force upward are balanced, with no horizontal resultant force.
- An object does not automatically follow the object supporting or carrying it. Its motion changes only if a force acts on it for sufficient time.
- The sharply flicked card moves away while the marble retains its horizontal state of rest.
- When the car stops suddenly, the loose passenger continues forward because the stopping force acts first on the car, not directly on the passenger.
- A larger mass has greater inertia: the loaded car undergoes a smaller motion change from a similar incoming collision.
- Slow pulling, sudden stopping, gravity after support removal, and collision forces are all examples of unbalanced forces changing motion.

### 4. Evidence

Students record:

- A prediction and observed result for the slow card pull.
- Results from three sharp-flick trials, including whether the marble landed in the cup.
- Before-and-after diagrams showing the card’s motion and the marble’s motion.
- Three measured passenger travel distances after the car stopped.
- Three measured car displacements after the marble collision for:
  - the unloaded car;
  - the car with the added 100 g.
- Median collision displacement for each car condition.

A suitable evidence table is:

| Event | Object considered | What force changed? | Recorded result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slow card pull | Marble | Horizontal friction acts for longer | Marble moves sideways |
| Sharp card flick | Marble | Very little horizontal impulse | Marble stays over cup, then falls |
| Car hits book | Loose cup | No immediate stopping force from book | Cup continues forward |
| Marble hits unloaded car | Car | Collision force | Larger displacement |
| Marble hits loaded car | Car | Similar incoming collision | Smaller displacement |

### 5. Feasibility

- All seven groups work simultaneously at their own benches.
- Marbles and cars remain inside cardboard lanes, with books or cups used as end catches.
- Only one low-speed marble and one car are moving in each group at a time.
- The teacher can pre-place cardboard pieces and tape marks and demonstrate one correct horizontal card flick during the five-minute introduction.
- No station rotation is required, which reduces congestion around fixed benches.
- The three events use the same small set of materials and can be reset quickly.
- The final four minutes include recording and packing, preserving the ten-minute synthesis discussion.

### 6. Failure risk

**Most likely failure:** Students interpret the events merely as tricks—for example, “the card moved too fast” or “the cup was thrown”—without identifying which object received which force.

**Mitigation:**

- Require students to name one specific object at a time: marble, card, car, passenger, or loaded car.
- Require a force-arrow and motion-arrow sketch before and after each event.
- Compare slow and rapid card pulls so students see that friction acts during the slow pull.
- Use the fixed marble-release line and three repeated collision trials rather than relying on one dramatic result.
- Keep the car speed low and the cup loose; if the passenger is taped down, the tape itself supplies the stopping force and destroys the intended comparison.

## 3. Balloon air-force trajectory mapping

### 1. Mechanism name
**Air-force trajectory and mass-response mapping**

### 2. Physical student action

Each group receives cardboard, a marble, a balloon, a ruler, tape, string, a plastic cup, two books, and 100 g and 200 g masses. The same balloon air jet is used first to change a moving object’s direction and then to compare the response of different masses.

One student is the designated balloon user so balloons are not shared.

**0–5 minutes: Construct the path and pendulum**

- Students tape a straight cardboard lane to the bench.
- One end is raised on a book to make a shallow marble ramp.
- A centreline and a finishing line approximately 40 cm beyond the ramp are marked with tape.
- For the later mass test, a ruler is placed across two stable book stacks.
- A 30 cm string is taped to the ruler, with the plastic cup secured at the lower end so that it can swing a few centimetres above the bench.
- A ruler or taped scale is placed behind the cup to measure sideways displacement.

**5–13 minutes: Baseline motion path**

- The marble is released from the same taped point on the ramp three times, without being pushed.
- Students mark or measure where it crosses the finishing line relative to the centreline.
- They note that the marble continues along the horizontal section after it has lost contact with the launcher.
- They also note any slowing and identify friction and rolling resistance as backward unbalanced forces rather than evidence that motion “runs out.”

**13–23 minutes: Sideways unbalanced force**

- The balloon is inflated to approximately the same 15 cm width for each trial, checked with a ruler.
- As the marble reaches a taped point on the horizontal section, the balloon operator releases air from a nozzle held approximately 5 cm from the path and perpendicular to it.
- Students measure the marble’s signed lateral displacement at the finishing line: for example, 6 cm left or 4 cm right.
- They conduct three no-air trials, three left-side air trials, and, if time permits, three right-side air trials.

The sideways air force changes the marble’s direction. Reversing the side of the balloon reverses the direction of the change.

**23–31 minutes: Mass and inertia using a suspended cup**

- Students place the 100 g mass in the suspended cup and allow it to become stationary.
- The balloon is inflated to the same measured size and its nozzle is held at a marked distance from the cup.
- The air is released sideways without touching the cup.
- An observer records the maximum sideways displacement of the cup.
- The test is repeated three times.
- Students replace the 100 g mass with the 200 g mass and repeat.

The same cup is used in both conditions, so its exposed shape is unchanged. The greater total mass should swing a smaller distance in response to a similar air impulse.

**31–35 minutes: Record and pack up**

Groups calculate median sideways displacements and draw force arrows for the stationary pendulum, the marble without crosswind, and the marble during the air blast.

### 3. Science payoff

Students should arrive at these specific ideas:

- A moving object continues moving after the launching contact ends; a continuing forward force is not required simply to maintain motion.
- With no significant sideways resultant force, the marble continues approximately along its existing direction.
- A sideways air force is unbalanced and changes the marble’s velocity by changing its direction.
- Reversing the unbalanced force reverses the direction of the change.
- Before the suspended cup is disturbed, tension upward and weight downward are balanced, so its motion does not change.
- When the air jet acts, there is an unbalanced sideways force and the cup begins moving.
- The 200 g condition shows a smaller response than the 100 g condition under a comparable air impulse. Greater mass therefore means greater inertia and greater resistance to a change in motion.
- The pendulum is not expected to continue in a straight line after the air stops because tension and gravity continue to act and change its direction.

### 4. Evidence

Students collect:

- Three signed lateral displacements for the marble with no air jet.
- Three displacements with the air jet from the left.
- Three displacements with the air jet from the right, if completed.
- A top-view sketch of the baseline and deflected marble paths.
- Three maximum cup displacements with 100 g.
- Three maximum cup displacements with 200 g.
- Median displacement for each mass.
- Force-arrow diagrams showing:
  - balanced tension and weight before the cup moves;
  - the additional sideways air force;
  - the sideways force and corresponding change in the marble’s path.

A typical result pattern would be near-zero lateral displacement in the baseline trials, displacement away from the air jet in crosswind trials, and a smaller pendulum displacement for 200 g than for 100 g.

### 5. Feasibility

- Seven groups work entirely at their own benches.
- The teacher can prepare seven cup-and-string harnesses before the lesson using only tape, string, and cups.
- The book stacks are low and stable; the cup swings close to the bench, so masses cannot fall far.
- Balloons are released rather than popped.
- Tape marks standardise the marble release point, balloon position, and nozzle distance.
- The full sequence allocates five minutes to construction, eighteen minutes to path tests, eight minutes to the mass comparison, and four minutes to recording and cleanup.
- The equipment packs flat or into cups and can be collected quickly before the ten-minute synthesis.

### 6. Failure risk

**Most likely failure:** Variable balloon inflation, nozzle direction, or release timing produces inconsistent measurements, obscuring the connection between unbalanced force and motion change.

**Mitigation:**

- Inflate to the same measured balloon width each time.
- Mark the nozzle position and distance with tape.
- Keep the same student as balloon operator for a set of trials.
- Use three trials and compare medians rather than relying on one result.
- Keep the same cup and suspension geometry when changing mass.
- If the air jet misses the marble, record the trial as invalid and repeat it.
- During synthesis, treat the measurements as qualitative evidence rather than claiming that the balloon supplies an exactly identical force in every trial.
