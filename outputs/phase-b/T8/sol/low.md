## Mechanism 1: Opposing-force car trials

### 1. Mechanism name
**Balanced and unbalanced car pulls**

### 2. Physical student action
Each group of 3–4 receives a toy car, two spring scales, string, tape, a ruler, stopwatch, and masses. Roles rotate between pullers, timer/observer, and recorder.

**Sequence for the 35-minute block:**

- **5 minutes — Setup and prediction**
  - Students tape short string loops to the front and rear of the toy car and connect a spring scale to each end.
  - They mark a starting line and a 30 cm distance on the bench with tape.
  - Before each trial, they predict whether the car will remain at rest, speed up, or move toward one puller.

- **10 minutes — Balanced and unbalanced forces**
  - Two students pull in opposite directions while keeping both scales at approximately 2 N.
  - The group records both scale readings and whether the car’s motion changes.
  - They repeat with 3 N against 1 N, then reverse the stronger force.
  - If the car is already rolling slowly, students briefly establish approximately equal opposing readings and observe whether there is a large change in its motion. The teacher explains that bench friction prevents a perfect constant-velocity demonstration, but equal applied forces do not produce the acceleration seen with unequal pulls.

- **12 minutes — Comparing inertia**
  - Students test the empty car and then the same car carrying a securely taped 500 g mass.
  - One student pulls each car forward with the same target force, such as 2 N, over the same 30 cm distance. The timer records the travel time.
  - Groups conduct three trials for each mass and calculate or identify the typical result.
  - If a car cannot safely hold 500 g, groups use 100 g and 200 g combinations that the car can carry securely.

- **5 minutes — Force diagrams**
  - Students sketch the car in three situations: equal opposite pulls, stronger pull to the right, and stronger pull to the left.
  - Arrow length represents the measured scale reading.

- **3 minutes — Group conclusion**
  - Each group writes one claim linking net force to motion and one claim linking mass to resistance to acceleration.

### 3. Science payoff
Students should arrive at the following ideas:

- Equal and opposite forces are balanced, so there is no net force and therefore no change in motion.
- Unequal forces are unbalanced, so the car accelerates in the direction of the larger force.
- Applying approximately the same force to a more massive car produces a smaller change in motion. Greater mass therefore means greater inertia.
- “No change in motion” can mean remaining at rest or continuing at constant velocity; it does not mean that an object must be stationary.

The teacher should explicitly distinguish force from motion: a force is a push or pull that can change motion, not something an object “uses up” while moving.

### 4. Evidence
Students record:

- Left and right spring-scale readings for each trial.
- The calculated or verbally identified net force, such as \(3\text{ N right} - 1\text{ N left} = 2\text{ N right}\).
- The direction of movement or change in movement.
- Three travel times for the empty and loaded car under the same target pull.
- Force-arrow sketches showing balanced and unbalanced cases.

A suitable evidence table is:

| Left pull | Right pull | Balanced? | Observed change in motion |
|---|---:|---|---|
| 2 N | 2 N | Yes | Little or no change |
| 1 N | 3 N | No | Changes toward right |

A second table compares empty-car and loaded-car travel times.

### 5. Feasibility
Seven groups can work simultaneously at fixed benches. All movement occurs over a 30 cm bench section, so cars do not need to travel through the room. Equipment consists only of listed materials.

The teacher can prepare seven trays containing a car, scales, strings, tape, ruler, stopwatch, and masses. String loops can be cut before the lesson, while students attach them during the setup period. Roles reduce crowding because only two students pull at one time. Cleanup involves detaching scales, removing tape, and returning masses to trays.

The five-minute introduction can establish “force as a push or pull.” The ten-minute synthesis can compare group results and formalise Newton’s First Law.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Friction and inconsistent pulling may make equal forces appear to cause movement or may obscure the mass comparison.

**Mitigation:**

- Require students to record actual scale readings rather than merely claiming that both pulls were equal.
- Use repeated trials and treat small jerks as measurement limitations.
- Keep the pulling distance short and use the same bench surface for both mass conditions.
- Discuss friction explicitly as another force. A stationary car can have balanced pulls even when the scale readings are not perfectly equal because friction may contribute to the force balance.
- Base the conclusion on the overall pattern across repeated trials, not a single run.

## Mechanism 2: Predict–test inertia stations

### 1. Mechanism name
**Inertia prediction stations**

### 2. Physical student action
Groups rotate through three short physical tests. The central strategy is to make a written prediction before revealing the result, then explain the result using forces and inertia.

**Sequence for the 35-minute block:**

- **4 minutes — Teacher instructions**
  - Each group receives a prediction sheet.
  - Students must sketch what they think will happen before touching the apparatus.

- **8 minutes — Station A: Object at rest**
  - Students place a plastic cup upright on the bench, put a piece of cardboard across its opening, and place a marble at the centre of the cardboard.
  - One student pulls the cardboard sideways slowly. The group records what happens.
  - They reset the system. One student then snaps the cardboard horizontally away with a quick, level movement while another watches the marble.
  - The marble tends to remain near its original horizontal position and falls into the cup when its support is removed.

- **9 minutes — Station B: Object in motion**
  - Students make a shallow ramp by resting one end of a ruler on a book.
  - They release a marble from the same marked point so it rolls onto a flat strip of cardboard.
  - They sketch the marble’s path and mark where it slows, strikes a taped cardboard barrier, or changes direction.
  - They identify the force associated with each change: friction slows it, the barrier stops or redirects it, and gravity acted while it travelled down the ramp.
  - The test is repeated three times from the same release point.

- **9 minutes — Station C: Greater mass, greater inertia**
  - Students place an empty toy car at a marked starting line.
  - A marble is released from a fixed point on the ruler ramp so that it strikes the car. Students measure how far the car moves.
  - They tape a 200 g or 500 g mass securely to the car, return it to the same starting line, and release the same marble from the same point.
  - They repeat each condition three times.
  - The loaded car should usually undergo a smaller change in motion from the same incoming marble interaction.

- **5 minutes — Evidence sort**
  - Groups arrange their observations under three headings:
    1. stays at rest unless acted on;
    2. motion changes when an unbalanced force acts;
    3. greater mass gives greater resistance to a change in motion.

Stations can be arranged so groups begin at different points and rotate once directed by the teacher.

### 3. Science payoff
The linked observations support three parts of Newton’s First Law:

- The marble on the card does not automatically move sideways with the card. Its inertia resists a change from rest. Once the support is removed, gravity is an unbalanced force and the marble falls.
- The rolling marble changes speed or direction only when forces such as friction or contact with a barrier act on it.
- The loaded car changes its motion less than the unloaded car when struck under approximately the same conditions. Greater mass means greater inertia.
- If all forces on an object were balanced, its velocity would not change. Everyday objects slow because forces such as friction are present, not because continued motion inherently requires a continuing forward force.

### 4. Evidence
Students collect:

- A prediction sketch and an observation sketch for the card-and-cup test.
- A marked path showing where the rolling marble slows, stops, or changes direction.
- A force label for each observed change in motion.
- Three measured travel distances for the unloaded car and three for the loaded car.
- A final claim–evidence statement, for example:  
  **Claim:** The loaded car has greater inertia.  
  **Evidence:** When struck by a marble released from the same point, it travelled a shorter distance in all three trials.

A possible table is:

| Car condition | Trial 1 distance | Trial 2 distance | Trial 3 distance | Typical result |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Unloaded |  |  |  |  |
| Loaded |  |  |  |  |

### 5. Feasibility
The three stations use only cups, cardboard, marbles, rulers, books, toy cars, tape, and masses. Each station can be duplicated across the room rather than requiring every group to queue at one location. For example, three groups can begin with the cup test, two with marble motion, and two with the collision comparison.

The marble ramps and barriers remain on bench tops. Cardboard barriers taped at the end of each run prevent marbles from falling onto the floor. Masses are taped firmly to cars and remain below bench height. The teacher circulates rather than running a separate demonstration for every group.

Cleanup is short: marbles go into cups, tape is removed from cars, and books and rulers return to their usual locations.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students may explain the card test as “the marble falls because the card is pulled” without recognising that the card exerts very little sideways force on the marble during a rapid pull.

**Mitigation:**

- Require comparison between a slow pull and a rapid snap. During the slow pull, friction has more time to accelerate the marble sideways; during the rapid pull, it does not.
- Ask students to separate horizontal and vertical events: the marble initially resists horizontal motion, then gravity pulls it vertically into the cup.
- In the collision test, use the same marble, ramp angle, release mark, car, and bench surface for both mass conditions.
- If collision distances vary, use three trials and compare the overall pattern rather than one result.

## Mechanism 3: Human motion-and-force model

### 1. Mechanism name
**Motion-state enactment**

### 2. Physical student action
Students build and run a physical model in which one student represents an object, other students represent forces, and another records the object’s motion. This is a rule-governed simulation rather than an apparatus experiment.

Each group creates a short “motion lane” beside or behind its bench using two tape marks approximately 1 m apart. Students move only by slow heel-to-toe steps; there is no running or physical pushing.

**Roles:**

- **Object:** moves through the lane.
- **Force students:** stand on either side holding cardboard arrows labelled with force size and direction.
- **Caller/timekeeper:** calls one-second beats.
- **Recorder:** marks position and motion state at each beat.

In groups of three, the caller also records.

**Sequence for the 35-minute block:**

- **5 minutes — Learn the model rules**
  - At each one-second call, the object student either remains still or takes the same size step in the current direction.
  - If the displayed forces are balanced, the motion state does not change.
  - If the displayed forces are unbalanced, the object changes its motion on the next beat: starts, speeds up by taking a larger step, slows down, or changes direction.
  - Force students do not touch the object student. Their arrows represent forces.

- **8 minutes — Scenario 1: At rest with balanced forces**
  - The object begins stationary.
  - Force students display equal arrows in opposite directions, such as 2 N left and 2 N right.
  - The object remains at the same tape mark for four beats.
  - One force student changes to a larger arrow. The object begins stepping toward the unbalanced force.

- **8 minutes — Scenario 2: Motion with no unbalanced force**
  - The object begins with one equal-sized step per beat.
  - Students display either no horizontal force arrows or equal opposite arrows.
  - The object continues in the same direction with the same step size.
  - A force arrow opposing the motion is then introduced, so the object reduces its step size and stops.
  - An arrow at right angles is displayed symbolically, and the object turns rather than simply speeding up or slowing down.

- **8 minutes — Scenario 3: Mass as inertia**
  - Groups run two paper versions of the same motion scenario, labelled “low mass” and “high mass.”
  - A fixed unbalanced-force arrow is displayed for one beat.
  - Under the model rule, the low-mass object changes by two motion units while the high-mass object changes by one motion unit.
  - Students enact both cases and compare the position records.
  - The teacher makes clear that students are modelling different masses; the body sizes of the students are not being compared.

- **4 minutes — Create and exchange a scenario**
  - Each group writes a sequence of force-arrow instructions for another group, including one balanced and one unbalanced interval.
  - The receiving group predicts and enacts the resulting motion.

- **2 minutes — Check the model**
  - Groups identify which outcomes are consequences of Newton’s First Law and which features are model conventions, such as the chosen step sizes.

### 3. Science payoff
This mechanism separates three quantities that students often confuse: force, motion, and change in motion.

Students should conclude that:

- Balanced forces do not necessarily mean “not moving.” They mean no change in velocity.
- An object already in motion continues at constant speed and direction when the net force is zero.
- An unbalanced force changes velocity by causing an object to start, stop, speed up, slow down, or turn.
- The direction of the unbalanced force determines the direction of the change in motion, not necessarily the object’s current direction of travel.
- With the same unbalanced force, a greater mass has a smaller change in motion because it has greater inertia.

This mechanism is especially useful for confronting the everyday misconception that a forward force must remain present simply to keep an object moving.

### 4. Evidence
Students produce a motion record for each scenario. At every one-second beat, they record:

- leftward force;
- rightward force;
- net force and its direction;
- position or step number;
- motion state: at rest, constant velocity, speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction.

For example:

| Time | Left force | Right force | Net force | Position | Motion description |
|---|---:|---:|---|---:|---|
| 0 s | 0 N | 0 N | 0 N | 0 | Moving right |
| 1 s | 2 N | 2 N | 0 N | 1 | Same velocity |
| 2 s | 2 N | 2 N | 0 N | 2 | Same velocity |
| 3 s | 4 N | 1 N | 3 N left | 2.5 | Slowing |

Students also draw position dots at equal time intervals. Equal spacing represents constant velocity, increasing spacing represents speeding up, and decreasing spacing represents slowing down.

The exchanged scenario gives a further recordable test: another group should produce the predicted motion from the same force sequence.

### 5. Feasibility
The mechanism requires only tape, cardboard, rulers for drawing arrows, and stopwatches if desired. No specialised equipment or room reconfiguration is needed. Seven groups operate in separate 1 m lanes beside their fixed benches.

Movement is restricted to slow steps, and force students never push or pull classmates. Roles rotate so that all students physically enact or record at least one scenario. The teacher can stop all groups at common checkpoints to correct the model before they proceed.

Setup consists of placing two tape marks for each group and distributing pre-cut cardboard. Cleanup requires removing fourteen tape marks and collecting the cardboard arrows.

### 6. Failure risk
**Most likely failure:** Students may treat the enactment rules as proof of Newton’s First Law rather than recognising that this is a scientific model constructed to represent the law.

**Mitigation:**

- State explicitly that the simulation does not discover the law through the students’ bodies; it makes the consequences of the law visible and testable for logical consistency.
- Connect each model rule to an observable example during synthesis, such as a toy car slowing because friction acts or the marble remaining over the cup during a rapid card pull.
- Require groups to distinguish “what the model represents” from “what is physically happening.” For example, cardboard arrows represent forces; they are not themselves forces on the student.
- Avoid using different students’ body sizes to represent different masses. Use labelled low-mass and high-mass scenarios so the mass comparison does not depend on strength, balance, or willingness to move.
